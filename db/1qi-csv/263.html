第一章 Chapter I
她曾以为在所有的婚姻中，这次婚姻将是一场冒险。倒不是那个男人本身对她极具魔力。他是个身材矮小、精瘦、相貌古怪的家伙，大她二十岁，有着棕色的眼睛和日渐灰白的头发。许多年前还是个小男孩时，他就从荷兰来美国了，当时他还是个小流浪儿。后来他从西部的金矿被赶到南部，去了墨西哥。现在他或多或少也算是个有钱人了，在马德雷山脉的荒地中拥有银矿。所以很明显，冒险之处在于他复杂的生活经历，而不是他本人。然而尽管历经磨难，身材矮小的他仍然是个精力充沛的人，他所取得的成就都是靠自己一个人完成的。这也是无法解释的人类奇事之一。 She had thought that this marriage, of all marriages, would be an adventure.Not that the man himself was exactly magical to her.A little, wiry, twisted fellow, twenty years older than herself, with brown eyes and greying hair, who had come to America a scrap of a wastrel, from Holland, years ago, as a tiny boy, and from the gold-mines of the west had been kicked south into Mexico, and now was more or less rich, owning silver-mines in the wilds of the Sierra Madre: it was obvious that the adventure lay in his circumstances, rather than his person.But he was still a little dynamo of energy, in spite of accidents survived, and what he had accomplished he had accomplished alone.One of those human oddments there is no accounting for.
当她亲眼目睹了他的成就时，不禁心生畏惧。被绿色覆盖的巨大山地和丘陵绵延不绝，而在这荒无人烟的地方，淡粉色的出自银矿的干土堆鲜明地显现了出来。在那些光秃秃的银矿下面是围墙圈着的砖坯平房。房里有个花园，还有个深深的内走廊，两侧爬满了热带攀缘植物。当你从这花丛围满的封闭式院子向上看时，你会看到用银泥废料堆成的巨大的粉红色圆锥体，还有顶天放着的提取设备用的器械。此外，再没别的了。 When she actually saw what he had accomplished, her heart quailed.Great green-covered, unbroken mountain-hills, and in the midst of the lifeless isolation, the sharp pinkish mounds of the dried mud from the silver-works.Under the nakedness of the works, the walled-in, one-storey adobe house, with its garden inside, and its deep inner verandah with tropical climbers on the sides.And when you looked up from this shut-in flowered patio, you saw the huge pink cone of the silver-mud refuse, and the machinery of the extracting plant against heaven above.No more.
当然，巨大的木门是常开着的。她会站在外面，站在广袤开阔的天地中。她会看着高高的、空旷的、被树木覆盖的山丘，绵延相连，不分首尾。在秋天，山丘是绿色的。而其他一切则是淡粉的、干巴巴的、抽象的。 To be sure, the great wooden doors were often open.And then she could stand outside, in the vast open world.And see great, void, tree-clad hills piling behind one another, from nowhere into nowhere.They were green in autumn time.For the rest, pinkish, stark dry, and abstract.
她的丈夫会开着他破旧的福特车，带她去那个死一般的、极为死气沉沉的、被遗忘在山峦里的西班牙小镇。那里有宏伟的、被太阳晒干了的、了无生趣的教堂，有死寂的大门，有令人绝望的有棚顶的市场。第一次去那里的时候，她看到了一只死狗躺在一个个肉摊和蔬菜摊之间，四仰八叉的，似乎永远都不会有人劳神去把它扔掉。死气中透着死气。 And in his battered Ford car her husband would take her into the dead, thrice-dead little Spanish town forgotten among the mountains.The great, sundried dead church, the dead portales, the hopeless covered market-place, where, the first time she went, she saw a dead dog lying between the meat stalls and the vegetable array, stretched out as if for ever, nobody troubling to throw it away.Deadness within deadness.
每个人都一边无力地谈论着银市，一边给别人看小块的银矿石。但是银市停业了。大战来来去去、变化不断。银市很萧条。她丈夫的银矿关闭了。但是她和他还生活在银矿下的砖坯房里。四周虽花丛围绕，但她却从不觉得繁花似锦。 Everybody feebly talking silver, and showing bits of ore.But silver was at a standstill.The great war came and went.Silver was a dead market.Her husband’s mines were closed down.But she and he lived on in the adobe house under the works, among the flowers that were never very flowery to her.
她有两个孩子，一个男孩，一个女孩。老大是男孩，他快十岁时，她才从那种一直被束缚的麻木中惊醒过来。如今她三十三岁，是个身材高大、蓝眼睛、神情有些恍惚的女人，身材开始发福了。她那身材矮小、精瘦而结实、相貌古怪、棕色眼睛的丈夫已经五十三岁了，身体如电缆一般强硬而坚韧，依旧那么精力充沛，只是因为银市的萧条，以及妻子对自己莫名的隔阂而略显消沉。 She had two children, a boy and a girl.And her eldest, the boy, was nearly ten years old before she aroused from her stupor of subjected amazement.She was now thirty-three, a large, blue-eyed, dazed woman, beginning to grow stout.Her little, wiry, tough, twisted, brown-eyed husband was fifty-three, a man as tough as wire, tenacious as wire, still full of energy, but dimmed by the lapse of silver from the market, and by some curious inaccessibility on his wife’s part.
他是一个有原则的人，是一个好丈夫。从某种程度上说，他对她宠爱有加。他从未摆脱对她的那种另他神魂颠倒的倾慕之情。但是本质上，他还是个单身汉。十岁的时候，当他还是个小单身汉时，就被遗弃到了这个世界上。他结婚时已经四十多了，已经有钱并结婚生活下去了。但是他的资产完全归一个单身汉自己所有。他掌控着自己的事业，而婚姻是他事业上的最后一环，也是最密切的一环。 He was a man of principles, and a good husband.In a way, he doted on her.He never quite got over his dazzled admiration of her.But essentially, he was still a bachelor.He had been thrown out on the world, a little bachelor, at the age of ten.When he married he was over forty, and had enough money to marry on.But his capital was all a bachelor’s.He was boss of his own works, and marriage was the last and most intimate bit of his own works.
他对妻子的欣赏举世无双；他欣赏她的身体，她所有的一切。对他而言，她永远都是最初认识时那个耀眼的、来自加州伯克利的女孩。像任何酋长一样，他将她置于奇瓦瓦山脉的保护之中。他小心守护着她，就像护着他的银矿一样：这说明了很多东西。 He admired his wife to extinction, he admired her body, all her points.And she was to him always the rather dazzling Californian girl from Berkeley, whom he had first known.Like any sheik, he kept her guarded among those mountains of Chihuahua.He was jealous of her as he was of his silver-mine: and that is saying a lot.
三十三岁了，除体形外，在其他方面，她真的还是那个来自伯克利的女孩。她的意识从结婚起就不可思议地不再发展了，完全停滞了。对她而言，她的丈夫从来都不是真实存在的，无论是精神上，还是生理上。尽管他对她有几分迟来的激情，但生理上，他于她而言什么都不是。只是在精神上，他左右着她，压制她，让她绝对地臣服。 At thirty-three she really was still the girl from Berkeley, in all but physique.Her conscious development had stopped mysteriously with her marriage, completely arrested.Her husband had never become real to her, neither mentally nor physically.In spite of his late sort of passion for her, he never meant anything to her, physically.Only morally he swayed her, downed her, kept her in an invincible slavery.
多少年来，洒满阳光的院子四周围着砖房，上面便是银矿。她丈夫从来都呆不住。当银市歇业时，他就在山下大概二十里开外的地方经营牧场，饲养纯种公猪，一种很好养的牲畜。同时，他又厌恶猪。他是个理想主义者，也是个爱吹毛求疵的流浪儿，他着实痛恨生活中物质的一面。他喜欢工作，工作，工作，喜欢制造东西。他的婚姻，他的孩子们，都是他制造出来的，是他生意的一部分，只不过收益的是情感。 So the years went by, in the adobe house strung round the sunny patio, with the silver-works overhead.Her husband was never still.When the silver went dead, he ran a ranch lower down, some twenty miles away, and raised pure-bred hogs, splendid creatures.At the same time, he hated pigs.He was a squeamish waif of an idealist, and really hated the physical side of life.He loved work, work, work, and making things.His marriage, his children, were something he was making, part of his business, but with a sentimental income this time.
渐渐地，她的精神方面开始出现问题：她必须走出去。她一定要走出去。所以他带她到埃尔帕索呆了三个月。至少那里还是美国。 Gradually her nerves began to go wrong: she must get out.She must get out.So he took her to El Paso for three months.And at least it was the United States.
但是他束缚她的魔咒还在。三个月结束了，她的人虽然回来了，但和以前一样，还是呆在她的砖房里，呆在常年是绿色或粉褐色的山丘之间。而唯一没有被发现的就是空虚。她教育孩子，管理墨西哥僮仆。有时候他丈夫带客人回来，西班牙人或者墨西哥人，偶尔也有白人。 But he kept his spell over her.The three months ended: back she was, just the same, in her adobe house among those eternal green or pinky-brown hills, void as only the undiscovered is void.She taught her children, she supervised the Mexican boys who were her servants.And sometimes her husband brought visitors, Spaniards or Mexicans or occasionally white men.
他十分喜欢有白人呆在这个地方。尽管他们在的时候，他就没有一刻安宁。他的妻子就好像是他矿里的某种独特的秘密矿脉，没人可以知道，除了他自己。她会被有时来作客的年轻绅士，也就是采矿工程师给迷住。他也被真正的绅士所吸引。但他是个有妻室的守旧矿工，如果有绅士看他的妻子，他就觉得好像自己的矿藏被抢掠了，秘密被探出了一样。 He really loved to have white men staying on the place.Yet he had not a moment’s peace when they were there.It was as if his wife were some peculiar secret vein of ore in his mines, which no one must be aware of except himself.And she was fascinated by the young gentlemen, mining engineers, who were his guests at times.He, too, was fascinated by a real gentleman.But he was an old-timer miner with a wife, and if a gentleman looked at his wife, he felt as if his mine were being looted, the secrets of it pryed out.
在这些年轻绅士中，有一位让她有了想法。当时他们正站在院子的高大木门外，看着外面的世界。九月的雨后，亘古不动的山峦满是绿色。除了废弃的矿井，废弃的银矿和一排半废弃的矿工居所外，没有任何其他东西的痕迹了。 It was one of these young gentlemen who put the idea into her mind.They were all standing outside the great wooden doors of the patio, looking at the outer world.The eternal, motionless hills were all green, it was September, after the rains.There was no sign of anything, save the deserted mine, the deserted works, and a bunch of half-deserted miner’s dwellings.
“我想知道，”那个年轻人说道，“这些荒芜的大山背后是什么。” “I wonder,” said the young man, “what there is behind those great blank hills.”
“更多的山，”莱德曼说，“如果你朝那边走，是索诺拉河和海岸。朝这边是沙漠——你就是从那里来的——而另一边，是丘陵和高山。” “More hills,” said Lederman.“If you go that way, Sonora and the coast.This way is the desert—you came from there—And the other way, hills and mountains.”
“是啊，但是在那些丘陵和高山里有什么活物呢？肯定有什么奇妙的东西吧？那边看上去完全不像是在地球上，而像是在月球上。” “Yes, but what lives in the hills and mountains?Surely there is something wonderful?It looks so like nowhere on earth: like being on the moon.”
“那里有很多猎物，如果你想打猎的话。还有印第安人，如果你觉得他们奇妙的话。” “There’s plenty of game, if you want to shoot.And Indians, if you call them wonderful.”
“野蛮的部落？” “Wild ones?”
“是够野蛮的。” “Wild enough.”
“那友善吗？” “But friendly?”
“不一定。有些非常野蛮，而且他们不让任何人靠近。他们一看到传教士就杀。而传教士都去不了的地方，就没人能去了。” “It depends.Some of them are quite wild, and they don’t let anybody near.They kill a missionary at sight.And where a missionary can’t get, nobody can.”
“那政府是怎么说的？” “But what does the government say?”
“政府就鞭长莫及了，根本不管他们。他们很狡猾。如果他们认为有麻烦，就会派个代表团去奇瓦瓦，正式表示臣服。政府也就乐得如此。” “They’re so far from everywhere, the government leaves ‘em alone.And they’re wily; if they think there’ll be trouble, they send a delegation to Chihuahua and make a formal submission.The government is glad to leave it at that.”
“他们就过那种原始的生活，保持他们野蛮人自己的风俗和宗教？” “And do they live quite wild, with their own savage customs and religion?”
“噢，对呀。他们只用弓箭。我在镇上就见过他们，在集市里面，戴着滑稽的帽子，上面插着花。他们一手拿弓，全身就穿了一件衬衫样的衣服，甚至天冷时也只穿这个——露着他们的野腿到处大步走。” “Oh, yes.They use nothing but bows and arrows.I’ve seen them in town, in the Plaza, with funny sort of hats with flowers round them, and a bow in one hand, quite naked except for a sort of shirt, even in cold weather—striding round with their savage’s bare legs.”
“可是，你不觉得这很奇妙吗，呆在山上他们那秘密的村庄里？” “But don’t you suppose it’s wonderful, up there in their secret villages?”
“不觉得。这有什么奇妙的？野蛮人就是野蛮人，所有野蛮人的举止都差不多：下贱、肮脏、不讲卫生，还会耍些花招，挣扎着填饱肚皮。” “No. What would there be wonderful about it?Savages are savages, and all savages behave more or less alike: rather low-down and dirty, unsanitary, with a few cunning tricks, and struggling to get enough to eat.”
“但是他们肯定有很古老、很古老的宗教和神秘仪式——一定很奇妙，一定。” “But surely they have old, old religions and mysteries—it must be wonderful, surely it must.”
“我不知道那些个神秘仪式——嗷嗷直叫，还有异教徒的一些做法，多少有些不雅。不，我看不出那种东西有什么奇妙的。我真不明白，你曾在伦敦、巴黎或者纽约住过，竟然会——” “I don’t know about mysteries—howling and heathen practices, more or less indecent.No, I see nothing wonderful in that kind of stuff.And I wonder that you should, when you have lived in London or Paris or New York—”
“啊，每个住在伦敦、巴黎或纽约的人——”那个年轻人说道，好像在和人辩论一样。 “Ah, everybody lives in London or Paris or New York—” said the young man, as if this were an argument.
他对于未知的印第安人那种特别而朦胧的热情在这个女人的心中引起了极强的共鸣。她被一种傻乎乎的浪漫情怀所征服，比少女的还要不真实。她感到命中注定她会陷入对群山里那些永恒、神秘、奇妙的印第安人的秘密思绪之中。 And his peculiar vague enthusiasm for unknown Indians found a full echo in the woman’s heart.She was overcome by a foolish romanticism more unreal than a girl’s.She felt it was her destiny to wander into the secret haunts of these timeless, mysterious, marvellous Indians of the mountains.
她保守着自己的秘密。那个年轻人要离开了，她的丈夫要和他一道去托雷翁办事——要离开一段日子。不过在离开前，她让丈夫给她讲了讲印第安人：讲游牧部落，这些部落的人很像至今仍在自由游牧的纳瓦霍人，还有索诺拉的雅基族人，以及奇瓦瓦州各个山谷里住着的不同部落。 She kept her secret.The young man was departing, her husband was going with him down to Torreon, on business:—would be away for some days.But before the departure, she made her husband talk about the Indians: about the wandering tribes, resembling the Navajo, who were still wandering free; and the Yaquis of Sonora: and the different groups in the different valleys of Chihuahua State.
应该有这么一个部落叫西尔西威部落，生活在南部的大峡谷中，是全印第安人中的神圣部落。摩特祖玛和古阿兹特克的后裔或者托托纳克的国王还和他们住在一起；老祭司们保留着古老的宗教，还保持着人祭的传统——据说是这样。有些科学家去过西尔西威村，由于饱尝饥饿和艰辛，回来时都面容憔悴、疲惫不堪。他们带回了各色古怪的野蛮人崇拜的物件。不过，并没有在这个饥馑荒凉的野蛮人村庄里见到什么特别离奇的事情。 There was supposed to be one tribe, the Chilchuis, living in a high valley to the south, who were the sacred tribe of all the Indians.The descendants of Montezuma and of the old Aztec or Totonac kings still lived among them, and the old priests still kept up the ancient religion, and offered human sacrifices—so it was said.Some scientists had been to the Chilchui country, and had come back gaunt and exhausted with hunger and bitter privation, bringing various curious, barbaric objects of worship, but having seen nothing extraordinary in the hungry, stark village of savages.
尽管莱德曼只是随口一讲，但很明显，他谈论古老又神秘的野蛮人时，感受到的是某种粗俗的兴奋。 Though Lederman talked in this off-hand way, it was obvious he felt some of the vulgar excitement at the idea of ancient and mysterious savages.
“他们离这里有多远？”她问。 “How far away are they?” she asked.
“哦——骑马三天的路程——穿过库奇提和一个小湖泊，往上走就是。” “Oh—three days on horseback—past Cuchitee and a little lake there is up there.”
她丈夫和那个年轻人出发了。女人给自己制订了疯狂的计划。近来，为了改变自己枯燥的生活，她磨着丈夫准许她偶尔和他一起骑在马背上。她从未被准许单独外出过。这里确实不安全，是无法无天的荒蛮之地。 Her husband and the young man departed.The woman made her crazy plans.Of late, to break the monotony of her life, she had harassed her husband into letting her go riding with him, occasionally, on horseback.She was never allowed to go out alone.The country truly was not safe, lawless and crude.
但她有自己的马，从孩提起，她就梦想着自由自在地徜徉在加州山间。 But she had her own horse, and she dreamed of being free as she had been as a girl, among the hills of California.
她九岁的女儿当时在五英里外，一个半废弃的西班牙矿区小镇上一家极小的女修道院里。 Her daughter, nine years old, was now in a tiny convent in the little half-deserted Spanish mining-town five miles away.
“曼纽尔，”她对她的家奴说道，“我要骑马去修道院看玛格丽塔，给她带些东西。我也许会在修道院过夜。你照顾好弗雷迪，我回来前要料理好所有事情。” “Manuel,” said the woman to her house-servant, “I’m going to ride to the convent to see Margarita, and take her a few things.Perhaps I shall stay the night in the convent.You look after Freddy and see everything is all right till I come back.”
“要我陪您一起骑老爷的马去吗，或者胡安陪您去？”仆人问道。 “Shall I ride with you on the master’s horse, or shall Juan?” asked the servant.
“你们两个都不用去。我自己去。” “Neither of you.I shall go alone.”
那个小伙子用抗议的目光直视她的眼睛。她自己单独骑马过去是完全不可能的！ The young man looked her in the eyes, in protest.Absolutely impossible that the woman should ride alone!
“我要自己去。”那个身材高大、面容温和、肤色白皙的女人用特别专横的语气强调了一遍。仆人缄默，不高兴地服从了。 “I shall go alone,” repeated the large, placid-seeming, fair-complexioned woman, with peculiar overbearing emphasis.And the man silently, unhappily yielded.
“为什么你要一个人去呢，妈妈？”当她在打包食物时，她儿子问道。 “Why are you going alone, mother?” asked her son, as she made up parcels of food.
“我就不能自己做点儿什么？这辈子一刻也不行？”她突然暴怒，大叫道。和仆人一样，孩子瑟缩着沉默了。 “Am I never to be let alone?Not one moment of my life?” she cried, with sudden explosion of energy.And the child, like the servant, shrank into silence.
她毫无疑惧地出发了，跨骑着她健壮的杂色马，穿着粗麻骑装，亚麻马裤上套着马裙，白罩衫上配着一条鲜红的领结，头戴黑毡帽。鞍囊里装着食物和装了水的军用水壶，马鞍后面捆了一大张土著人的披毯。她凝视着远处，从家里出发了。曼纽尔和这个小男孩站在门廊目送她离开。她甚至都没有回头向他们挥手告别。 She set off without a qualm, riding astride on her strong roan horse, and wearing a riding suit of coarse linen, a riding skirt over her linen breeches, a scarlet neck-tie over her white blouse, and a black felt hat on her head.She had food in her saddle-bags, an army canteen with water, and a large, native blanket tied on behind the saddle.Peering into the distance, she set off from her home.Manuel and the little boy stood in the gateway to watch her go.She did not even turn to wave them farewell.
然而，当她骑了大约一公里后，她没有走大路，而是走了右面的一条小路。这条小路经过陡峭的地区和高大的树木，穿过另一处荒废的矿区，通往另一个山谷。九月里，水在溪涧里自由地流淌，灌满了现已荒废的矿井。她下马去喝了点儿水，也让马饮了水。 But when she had ridden about a mile, she left the wild road and took a small trail to the right, that led into another valley, over steep places and past great trees, and through another deserted mining-settlement.It was September, the water was running freely in the little stream that had fed the now-abandoned mine.She got down to drink, and let the horse drink too.
她看到一些土著人穿过树林，上了斜坡。他们看见她了，正紧紧地盯着她看。她也反过来盯着他们。一共有三个人，两个女人和一个小伙子，正在绕一个大弯儿，以避免离她太近。她毫不在乎。她上了马，马儿一路小跑着向静谧的山谷上奔去，远离银矿，远离所有有矿井踪迹的地方。那里还有一条崎岖不平的小路，穿过岩石和松散的石头，通向远处的山谷。这条小道，她曾经和丈夫骑马走过。除此之外，她知道她必须往南走。 She saw natives coming through the trees, away up the slope.They had seen her, and were watching her closely.She watched in turn.The three people, two women and a youth, were making a wide detour, so as not to come too close to her.She did not care.Mounting, she trotted ahead up the silent valley, beyond the silver-works, beyond any trace of mining.There was still a rough trail, that led over rocks and loose stones into the valley beyond.This trail she had already ridden, with her husband.Beyond that she knew she must go south.
说来也怪，她并不害怕，尽管这是一个令人害怕的乡村，那沉寂的、看上去要命的山坡，树林里偶尔出现的那些冷漠、多疑、难以捉摸的土著人，还有远处巨大的食腐鸟，像巨型苍蝇一样间或盘旋在某块腐肉、某间牧场主住宅或某排茅舍的上方。 Curiously she was not afraid, although it was a frightening country, the silent, fatal-seeming mountain-slopes, the occasional distant, suspicious, elusive natives among the trees, the great carrion birds occasionally hovering, like great flies, in the distance, over some carrion or some ranch house or some group of huts.
她向上爬着，树木向后退去，小路穿过一个荆棘丛生的低矮灌木丛，上面蔓生着众多蓝色的旋花，间或出现一个粉红色的匍匐植物。然后，这些花木渐渐消失。她接近松树林了。 As she climbed, the trees shrank and the trail ran through a thorny scrub, that was trailed over with blue convolvulus and an occasional pink creeper.Then these flowers lapsed.She was nearing the pine-trees.
她已经到了坡顶，在她面前的是又一个宁静空旷、郁郁葱葱的山谷。已经过了中午了。她的马跑向一条小溪，于是她就下马去吃午餐。她默默地坐着看那一动不动的、没有生气的山谷，看着尖顶的山丘向上伸向南面的岩石和松树林。在气温最高的正午，她休息了两个小时，并让马就在身边吃草。 She was over the crest, and before her another silent, void, green-clad valley.It was past midday.Her horse turned to a little runlet of water, so she got down to eat her midday meal.She sat in silence looking at the motionless unliving valley, and at the sharp-peaked hills, rising higher to rock and pine-trees, southwards.She rested two hours in the heat of the day, while the horse cropped around her.
奇怪的是，她既不感到害怕，也不感到孤独。实际上，孤独就像给极度口渴的人饮凉水一般。一种莫名的振奋之感在体内支撑着她。 Curious that she was neither afraid nor lonely.Indeed, the loneliness was like a drink of cold water to one who is very thirsty.And a strange elation sustained her from within.
她继续向前走，晚上在山谷一条小溪边的灌木丛里露营。她看见了牛群，也穿过了几条小道。不远处肯定是座牧场。她听见一只美洲狮奇怪的哀号声和回应的狗吠声。但是当她身处隐秘的空旷之地，坐在小小的篝火旁时，她并没有怎么真正感到害怕。内心里某种奇怪的、喜悦振奋的情绪一直在鼓舞着她。 She travelled on, and camped at night in a valley beside a stream, deep among the bushes.She had seen cattle and had crossed several trails.There must be a ranch not far off.She heard the strange wailing shriek of a mountain-lion, and the answer of dogs.But she sat by her small camp fire in a secret hollow place and was not really afraid.She was buoyed up always by the curious, bubbling elation within her.
天亮之前，天气异常寒冷。她裹在毯子里躺着，看着星空，听马直打哆嗦，觉得自己像个已经死去、灵魂超脱了的女人。夜里，她似乎听到从自己身体的中心发出一声巨大的破裂声，这就是她自己死亡的声音。又或者是地球中心的破裂声，意味着某件重大而神秘的事件。 It was very cold before dawn.She lay wrapped in her blanket looking at the stars, listening to her horse shivering, and feeling like a woman who has died and passed beyond.She was not sure that she had not heard, during the night, a great crash at the centre of herself, which was the crash of her own death.Or else it was a crash at the centre of the earth, and meant something big and mysterious.
当第一缕阳光隐约可见时，她就起来了。她整个人都冻麻木了，于是生了火。很快她就吃完了早饭，并给马喂了几块油籽饼，然后就再次出发了。她避免与任何人邂逅——她没有遇见任何人，很明显她也被别人避开了。终于库西蒂村出现在她眼前，一小片带微红色屋顶的黑房子，昏暗而沉闷，聚集在另一处寂静的、长期荒废的矿井下面。远处，长长的巍峨山腰向上延伸，山色由鲜绿而明亮渐渐变为松树那种较为暗沉而杂乱的绿色。松树林再向前是光秃秃的岩石顶空而立。岩石已经裂开，上面斑斑驳驳地覆盖着一条条的白色雪带。高空中，雪又开始下了。 With the first peep of light she got up, numb with cold, and made a fire.She ate hastily, gave her horse some pieces of oil-seed cake, and set off again.She avoided any meeting—and since she met nobody, it was evident that she in turn was avoided.She came at last in sight of the village of Cuchitee, with its black houses with their reddish roofs, a sombre, dreary little cluster below another silent, long-abandoned mine.And beyond, a long, great mountain-side, rising up green and light to the darker, shaggier green of pine trees.And beyond the pine trees stretches of naked rock against the sky, rock slashed already and brindled with white stripes of snow.High up, the new snow had already begun to fall.
此时，她差不多快到目的地了，她开始变得有些茫然和沮丧。她绕过白杨林间的小湖。白杨树叶子正在变黄，白色的树枝圆而平滑，像女人白皙圆润的臂膀。多可爱的地方啊！如果是在加州，她早就热情颂扬起来了。但是在这里，她看着虽赏心悦目，却并不在意。她累了，在野外过了两夜，又害怕起将要来临的夜晚。她不知道自己要去哪里，也不知道自己为了什么而去。她的马垂头丧气、沉重而缓慢地行进着，顺着一条满是石头的小道，向险峻的大山坡走去。如果她还有一丝自己的意志，她就会折回，回到村庄里去，被保护着，被人送回家，回到丈夫身边去。 And now, as she neared, more or less, her destination, she began to go vague and disheartened.She had passed the little lake among yellowing aspen trees whose white trunks were round and suave like the white round arms of some woman.What a lovely place!In California she would have raved about it.But here she looked and saw that it was lovely, but she didn’t care.She was weary and spent with her two nights in the open, and afraid of the coming night.She didn’t know where she was going, or what she was going for.Her horse plodded dejectedly on, towards that immense and forbidding mountain-slope, following a stony little trail.And if she had had any will of her own left, she would have turned back, to the village, to be protected and sent home to her husband.
可是她没有自己的意志力。她的马趟过小溪，转而向上往掩映在那无边无际、发黄的棉白杨树林下的一个山谷走去。她一定是在接近海拔九千英尺的地方，脑袋因为高海拔和疲劳而轻飘飘的。透过棉白杨树林，她可以看见两边峻峭的山坡环绕着她。山坡上显眼地披着一层层的白杨，更高的地方生长着正在抽芽的、尖尖的云杉和松树。马机械地向前走着。在这陡峭的山谷里，在这条狭窄的小道上，除了往上爬，别无他路可走。 But she had no will of her own.Her horse splashed through a brook, and turned up a valley, under immense yellowing cotton-wood trees.She must have been near nine thousand feet above sea-level, and her head was light with the altitude and with weariness.Beyond the cotton-wood trees she could see, on each side, the steep sides of mountain-slopes hemming her in, sharp-plumaged with overlapping aspen, and, higher up, with sprouting, pointed spruce and pine tree.Her horse went on automatically.In this tight valley, on this slight trail, there was nowhere to go but ahead, climbing.
突然，马受惊跳了起来，她面前的小道上出现了三个披着深色毯子的男人。 Suddenly her horse jumped, and three men in dark blankets were on the trail before her.
“一路平安！”传来带印第安口音的饱满而拘谨的问候声。 “Adios!” came the greeting, in the full, restrained Indian voice.
“一路平安！”她用美国女性自信的声音回答道。 “Adios!” she replied, in her assured, American woman’s voice.
“你要去哪儿？”对方用西班牙语平静地问道。 “Where are you going?” came the quiet question, in Spanish.
这些穿深色瑟拉佩披肩的男人走近了些，正抬头看她着。 The men in the dark sarapes had come closer, and were looking up at her.
“往前去。”她用生硬的萨克逊式的西班牙语冷淡地回答道。 “On ahead,” she replied coolly, in her hard, Saxon Spanish.
对她而言，他们只是几个土著人，几个面庞浅黑、身材健壮、穿深色瑟拉佩披肩和戴草帽的男人。他们本来看起来和她丈夫的工人没什么不同，只是很奇怪，他们都长发过肩。她注意到了他们长长的黑头发，不由心生厌恶。这些人多半是她要看的野蛮的印第安人。 These were just natives to her: dark-faced, strongly-built men in dark sarapes and straw hats.They would have been the same as the men who worked for her husband, except, strangely, for the long black hair that fell over their shoulders.She noted this long black hair with a certain distaste.These must be the wild Indians she had come to see.
“你从哪儿来？”那个人又问道。说话的人总是他。他很年轻，灵敏而明亮的黑色大眼睛斜望着她。他浅黑色的脸庞上长着柔软的黑色胡髭，下巴上长着一撮稀疏的胡须和松散的毛发。他黑色的长发，充满活力，随意地披散在肩头。尽管他肤色浅黑，但看起来好像最近都没有梳洗过。 “Where do you come from?” the same man asked.It was always the one man who spoke.He was young, with quick, large, bright black eyes that glanced sideways at her.He had a soft black moustache on his dark face, and a sparse tuft of beard, loose hairs on his chin.His long black hair, full of life, hung unrestrained on his shoulders.Dark as he was, he did not look as if he had washed lately.
他的两个同伴也是一样，但看上去年纪大些，威严而沉默。一个留着一道稀疏的黑胡髭，但没有胡须。另一个脸颊光滑，下巴的两边有稀少的黑色毛发，留着印第安人典型的胡须。 His two companions were the same, but older men, powerful and silent.One had a thin black line of moustache, but was beardless.The other had the smooth cheeks and the sparse dark hairs marking the lines of his chin with the beard characteristic of the Indians.
“我来自远方。”她回答道，半开玩笑似的回避了问题。 “I come from far away,” she replied, with half-jocular evasion.
接着是一阵沉默。 This was received in silence.
“那你住在哪里？”年轻人用同样平静的语气继续问道。 “But where do you live?” asked the young man, with that same quiet insistence.
“北方。”她漫不经心地说。 “In the north,” she replied airily.
又是一阵沉默。年轻人和另两个同伴用印第安语轻声交谈着。 Again there was a moment’s silence.The young man conversed quietly, in Indian, with his two companions.
“你往这边走，是要去哪里？”他突然带着质疑和权威的口吻问道，指了指那条小路。 “Where do you want to go, up this way?” he asked suddenly, with challenge and authority, pointing briefly up the trail.
“去西尔西威印第安人那里。”她回答得很干脆。 “To the Chilchui Indians,” answered the woman laconically.
年轻人看着她。他的黑眼睛敏锐而不近人情。在饱满的落日霞光中，他看见她镇定自若、肤色鲜嫩的大脸盘上微微闪现出一种自信的浅笑，蓝色大眼睛下有疲惫的浅蓝色眼圈。在她俯视他时，她的眼中有一种来自女性自身力量的半幼稚、半傲慢的自信。但在她眼中也有一丝奇怪的恍惚的神情。 The young man looked at her.His eyes were quick and black, and inhuman.He saw, in the full evening light, the faint sub-smile of assurance on her rather large, calm, fresh-complexioned face; the weary, bluish lines under her large blue eyes; and in her eyes, as she looked down at him, a half-childish, half-arrogant confidence in her own female power.But in her eyes also, a curious look of trance.
“你是位女士？”印第安人问道。 “You are a lady? ” the Indian asked her.
“对，我是位女士。”她自得地回答道。 “Yes, I am a lady,” she replied complacently.
“有家庭？” “With a family?”
“有丈夫和两个孩子，一儿一女。”她说。 “With a husband and two children, boy and girl,” she said.
这个印第安人转向同伴们，翻译给他们听，声音缓慢低沉，好似汩汩的暗泉。他们显然困惑不解。 The Indian turned to his companions and translated, in the low, gurgling speech, like hidden water running.They were evidently at a loss.
“你丈夫在哪里？”他问道。 “Where is your husband?” asked the young man.
“谁知道呢？”她满不在乎地回答，“他出差已经一个星期了。” “Who knows?” she replied airily.“He has gone away on business for a week.”
那双黑眼睛机敏地打量着她。而她，因为疲乏，微微地笑着，得意于自己的历险、自己的女性身份，以及自己身上的疯狂魔咒。 The black eyes watched her shrewdly.She, for all her weariness, smiled faintly in the pride of her own adventure and the assurance of her own womanhood, and the spell of the madness that was on her.
“那你想做什么？”印第安人问她。 “And what do you want to do?” the Indian asked her.
“我想去拜访西尔西威印第安人——看看他们的房子，了解他们的神灵。”她答道。 “I want to visit the Chilchui Indians—to see their houses and to know their gods,” she replied.
这个年轻人回过头去，迅速翻译着，接着是一片沉寂，几乎是惊呆了。那两个年长的，面色严肃，从花帽子下面，拿眼睛斜瞟她，表情怪异。然后他们用低沉的胸声对那个年轻人说了些什么。 The young man turned and translated quickly, and there was a silence almost of consternation.The grave elder men were glancing at her sideways, with strange looks, from under their decorated hats.And they said something to the young man, in deep chest voices.
后者还在犹豫。之后他转向这个女人。 The latter still hesitated.Then he turned to the woman.
“好吧！”他说，“我们走吧。但是我们明天才能到达。我们今晚得宿营。” “Good!” he said.“Let us go.But we cannot arrive until to-morrow.We shall have to make a camp to-night.”
“好的！”她说，“我可以宿营。” “Good!” she said.“I can make a camp.”
没有再说什么，他们即刻出发了，沿着多石的小道前行。年轻的印第安人和她的马头并排跑着，另两个在后面跑。其中一个拿着一根粗棍子，偶尔响亮地抽打马臀，鞭策它向前跑。马被打得蹦起来，把她颠到了马鞍的后部，尽管她很疲倦，但对此还是很恼火。 Without more ado, they set off at a good speed up the stony trail.The young Indian ran alongside her horse’s head, the other two ran behind.One of them had taken a thick stick, and occasionally he struck her horse a resounding blow on the haunch, to urge him forward.This made the horse jump, and threw her back in the saddle, which, tired as she was, made her angry.
“别这样！”她叫道，回头怒视着那个家伙。她看到了他那双乌黑明亮的大眼睛，精神第一次畏缩了。那男人的眼睛于她而言不是人类的眼睛，没有把她当作一个漂亮的白人女子在看。他用黑亮的、毫无人味的眼神看着她，根本没把她当作女人看。好像她是某种奇怪的、不可名状的东西，他无法理解，却又充满敌意。她惊奇地坐在马鞍上，又一次觉得自己好像死了一样。他再次抽打她的马，这使她在马鞍上颠簸得非常厉害。 “Don’t do that!” she cried, looking round angrily at the fellow.She met his black, large, bright eyes, and for the first time her spirit really quailed.The man’s eyes were not human to her, and they did not see her as a beautiful white woman.He looked at her with a black, bright inhuman look, and saw no woman in her at all.As if she were some strange, unaccountable thing, incomprehensible to him, but inimical.She sat in her saddle in wonder, feeling once more as if she had died.And again he struck her horse, and jerked her badly in the saddle.
这位被宠惯的白人女子心里涌出强烈的愤怒之情。她勒住马，让马停下来，眼睛里燃着怒火，转过去看着马勒旁的男人。 All the passionate anger of the spoilt white woman rose in her.She pulled her horse to a standstill, and turned with blazing eyes to the man at her bridle.
“告诉那个家伙别再碰我的马了。”她喊道。她看着年轻人的眼睛。在他那乌黑、明亮、神秘莫测的眼神里，她看到一丝亮光，像蛇眼里的光，带着嘲讽。他对后面的同伴，低声用印第安语说了几句。拿着棍子的那人没有看，只是听着。然后，他对马发出一声怪异低沉的吼叫，又朝马臀抽打了一下。于是，马抽搐地向前跳跃，奔上了多石的小道，踢得石子四处都是，疲倦的女人在马鞍上颠簸着。 “Tell that fellow not to touch my horse again,” she cried.She met the eyes of the young man, and in their bright black inscrutability she saw a fine spark, as in a snake’s eye, of derision.He spoke to his companion in the rear, in the low tones of the Indian.The man with the stick listened without looking.Then, giving a strange low cry to the horse, he struck it again on the rear, so that it leaped forward spasmodically up the stony trail, scattering the stones, pitching the weary woman in her seat.
怒气疯了般蹿进她的眼里，她两腮都气白了。她狠狠地勒住马。但她还没来得及转身，年轻的印第安人就抓住了马喉咙下的缰绳，向前一扯，带着马在前面快速地小跑起来。 The anger flew like a madness into her eyes, she went white at the gills.Fiercely she reined in her horse.But before she could turn, the young Indian had caught the reins under the horse’s throat, jerked them forward, and was trotting ahead rapidly, leading the horse.
妇人无能为力了。随着暴怒而来的是轻微的欣喜。她知道自己死了。 The woman was powerless.And along with her supreme anger there came a slight thrill of exultation.She knew she was dead.

顺着最宽的路——一条在草和落叶之间的平坦的窄径，这条小路长年累月被人的足迹踏得平整，没有马的蹄印，也没有轮印来破坏它的平整——他们来到一条湍急而清亮的小河前，穿过独木桥。那里万籁俱静，渺无人烟。这条路穿过绚烂的棉白杨树林。突然，它又出现在中央广场或者说是村庄广场的外面。 Following the broadest path—a soft narrow track between leaves and grass, a path worn smooth by centuries of human feet, no hoof of horse nor any wheel to disfigure it—they came to the little river of swift bright water, and crossed on a log bridge.Everything was silent—there was no human being anywhere.The road went on under magnificent cotton-wood trees.It emerged suddenly outside the central plaza or square of the village.
这是个由低矮的平屋顶白房子组成的长方形建族群，长方形的两端耸立着两栋高大些的建筑，错开着，看起来就像正方形的小茅屋堆在大些的长茅屋上面。每一栋小房子都是耀眼的白色——除了平屋檐下面突出的巨大圆屋梁和平屋顶外。两栋高大些的建筑周围和广场外面是一个牲畜围栏，里面是种满了花和树木的花园及各种各样的小屋子。 This was a long oblong of low white houses with flat roofs, and two bigger buildings, having as it were little square huts piled on top of bigger long huts, stood at either end of the oblong, facing each other rather askew.Every little house was a dazzling white, save for the great round beam-ends which projected under the flat eaves, and for the flat roofs.Round each of the bigger buildings, on the outside of the square, was a stockyard fence, inside which was garden with trees and flowers, and various small houses.
眼前一个人都没有。他们静静地在房子之间穿行，走到中央广场。广场很空旷也很干旱，土地被一代代走过的人踏平，连接着家家户户。所有房子没有窗户，门全朝着这空荡荡的广场，但是门都关着。门槛边有柴火，陶土炉子还在冒烟，但是没有任何可以活动的生物的痕迹。 Not a soul was in sight.They passed silently between the houses into the central square.This was quite bare and arid, the earth trodden smooth by endless generations of passing feet, passing across from door to door.All the doors of the windowless houses gave on to this blank square, but all the doors were closed.The firewood lay near the threshold, a clay oven was still smoking, but there was no sign of moving life.
老人径直穿过广场，来到位于尽头的大房子。它的上两层，就像积木搭建的房子一样，越往上，每一层就越小。屋外的石阶通往第一层的楼顶。 The old man walked straight across the square to the big house at the end, where the two upper storeys, as in a house of toy bricks, stood each one smaller than the lower one.A stone staircase, outside, led up to the roof of the first storey.
轿夫停在石阶脚下，把她放了下来。 At the foot of this staircase the litter-bearers stood still, and lowered the woman to the ground.
“你上来。”讲西班牙语的年轻印第安人说。 “You will come up,” said the young Indian who spoke Spanish.
她顺着石阶而上，到达第一栋房子的土屋顶，这屋顶围着第二层房屋的墙形成了一个平台。她沿着这个平台来到大房子的后面。从那里他们再次下来，进入后面的花园。 She mounted the stone stairs to the earthen roof of the first house, which formed a platform round the wall of the second storey.She followed around this platform to the back of the big house.There they descended again, into the garden at the rear.

屋子里很暗。里面有男人喃喃的低语声。有几个男人在场，他们的白衬衫在黑暗中很分明，黑脸却看不到了。他们坐在一根有些年头的平滑的大圆木上，与对面的墙平行放置着。除了这根圆木，房间里似乎一无所有。但是不对，在黑暗的一角有一把躺椅，像一种床，有人躺在那里，盖着毛皮。 It was dark inside.There was a low murmur of men’s voices.Several men were present, their white shirts showing in the gloom, their dark faces invisible.They were sitting on a great log of smooth old wood, that lay along the far wall.And save for this log, the room seemed empty.But no, in the dark at one end was a couch, a sort of bed, and someone lying there, covered with furs.

头发灰白的印第安人又说话了，然后年轻的印第安人拉着女人的手，带她向前。她身着亚麻骑装，黑靴黑帽，红领结已经破破烂烂了。她站在那儿，旁边是那位很老的老人的毛皮覆盖的床。这位老人支着一只胳膊肘坐起身来，遥远得像个鬼魂。他白色的长发随意披散，脸几乎是黑的，但神情遥远而专注——一种不属于这个世界的专注。他向前倾注视着她。 The grey-haired Indian spoke again, and then the young Indian, taking the woman’s hand, led her forward.In her linen riding habit, and black boots and hat, and her pathetic bit of a red tie, she stood there beside the fur-covered bed of the old, old man, who sat reared up, leaning on one elbow, remote as a ghost, his white hair streaming in disorder, his face almost black, yet with a far-off intentness, not of this world, leaning forward to look at her.
他的面容十分衰老，就像深色的玻璃一般，嘴唇周围和下巴上不可思议地长出几根卷曲的白色毛发。白色的长发没有编辫，披散在那有如深色玻璃的脸颊两侧。在刷了一层薄粉似的白色眉毛下面，老酋长用那双黑色的眼睛看着她；那眼神像是死了很久很久的人的，看到了一些永远都看不到的东西似的。 His face was so old, it was like dark glass, and the few curling hairs that sprang white from his lips and chin were quite incredible.The long white locks fell unbraided and disorderly on either side of the glassy dark face.And under a faint powder of white eyebrows, the black eyes of the old chief looked at her as if from the far, far dead, seeing something that was never to be seen.
最后，他声音低沉地说了几句空洞的话，像是对着黑暗的空气说的。 At last he spoke a few deep, hollow words, as if to the dark air.
“他说，你把自己的心带来给西尔西威的神灵吗？”年轻的印第安人翻译道。 “He says, do you bring your heart to the god of the Chilchui?” translated the young Indian.
“告诉他说是的。”她机械地说。 “Tell him yes,” she said, automatically.
停顿了一会儿。年老的印第安人又开始说话，似乎是对着空气说的。　其中一个在场的人出去了。一阵沉默，似乎是永恒的沉默。在这间暗黑的屋子里，只有敞开的门带来一线光亮。 There was a pause.The old Indian spoke again, as if to the air.One of the men present went out.There was a silence as if of eternity, in the dim room that was lighted only through the open door.
女人环视四周。四个头发灰白的老人坐在靠墙、对着门的圆木上。另外两个人，强壮而冷漠，站在门边。他们都留着长发，穿白色衬衣，束在腰布里。他们健壮的腿赤裸着，肤色浅黑。一阵永恒似的沉默。 The woman looked round.Four old men with grey hair sat on the log by the wall facing the door.Two other men, powerful and impassive, stood near the door.They all had long hair, and wore white shirts gathered into a loin-cloth.Their powerful legs were naked and dark.There was a silence like eternity.

“你必须脱掉你的衣服，穿上这些。” “You must take off your clothes, and put these on.”
“如果你们所有男人出去的话就行。”她说。 “If all you men will go out,” she said.
“没有人会伤害你的。”他轻声说道。 “No one will hurt you,” he said quietly.
“如果你们男人在这里的话，不行。”她说。 “Not while you men are here,” she said.
他朝门边的两人看了一眼。他们迅速过来，突然趁她站着时抓住她的胳膊，没有伤到她，但是抓得很用力。然后，两个老人走过来，用锋利的刀子很有技巧地划开她的靴子，然后剥下来，接着划开她的衣服，让它们从她身上滑落。一会儿工夫，她站在那里，白白的，一丝不挂。在床上的老人说话了，于是他们把她转向他，让他看看。他又说话了，年轻的印第安人灵巧地把发卡和发插从她金色的头发里取下来，让头发一团乱地披落在她肩头。 He looked at the two men by the door.They came quickly forward, and suddenly gripped her arms as she stood, without hurting her, but with great power.Then two of the old men came, and with curious skill slit her boots down with keen knives, and drew them off, and slit her clothing so that it came away from her.In a few moments she stood there white and uncovered.The old man on the bed spoke, and they turned her round for him to see.He spoke again, and the young Indian deftly took the pins and comb from her fair hair, so that it fell over her shoulders in a bunchy tangle.
接着老人再次说话了。年轻的印第安人把她带到床边。白发苍苍、深色玻璃般脸色的老人把指尖放到嘴里蘸湿，然后轻轻地触摸她的胸部、身体及后背。每次指尖接触到她时，她都奇怪地瑟缩一下，似乎触到她的是死神本身。 Then the old man spoke again.The Indian led her to the bedside.The white-haired, glassy-dark old man moistened his finger-tips at his mouth, and most delicately touched her on the breasts and on the body, then on the back.And she winced strangely each time, as the fingertips drew along her skin, as if Death itself were touching her.
而她几乎是悲哀地感到疑惑，为什么她没有对自己当时赤身露体感到羞耻。她只感到悲哀和迷茫。因为没人感到羞耻。年长的男人都很阴郁、紧张，带着某种深沉、忧郁、无法理解的情绪，这种情绪让她暂时忘记了自己的所有烦乱，而年轻的印第安人脸上有一种怪异的狂喜。而她，她只是感到非常奇怪，她无法理解，仿佛她的身体不是自己的。 And she wondered, almost sadly, why she did not feel shamed in her nakedness.She only felt sad and lost.Because nobody felt ashamed.The elder men were all dark and tense with some other deep, gloomy, incomprehensible emotion, which suspended all her agitation, while the young Indian had a strange look of ecstasy on his face.And she, she was only utterly strange and beyond herself, as if her body were not her own.
他们把新衣服递给她：一条白色棉质的宽松直筒连衣裙，长度及膝，一件厚厚的蓝色羊毛短上衣，上面绣着鲜红色和绿色的花朵。衣服只在一边肩膀上固定，用一条红黑夹杂的羊毛穗带系住。 They gave her the new clothing: a long white cotton shift, that came to her knees: then a tunic of thick blue woollen stuff, embroidered with scarlet and green flowers.It was fastened over one shoulder only, and belted with a braid sash of scarlet and black wool.
当她穿好衣服，他们就带走光着脚的她，去栅栏围住的花园中的一间小房子里。年轻的印第安人告诉她说，她想要什么就会得到什么。她向他要水来清洗。他拿来一坛水和一个长木碗。然后他关好她房间的门，把她囚在里面。透过房子门上木条间的缝隙，她可以看到花园里的红花和啾啾鸣叫的小鸟。然后，从大房子的屋顶上，她可以听到悠长沉重的鼓声，听到它的召唤，她觉得不像是人间的声音。还有振奋的声音从屋顶传来，说着奇怪的语言，和着遥远、冷淡的声调，在发表某种讲话或者传递什么信息。她听着像是从死人嘴里发出来的声音。 When she was thus dressed, they took her away, barefoot, to a little house in the stockaded garden.The young Indian told her she might have what she wanted.She asked for water to wash herself.He brought it in a jar, together with a long wooden bowl.Then he fastened the gate-door of her house, and left her a prisoner.She could see through the bars of the gate-door of her house, the red flowers of the garden, and a humming bird.Then from the roof of the big house she heard the long, heavy sound of a drum, unearthly to her in its summons, and an uplifted voice calling from the house-top in a strange language, with a far-away emotionless intonation, delivering some speech or message.And she listened as if from the dead.
但是她很累。她躺在一张皮睡床上，盖着深色的羊毛毯，然后她睡着了，抛弃了一切。 But she was very tired.She lay down on a couch of skins, pulling over her the blanket of dark wool, and she slept, giving up everything.
当她醒来的时候，已经是傍晚时分了。年轻的印第安人正走进来，端着一个篮子似的托盘，里面盛着食物，玉米粉圆饼和肉末玉米糊，肉末大概是羊肉，还有蜂蜜做的饮料，以及一些新鲜的李子。他还给她带了一个长花环，花环由红黄花朵编成，末尾还系着蓝色蓓蕾花结。他取坛子里的水洒在花环上，然后微笑着递给她。他看上去很温柔、很体贴。在他的脸上，在他的黑眼睛里，有一种奇特的胜利和狂喜的神情，这有点吓着她了。当这种光芒从黑色卷翘睫毛下的黑眼睛中隐去时，他会用这种怪异、温柔的狂喜眼神看她，这种眼神不太像人类的，又不带个人情绪，让她心神不安。 When she woke it was late afternoon, and the young Indian was entering with a basket-tray containing food, tortillas and corn-mush with bits of meat, probably mutton, and a drink made of honey, and some fresh plums.He brought her also a long garland of red and yellow flowers with knots of blue buds at the end.He sprinkled the garland with water from a jar, then offered it to her, with a smile.He seemed very gentle and thoughtful, and on his face and in his dark eyes was a curious look of triumph and ecstasy, that frightened her a little.The glitter had gone from the black eyes, with their curving dark lashes, and he would look at her with this strange soft glow of ecstasy that was not quite human, and terribly impersonal, and which made her uneasy.
“你要什么东西吗？”他说，声音低沉、缓慢、悦耳，音量总是有所保留，好似他在跟其他人说话，或者他不想让声音传到她耳朵里去。 “Is there anything you want?” he said, in his low, slow, melodious voice, that always seemed withheld, as if he were speaking aside to somebody else, or as if he did not want to let the sound come out to her.
“我要在这里当囚犯吗？”她问。 “Am I going to be kept a prisoner here?” she asked.
“不，你明天就能到花园里去走走。”他温柔地说。这种关切总是这么奇怪。 “No, you can walk in the garden to-morrow,” he said softly.Always this curious solicitude.
“你喜欢那饮料吗？”他问，递给她一个小土陶杯子，“很提神的。” “Do you like that drink?” he said, offering her a little earthenware cup.“It is very refreshing.”
她好奇地啜了一口这液体。它是用草药做的，放了蜂蜜当甜味剂，有一种奇异的、经久不消的味道。年轻人欣喜地看着她。 She sipped the liquor curiously.It was made with herbs and sweetened with honey, and had a strange, lingering flavour.The young man watched her with gratification.
“味道很特别。”她说。 “It has a peculiar taste,” she said.
“它很提神的。”他回答道。他看她时，黑眼睛里总是带着那种心满意足的狂喜。然后他出去了。不久她就开始感到不舒服，开始剧烈地呕吐，似乎无法控制自己了。 “It is very refreshing,” he replied, his black eyes resting on her always with that look of gratified ecstasy.Then he went away.And presently she began to be sick, and to vomit violently, as if she had no control over herself.
之后，她感到一阵舒适的慵懒偷偷地袭遍她的全身，感到四肢强健而松懈、懒洋洋的。她躺在睡床上听着村庄里的声音，看着变黄的天空，闻着雪松木或是松木燃烧的味道。她可以非常清楚地听到小狗的吠声，遥远的拖沓的脚步声，喃喃的话语声；她能非常清楚地察觉到烟味，花香和夜幕降临；她能够非常清晰地看到一颗明亮的星星遥挂天际，徐徐在落日上方挪动；她觉得仿佛自己所有的感官都散布在空气中，她能够辨别夜里花开的声音，以及大气层悄悄移动时天空真实而清澈的声音，觉得大气中水汽升降的声音就像宇宙中某种竖琴的声音一样。 Afterwards she felt a great soothing languor steal over her, her limbs felt strong and loose and full of languor, and she lay on her couch listening to the sounds of the village, watching the yellowing sky, smelling the scent of burning cedar-wood, or pine-wood.So distinctly she heard the yapping of tiny dogs, the shuffle of far-off feet, the murmur of voices, so keenly she detected the smell of smoke, and flowers, and evening falling, so vividly she saw the one bright star infinitely remote, stirring above the sunset, that she felt as if all her senses were diffused on the air, that she could distinguish the sound of evening flowers unfolding, and the actual crystal sound of the heavens, as the vast belts of the world-atmosphere slid past one another, and as if the moisture ascending and the moisture descending in the air resounded like some harp in the cosmos.
她被关在屋子里和栅栏围住的花圃里了，但是她不怎么在意。几天过去了，她才意识到她从没有见到过第二个女人。只有男人，大房子里年长的男人们，她想象那房子肯定是某种神殿，而他们是某种祭司。因为他们总是穿同样颜色的衣服，红色、橙色、黄色、黑色，有相同的庄严而出神的神态。 She was a prisoner in her house and in the stockaded garden, but she scarcely minded.And it was days before she realised that she never saw another woman.Only the men, the elderly men of the big house, that she imagined must be some sort of temple, and the men priests of some sort.For they always had the same colours, red, orange, yellow, and black, and the same grave, abstracted demeanour.
有时候一位老人会进来，寂然无声地和她一起坐在屋子里。除了那个年轻人外，这里的每个人都只会说印第安语。那些老人会对她微笑，每次和她一起坐上一个小时。有时候当她说西班牙语时，他们会笑着看她，但是回应她的只有这种徐徐的、看起来很慈祥的微笑。他们散发着近乎父亲般的关怀。但是他们的黑眼睛停留在她身上，眼神中有一种距离感，深深地隐藏着令人敬畏的凶猛和冷酷。一旦察觉到她的眼神时，他们就会马上用微笑来掩饰。但是她已经看到了。 Sometimes an old man would come and sit in her room with her, in absolute silence.None spoke any language but Indian, save the one younger man.The older men would smile at her, and sit with her for an hour at a time, sometimes smiling at her when she spoke in Spanish, but never answering save with this slow, benevolent-seeming smile.And they gave off a feeling of almost fatherly solicitude.Yet their dark eyes, brooding over her, had something away in their depths that was awesomely ferocious and relentless.They would cover it with a smile, at once, if they felt her looking.But she had seen it.
他们一直用这种奇怪的、不牵涉任何个人情感的关心对待她，用这种全然不牵涉个人情感的温柔善待她，就像老人对待孩子一样。但是在这种温情下面，她感觉有别的东西，别的可怕的东西。当探访她的老人家带着他沉默、隐蔽、父亲式的关怀走了之后，恐惧就占据了她全身，尽管她不知道她恐惧的是什么。 Always they treated her with this curious impersonal solicitude, this utterly impersonal gentleness, as an old man treats a child.But underneath it she felt there was something else, something terrible.When her old visitor had gone away, in his silent, insidious, fatherly fashion, a shock of fear would come over her; though of what she knew not.
年轻的印第安人会坐着和她无拘无束地交谈，似乎很坦诚相见。但是，同样，和他在一起时，她觉得真相没有被说出来。也许真相是难以启齿的。他黑色的大眼睛会停留在她身上，眼里露出几近珍爱的神情，透着一股狂喜。他会用好听、缓慢而慵懒的声音慢慢地说出简单而不合语法的西班牙语。他告诉她，他是那个很老的老人家的孙子，是那个穿斑点瑟拉佩披肩的男人的儿子。他们是酋长，从很久很久以前传下来的国王，那个时候甚至西班牙人都还没有来过这里。但是他自己去过墨西哥城和美国。他做过工人，在洛杉矶修过路。他去过的最远的地方是芝加哥。 The young Indian would sit and talk with her freely, as if with great candour.But with him, too, she felt that everything real was unsaid.Perhaps it was unspeakable.His big dark eyes would rest on her almost cherishingly, touched with ecstasy, and his beautiful, slow, languorous voice would trail out its simple, ungrammatical Spanish.He told her he was the grandson of the old, old man, son of the man in the spotted sarape: and they were caciques, kings from the old, old days, before even the Spaniards came.But he himself had been in Mexico City, and also in the United States.He had worked as a labourer, building the roads in Los Angeles.He had travelled as far as Chicago.
“那你不说英语吗？”她问。 “Don’t you speak English, then?” she asked.
他的眼睛停留在她身上，奇怪的眼神里透露出口是心非和矛盾挣扎，然后他沉默地摇摇头。 His eyes rested on her with a curious look of duplicity and conflict, and he mutely shook his head.
“在美国的时候，你怎么对待自己的长发？”她问，“你把头发剪掉吗？” “What did you do with your long hair, when you were in the United States?” she asked.“Did you cut it off?”
他的眼里再次露出痛苦的神情，他摇了摇头。 Again, with the look of torment in his eyes, he shook his head.
“不是的，”他说，声音低沉压抑，“我用手帕包着头，戴着帽子。” “No,” he said, in a low, subdued voice, “I wore a hat, and a handkerchief tied round my head.”
他重新陷入沉默，像是陷入了痛苦的回忆中。 And he relapsed into silence, as if of tormented memories.
“你是你的族人中唯一去过美国的吗？”她问他。 “Are you the only man of your people who has been to the United States?” she asked him.
“对。我是唯一长时间离开这里的。其他人在一个星期之内，很快就回来了。他们不外出的。老人们不许他们这样。” “Yes.I am the only one who has been away from here for a long time.The others come back soon, in one week.They don’t stay away.The old men don’t let them.”
“那为什么你要走？” “And why did you go?”
“老人家要我去的——因为我会成为酋长——” “The old men want me to go—because I shall be the Cacique—”
他说话时常常带着同一种天真的表情，一种近乎孩子气的坦诚。但她觉得也许是他的西班牙语让她有这种感觉。也可能言语对他来说都不真实。不管怎样，她总觉得真相都被掩藏起来了。 He talked always with the same naveté, an almost childish candour.But she felt that this was perhaps just the effect of his Spanish.Or perhaps speech altogether was unreal to him.Anyhow, she felt that all the real things were kept back.
他常常来和她一起坐坐——有时候比她希望的要频繁——就好像他想要接近她似的。她问他结婚了没有。他说他结婚了——有两个孩子。 He came and sat with her a good deal—sometimes more than she wished—as if he wanted to be near her.She asked him if he was married.He said he was—with two children.
“我想看看你的孩子。”她说。 “I should like to see your children,” she said.
但他只是用那种微笑回答她，一种甜甜的、近似狂喜的微笑。笑容上面的黑眼睛里那种高深莫测的出神几乎从没变过。 But he answered only with that smile, a sweet, almost ecstatic smile above which the dark eyes hardly changed from their enigmatic abstraction.
奇怪的是，他总是按钟头和她一起坐着，甚至不会让她觉得害羞或想到性。他好像没有性别一样：他坐在那里，极为安静和温和，表情恭顺，头稍稍向前低着，黑得发亮的头发像女子一样披着，就像河流一样在肩头流淌。 It was curious, he would sit with her by the hour, without even making her self-conscious, or sex-conscious.He seemed to have no sex, as he sat there so still and gentle and apparently submissive, with his head bent a little forward, and the river of glistening black hair streaming maidenly over his shoulders.
可是，当她再看他时，她发现他的肩膀宽阔有力，眉毛浓黑平直，低垂的眼睛上有着短短的、卷曲而坚挺的黑睫毛，淡黑的厚嘴唇上留着细细的、绒毛样的胡髭，还有下巴轮廓分明。她知道从其他某种神秘的角度看，他是一个很深邃、很强大的男人。当他感到她在看他的时候，他会迅速抬头看她一眼，眼神神神秘秘、鬼鬼祟祟的，然后立即用半带忧伤的笑容掩饰过去。 Yet when she looked again, she saw his shoulders broad and powerful, his eyebrows black and level, the short, curved, obstinate black lashes over his lowered eyes, the small, fur-like line of moustache above his blackish, heavy lips, and the strong chin, and she knew that in some other mysterious way he was darkly and powerfully male.And he, feeling her watching him, would glance up at her swiftly with a dark, lurking look in his eyes, which immediately he veiled with that half-sad smile.
日子一天天、一周周过去了，她生活在一种模糊不清的满足中。她有时候很不安，感觉对自己失去了控制。她没办法掌控自己，她被其他的某种控制力迷住了。有时候，她很恐惧，很害怕。不过，那些印第安人会过来和她一起坐坐，他们非常安静地呆在一旁，通过这种沉默的、无性的、有影响力的身体的存在，便把隐伏的咒语投在了她身上。当他们坐着时，他们似乎把她的意志带走了，让她成为一个无意志的、对自我冷漠的殉葬品。那个年轻人会给她带来很甜的饮料，常常是同一种催吐的饮料，有时候是其他的。喝完之后，慵懒充斥着她沉重的四肢，她的感官似乎都漂浮在空中，倾听着并不断听到着。他们给她带来一只小母狗，她给它起名叫弗洛拉。一次，在她神智恍惚之时，她感觉自己听到她的小狗怀孕了，在它小小的子宫里孕育着新的生命。又有一天，她能听到地球转动的巨大声音，就像某种庞大的弓弦隆隆作响的声音。 The days and the weeks went by, in a vague kind of contentment.She was uneasy sometimes, feeling she had lost the power over herself.She was not in her own power, she was under the spell of some other control.And at times she had moments of terror and horror.But then these Indians would come and sit with her, casting their insidious spell over her by their very silent presence, their silent, sexless, powerful physical presence.As they sat they seemed to take her will away, leaving her will-less and victim to her own indifference.And the young man would bring her sweetened drink, often the same emetic drink, but sometimes other kinds.And after drinking, the languor filled her heavy limbs, her senses seemed to float in the air, listening, hearing.They had brought her a little female dog, which she called Flora.And once, in the trance of her senses, she felt she heard the little dog conceive, in her tiny womb, and begin to be complex, with young.And another day she could hear the vast sound of the earth going round, like some immense arrow-string booming.
但是当白天越来越短、越来越冷的时候，她一冷就会突然恢复意志，想要出去，想要离开。她坚持要求年轻印第安人让她出去。 But as the days grew shorter and colder, when she was cold, she would get a sudden revival of her will, and a desire to go out, to go away.And she insisted to the young man, she wanted to go out.
于是有一天，他们让她爬到她住的大房子的最高层的屋顶，俯视广场。那天是举办盛大舞会的日子，但并不是每个人都在跳舞。抱着婴儿的女人们站在门口看着。对面，在广场的另一边，另一栋大房子前面有一群人，而在第一层平台的屋顶，在上一层大开的门前，有一小群穿着艳丽的人。透过这些大开的门，她可以看见火在黑暗中闪动，祭司们戴着黑、黄、鲜红三色羽毛的头饰，穿着袍子似的、带绿色流苏的黑红黄三色披毯，在四处走动。在浓厚的、印第安式的沉默中，一只大鼓慢慢地、有节奏地敲着。下面的人群等着—— So one day, they let her climb to the topmost roof of the big house where she was, and look down the square.It was the day of the big dance, but not everybody was dancing.Women with babies in their arms stood in their doorways, watching.Opposite, at the other end of the square, there was a throng before the other big house, and a small, brilliant group on the terrace-roof of the first storey, in front of wide open doors of the upper storey.Through these wide open doors she could see fire glinting in darkness and priests in headdresses of black and yellow and scarlet feathers, wearing robe-like blankets of black and red and yellow, with long green fringes, were moving about.A big drum was beating slowly and regularly, in the dense, Indian silence.The crowd below waited—
然后鼓敲得震天响，突然间爆发出男人低沉有力的歌声。那是一种野性的音乐，就如同在某个永恒不变的森林里呼啸的风，很多成年男子异口同声地唱歌，如同风一般；长列的舞者从下面的大房子里走了出来。男人们裸露着金铜色的身体，披着瀑布般的黑发，胳膊上束着一簇簇红色和黄色的羽毛，身着白色厚粗绒百褶裙，腰间系着一条深红、黑色和绿色相间的刺绣彩带，身体向前微倾，随着舞蹈全神贯注地跺着一成不变的步伐，一张狐狸皮从后腰处连到前面的鼻子上吊着，，随身摇出狐狸皮华丽的美感，尾尖在舞者的脚踝处扭摆。每一个男人的身后都有一个戴着奇异而繁复的头饰的女人，头饰由羽毛和贝壳作为装饰。她们穿着黑色短上衣，挺直身体移动着，每只手都握着几簇羽毛，有节奏地摇着手腕，赤足轻轻跺地。 Then a drum started on a high beat, and there came the deep, powerful burst of men singing a heavy, savage music, like a wind roaring in some timeless forest, many mature men singing in one breath, like the wind; and long lines of dancers walked out from under the big house.Men with naked, golden-bronze bodies and streaming black hair, tufts of red and yellow feathers on their arms, and kilts of white frieze with a bar of heavy red and black and green embroidery round their waists, bending slightly forward and stamping the earth in their absorbed, monotonous stamp of the dance, a fox-fur, hung by the nose from their belt behind, swaying with the sumptuous swaying of a beautiful fox-fur, the tip of the tail writhing above the dancer’s heels.And after each man, a woman with a strange elaborate headdress of feathers and seashells, and wearing a short black tunic, moving erect, holding up tufts of feathers in each hand, swaying her wrists rhythmically and subtly beating the earth with her bare feet.
于是，长长的舞蹈队伍从对面的大房子前展开来。从她楼下的大房子里传出一阵香料的奇特香味；房里有一种怪异而紧张的沉寂，接着男人们突然回应似的爆发出野蛮的歌声，然后长长的舞蹈队伍展开了队形。 So, the long line of the dance unfurling from the big house opposite.And from the big house beneath her, strange scent of incense, strange tense silence, then the answering burst of inhuman male singing, and the long line of the dance unfurling.
这持续了一整天，鼓声不断，男人们吼出洪亮、空灵、雷鸣般的歌声。狐狸皮在强健有力的金铜色的腿后面，随着脚跺地的动作而不停地摇晃。秋日的阳光从蔚蓝的天空上倾泻到男人和女人如河流般的黑发上；山谷岿然不动，远处的岩壁、巍峨的山峰映衬着纯净的天空，山上白雪皑皑。 It went on all day, the insistence of the drum, the cavernous, roaring, storm-like sound of male singing, the incessant swinging of the fox-skins behind the powerful, gold-bronze, stamping legs of the men, the autumn sun from a perfect blue heaven pouring on the rivers of black hair, men’s and women’s, the valley all still, the walls of rock beyond, the awful huge bulking of the mountain against the pure sky, its snow seething with sheer whiteness.
好几个钟头，她都入迷地观看着，像是被麻醉了。在长久喧闹的鼓声和年代久远、急促而深沉的歌声中，吊着狐狸皮的男人们没完没了地跺脚跳着舞，女人们穿着黑色短上衣挺直身体沉重地踏步。她看着这些，最后似乎感到了自己的死亡，她自己的彻底毁灭。仿佛她会再次从生命之野消失。看着一成不变、全神贯注的女人们头上那怪异、高耸的标志，她仿佛再次读到不祥之兆。她的那种女性身份，具有强烈的个性和独特性，会再次被摧毁，巨大古老的象征会又一次凌驾于轰塌的女性个体独立之上。这位受过高等教育的白人女性的那种敏锐和震颤的神经质的意识会被再次摧毁，女性身份会再次被抛入那条不带个人情感的性和激情的洪流里去。怪异的是，好像能看见未来一样，她看见宏大的祭品已经准备好了。在痛苦的恍惚状态中，她回到自己的小屋里去了。 For hours and hours she watched, spell-bound, and as if drugged.And in all the terrible persistence of the drumming and the primeval, rushing deep singing, and the endless stamping of the dance of fox-tailed men, the tread of heavy, bird-erect women in their black tunics, she seemed at last to feel her own death; her own obliteration.As if she were to be obliterated from the field of life again.In the strange towering symbols on the heads of the changeless, absorbed women she seemed to read once more the Mene Mene Tekel Upharsin.Her kind of womanhood, intensely personal and individual, was to be obliterated again, and the great primeval symbols were to tower once more over the fallen individual independence of woman.The sharpness and the quivering nervous consciousness of the highly-bred white woman was to be destroyed again, womanhood was to be cast once more into the great stream of impersonal sex and impersonal passion.Strangely, as if clairvoyant, she saw the immense sacrifice prepared.And she went back to her little house in a trance of agony.
那之后，晚上当她听到鼓声和男人们绕着鼓高声地唱怪异野性的歌，就像野生动物对着看不见的月神和消失的日神嚎叫一样时，她就感到某种痛苦。他们的声音有点像丛林狼的哀号，有点像狐狸得意的鸣叫，有点像远处狼的孤独号叫，也有点像美洲狮折磨人的尖叫，还有点像凶猛的原始男性的执着，带着短暂的温情和持久的残暴。 After this, there was always a certain agony when she heard the drums at evening, and the strange uplifted savage sound of men singing round the drum, like wild creatures howling to the invisible gods of the moon and the vanished sun.Something of the chuckling, sobbing-cry of the coyote, something of the exultant bark of the fox, the far-off wild melancholy exultance of the howling wolf, the torment of the puma’s scream, and the insistence of the ancient fierce human male, with his lapses of tenderness and his abiding ferocity.
有时候，在夜幕降临后，她会爬上高高的屋顶，听广场远处的桥上一群轮廓模糊的年轻人围着鼓按钟点在唱歌。有时候会生一堆火，火光中，男人们穿着白衬衣，或者只穿一件腰布，在黑暗寒冷的空气中，一个小时接着一个小时地像幽灵一样跳舞跺脚。他们在火光中像火鸡一样一直跳舞跺脚，或者突然蹲下来在火边休息，把披毯扔在周边。 Sometimes she would climb the high roof after nightfall, and listen to the dim cluster of young men round the drum on the bridge just beyond the square, singing by the hour.Sometimes there would be a fire, and in the fire-glow, men in their white shirts or naked save for a loin-cloth, would be dancing and stamping like spectres, hour after hour in the dark cold air, within the fire-glow, forever dancing and stamping like turkeys, or dropping squatting by the fire to rest, throwing their blankets round them.
“为什么你们都穿一样的颜色？”她问年轻的印第安人，“为什么你们都在白衬衣外面穿红黄黑三色的衣服？女人为什么穿黑色短上衣？” “Why do you all have the same colours?” she asked the young Indian.“Why do you all have red and yellow and black, over your white shirts?And the women have black tunics?”
他奇怪地看着她，模糊而难以捉摸的微笑浮上面颊。笑容的背后藏着某种温和又古怪的恶意。 He looked into her eyes, curiously, and the faint, evasive smile came on to his face.Behind the smile lay a soft, strange malignancy.
“因为我们的男人是火和白天，我们的女人是夜晚星星之间的天空。”他说。 “Because our men are the fire and the daytime, and our women are the spaces between the stars at night,” he said.
“女人连星星都不是吗？”她问。 “Aren’t the women even stars?” she said.
“是的。我们说她们是星星之间的天空，把星星彼此间隔。” “No. We say they are the spaces between the stars, that keep the stars apart.”
他奇怪地看着她，眼中再次闪过一丝嘲弄的神情。 He looked at her oddly, and again the touch of derision came into his eyes.
“白人，”他说，“他们什么都不懂。他们像孩子，常常带着玩具。我们了解太阳，我们也了解月亮。并且我们说，当一个白人女子把自己献给我们的神灵时，我们的神灵就会重新开创世界，白人的上帝会粉身碎骨。” “White people,” he said, “they know nothing.They are like children, always with toys.We know the sun, and we know the moon.And we say, when a white woman sacrifice herself to our gods, then our gods will begin to make the world again, and the white man’s gods will fall to pieces.”
“她怎么献出自己？”她立刻问道。 “How sacrifice herself?” she asked quickly.
他十分迅速地用难以觉察的微笑掩饰自己。 And he, as quickly covered, covered himself with a subtle smile.
“她放弃自己的上帝，投靠我们的神灵，这就是我的意思。”他安抚似的说道。 “She sacrifice her own gods and come to our gods, I mean that,” he said, soothingly.
但她并不安心。一阵极为痛苦的冰冷的恐惧感萦绕在她心头。 But she was not reassured.An icy pang of fear was at her heart.
“太阳在天空的一端活着，”他继续说，“而月亮住在另一端。男人要一直让太阳在他那边开开心心，女人要让月亮在她那边安安静静。一直以来，她不得不做这项工作。在天空中，太阳不能去月亮的屋子，月亮也不能去太阳的屋子。所以女人，她让月亮到她身体里面，她的洞穴里去。而男人，他让太阳落下，直到他有太阳的威力。一直以来，这是他的工作。然后当男人得到一个女人时，太阳便进入月亮的洞穴，这就是世间万物的起源。” “The sun he is alive at one end of the sky,” he continued, “and the moon lives at the other end.And the man all the time have to keep the sun happy in his side of the sky, and the woman have to keep the moon quiet at her side of the sky.All the time she have to work at this.And the sun can’t ever go into the house of the moon, and the moon can’t ever go into the house of the sun, in the sky.So the woman, she asks the moon to come into her cave, inside her.And the man, he draws the sun down till he has the power of the sun.All the time he do this.Then when the man gets a woman, the sun goes into the cave of the moon, and that is how everything in the world starts.”
她听着，定睛看着他，好像敌对的一方在看说着双关语的另一方。 She listened, watching him closely, as one enemy watches another who is speaking with double meaning.
“那么，”她说“为什么你们印第安人不统治白人？” “Then,” she said, “why aren’t you Indians masters of the white men?”
“因为，”他说，“印第安人变弱了，失去了太阳的威力，于是白人偷走了太阳。但是他们没办法保存太阳——他们不知道怎么去做。他们抓住他，但是不知道该拿他怎么办，就像一个男孩抓到一只大灰熊，却没办法杀死它，也逃不开它。当他想逃走的时候，大灰熊就会吃了他。白人男子不知道拿太阳怎么办，而白人女子不知道拿月亮怎么办。月亮对她们很生气，就像美洲豹对杀死自己幼崽的人生气一样。月亮在咬白人女子——就在里边这儿，”他按了一下身体的一边，“月亮在白人女子的洞穴里生气。印第安人能看到——很快，”他补充道，“印第安女人会夺回月亮，把她安安静静地留在自己的房子里。印第安男人会夺回太阳和统治全球的力量。白人男子不知道太阳是什么。他们从来都不知道。” “Because,” he said, “the Indian got weak, and lost his power with the sun, so the white men stole the sun.But they can’t keep him—they don’t know how.They got him, but they don’t know what to do with him, like a boy who catch a big grizzly bear, and can’t kill him, and can’t run away from him.The grizzly bear eats the boy that catch him, when he want to run away from him.White men don’t know what they are doing with the sun, and white women don’t know what they do with the moon.The moon she got angry with white women, like a puma when someone kills her little ones.The moon, she bites white women—here inside,” and he pressed his side.“The moon, she is angry in a white woman’s cave.The Indian, can see it—And soon,” he added, “the Indian women get the moon back and keep her quiet in their house.And the Indian men get the sun, and the power over all the world.White men don’t know what the sun is.They never know.”
他渐渐地陷入奇怪而得意的沉默之中。 He subsided into a curious exultant silence.
“但是，”她支吾地说，“为什么你们那么恨我们？为什么你们恨我？” “But,” she faltered, “why do you hate us so?Why do you hate me?”
他忽然抬头看，脸上容光焕发，露出令人吃惊的灿烂微笑。 He looked up suddenly with a light on his face, and a startling flame of a smile.
“不，我们不恨。”他柔和地说道，看着她的脸，眼神里闪着好奇的光。 “No, we don’t hate,” he said softly, looking with a curious glitter into her face.
“你恨。”她凄凉而无助地说。 “You do,” she said, forlorn and hopeless.
沉默了一会儿，他站起来走开了。 And after a moment’s silence, he rose and went away.
第三章 Chapter III
在深谷里，冬天已经来临，雪在白日的阳光下融化了，而在夜晚却寒冷刺骨。她还活在一种恍恍惚惚的状态中，感觉到自己的力量一点一点地在衰退，好像意志也在远离自己。她感觉自己总是处于一样的放松、迷惑、受伤害的状态中，除非那种甜甜的草药饮料完全麻醉了她的思想，让她的感官放松，变得更为敏锐并不可思议地锐利起来，让她感觉自己仿佛正在扩散开来，并和谐地融入世界万物之中。终于，这成了她能意识到的唯一的精神状态：一种扩散开并融入更美丽、更和谐的万物中的奇妙感觉。然后，她能真真切切地听到天空中的星星。她从门缝中看到星星，当它们像铃铛一样在天宇的地面上踩出完美的涟漪，经过彼此的黑暗空间，圈成无止境的舞蹈，它们是在通过自己的运动和光亮在说话，在完美无缺地向宇宙倾诉。她可以听见寒冷多云的天气里，雪在天空嘁嘁喳喳，窸窸簌簌地飘落，好似鸟类在秋天结群飞走，突然向看不见的月亮告别，溜出天空平原，释放出平和的温暖。她自己会呼唤羁留的雪从高空落下。她会呼唤那看不见的月亮停止愤怒，像个不再在家发怒的女人一样，和看不见的太阳再度言归于好。当雪在淡淡的冷香中悠然飘落时，她会在冬季天空中闻到月亮香甜的味道弥漫太阳四周，就像太阳的平静再次和月亮的宁静不约而同地融合在一起。 Winter had now come, in the high valley, with snow that melted in the day’s sun, and nights that were bitter cold.She lived on, in a kind of daze, feeling her power ebbing more and more away from her, as if her will were leaving her.She felt always in the same relaxed, confused, victimised state, unless the sweetened herb drink would numb her mind altogether, and release her senses into a sort of heightened, mystic acuteness and a feeling as if she were diffusing out deliciously into the harmony of things.This at length became the only state of consciousness she really recognised: this exquisite sense of bleeding out into the higher beauty and harmony of things.Then she could actually hear the great stars in heaven, which she saw through her door, speaking from their motion and brightness, saying things perfectly to the cosmos, as they trod in perfect ripples, like bells on the floor of heaven, passing one another and grouping in the timeless dance, with the spaces of dark between.And she could hear the snow on a cold, cloudy day twittering and faintly whistling in the sky, like birds that flock and fly away in autumn, suddenly calling farewell to the invisible moon, and slipping out of the plains of the air, releasing peaceful warmth.She herself would call to the arrested snow to fall from the upper air.She would call to the unseen moon to cease to be angry, to make peace again with the unseen sun like a woman who ceases to be angry in her house.And she would smell the sweetness of the moon relaxing to the sun in the wintry heaven, when the snow fell in a faint, cold-perfumed relaxation, as the peace of the sun mingled again in a sort of unison with the peace of the moon.
她也很清楚笼罩在山谷里的印第安人身上的那种阴影，一种深深的、坚忍的悲伤，其深处几乎上升到宗教的高度。 She was aware too of the sort of shadow that was on the Indians of the valley, a deep, stoical disconsolation, almost religious in its depth.
“我们失去了对太阳的控制力，我们正努力试着把他找回来。但他像脱缰的马一样有戒心，对我们很狂野。我们可有的受了。”这是年轻的印第安人对她说的，同时别有深意地看着她的眼睛。而她，好像被迷惑了，回答道： “We have lost our power over the sun, and we are trying to get him back.But he is wild with us, and shy like a horse that has got away.We have to go through a lot.”So the young Indian said to her, looking into her eyes with a strained meaning.And she, as if bewitched, replied:
“我希望你们能把他找回来。” “I hope you will get him back.”
胜利的笑容浮现在他脸上。 The smile of triumph flew over his face.
“你是这么希望的吗？”他说。 “Do you hope it?” he said.
“是的。”她认命似的答道。 “I do,” she answered fatally.
“那么好吧，”他说，“我们会把他找回来的。” “Then all right,” he said.“We shall get him.”
他得意地走了。 And he went away in exultance.
她感觉自己正渐渐趋近于某种终结，她无意避免这终结，然而这对她是沉重的，最终也是可怕的。 She felt she was drifting on some consummation, which she had no will to avoid, yet which seemed heavy and finally terrible to her.
当她被再次带到老人家面前，脱光了衣服，被苍老的手指尖触摸的时候，应该是快十二月了，因为白天变短了。 It must have been almost December, for the days were short, when she was taken again before the aged man, and stripped of her clothing, and touched with the old finger-tips.
老酋长用孤寂、悠远、黑色、专注的眼神看着她的眼睛，低声跟她说了些话。 The aged cacique looked her in the eyes, with his eyes of lonely, far-off, black intentness, and murmured something to her.
“他想要你做和平的姿势。”年轻人翻译道，向她展示该做的姿势，“和平地向他道别。” “He wants you to make the sign of peace,” the young man translated, showing her the gesture.“Peace and farewell to him.”
她被老酋长玻璃似的、专注的黑眼睛迷住了；它们像小蜥蜴的眼睛一样不眨眼地看着她，控制着她。在他的眼睛深处，她也看到了某种父亲般的怜悯和恳求。她把手放在脸前面，按照要求的那样，作出和平和道别的手势。他也用道别的手势回应她，然后就缩进自己的皮草中去了。她想他快死了，他自己也知道。 She was fascinated by the black, glass-like, intent eyes of the old cacique, that watched her without blinking, like a basilisk’s, overpowering her.In their depths also she saw a certain fatherly compassion, and pleading.She put her hand before her face, in the required manner, making the sign of peace and farewell.He made the sign of peace back again to her, then sank among his furs.She thought he was going to die, and that he knew it.
接下来是举行仪式的日子，她被带到所有人面前，穿着一件缀白色流苏的蓝色披毯，手里拿着蓝色羽毛。在一栋房子的祭坛前，她被香薰，还被洒上了香灰。在对面房子的祭坛前，她又被令人恐惧的、穿着黄红黑三色华丽服饰的祭司们用香薰了一次。他们的脸上涂了鲜红色的颜料。然后他们又朝她身上泼了些水。期间，她只是隐约意识到那祭坛上的火，那沉重的鼓声，还有男人们开始响亮、有力、低沉而又野性的歌唱，以及下面广场上群众的脸摇晃起来，成队地跳起祭祀的舞蹈。 There followed a day of ceremonial, when she was brought out before all the people, in a blue blanket with white fringe, and holding blue feathers in her hands.Before an altar of one house, she was perfumed with incense and sprinkled with ash.Before the altar of the opposite house she was fumigated again with incense by the gorgeous, terrifying priests in yellow and scarlet and black, their faces painted with scarlet paint.And then they threw water on her.Meanwhile she was faintly aware of the fire on the altar, the heavy, heavy sound of a drum, the heavy sound of men beginning powerfully, deeply, savagely to sing, the swaying of the crowd of faces in the plaza below, and the formation for a sacred dance.
但是，在这个时候，她的日常意识已经麻木了。她发现眼前自己的周遭都是影子，几乎是无形的。通过被净化和强化了的感官，她能听见地球在自己的轨道上飞行的声音，就像一支射出的箭，空气中涟漪般的沙沙作响声，还有巨大的弓弦嗡嗡作响的声音。对她来说，似乎在高空中有两股巨大的影响力，一股是朝向太阳的金色，另一股是看不见的银色。第一股像雨一样朝着金色的太阳上升，第二股似雨一般银光闪闪地顺着太空的阶梯，爬向盘旋在雪山之巅的隐匿的云层。在它们之间，有另一种神灵在等着从湿气之中挣脱，从神秘地聚在他周边那沉重的皑皑白雪之中挣脱。在夏天，像一只烧焦的鹰，他会等着伺机抖掉身上的沉重的阳光。他有着似火的颜色。他总是把身上的雪或者酷热抖掉，像只鹰一样急速飞离。 But at this time her commonplace consciousness was numb, she was aware of her immediate surroundings as shadows, almost immaterial.With refined and heightened senses she could hear the sound of the earth winging on its journey, like a shot arrow, the ripple-rustling of the air, and the boom of the great arrow-string.And it seemed to her there were two great influences in the upper air, one golden towards the sun, and one invisible silver; the first travelling like rain ascending to the gold presence sunwards, the second like rain silverily descending the ladders of space towards the hovering, lurking clouds over the snowy mountain-top.Then between them, another presence, waiting to shake himself free of moisture, of heavy white snow that had mysteriously collected about him.And in summer, like a scorched eagle, he would wait to shake himself clear of the weight of heavy sunbeams.And he was coloured like fire.And he was always shaking himself clear, of snow or of heavy heat, like an eagle rustling.
还有一种更奇怪的神灵，站在蓝色的远处观望着，总是观望着。有时候，他在风中奔跑，或者在热浪中闪烁。蓝色的风本身似乎是冲出地球上的洞，冲向天空，又冲出天空，向下冲向地球。这蓝色的风就是中间人，就是属于两界的隐形幽灵，捻拨着上升和下降的雨弦。 Then there was a still stranger presence, standing watching from the blue distance, always watching.Sometimes running in upon the wind, or shimmering in the heat-waves.The blue wind itself, rushing as it were out of the holes in the earth into the sky, rushing out of the sky down upon the earth.The blue wind, the go-between, the invisible ghost that belonged to two worlds, that played upon the ascending and the descending chords of the rains.
她平常的自我意识越来越远离她了。她进入到另一种激情澎湃的宇宙意识中，像被麻醉了似的。印第安人带着自己的浓重的宗教天性，使她屈从于他们的视野。 More and more her ordinary personal consciousness had left her, she had gone into that other state of passional cosmic consciousness, like one who is drugged.The Indians, with their heavily religious natures, had made her succumb to their vision.
她只问了年轻的印第安人一个涉及个人的问题： Only one personal question she asked the young Indian:
“为什么只有我穿蓝色？” “Why am I the only one that wears blue?”
“这是风的颜色。这是种一去不复返的颜色，但是它总是在这里，像是徘徊在我们中间的死亡一样。它是逝者的颜色。它是远远地站着，眺望着我们，却又无法靠近的颜色。当我们靠近，它就走远了。无法靠近它。我们都有棕色、黄色和黑色的头发，白色的牙齿和红色的血。我们是这里的人。你长着蓝眼睛，是从远处来的使者，你不能留下，现在该是你回去的时候了。” “It is the colour of the wind.It is the colour of what goes away and is never coming back, but which is always here, waiting like death among us.It is the colour of the dead.And it is the colour that stands away off, looking at us from the distance, that cannot come near to us.When we go near, it goes farther.It can’t be near.We are all brown and yellow and black hair, and white teeth and red blood.We are the ones that are here.You with blue eyes, you are the messengers from the far-away, you cannot stay, and now it is time for you to go back.”
“去哪里？”她问。 “Where to?” she asked.
“去远处的事物那里，去像太阳和雨的蓝色母亲那里，告诉他们，我们又是世上的那个民族了，我们能够再次把太阳带到月亮那里去，像把红色公马带到蓝色母马那里一样；我们就是那个民族。白人女子已经把月亮赶回天上去了，不让她到太阳那里去。所以太阳愤怒了。印第安人必须把月亮交给太阳。” “To the way-off things like the sun and the blue mother of rain, and tell them that we are the people on the world again, and we can bring the sun to the moon again, like a red horse to a blue mare; we are the people.The white women have driven back the moon in the sky, won’t let her come to the sun.So the sun is angry.And the Indian must give the moon to the sun.”
“怎么做？”她说。 “How?” she said.
“这个白人女子得死，然后像风一样去太阳那里，告诉他，印第安人会为他敞开大门。印第安女人会为月亮打开大门。白人女子不让月亮从蓝珊瑚里下来。月亮以前时常来到印第安女人中间，就像白山羊来到花丛中一样。太阳想去印第安男人那里，就像老鹰想飞上松树一样。太阳被白人男子挡在背后，月亮被拦在白人女子背后，他们没法走开。他们发怒了，世界上所有东西都变得愈加愤怒了。印第安人说，他会把白人女子献给太阳，于是太阳会越过白人男子，再次来到印第安人身边。月亮会得到惊喜，她会看见大门敞开，她不知道该走哪条路。但是印第安女人会呼唤月亮：来呀！来呀！回到我的草地上。邪恶的白人女子再也不能伤害你了。接着太阳的目光会越过白人男子的头顶，看到我们女人的草原上的月亮，还有红种人像松树一般站在周边。然后，他会从白人男子的头顶跨过，穿过云杉树林，来到印第安人这里。而我们这些穿着红黑黄三色衣服的人，我们是留下来的人，我们会右手执太阳，左手执月亮。所以我们能把雨从蓝色草地中带出来，从黑色草地中带上去。我们能够呼唤风，风会叫玉米生长；我们会让云层裂开，让绵羊生出双胞胎羊羔。我们会像春天一样充满力量。而白人会成为严冬，没有雪——” “The white woman got to die and go like a wind to the sun, tell him the Indians will open the gate to him.And the Indian women will open the gate to the moon.The white women don’t let the moon come down out of the blue coral.The moon used to come down among the Indian women, like a white goat among the flowers.And the sun want to come down to the Indian men, like an eagle to the pine-trees.The sun, he is shut out behind the white man, and the moon she is shut out behind the white woman, and they can’t get away.They are angry, everything in the world gets angrier.The Indian says, he will give the white woman to the sun, so the sun will leap over the white man and come to the Indian again.And the moon will be surprised, she will see the gate open, and she not know which way to go.But the Indian woman will call to the moon, Come!Come!Come back into my grasslands.The wicked white woman can’t harm you any more.Then the sun will look over the heads of the white men, and see the moon in the pastures of our women, with the Red Men standing around like pine trees.Then he will leap over the heads of the white men, and come running past to the Indians through the spruce trees.And we, who are red and black and yellow, we who stay, we shall have the sun on our right hand and the moon on our left.So we can bring the rain down out of the blue meadows, and up out of the black; and we can call the wind that tells the corn to grow, when we ask him, and we shall make the clouds to break, and the sheep to have twin lambs.And we shall be full of power, like a spring day.But the white people will be a hard winter, without snow—”
“可是，”这位白人女子说道，“我没有把月亮关在外面——我怎么会呢？” “But,” said the white woman, “I don’t shut out the moon—how can I?”
“会，”他说，“你关上门，接着大笑，觉得自己用自己的方式得到了它。” “Yes,” he said, “you shut the gate, and then laugh, think you have it all your own way.”
她永远都不太理解他看她的方式。奇怪的是，他总是那么温文尔雅，他的微笑总是那么柔和。然而，他的眼睛里闪着这样的光，他的话语中有这样一种冷酷无情的恨，一种怪异、深沉、非个人的恨。从私人的角度来说，他喜欢她，她很确信这一点。他对她很温和，以某种古怪、柔和而平淡的方式被她所吸引。但是除开个人的因素，他对她怀有一种神秘的恨意。他会迷人地对她微笑。然而，如果下一刻，她出其不意地瞟视他一眼，她会捕捉到他眼中闪着百分百的余恨。 She could never quite understand the way he looked at her.He was always so curiously gentle, and his smile was so soft.Yet there was such glitter in his eyes, and an unrelenting sort of hate came out of his words, a strange, profound, impersonal hate.Personally he liked her, she was sure.He was gentle with her, attracted by her in some strange, soft, passionless way.But impersonally he hated her with a mystic hatred.He would smile at her, winningly.Yet if, the next moment, she glanced round at him unawares, she would catch that gleam of pure after-hate in his eyes.
“我必须得死，然后献给太阳？”她问。 “Have I got to die and be given to the sun?” she asked.
“在某个时候，”他说，难以捉摸地笑着，“在某个时候我们都会死。” “Sometime,” he said, laughing evasively.“Sometime we all die.”
他们对她很温和，体贴入微。这些奇怪的男人们，老祭司和年轻的酋长都一样，他们都像女人一样照管她，关心她。在他们温柔、暗藏的谅解中，有些女性的特质。但在他们的眼睛里闪烁着奇怪的光芒。他们暗色的、紧闭的嘴巴和宽下巴，以及小而结实的白牙，都带有某种原始的雄性和残忍。 They were gentle with her, and very considerate with her.Strange men, the old priests and the young cacique alike, they watched over her and cared for her like women.In their soft, insidious understanding, there was something womanly.Yet their eyes, with that strange glitter, and their dark, shut mouths that would open to the broad jaw, the small, strong, white teeth, had something very primitively male and cruel.
冬日的一天，下着雪，他们把她带到大房子里的一间昏暗的大屋子里。角落里的高台上生了火，高台上面是一种幔帐或土砖砌成的天篷。她看见火光中几乎赤裸的祭司们发光的身体，以及屋顶和墙壁上的奇异符号。屋里没有门窗，他们从屋顶上通过梯子爬下来。松木烧成的火不停地跳动着，照亮墙上绘着的那些她看不懂的奇特装置，还有屋顶的柱子上画着的那些怪异的黑红黄三色图案，以及壁凹或壁龛，里面是她无法辨认的稀奇古怪的物件。 One wintry day, when snow was falling, they took her to a great dark chamber in the big house.The fire was burning in a corner on a high raised dais under a sort of hood or canopy of adobe-work.She saw in the fire-glow, the glowing bodies of the almost naked priests, and strange symbols on the roof and walls of the chamber.There was no door or window in the chamber, they had descended by a ladder from the roof.And the fire of pinewood danced continually, showing walls painted with strange devices, which she could not understand, and a ceiling of poles making a curious pattern of black and red and yellow, and alcoves or niches in which were curious objects she could not discern.
年长的祭司们在火堆旁静静地进行着某种仪式，这是一种印第安式的宁静。她坐在墙脚一处低矮的凸出物上，对面是火；两个男人坐在她旁边。不久，他们给了她一杯饮料，她很愉快地喝了，因为它会诱发半恍惚的状态。 The older priests were going through some ceremony near the fire, in silence, intense Indian silence.She was seated on a low projection of the wall, opposite the fire, two men seated beside her.Presently they gave her a drink from a cup, which she took gladly, because of the semi-trance it would induce.
在黑暗和寂静中，她准确地知道发生在她身上的每一件事：他们如何脱掉她的衣服，把她立在墙上一个涂成蓝白黑色的、怪异的大装置前面，然后用水和皂角液清洗她全身上下，甚至连她的头发也洗了，洗得很轻柔、很仔细，而后用白布擦干，直擦得柔软发亮。接着，他们把她放到长椅上，在另一座巨大的、难以辨明的红黑黄色雕像下面，再在她全身擦上芳香的油，并长时间按摩她的四肢、后背和两肋，奇特又催眠。他们浅黑色的手掌异乎寻常地有力，却也很柔软，是那种她无法理解的湿润的柔软。他们浅黑色的脸庞靠近她白色的身体时，她看到上面涂有红色的颜料，脸颊上是黄色的条纹。当他们的手清洁她那柔软而白皙的身体时，他们的黑眼睛闪烁着，全神贯注。 In the darkness and in the silence she was accurately aware of everything that happened to her: how they took off her clothes, and, standing her before a great, weird device on the wall, coloured blue and white and black, washed her all over with water and the amole infusion; washed even her hair, softly, carefully, and dried it on white cloths, till it was soft and glistening.Then they laid her on a couch under another great indecipherable image of red and black and yellow, and now rubbed all her body with sweet-scented oil, and massaged all her limbs, and her back, and her sides, with a long, strange, hypnotic massage.Their dark hands were incredibly powerful, yet soft with a watery softness she could not understand.And the dark faces, leaning near her white body, she saw were darkened with red pigment, with lines of yellow round the cheeks.And the dark eyes glittered absorbed, as the hands worked upon the soft white body of the woman.
他们是如此地没有个人情感，专注于她无法理解的某种事情。他们从没有把她当成女儿身看待，她能看得出来。她于他们而言是某种神秘的物件，是件器皿，装载着对她来说遥远到够不着的强烈情感。她处于一种恍惚之中，看着他们的面孔俯在她身上。浅黑色的面孔上，透明的红色颜料闪着古怪的光，涂着黄色的条纹。在这张活生生的脸上带着的那副怪异的、黑得发亮的面具上，一双眼睛充满了坚定不移的亮光；涂成浅紫色的嘴唇闭着，显得十分邪恶、哀伤而严肃。当她躺下来，被他们神奇的黑手揉搓出雾般的光泽时，她能从他们脸上看出这些神情——无边而原始的哀伤、下定决心的严肃、不变的仇恨，以及即将胜利的人那初生的喜悦。她的四肢、她的肉体、她的骨头最终似乎弥漫在了玫瑰色的雾中。在雾里，她的意识像红云里的阳光一样盘旋着。 They were so impersonal, absorbed in something that was beyond her.They never saw her as a personal woman: she could tell that.She was some mystic object to them, some vehicle of passions too remote for her to grasp.Herself in a state of trance, she watched their faces bending over her, dark, strangely glistening with the transparent red paint, and lined with bars of yellow.And in this weird, luminous-dark mask of living face, the eyes were fixed with an unchanging steadfast gleam, and the purplish-pigmented lips were closed in a full, sinister, sad grimness.The immense fundamental sadness, the grimness of ultimate decision, the fixity of revenge, and the nascent exultance of those that are going to triumph—these things she could read in their faces, as she lay and was rubbed into a misty glow, by their uncanny dark hands.Her limbs, her flesh, her very bones at last seemed to be diffusing into a roseate sort of mist, in which her consciousness hovered like some sun-gleam in a flushed cloud.
她知道光会消退，云会变灰。但是此刻，她不相信这个。她知道她是个受害者，所有对她做的这些精细的事，都是为了献祭她。但是她不在意。她就要这样。 She knew the gleam would fade, the cloud would go grey.But at present she did not believe it.She knew she was a victim; that all this elaborate work upon her was the work of victimising her.But she did not mind.She wanted it.
迟些，他们给她穿上一件蓝色的短上衣，带她到楼上的平台，把她展示给人群看。她看见下面的广场上全都是浅黑色的面孔和闪闪发亮的眼睛。没有怜悯，只有古怪而冷酷的狂喜。人们看到她时，发出一声压抑的叫喊，她战栗了一下。但是，她几乎不在意了。 Later, they put a short blue tunic on her and took her to the upper terrace, and presented her to the people.She saw the plaza below her full of dark faces and of glittering eyes.There was no pity: only the curious hard exultance.The people gave a subdued cry when they saw her, and she shuddered.But she hardly cared.
第二天是最后一天。她睡在那座大房子的一个房间里。破晓的时候，他们给她穿上一件宽大的缀流苏的蓝色大披毯，领她出门去广场，来到一堆堆身着黑色披毯的沉默的人群中。地上有纯白的雪，浅黑色皮肤的人们穿着暗棕色的披毯，看上去像另一个世界的居民。 Next day was the last.She slept in a chamber of the big house.At dawn they put on her a big blue blanket with a fringe, and led her out into the plaza, among the throng of silent, dark-blanketed people.There was pure white snow on the ground, and the dark people in their dark-brown blankets looked like inhabitants of another world.
一面大鼓缓慢地敲着，一位老祭司正在屋顶上宣读着什么。不过直到中午才来了一乘轿子，人们发出那种低沉、野兽般的叫喊，声音是如此动人。那乘麻袋似的轿子上坐着那位很老、很老的酋长。他的白发上编着黑穗带和大绿松石。他的脸像一块黑曜石。他举手示意，轿子在她面前停住了。他用苍老的眼睛盯着她，声音空洞地和她说了一会儿话。没有人翻译。 A large drum was slowly pounding, and an old priest was declaring from a housetop.But it was not till noon that a litter came forth, and the people gave that low, animal cry which was so moving.In the sack-like litter sat the old, old cacique, his white hair braided with black braid and large turquoise stones.His face was like a piece of obsidian.He lifted his hand in token, and the litter stopped in front of her.Fixing her with his old eyes, he spoke to her for a few moments, in his hollow voice.No one translated.
又来了一乘轿子，她被放了进去。四个祭司在前面走着，穿着鲜红、黄、黑三色的服饰，戴着羽毛头饰。接着是老酋长的轿子。随后，轻微的鼓声响起，两组歌手突然同时开始唱起歌来，歌声雄壮而野性。那些金红色的、几乎赤裸的男人们，戴着仪式用的羽毛，身着短褶裙，河流一般的黑发披在背上。他们形成两队，开始跳踏地舞。于是他们排成两列华丽的队伍，显出暗金红色、黑色及皮毛，摇摇摆摆地跳出白雪满地的广场，身上的贝壳和燧石发出细碎的叮叮当当声。这两队蜜蜂似的男人围着鼓唱着，在雪地里蜿蜒行走着。 Another litter came, and she was placed in it.Four priests moved ahead, in their scarlet and yellow and black, with plumed headdresses.Then came the litter of the old cacique.Then the light drums began, and two groups of singers burst simultaneously into song, male and wild.And the golden-red, almost naked men, adorned with ceremonial feathers and kilts, the rivers of black hair down their backs, formed into two files and began to tread the dance.So they threaded out of the snowy plaza, in two long, sumptuous lines of dark red-gold and black and fur, swaying with a faint tinkle of bits of shell and flint, winding over the snow between the two bee-clusters of men who sang around the drum.
慢慢地，他们出来了，然后是她的轿子，后面跟着插着羽毛、穿着绚丽、跳着舞的祭司们。每个人都跳着踏地舞，甚至连轿夫也在轻轻跳着。他们走出了广场，经过冒烟的炉子，走上那条去往高大的棉白杨树的小路。那些树光秃秃的，高雅地在雪地上挺立着，像银灰色的蕾丝映衬着蓝色的天空。河流变窄了，在冰尖之间奔腾着。篱笆里面那方格形的花园里白雪皑皑，白房子现在看着发黄。 Slowly they moved out, and her litter, with its attendance of feathered, lurid, dancing priests, moved after.Everybody danced the tread of the dance-step, even, subtly, the litter-bearers.And out of the plaza they went, past smoking ovens, on the trail to the great cotton-wood trees, that stood like grey-silver lace against the blue sky, bare and exquisite above the snow.The river, diminished, rushed among fangs of ice.The chequer-squares of gardens within fences were all snowy, and the white houses now looked yellowish.
整个村庄一直到远处耸立的岩壁都披上了纯白的雪，闪着让人难以忍受的白光。长队的舞者蜿蜒着穿过平坦的雪床，他们身着橘红和黑色的衣服，缓慢地、华丽地摇摆着。兴奋的鼓快节奏地发出砰砰声，在晶莹剔透的、凝固的空气中，野人们爆发出的吼叫声和歌声让人痴狂。 The whole valley glittered intolerably with pure snow, away to the walls of the standing rock.And across the flat cradle of snow-bed wound the long thread of the dance, shaking slowly and sumptuously in its orange and black motion.The high drums thudded quickly, and on the crystalline frozen air the swell and roar of the chant of savages was like an obsession.
她坐在轿子里，睁着蓝色的大眼睛呆呆地往外看，眼睛下面是她麻醉了的倦容。她知道她会死去，死在这雪地的闪光之中，死在这些野蛮、衣着华丽的人的手中。当她凝视着被砍伐的、笨重的大山上那闪耀着的蓝天的光辉时，她想道：“我已经死了。从我现在已经死了过渡到我很快就会死，又有什么区别呢！”然而她的灵魂生病了，感到很虚弱。 She sat looking out of her litter with big, transfixed blue eyes, under which were the wan markings of her drugged weariness.She knew she was going to die, among the glisten of this snow, at the hands of this savage, sumptuous people.And as she stared at the blaze of blue sky above the slashed and ponderous mountain, she thought: “I am dead already.What difference does it make, the transition from the dead I am to the dead I shall be, very soon!”Yet her soul sickened and felt wan.
奇怪的队伍在无止境的舞蹈中继续行进着，他们缓缓地穿过雪原，然后进入松树林之间的斜坡。她看见古铜色的男人在浅铜色的树干间，跳着踏地舞前进着。最后，她也在摇晃的轿子中进入松树林。 The strange procession trailed on, in perpetual dance, slowly across the plain of snow, and then entered the slopes between the pine-trees.She saw the copper-dark men dancing the dance-tread, onwards, between the copper-pale tree trunks.And at last she, too, in her swaying litter, entered the pine-trees.
他们继续前行，向上走，踏过树下的积雪，经过一棵棵伟岸的、树皮脱落了的浅铜色松树；穿梭其间的舞队所发出的簌簌声、摇摆声和踏地声穿透了树林，穿透了大山。他们顺着一条河床行进。不过，河流已经干涸，像在夏季一样，因为水源被冻住了。那里有暗色和红铜色的柳树林，枝条像狂舞的头发，还有苍白的白杨树，在雪的映衬下，看上去像冷肉。然后是凸出的黑色岩石。 They were travelling on and on, upwards, across the snow under the trees, past the superb shafts of pale, flaked copper, the rustle and shake and tread of the threading dance, penetrating into the forest, into the mountain.They were following a stream-bed: but the stream was dry, like summer, dried up by the frozenness of the head-waters.There were dark, red-bronze willow bushes with wattles like wild hair, and pallid aspen trees looking like cold flesh against the snow.Then jutting dark rocks.
终于，她能够分辨出舞者没有再向前移动了。她离鼓声越来越近，像是接近了某种神秘野兽的巢穴。接着她穿过灌木丛，出现在一个奇怪的竞技场中。面对着的是一大堵空心岩壁，它前方的下面悬着一大条下垂的、獠牙般的冰条。冰从上面的悬崖倾泻到这块岩石上，从高空滴下来，然后止住了，几乎要碰到下面的空石头，那下面本来是个水塘。但是，水塘干涸了。 At last she could tell that the dancers were moving forward no more.Nearer and nearer she came upon the drums, as to a lair of mysterious animals.Then through the bushes she emerged into a strange amphitheatre.Facing was a great wall of hollow rock, down the front of which hung a great, dripping, fang-like spoke of ice.The ice came pouring over the rock from the precipice above, and then stood arrested, dripping out of high heaven, almost down to the hollow stones where the stream-pool should be below.But the pool was dry.
在干涸的水塘的两边，舞者已经站好队形，以灌木丛为背景，舞蹈一直持续不断。 On either side the dry pool, the lines of dancers had formed, and the dance was continuing without intermission, against a background of bushes.
不过，她感觉到的却是从高处黑暗的悬崖边上垂下来的那条倒置的、獠牙状的尖冰块。在这串巨大的冰块后面，她看见祭司们那豹子似的身影，在空旷的峭壁表面上爬行，爬向一个洞穴。这个洞穴位于峭壁的中间，好似凿出的一个暗色的插孔。 But what she felt was that fanged inverted pinnacle of ice, hanging from the lip of the dark precipice above.And behind the great rope of ice, she saw the leopard-like figures of priests climbing the hollow cliff face, to the cave that, like a dark socket, bored a cavity, an orifice, half way up the crag.
在她意识到之前，轿夫已在脚步摇晃着爬上岩石了。她也在冰块的后面了。它在那里挂着，像没有拉开的帘子一样，但悬挂起来像巨大的獠牙。在她上面不远的地方就是深陷在岩石里的岩洞。在她被摇晃着抬上去时，她一直看着它。 Before she could realise, her litter-bearers were staggering in the footholds, climbing the rock.She, too, was behind the ice.There it hung, like a curtain that is not spread, but hangs like a great fang.And near above her was the orifice of the cave sinking dark into the rock.She watched it as she swayed upwards.
山洞的平台上站着祭司们，戴着绚丽的羽毛，穿着带流苏的袍子等着，看着她上来。其中两个俯身去帮助轿夫。终于，她出现在山洞的平台上，身后的远处是冰柱，冰柱下面是灌木丛中空荡荡的竞技场。男人们在那里舞蹈，全村庄的人都沉默地聚集在一起。 On the platform of the cave stood the priests, waiting in all their gorgeousness of feathers and fringed robes, watching her ascent.Two of them stooped to help her litter-bearer.And at length she was on the platform of the cave, far in behind the shaft of ice, above the hollow amphitheatre among the bushes below, where men were dancing, and the whole populace of the village was clustered in silence.
在左边，太阳正从下午的天空中滑落。她知道这是一年中最短的一天，也是她生命中的最后一天。他们让她面对七彩的冰棱站着，冰棱在离她有点距离的前方壮观地从上面垂了下来。 The sun was sloping down the afternoon sky, on the left.She knew that this was the shortest day of the year, and the last day of her life.They stood her facing the iridescent column of ice, which fell down marvellously arrested, away in front of her.
某种信号发出之后，下面的舞蹈便停止了。此时万籁俱寂。他们给了她一点儿喝的，然后两个祭司脱掉她的斗篷和短上衣。她脸色惨白地站在穿着斑斓袍子的祭司们之间，站在冰柱的后面，站在下面浅黑色皮肤的人群上方。下面的人群发出低沉、野蛮的叫喊。接着祭司们让她转身，于是她背对外面的世界站着，她的金色长发对着下面的人们。他们又叫喊了一声。 Some signal was given, and the dance below stopped.There was now absolute silence.She was given a little to drink, then two priests took off her mantle and her tunic, and in her strange pallor she stood there, between the lurid robes of the priests, beyond the pillar of ice, beyond and above the dark-faced people.The throng below gave the low, wild cry.Then the priests turned her round, so she stood with her back to the open world, her long blond hair to the people below.And they cried again.
她面对着岩洞。里面一堆火正燃烧着，火光闪烁。四个祭司脱掉自己的袍子，几乎和她一样赤裸。他们都处在人生最年富力强的年纪，涂了颜料的黑脸一直低着。 She was facing the cave, inwards.A fire was burning and flickering in the depths.Four priests had taken off their robes, and were almost as naked as she was.They were powerful men in the prime of life, and they kept their dark, painted faces lowered.
从火堆中，那位很老、很老的祭司来了，端着香盘。他赤身裸体，处于一种野性的狂喜之中。他用香薰他的祭品，同时念念有词，声音空洞。他身后来了另一个脱掉袍子的祭司，带着两把燧石刀。 From the fire came the old, old priest, with an incense-pan.He was naked and in a state of barbaric ecstasy.He fumigated his victim, reciting at the same time in a hollow voice.Behind him came another robeless priest, with two flint knives.
在她被香薰的时候，他们把她放在一块大而平的石头上，四个强壮有力的男人抓着她摊开的四肢。后面站着那位上了年纪的男人，他像一个蒙着深色玻璃的骷髅，拿着一把刀，一动不动地看着太阳。他身后又有一位赤身裸体的祭司，也拿着一把刀。 When she was fumigated, they laid her on a large flat stone, the four powerful men holding her by the outstretched arms and legs.Behind stood the aged man, like a skeleton covered with dark glass, holding a knife and transfixedly watching the sun; and behind him again was another naked priest, with a knife.
她几乎没有知觉，尽管她知道正在发生的一切。她转向天空，看着黄色的太阳。太阳在下沉。冰柱像她和太阳之间的一道阴影。她意识到黄色的光线正照满半个岩洞，但是还没有照到生了火的祭坛那里。祭坛在远远的漏斗形的岩洞的另一边。 She felt little sensation, though she knew all that was happening.Turning to the sky, she looked at the yellow sun.It was sinking.The shaft of ice was like a shadow between her and it.And she realised that the yellow rays were filling half the cave, though they had not reached the altar where the fire was, at the far end of the funnel-shaped cavity.
是的，光线在周围慢慢地爬动着。随着光线越变越红，它们会照得更远。当红太阳快要西沉时，它会穿过冰柱，照满山洞，照进空荡荡的山洞深处，照到最里面去。 Yes, the rays were creeping round slowly.As they grew ruddier, they penetrated farther.When the red sun was about to sink, he would shine full through the shaft of ice deep into the hollow of the cave, to the innermost.
现在她明白，这就是他们在等的。甚至那些扣住她的人也弯下腰，扭转身体。他们黑色的眼睛看着太阳，眼中闪着热忱、敬畏和渴求的目光。老酋长的黑眼睛像黑色的镜子一样盯着太阳看，好像失明了一般，却又包含着某种恐怖的回应，回应这个冬季中发红的天体。在沉寂、冰冷、发红的冬季下午，所有祭司们的眼睛都盯着这个正下沉的天体看，眼睛发光。 She understood now that this was what the men were waiting for.Even those that held her down were bent and twisted round, their black eyes watching the sun with a glittering eagerness, and awe, and craving.The black eyes of the aged cacique were fixed like black mirrors on the sun, as if sightless, yet containing some terrible answer to the reddening winter planet.And all the eyes of the priests were fixed and glittering on the sinking orb, in the reddening, icy silence of the winter afternoon.
他们很焦急，非常焦急，而且残暴。他们的残暴需要某种东西来发泄，他们正在等着这一刻。他们的残暴准备着跃进某种对胜利的神秘的狂喜中去。然而他们还是很焦急。 They were anxious, terribly anxious, and fierce.Their ferocity wanted something, and they were waiting the moment.And their ferocity was ready to leap out into a mystic exultance, of triumph.But still they were anxious.
只有那位最老的男人的眼睛里没有焦虑。那双黑色的眼睛好像失明了一般，一动不动地盯着太阳看，并越过它向后望去。在那双空洞而专注的黑眼睛里，有种力量，这力量极其抽象而遥远，但是很深，深入地心和日心。他一丝不动地看着，直到红红的太阳光穿过冰柱。然后，老人会敲击，狠狠敲击，完成祭祀，获得这力量。 Only the eyes of that oldest man were not anxious.Black, and fixed, and as if sightless, they watched the sun, seeing beyond the sun.And in their black, empty concentration there was power, power intensely abstract and remote, but deep, deep to the heart of the earth, and the heart of the sun.In absolute motionlessness he watched till the red sun should send his ray through the column of ice.Then the old man would strike, and strike home, accomplish the sacrifice and achieve the power.
这是人类必须掌握的统治权，它会从一个种族传到另一个种族。 The mastery that man must hold, and that passes from race to race.
