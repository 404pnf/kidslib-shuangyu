第一部分 PART I
在瑞士小镇沃韦，有一家特别舒适的旅店。实际上，这里有许多旅店，因为接待旅行者是当地最主要的行业。正如许多游客一定会记住的那样，小镇坐落在一条湖畔，湖水尤其蓝——而正是这片湖水吸引着每一位游客的到来。湖岸边整齐地排列着一排各式旅店，从最新式的“大饭店”，到旧时小巧别致的瑞士食宿公寓。大饭店都装有一扇粉笔白的正门和上百个阳台，十几面旗子从楼顶飘下。而瑞士公寓则将它的名字用看起来像德文字母的字体刻在一面粉色或黄色的墙壁上，花园的角落里还安放了一座不伦不类的凉亭。然而，在沃韦镇的诸多旅店里，有一家颇负盛名，甚至堪称经典，以其奢华、成熟的气派在相邻的暴发户中显得出类拔萃。六月份，这一地区的美国游客数不胜数。的确可以这么说，韦沃镇在这个季节具备美国温泉胜地的诸多特点。有一些景象和声音会使人想到纽波特和萨拉托加的情景和声音。时髦的年轻姑娘迈着轻快的脚步到处穿梭，平纹细布裙子的荷叶边发出沙沙的声音；上午的几个小时里有欢闹的舞曲，整天都能听到人们的尖叫声。你在美丽的三冠酒店对这些事物产生印象后，你的想象力会把你带到海洋大厦或国会大厅。但必须补充的是，在三冠酒店，其他的一些特点与这些联想截然不同：穿着整洁的德国服务生，看上去像德国公使馆的秘书；坐在花园里的俄罗斯公主；手牵着手、由家庭教师陪着四处散步的波兰男孩们；阳光灿烂的南峰峰顶的景色，以及风景如画的希永城堡塔楼。 At the little town of Vevey, in Switzerland, there is a particularly comfortable hotel.There are, indeed, many hotels, for the entertainment of tourists is the business of the place, which, as many travelers will remember, is seated upon the edge of a remarkably blue lake—a lake that it behooves every tourist to visit.The shore of the lake presents an unbroken array of establishments of this order, of every category, from the “grand hotel” of the newest fashion, with a chalk-white front, a hundred balconies, and a dozen flags flying from its roof, to the little Swiss pension of an elder day, with its name inscribed in German-looking lettering upon a pink or yellow wall and an awkward summerhouse in the angle of the garden.One of the hotels at Vevey, however, is famous, even classical, being distinguished from many of its upstart neighbors by an air both of luxury and of maturity.In this region, in the month of June, American travelers are extremely numerous; it may be said, indeed, that Vevey assumes at this period some of the characteristics of an American watering place.There are sights and sounds which evoke a vision, an echo, of Newport and Saratoga.There is a flitting hither and thither of “stylish” young girls, a rustling of muslin flounces, a rattle of dance music in the morning hours, a sound of high-pitched voices at all times.You receive an impression of these things at the excellent inn of the “Trois Couronnes” and are transported in fancy to the Ocean House or to Congress Hall.But at the “Trois Couronnes,” it must be added, there are other features that are much at variance with these suggestions: neat German waiters, who look like secretaries of legation; Russian princesses sitting in the garden; little Polish boys walking about held by the hand, with their governors; a view of the sunny crest of the Dent du Midi and the picturesque towers of the Castle of Chillon.
两三年前，一个年轻的美国人坐在三冠酒店的花园里，相当悠闲地四处打量着我先前提到的优雅景致。我几乎不知道他想到的最多的是相同之处，还是不同之处。那是一个美丽的夏天的早晨，这个年轻的美国人无论用什么方式看待事物，这些事物对他而言肯定是迷人的。前一天他乘坐小汽船从日内瓦来看望住在酒店的姑妈。他在日内瓦住了很长时间。可是他的姑妈正患头疼——他姑妈几乎总是头疼——现在她把自己关在房间里，闻着樟脑油，因而他就能四处去逛逛。他大约二十七岁，他的朋友们说起他时，通常会说他在日内瓦“学习”。他的仇人们说起他时，会说——不过话说回来，他根本没有仇人，他是个特别和蔼可亲的人，受到大家的喜欢。简而言之，我应该说的是当某些人说起他时，他们断言，他在日内瓦呆那么长时间是因为他非常倾心于一个住在那儿的小姐——一位外国小姐——一位比他年长的小姐。很少有美国人——实际上我认为没有一个美国人——曾经见过这位小姐，关于她却有一些奇异的故事。但是，温特博恩对加尔文派的这个小小的中心城市有一种由来已久的感情；还是个小男孩的时候他就被送到那儿上学，随后又在那儿上大学，这些经历他在年轻的时候就结下了许多友谊。他一直与很多人保持着友谊，对于他来说，友谊是巨大满足感的来源。 I hardly know whether it was the analogies or the differences that were uppermost in the mind of a young American, who, two or three years ago, sat in the garden of the “Trois Couronnes,” looking about him, rather idly, at some of the graceful objects I have mentioned.It was a beautiful summer morning, and in whatever fashion the young American looked at things, they must have seemed to him charming.He had come from Geneva the day before by the little steamer, to see his aunt, who was staying at the hotel—Geneva having been for a long time his place of residence.But his aunt had a headache—his aunt had almost always a headache—and now she was shut up in her room, smelling camphor, so that he was at liberty to wander about.He was some seven-and-twenty years of age; when his friends spoke of him, they usually said that he was at Geneva “studying.”When his enemies spoke of him, they said—but, after all, he had no enemies; he was an extremely amiable fellow, and universally liked.What I should say is, simply, that when certain persons spoke of him they affirmed that the reason of his spending so much time at Geneva was that he was extremely devoted to a lady who lived there—a foreign lady—a person older than himself.Very few Americans—indeed, I think none—had ever seen this lady, about whom there were some singular stories.But Winterbourne had an old attachment for the little metropolis of Calvinism; he had been put to school there as a boy, and he had afterward gone to college there—circumstances which had led to his forming a great many youthful friendships.Many of these he had kept, and they were a source of great satisfaction to him.
敲了敲姑妈的门，知道她不舒服之后，他就去小镇上四处转悠了，然后回来吃早饭。现在他吃完了早饭，正喝着一小杯咖啡，这杯咖啡是一位看上去像使馆馆员的服务生给他端到花园的小桌上的。最后，他喝完咖啡，点了一支烟。一会儿，一个小男孩儿沿着小径走来——一个九岁或十岁左右的淘气鬼。对于他的年龄而言，他显得有些矮小，但脸上透露出的却是大人的表情：脸色苍白，轮廓鲜明。他穿着灯笼裤和红色长筒袜，露出两条像车轴一样细得可怜的小腿；他还系着一条亮红色的领巾。他手里拿着一根长长的登山杖，并用尖锐的杖尖刺进他接近的每件东西——花坛、花园的长凳、女士们连衣裙的拖裙。在温特博恩面前，他停了下来，用一双明亮的、富有穿透力的小眼睛看着他。 After knocking at his aunt’s door and learning that she was indisposed, he had taken a walk about the town, and then he had come in to his breakfast.He had now finished his breakfast; but he was drinking a small cup of coffee, which had been served to him on a little table in the garden by one of the waiters who looked like an attache.At last he finished his coffee and lit a cigarette.Presently a small boy came walking along the path—an urchin of nine or ten.The child, who was diminutive for his years, had an aged expression of countenance, a pale complexion, and sharp little features.He was dressed in knickerbockers, with red stockings, which displayed his poor little spindle-shanks; he also wore a brilliant red cravat.He carried in his hand a long alpenstock, the sharp point of which he thrust into everything that he approached—the flowerbeds, the garden benches, the trains of the ladies’ dresses.In front of Winterbourne he paused, looking at him with a pair of bright, penetrating little eyes.
“你能给我一块糖吗？”他用尖锐刺耳的小嗓音问道——嗓音不够成熟，然而不知为什么，也并不年轻。 “Will you give me a lump of sugar?” he asked in a sharp, hard little voice—a voice immature and yet, somehow, not young.
温特博恩瞥了一眼他旁边的小桌子，上面摆放着他的咖啡器皿，他看到上面还留着几小块糖。“行，你可以拿一块，”他答道，“可我觉得吃糖对小男孩没好处。” Winterbourne glanced at the small table near him, on which his coffee service rested, and saw that several morsels of sugar remained.“Yes, you may take one,” he answered; “but I don’t think sugar is good for little boys.”
这个小男孩走上前，仔细挑选出了三块让他垂涎欲滴的糖块，两块塞进灯笼裤的口袋里，还有一块迅速地放到另一个地方。他将长矛式的登山杖插进温特博恩的长凳里，然后试图用牙咬碎糖块。 This little boy stepped forward and carefully selected three of the coveted fragments, two of which he buried in the pocket of his knickerbockers, depositing the other as promptly in another place.He poked his alpenstock, lance-fashion, into Winterbourne’s bench and tried to crack the lump of sugar with his teeth.
“噢！该死！好——硬！”他大喊道，用一种奇怪的方式发出这个形容词的音。 “Oh, blazes; it’s har-r-d!” he exclaimed, pronouncing the adjective in a peculiar manner.
温特博恩立刻察觉到，他可以荣幸地声称男孩是自己的同胞。“小心，不要伤到你的牙齿。”他说，像个父亲一样。 Winterbourne had immediately perceived that he might have the honor of claiming him as a fellow countryman.“Take care you don’t hurt your teeth,” he said, paternally.
“我没有牙齿可以伤到了。它们全掉了。我只有七颗牙。昨晚我妈妈数过了，之后一颗牙又掉了。她说如果牙再掉就会扇我耳光。我也没办法。就因为这个老朽的欧洲。这里的气候使我的牙掉了。在美国它们没掉过。就因为这些旅店。” “I haven’t got any teeth to hurt.They have all come out.I have only got seven teeth.My mother counted them last night, and one came out right afterward.She said she’d slap me if any more came out.I can’t help it.It’s this old Europe.It’s the climate that makes them come out.In America they didn’t come out.It’s these hotels.”
温特博恩觉得非常好玩。“如果你吃下三块糖，你妈妈肯定会扇你耳光。”他说。 Winterbourne was much amused.“If you eat three lumps of sugar, your mother will certainly slap you,” he said.
“那她就得给我一些糖果。”他的年轻交谈者回答。“在这里我找不到任何糖果——任何美国糖果。美国糖果是最好的糖果。” “She’s got to give me some candy, then,” rejoined his young interlocutor.“I can’t get any candy here—any American candy.American candy’s the best candy.”
“那美国的小男孩是最好的小男孩吗？”温特博恩问。 “And are American little boys the best little boys?” asked Winterbourne.
“我不知道。我是个美国男孩。”孩子说。 “I don’t know.I’m an American boy,” said the child.
“我看你是最好的男孩之一！”温特伯恩大笑。 “I see you are one of the best!” laughed Winterbourne.
“你是美国男人吗？”这个活泼的小孩追问道。然后，听到温特伯恩的肯定回答后，他大声宣布：“美国男人是最好的男人！” “Are you an American man?” pursued this vivacious infant.And then, on Winterbourne’s affirmative reply—“American men are the best,” he declared.
男孩的伙伴对他的恭维话表示了感谢，男孩此时已经跨在登山杖上，一边站着四处张望，一边咬着第二块糖。温特博恩不知道自己小时候是否也是这样，因为在差不多这个年龄的时候，他已经被带来欧洲了。 His companion thanked him for the compliment, and the child, who had now got astride of his alpenstock, stood looking about him, while he attacked a second lump of sugar.Winterbourne wondered if he himself had been like this in his infancy, for he had been brought to Europe at about this age.
“我姐姐来了！”一会儿，这孩子喊道。“她是个美国女孩儿。” “Here comes my sister!” cried the child in a moment.“She’s an American girl.”
温特博恩沿着小径望去，看到一位年轻漂亮的小姐正在走来。“美国女孩是最好的女孩！”他愉快地对他的小伙伴说。 Winterbourne looked along the path and saw a beautiful young lady advancing.“American girls are the best girls,” he said cheerfully to his young companion.
“我姐姐不是最好的！”孩子宣称，“她总是冲我发火。” “My sister ain’t the best!” the child declared.“She’s always blowing at me.”
“我猜那是你的错，不是她的错。”温特博恩说。与此同时，年轻小姐已经走近了。她身穿白色平纹细布裙，裙上有上百个褶皱和荷叶边，还有一些浅色的缎带结。她没戴帽子，但手里拿着一把大阳伞，伞上有绣花的宽边，她非常漂亮，引人注目，让人很是喜欢。“她们好漂亮啊！”温特博恩想，在椅子上直起身，好像他准备站起来的样子。 “I imagine that is your fault, not hers,” said Winterbourne.The young lady meanwhile had drawn near.She was dressed in white muslin, with a hundred frills and flounces, and knots of pale-colored ribbon.She was bareheaded, but she balanced in her hand a large parasol, with a deep border of embroidery; and she was strikingly, admirably pretty.“How pretty they are!” thought Winterbourne, straightening himself in his seat, as if he were prepared to rise.
年轻小姐在他的长凳前停下来。长凳离花园栏杆很近，正对着湖水。小男孩现在已经将登山杖变成了撑杆，借助它的帮助，在砂砾地上四处跳跃，踢起了很多砂砾。 The young lady paused in front of his bench, near the parapet of the garden, which overlooked the lake.The little boy had now converted his alpenstock into a vaulting pole, by the aid of which he was springing about in the gravel and kicking it up not a little.
“伦道夫，”年轻小姐说，“你在干什么？” “Randolph,” said the young lady, “what ARE you doing?”
“我在登阿尔卑斯山，”伦道夫回答，“就是这样登山的！”他又蹦了一下，将砾石溅到了温特伯恩的耳朵周围。 “I’m going up the Alps,” replied Randolph.“This is the way!”And he gave another little jump, scattering the pebbles about Winterbourne’s ears.
“那是人们下山的方法，”温特博恩说。 “That’s the way they come down,” said Winterbourne.
“他是个美国男人！”伦道夫用尖锐的小嗓音喊道。 “He’s an American man!” cried Randolph, in his little hard voice.
年轻小姐并没有注意这句话，而是直盯着他的弟弟看。“好了，我想你最好安静一下。”她简短地说了一句。 The young lady gave no heed to this announcement, but looked straight at her brother.“Well, I guess you had better be quiet,” she simply observed.
在温特博恩看来，他似乎已经以某一种方式被人作了介绍。他站起身，缓慢走向年轻女孩，还扔掉了他的香烟。“这个小男孩和我已经认识了。”他很有礼貌地说。他非常清楚地意识到，在日内瓦，一个年轻男子不能随便和一个年轻的未婚女子说话，除非是某些极个别的特殊场合；但这里是沃韦，还有什么情况比现在更好呢？——在花园里，一个漂亮的美国姑娘来了并站在你的面前。然而，听到温特博恩的话，这个漂亮的美国姑娘只是瞥了他一眼，随后她就转过头，目光越过栏杆，看着湖水和对面的山。他不知道自己是否做得过分了，但决定必须前进，而不是后退。他正在考虑说点别的话，年轻小姐又一次转向小男孩。 It seemed to Winterbourne that he had been in a manner presented.He got up and stepped slowly toward the young girl, throwing away his cigarette.“This little boy and I have made acquaintance,” he said, with great civility.In Geneva, as he had been perfectly aware, a young man was not at liberty to speak to a young unmarried lady except under certain rarely occurring conditions; but here at Vevey, what conditions could be better than these?—a pretty American girl coming and standing in front of you in a garden.This pretty American girl, however, on hearing Winterbourne’s observation, simply glanced at him; she then turned her head and looked over the parapet, at the lake and the opposite mountains.He wondered whether he had gone too far, but he decided that he must advance farther, rather than retreat.While he was thinking of something else to say, the young lady turned to the little boy again.
“我想知道你是在哪儿得到那根杆的。”她说。 “I should like to know where you got that pole,” she said.
“我买的。”伦道夫回答。 “I bought it,” responded Randolph.
“你不是想说你要把它带到意大利吧？” “You don’t mean to say you’re going to take it to Italy?”
“对，我要把它带到意大利。”孩子宣布。 “Yes, I am going to take it to Italy,” the child declared.
年轻姑娘扫了一眼自己裙子的前面，把一两个缎带结弄平了。而后她的目光再次落到景色上。“好了，我想你最好把它留在某个地方。”过了片刻，她说道。 The young girl glanced over the front of her dress and smoothed out a knot or two of ribbon.Then she rested her eyes upon the prospect again.“Well, I guess you had better leave it somewhere,” she said after a moment.
“你们要去意大利吗？”温特博恩用一种十分恭敬的语气询问道。 “Are you going to Italy?” Winterbourne inquired in a tone of great respect.
年轻小姐又瞥了他一眼。“是的，先生。”她回答。接着她又不说话了。 The young lady glanced at him again.“Yes, sir,” she replied.And she said nothing more.
“你们……要……翻越辛普朗山吗？”温特博恩继续问，稍有点尴尬。 “Are you—a—going over the Simplon?”Winterbourne pursued, a little embarrassed.
“我不知道。”她说，“我想是某座山吧。伦道夫，我们打算翻越什么山？” “I don’t know,” she said.“I suppose it’s some mountain.Randolph, what mountain are we going over?”
“去哪儿？”孩子问。 “Going where?” the child demanded.
“去意大利。”温特博恩解释。 “To Italy,” Winterbourne explained.
“我不知道。”伦道夫说，“我不想去意大利。我想去美国。” “I don’t know,” said Randolph.“I don’t want to go to Italy.I want to go to America.”
“哦，意大利是个美丽的地方！”年轻人再次加入谈话。 “Oh, Italy is a beautiful place!” rejoined the young man.
“在那儿能买到糖果吗？”伦道夫大声询问。 “Can you get candy there?” Randolph loudly inquired.
“我希望买不到。”他姐姐说，“我想你已经吃了足够多的糖果了，妈妈也这么认为。” “I hope not,” said his sister.“I guess you have had enough candy, and mother thinks so too.”
“我已经好长时间没吃了——有一百个星期了！”男孩嚷嚷，还在到处蹦蹦跳跳。 “I haven’t had any for ever so long—for a hundred weeks!” cried the boy, still jumping about.
年轻小姐仔细看了看她的荷叶边，再次将她的缎带结抚平；片刻后，温特博恩冒险大胆地对美丽的景色作了评论。他不再感到尴尬，因为他开始意识到，她本人一点也不尴尬。她迷人的面容一点也没有变化，很明显她既没有生气，也没有觉得受到奉承。如果他和她说话，她却看着别处，似乎没有特别听他说话，这仅仅是她的习惯、她的方式而已。然而，当他多讲了一点，并指出视野内一些有趣的但似乎她很陌生的物体时，她开始逐渐向他投去了更多的目光；这时他发现这种目光相当直接和坚定。然而，这不是所谓的不庄重的目光，因为这年轻姑娘的眼睛异常地诚实和单纯。那是美丽绝伦的眼睛。事实上，温特博恩已经很长时间没有看到过比他的这位美丽的女同胞五官更漂亮的了——她的面容，她的鼻子，她的耳朵，还有她的牙齿。他非常喜爱女性的美，他痴迷于观察和分析美。关于这位年轻女士的脸，观察后他做了几点总结。她的脸也不是没有味道，可就是没有恰到好处地表现出来；尽管非常精致，但温特博恩心里还是指责它——非常仁慈地——不完美。他想，很可能伦道夫少爷的姐姐是个卖弄风情的女人，他确信她有自己的性格，可是在她亮丽、甜美、肤浅的小脸上，没有嘲弄，没有讽刺。很快，有一点就变得很明显了，她其实非常乐于交谈。她告诉他，他们打算去罗马过冬——她，她母亲，和伦道夫。她问他是否是“真正的美国人”；她不该把他当作一个美国人，他看起来更像德国人——这是稍稍犹豫后说的——尤其是当他说话的时候。温特博恩大笑着回答，他遇到过说话像美国人的德国人，但在他的记忆范围内，他还没有遇到过说话像德国人的美国人。然后，他问她，如果她坐在他刚刚离开的长凳上，是不是会更舒服一点。她回答说，她喜欢站着或者到处走走，但很快她还是坐下了。她告诉他，她来自纽约州——“如果你知道它在哪儿的话。”温特博恩抓住她瘦小、跑来跑去的弟弟，让他在自己身边站了几分钟，于是了解到更多有关她的情况。 The young lady inspected her flounces and smoothed her ribbons again; 温特博恩犹豫了一刻。“我更应该和你一起去希永城堡。” Winterbourne hesitated a moment.“I should much rather go to Chillon with you.”
“和我？”年轻女孩带着同样的平静问道。 “With me?” asked the young girl with the same placidity.
她不像一般的日内瓦女孩，并没有起身，脸倒是红了；但温特博恩意识到刚才他很胆大，心想可能她会生气。“和你妈妈一起。”他很恭敬地说。 She didn’t rise, blushing, as a young girl at Geneva would have done; and yet Winterbourne, conscious that he had been very bold, thought it possible she was offended.“With your mother,” he answered very respectfully.
可是，似乎他的鲁莽和恭敬都没有引起戴西·米勒小姐的注意。“我猜我妈妈最终也不会去，”她说，“她不喜欢下午到处乘车走。可是你刚才说的话是真的吗？你愿意去那儿？” But it seemed that both his audacity and his respect were lost upon Miss Daisy Miller.“I guess my mother won’t go, after all,” she said.“She don’t like to ride round in the afternoon.But did you really mean what you said just now—that you would like to go up there?”
“非常认真。”温特博恩郑重地说道。 “Most earnestly,” Winterbourne declared.
“那我们可以安排一下。如果妈妈和伦道夫呆在一起，我想欧金尼奥也会。” “Then we may arrange it.If mother will stay with Randolph, I guess Eugenio will.”
“欧金尼奥？”年轻人问。 “Eugenio?” the young man inquired.
“欧金尼奥是我们的侍从。他不喜欢和伦道夫在一起。他是我见过的最挑剔的男人。可他是个出色的侍从。我猜，如果妈妈陪伦道夫，他会呆在家，那么我们就能去城堡了。” “Eugenio’s our courier.He doesn’t like to stay with Randolph; he’s the most fastidious man I ever saw.But he’s a splendid courier.I guess he’ll stay at home with Randolph if mother does, and then we can go to the castle.”
温特博恩深思了片刻，希望尽可能地弄明白——“我们”只可能意味着戴西·米勒小姐和他自己。这个计划看似太合他的想法了，他觉得他似乎应该吻一下这位年轻小姐的手。可能他要是这么做会破坏这个计划，可就在这一刻，另一个人，可能是欧金奥尼，出现了。一个高个子的英俊男人，有漂亮的连鬓胡子，穿着丝绒晨礼服，戴着一根亮亮的表链；他走近米勒小姐，用锐利的目光看着她的同伴。“噢，欧金尼奥！”米勒小姐友好地说。 Winterbourne reflected for an instant as lucidly as possible—“we” could only mean Miss Daisy Miller and himself.This program seemed almost too agreeable for credence; he felt as if he ought to kiss the young lady’s hand.Possibly he would have done so and quite spoiled the project, but at this moment another person, presumably Eugenio, appeared.A tall, handsome man, with superb whiskers, wearing a velvet morning coat and a brilliant watch chain, approached Miss Miller, looking sharply at her companion.“Oh, Eugenio!” said Miss Miller with the friendliest accent.
欧金尼奥从头到脚打量了温特博恩，现在，他毕恭毕敬地向年轻小姐鞠躬。“我荣幸地通知小姐，午餐已经上桌了。” Eugenio had looked at Winterbourne from head to foot; he now bowed gravely to the young lady.“I have the honor to inform mademoiselle that luncheon is upon the table.”
米勒小姐慢慢站起身。“看这儿，欧金尼奥！”她说，“无论如何，我都打算去古堡。” Miss Miller slowly rose.“See here, Eugenio!” she said; “I’m going to that old castle, anyway.”
“去希永古堡吗？”侍从问，“小姐作了安排吗？”他补充说道，说话的语气让温特博恩觉得非常无礼。 “To the Chateau de Chillon, mademoiselle?” the courier inquired.“Mademoiselle has made arrangements?” he added in a tone which struck Winterbourne as very impertinent.
就连米勒小姐也感觉到，欧金尼奥的语气明显是对她处境的一种轻微讽刺。她转向温特博恩，稍有点脸红——一点点。“你不会收回你的话吧？”她说。 Eugenio’s tone apparently threw, even to Miss Miller’s own apprehension, a slightly ironical light upon the young girl’s situation.She turned to Winterbourne, blushing a little—a very little.“You won’t back out?” she said.
“如果我们不去，我是不会开心的！”他表示。 “I shall not be happy till we go!” he protested.
“你也住在这家旅店吧？”她继续问，“你真的是美国人吗？” “And you are staying in this hotel?” she went on.“And you are really an American?”
侍从站着，看温特博恩的眼神很不友善。至少这个年轻人认为侍从看人的方式是对米勒小姐的冒犯；它传递了对她“随便结识人”的指责。“我将荣幸地给你介绍一个人，他会告诉你我的全部。”他微笑着说，指的是他的姑妈。 The courier stood looking at Winterbourne offensively.The young man, at least, thought his manner of looking an offense to Miss Miller; it conveyed an imputation that she “picked up” acquaintances.“I shall have the honor of presenting to you a person who will tell you all about me,” he said, smiling and referring to his aunt.
“哦，好的，我改天去。”米勒小姐说。她冲他微笑了一下，转身离开了。她撑起阳伞和欧金尼奥一起走回了旅店。温特博恩站在她身后看着她；当她拖着她的平纹细布长裙走过沙坑时，他对自己说，她有公主的模样。 “Oh, well, we’ll go some day,” said Miss Miller.And she gave him a smile and turned away.She put up her parasol and walked back to the inn beside Eugenio.Winterbourne stood looking after her; and as she moved away, drawing her muslin furbelows over the gravel, said to himself that she had the tournure of a princess.
然而，在许诺将他的姑妈科斯特洛夫人介绍给戴西·米勒小姐这件事上，他答应得很好，但是实际没有完全做到。科斯特洛女士的头疼刚有好转，他就在她的房间里服侍她。就她的健康作了适当的询问后，他就问她是否注意到旅店里的一个美国家庭——一个妈妈，一个女儿，和一个小男孩。 He had, however, engaged to do more than proved feasible, in promising to present his aunt, Mrs. Costello, to Miss Daisy Miller.As soon as the former lady had got better of her headache, he waited upon her in her apartment; and, after the proper inquiries in regard to her health, he asked her if she had observed in the hotel an American family—a mamma, a daughter, and a little boy.
“还有一个侍从？”科斯特洛夫人问。“噢，对，我注意到他们了。见过他们，听过他们说话，现在尽量避开他们。”科斯特洛夫人是个有大笔财产的寡妇，一个很有声望的人，她经常宣称，要不是她那么容易犯头疼病，她也许会在有生之年给人留下更深的印象。她的脸很长很苍白，一个高鼻子，白发很多很扎眼，在头上梳成大而松的发卷。她有两个儿子在纽约成了家，另一个现在正在欧洲。这个年轻人正在汉堡自娱自乐。并且，虽然他在旅行，但却很少去参观任何他母亲选定的城市。这个专门来沃韦看望她的侄子，如她所说，要比她的亲骨肉，更加贴心。他在日内瓦就被灌输了这种观点，一个人应该经常关心他的姑妈。科斯特洛夫人很多年没见过他了，见到他非常高兴。为了表明她对他的认可，她向他传授了很多获得社会影响力的秘密。她还告诉他，她在美国的首都就运用过这一套。她承认自己是非常孤傲的，可是，如果他熟悉纽约，他会看到，一个人不得不这样。她从不同角度向他描绘出那个城市细微的社会等级，这对于温特博恩之前的想象，是一种沉重的打击。 “And a courier?” said Mrs. Costello.“Oh yes, I have observed them.Seen them—heard them—and kept out of their way.”Mrs. Costello was a widow with a fortune; a person of much distinction, who frequently intimated that, if she were not so dreadfully liable to sick headaches, she would probably have left a deeper impress upon her time.She had a long, pale face, a high nose, and a great deal of very striking white hair, which she wore in large puffs and rouleaux over the top of her head.She had two sons married in New York and another who was now in Europe.This young man was amusing himself at Hamburg, and, though he was on his travels, was rarely perceived to visit any particular city at the moment selected by his mother for her own appearance there.Her nephew, who had come up to Vevey expressly to see her, was therefore more attentive than those who, as she said, were nearer to her.He had imbibed at Geneva the idea that one must always be attentive to one’s aunt.Mrs. Costello had not seen him for many years, and she was greatly pleased with him, manifesting her approbation by initiating him into many of the secrets of that social sway which, as she gave him to understand, she exerted in the American capital.She admitted that she was very exclusive; but, if he were acquainted with New York, he would see that one had to be.And her picture of the minutely hierarchical constitution of the society of that city, which she presented to him in many different lights, was, to Winterbourne’s imagination, almost oppressively striking.
从她的语气里他立刻意识到，戴西·米勒小姐在社会等级上是低的。“我恐怕您并不赞同他们。”他说。 He immediately perceived, from her tone, that Miss Daisy Miller’s place in the social scale was low.“I am afraid you don’t approve of them,” he said.
“他们非常粗俗。”科斯特洛夫人宣称，“他们是那种根本不承担责任的美国人。” “They are very common,” Mrs. Costello declared.“They are the sort of Americans that one does one’s duty by not—not accepting.”
“哦，您不接受他们？”年轻人说。 “Ah, you don’t accept them?” said the young man.
“我不能，我亲爱的弗雷德里克。如果我能的话我会的，但我不能。” “I can’t, my dear Frederick.I would if I could, but I can’t.”
“那年轻姑娘很漂亮。”过了一会儿，温特博恩说道。 “The young girl is very pretty,” said Winterbourne in a moment.
“她当然漂亮。可她非常俗气。” “Of course she’s pretty.But she is very common.”
“当然，我明白您什么意思。”停顿了一下后，温特博恩才说。 “I see what you mean, of course,” said Winterbourne after another pause.
“她有着他们那种人都有的迷人外表。”他的姑妈接着说，“我想不出他们是从哪儿获得这么迷人的外表。而且她穿着很讲究——不，你都不知道她穿得有多好。我想不出他们是从哪儿获得他们的品位的。” “She has that charming look that they all have,” his aunt resumed.“I can’t think where they pick it up; and she dresses in perfection—no, you don’t know how well she dresses.I can’t think where they get their taste.”
“可是，我亲爱的姑妈，毕竟她不是一个科曼奇族野人。” “But, my dear aunt, she is not, after all, a Comanche savage.”
“她是个年轻小姐，”科斯特洛夫人说，“和她妈妈的侍从有一种亲密的关系。” “She is a young lady,” said Mrs. Costello, “who has an intimacy with her mamma’s courier.”
“和侍从很亲密？”年轻人急切地问道。 “An intimacy with the courier?” the young man demanded.
“噢，她母亲也不是一个好货色！他们对待侍从就像对一个熟悉的朋友——一个绅士。如果他同他们一起吃饭，我也没什么好奇怪的。很有可能，他们从未见过一个男人，有这么好的举止，这么精美的衣服，这么像一个绅士。他可能吻合那位年轻小姐理想中的伯爵形象。晚上他同他们坐在花园里。我想他还吸烟呢。” “Oh, the mother is just as bad!They treat the courier like a familiar friend—like a gentleman.I shouldn’t wonder if he dines with them.Very likely they have never seen a man with such good manners, such fine clothes, so like a gentleman.He probably corresponds to the young lady’s idea of a count.He sits with them in the garden in the evening.I think he smokes.”
温特博恩饶有兴趣地听着姑妈的揭发，这些事件能让他最终决定怎么看待戴西小姐。显然她相当野性。“嗯，”他说，“我不是侍从，可是她对我也很好。” Winterbourne listened with interest to these disclosures; they helped him to make up his mind about Miss Daisy.Evidently she was rather wild.“Well,” he said, “I am not a courier, and yet she was very charming to me.”
“你最好一开始就和我说明，”科斯特洛夫人带着威严说，“你已经结识她了。” “You had better have said at first,” said Mrs. Costello with dignity, “that you had made her acquaintance.”
“我们只是在花园里遇到，并且讲了点话。” “We simply met in the garden, and we talked a bit.”
“好极了！请说说你们说了什么？” “Tout bonnement!And pray what did you say?”
“我说我应该冒昧把她介绍给我令人钦佩的姑妈。” “I said I should take the liberty of introducing her to my admirable aunt.”
“我非常感谢你。” “I am much obliged to you.”
“那会担保我是一个体面人士。”温特博恩说。 “It was to guarantee my respectability,” said Winterbourne.
“请说说，谁能为她担保呢？” “And pray who is to guarantee hers?”
“哦，您好残忍！”年轻人说。“她是一个非常好的年轻姑娘。” “Ah, you are cruel!” said the young man.“She’s a very nice young girl.”
“你不要这么说，好像你真的相信似的。”科斯特洛夫人评论道。 “You don’t say that as if you believed it,” Mrs. Costello observed.
“她完全没有教养。”温特博恩继续说，“但是她美丽非凡，简而言之，她非常好。为了证明我相信这点，我打算带她去希永古堡。” “She is completely uncultivated,” Winterbourne went on.“But she is wonderfully pretty, and, in short, she is very nice.To prove that I believe it, I am going to take her to the Chateau de Chillon.”
“你们两个打算一起去那儿吗？我要说，事实证明正好相反。我可以问一下吗，当这个有趣的计划形成的时候，你认识她多久了？你在旅店还不到二十四个小时。” “You two are going off there together?I should say it proved just the contrary.How long had you known her, may I ask, when this interesting project was formed?You haven’t been twenty-four hours in the house.”
“我已经认识她半个小时了！”温特博恩说，微笑着。 “I have known her half an hour!” said Winterbourne, smiling.
“老天！”科斯特洛夫人大喊，“多么可怕的姑娘啊！” “Dear me!” cried Mrs. Costello.“What a dreadful girl!”
她的侄子沉默了些许时刻。“那么，您真的认为，”他开始变得很诚恳，希望能够得到可靠的信息，“您真的认为——”但他再次停顿下来。 Her nephew was silent for some moments.“You really think, then,” he began earnestly, and with a desire for trustworthy information—“you really think that—” But he paused again.
“认为什么，先生？”他的姑妈说。 “Think what, sir?” said his aunt.
“她是那种期望一个男人迟早把她夺走的女孩？” “That she is the sort of young lady who expects a man, sooner or later, to carry her off?”
“这样的小姐们期望男人做什么，我一点也不知道。可是我真的认为你最好不要和那些，如你所说的没有教养的美国姑娘搞在一起。你在美国以外生活的时间太长了。你肯定要犯大错的。你太天真了。” “I haven’t the least idea what such young ladies expect a man to do.But I really think that you had better not meddle with little American girls that are uncultivated, as you call them.You have lived too long out of the country.You will be sure to make some great mistake.You are too innocent.”
“我亲爱的姑妈，我不像您说的那么天真。”温特博恩说，微笑着卷了卷胡子。 “My dear aunt, I am not so innocent,” said Winterbourne, smiling and curling his mustache.
“那么，你也犯了什么错吧！” “You are guilty too, then!”
温特博恩继续卷他的胡子，沉思着。“那么，您不会让那个可怜的姑娘认识您了？”他最后问。 Winterbourne continued to curl his mustache meditatively.“You won’t let the poor girl know you then?” he asked at last.
“她打算和你去希永古堡，这是真的吗？” “Is it literally true that she is going to the Chateau de Chillon with you?”
“我想她真的打算这么做。” “I think that she fully intends it.”
“那么，我亲爱的弗雷德里克，”科斯特洛夫人说，“我必须拒绝见她，我没这个荣幸。我是个老太太，可我没有老到对什么都无动于衷的地步，感谢上天！” “Then, my dear Frederick,” said Mrs. Costello, “I must decline the honor of her acquaintance.I am an old woman, but I am not too old, thank Heaven, to be shocked!”
“可是，难道她们——在美国的年轻姑娘，不是都这么做吗？”温特博恩问。 “But don’t they all do these things—the young girls in America?” Winterbourne inquired.
科斯特洛夫人凝视了一会儿。“我倒愿意看看我的孙女们这么做！”她语气很冷酷。 Mrs. Costello stared a moment.“I should like to see my granddaughters do them!” she declared grimly.
这似乎能够说明点什么，因为温特博恩记得曾经听说过，他在纽约的漂亮侄女们都“非常地会卖弄风骚。”因此，如果戴西·米勒小姐超过了这些年轻女士的自由权限，那么她做出任何事都是很可能的。温特博恩急切地想再次见到她，同时他也恼火自己，他竟然不能凭直觉公证地评价她。 This seemed to throw some light upon the matter, for Winterbourne remembered to have heard that his pretty cousins in New York were “tremendous flirts.”If, therefore, Miss Daisy Miller exceeded the liberal margin allowed to these young ladies, it was probable that anything might be expected of her.Winterbourne was impatient to see her again, and he was vexed with himself that, by instinct, he should not appreciate her justly.
尽管他急切地要见她，他几乎不知道怎么说明他姑妈拒绝认识她这件事，但他很快发现，和戴西·米勒小姐在一起，根本没有必要太小心翼翼。那天晚上，他在花园里看到，在温暖的星光下像一个慵懒的空气精灵一样四处漫步，来回摇动着一把他见过的最大的扇子。当时是十点钟。他和他的姑妈吃过了晚饭。晚饭后一直和她坐着，刚刚和她告别说明天再见。戴西米勒小姐似乎非常高兴见到他，她宣告说那是她度过的最长的夜晚。 Though he was impatient to see her, he hardly knew what he should say to her about his aunt’s refusal to become acquainted with her; but he discovered, promptly enough, that with Miss Daisy Miller there was no great need of walking on tiptoe.He found her that evening in the garden, wandering about in the warm starlight like an indolent sylph, and swinging to and fro the largest fan he had ever beheld.It was ten o’clock.He had dined with his aunt, had been sitting with her since dinner, and had just taken leave of her till the morrow.Miss Daisy Miller seemed very glad to see him; she declared it was the longest evening she had ever passed.
“你一直独自呆着吗？”他问。 “Have you been all alone?” he asked.
“我和妈妈到处走了走。可是妈妈厌倦了四处散步。”她回答。 “I have been walking round with mother.But mother gets tired walking round,” she answered.
“她上床睡觉了吗？” “Has she gone to bed?”
“没有，她不喜欢上床睡觉。”年轻姑娘说，“她不怎么睡觉——睡不上三小时。她说她不知道怎么活下来的。她极其神经质。我猜她实际睡得比她认为的多。她不知去什么地方找伦道夫去了。她想设法让他上床睡觉。他不喜欢上床睡觉。” “No; she doesn’t like to go to bed,” said the young girl.“She doesn’t sleep—not three hours.She says she doesn’t know how she lives.She’s dreadfully nervous.I guess she sleeps more than she thinks.She’s gone somewhere after Randolph; she wants to try to get him to go to bed.He doesn’t like to go to bed.”
“希望她能说服他。”温特博恩说。 “Let us hope she will persuade him,” observed Winterbourne.

刚才谈及的女士停止向前，在原地徘徊，模模糊糊的。 The lady in question, ceasing to advance, hovered vaguely about the spot at which she had checked her steps.

“噢，那么，”温特博恩说，“我最好离开你吧。” “Ah, then,” said Winterbourne, “I had better leave you.”
“哦，别，还是跟着我来吧！”戴西·米勒小姐催促道。 “Oh, no; come on!” urged Miss Daisy Miller.
“恐怕你母亲不会赞同我和你散步。” “I’m afraid your mother doesn’t approve of my walking with you.”

Well, I don’t know who it’s for!But mother doesn’t like any of my gentlemen friends.She’s right down timid.She always makes a fuss if I introduce a gentleman.But I DO introduce them—almost always.If I didn’t introduce my gentlemen friends to Mother,” the young girl added in her little soft, flat monotone, “I shouldn’t think I was natural.”
“要介绍我，”温特博恩说，“你必须知道我的名字。”接着他把他的名字说了一遍。 “To introduce me,” said Winterbourne, “you must know my name.”And he proceeded to pronounce it.

她的母亲矮小、瘦削、轻盈，眼神游离，细小的鼻子，宽大的额头，上面垂落着不少纤细的卷发。和她女儿一样，米勒夫人穿着极为优雅，她在耳朵上戴着巨大的钻石。据温特博恩能观察，她没有和他打招呼——她肯定没有在看他。戴西在她身边，拉直她的披肩。“你在干什么，在这儿到处瞎走？”这个年轻小姐问道，虽然这句话带有粗暴的意味，但是她的语调中却丝毫没有。 Her mother was a small, spare, light person, with a wandering eye, a very exiguous nose, and a large forehead, decorated with a certain amount of thin, much frizzled hair.Like her daughter, Mrs. Miller was dressed with extreme elegance; she had enormous diamonds in her ears.So far as Winterbourne could observe, she gave him no greeting—she certainly was not looking at him.Daisy was near her, pulling her shawl straight.“What are you doing, poking round here?” this young lady inquired, but by no means with that harshness of accent which her choice of words may imply.

“是的，或者坐船。”温特博恩说。 “Yes, or in the boat,” said Winterbourne.
“哦，当然，我不知道。”米勒夫人说，“我从未去过那座城堡。” “Well, of course, I don’t know,” Mrs. Miller rejoined.“I have never been to that castle.”

科斯特洛夫人稍稍嗅了一下她的樟脑瓶。“那么，”她厉声说，“那个就是你想让我认识的年轻人吧！” Mrs. Costello sniffed a little at her smelling bottle.“And that,” she exclaimed, “is the young person whom you wanted me to know!”

温特伯恩去希永去远足后，于次日返回了日内瓦，转年一月底去了罗马。他姑妈已经在那里住了几个星期，他收到了她的两三封信。“去年夏天在沃韦你如此关心的那些人来这里了，侍从等等。”她写道，“他们似乎已经结交了一些人，可那个侍从仍然是最亲密的。然而，那位年轻小姐还和一些三流意大利人非常亲密，她和他们寻欢作乐，引来了不少闲言碎语。给我带谢尔比列那本动人的小说《波勒·梅雷》，不要晚于23号到。” Winterbourne, who had returned to Geneva the day after his excursion to Chillon, went to Rome toward the end of January.His aunt had been established there for several weeks, and he had received a couple of letters from her.“Those people you were so devoted to last summer at Vevey have turned up here, courier and all,” she wrote.“They seem to have made several acquaintances, but the courier continues to be the most intime.The young lady, however, is also very intimate with some third-rate Italians, with whom she rackets about in a way that makes much talk.Bring me that pretty novel of Cherbuliez’s—Paule Mere—and don’t come later than the 23rd.”
按照常规，温特伯恩一到罗马，就会在美国银行家那里立刻查明米勒夫人的地址，去向戴西小姐问好。“经历了沃韦发生的事情，我想我肯定应该拜访她们。”他对科斯特洛夫人说。 In the natural course of events, Winterbourne, on arriving in Rome, would presently have ascertained Mrs. Miller’s address at the American banker’s and have gone to pay his compliments to Miss Daisy.“After what happened at Vevey, I think I may certainly call upon them,” he said to Mrs. Costello.
“如果经历了沃韦和别处发生的那些事情之后，你还渴望继续交往，那你随便。当然，一个男人可以认识每个人。男人有这种特权的！” “If, after what happens—at Vevey and everywhere—you desire to keep up the acquaintance, you are very welcome.Of course a man may know everyone.Men are welcome to the privilege!”
“请问，这里发生了什么事？比如——”温特伯恩问道。 “Pray what is it that happens—here, for instance?” Winterbourne demanded.
“那个姑娘独自和一些外国人四处游荡。至于还发生了什么，你就得去别处了解情况了。她随便结识了半打专门追逐有钱女子的罗马男人，还带着他们到处去别人家。当她来参加聚会的时候，总会带着一个很有礼貌、长着漂亮胡子的绅士。” “The girl goes about alone with her foreigners.As to what happens further, you must apply elsewhere for information.She has picked up half a dozen of the regular Roman fortune hunters, and she takes them about to people’s houses.When she comes to a party she brings with her a gentleman with a good deal of manner and a wonderful mustache.”
“那位母亲在哪里？” “And where is the mother?”
“我一点也不知道。他们是非常可怕的人。” “I haven’t the least idea.They are very dreadful people.”
温特伯恩沉思片刻。“他们非常无知——只是非常天真。真的，他们不是坏人。” Winterbourne meditated a moment.“They are very ignorant—very innocent only.Depend upon it they are not bad.”
“他们粗俗得无可救药。”科斯特洛夫人说。“粗俗得无可救药是否算‘坏’是玄学家考虑的问题。不管怎样，他们坏得足以令人生厌，对于这短暂的人生，那就已经足够了。” “They are hopelessly vulgar,” said Mrs. Costello.“Whether or no being hopelessly vulgar is being ‘bad’ is a question for the metaphysicians.They are bad enough to dislike, at any rate; and for this short life that is quite enough.”
听闻戴西·米勒被一打漂亮胡子围着，温特伯恩打消了直接去看她的冲动。或许他并未明确地认为自己已在她心中留下了难以忘怀的印象，可是，听到一种与他最近沉思中里里外外掠过的形象如此不和谐的状况，他感到很恼火；那是这样一种形象：一位非常漂亮的姑娘正从一个老式罗马窗户向外张望，急迫地问自己温特伯恩先生什么时候会到来。然而，他决定稍等一下再去提醒米勒小姐要顾及他的权利，他就很快去拜访了两三个其他的朋友。这些朋友中有一个是位美国女士，她已经在日内瓦度过了好几个冬天，她把孩子们安置在那里上学。她是位非常有成就的女性，住在维亚·格雷戈里亚娜大街。温特伯恩在三楼的一个深红色的小客厅里找到了她；房间里充满了南方的阳光。他在那里呆了还不到十分钟，仆人就进来宣布“米拉夫人到！”话音未落，小伦道夫·米勒便走了进来，他停在了房间中央，站在那儿盯着温特伯恩看。片刻后，他漂亮的姐姐跨过了门槛；然后，过了一段相当长的间隔，米勒夫人慢慢走了进来。 The news that Daisy Miller was surrounded by half a dozen wonderful mustaches checked Winterbourne’s impulse to go straightway to see her.He had, perhaps, not definitely flattered himself that he had made an ineffaceable impression upon her heart, but he was annoyed at hearing of a state of affairs so little in harmony with an image that had lately flitted in and out of his own meditations; the image of a very pretty girl looking out of an old Roman window and asking herself urgently when Mr. Winterbourne would arrive.If, however, he determined to wait a little before reminding Miss Miller of his claims to her consideration, he went very soon to call upon two or three other friends.One of these friends was an American lady who had spent several winters at Geneva, where she had placed her children at school.She was a very accomplished woman, and she lived in the Via Gregoriana.Winterbourne found her in a little crimson drawing room on a third floor; the room was filled with southern sunshine.He had not been there ten minutes when the servant came in, announcing “Madame Mila!”This announcement was presently followed by the entrance of little Randolph Miller, who stopped in the middle of the room and stood staring at Winterbourne.An instant later his pretty sister crossed the threshold; and then, after a considerable interval, Mrs. Miller slowly advanced.
“我认识你！”伦道夫说。 “I know you!” said Randolph.
“我肯定你知道很多事。”温特伯恩大声说，抓住了他的手。“你学上得怎么样？” “I’m sure you know a great many things,” exclaimed Winterbourne, taking him by the hand.“How is your education coming on?”
戴西正热情地和女主人互相问候，可是当她听到温特伯恩的声音，便迅速地扭过头来。“嘿！我说么！”她叫道。 Daisy was exchanging greetings very prettily with her hostess, but when she heard Winterbourne’s voice she quickly turned her head.“Well, I declare!” she said.
“我告诉过你我会来，你知道。”温特伯恩回答道，微笑着。 “I told you I should come, you know,” Winterbourne rejoined, smiling.
“哦，我没相信。”戴西小姐说。 “Well, I didn’t believe it,” said Miss Daisy.
“我非常感谢你。”年轻人大笑。 “I am much obliged to you,” laughed the young man.
“你早就应该来看我了！”戴西说。 “You might have come to see me!” said Daisy.
“我昨天才到的。” “I arrived only yesterday.”
“我不相信！”年轻姑娘说道。 “I don’t believe that!” the young girl declared.
温特伯恩带着抗议的微笑转向她的母亲，但是这位女士避开了他的目光，并且坐了下来，将目光固定在她的儿子身上。“我们找到了一个比这儿大的地方。”伦道夫说，“墙上全是金子。” Winterbourne turned with a protesting smile to her mother, but this lady evaded his glance, and, seating herself, fixed her eyes upon her son.“We’ve got a bigger place than this,” said Randolph.“It’s all gold on the walls.”
米勒夫人在她的椅子上不安地转过身来。“我和你说过要是带你来，你准会说点什么！”她轻声说。 Mrs. Miller turned uneasily in her chair.“I told you if I were to bring you, you would say something!” she murmured.
“我和你说过！”伦道夫喊道。“我告诉你，先生！”他开玩笑地加上一句，在温特伯恩的膝盖上捶了一下。“还比这儿大！” “I told YOU!” Randolph exclaimed.“I tell YOU, sir!” he added jocosely, giving Winterbourne a thump on the knee.“It IS bigger, too!”
戴西已经开始和女主人愉快地交谈了，温特伯恩觉得该和她母亲说上几句话。“我想自我们在沃韦分别后，您一直很好吧。”他说。 Daisy had entered upon a lively conversation with her hostess; Winterbourne judged it becoming to address a few words to her mother.“I hope you have been well since we parted at Vevey,” he said.
米勒夫人现在注视着他——盯他的下巴。“不太好，先生。”她回答。 Mrs. Miller now certainly looked at him—at his chin.“Not very well, sir,” she answered.
“她消化不良。”伦道夫说，“我也是。父亲也是。我最严重！” “She’s got the dyspepsia,” said Randolph.“I’ve got it too.Father’s got it.I’ve got it most!”
这番话没有使米勒夫人难堪，相反，似乎使她更轻松了。“我肝脏很难受。”她说，“我想是因为这气候，这里没有斯克内克塔迪气候宜人，尤其是冬季。我不知道你是否知道我们居住在斯克内克塔迪。我对戴西说，我当然找不到像戴维斯医生那样的人，我觉得我是不会找到的。哦，在斯克内克塔迪他是一流的；大家都佩服他。他那么忙，却愿意为我做一切事情。他说他从未见过我这样的消化不良症，但我这病他一定会治好。我确信没有什么他不愿意尝试的。我们出来的时候他正打算尝试某个新玩意儿。米勒先生想让戴西亲眼看看欧洲。可我写信给米勒先生说，看起来似乎没有戴维斯医生我就不能活。在斯克内克塔迪他是最顶尖的，在那里我也经常生病。很影响我的睡眠。” This announcement, instead of embarrassing Mrs. Miller, seemed to relieve her.“I suffer from the liver,” she said.“I think it’s this climate; it’s less bracing than Schenectady, especially in the winter season.I don’t know whether you know we reside at Schenectady.I was saying to Daisy that I certainly hadn’t found any one like Dr. Davis, and I didn’t believe I should.Oh, at Schenectady he stands first; they think everything of him.He has so much to do, and yet there was nothing he wouldn’t do for me.He said he never saw anything like my dyspepsia, but he was bound to cure it.I’m sure there was nothing he wouldn’t try.He was just going to try something new when we came off.Mr. Miller wanted Daisy to see Europe for herself.But I wrote to Mr. Miller that it seems as if I couldn’t get on without Dr. Davis.At Schenectady he stands at the very top; and there’s a great deal of sickness there, too.It affects my sleep.”
温特伯恩和戴维斯医生的病人聊了一大堆病理学方面的话题，其间戴西和她自己的伙伴不停歇地聊着天。年轻人问米勒夫人是不是喜欢罗马。“哦，我必须说，我感到失望。”她回答。“我们经常听说罗马，我想我们听得太多了。可是我们也无可奈何。这致使事实与我们的期望不太相同。” Winterbourne had a good deal of pathological gossip with Dr. Davis’s patient, during which Daisy chattered unremittingly to her own companion.The young man asked Mrs. Miller how she was pleased with Rome.“Well, I must say I am disappointed,” she answered.“We had heard so much about it; I suppose we had heard too much.But we couldn’t help that.We had been led to expect something different.”
“啊，稍等一等，你就会非常喜欢这里的。”温特伯恩说。 “Ah, wait a little, and you will become very fond of it,” said Winterbourne.
“我每天都越来越恨它！”伦道夫大喊。 “I hate it worse and worse every day!” cried Randolph.
“你像小汉尼巴尔。”温特伯恩说。 “You are like the infant Hannibal,” said Winterbourne.
“不，我不是！”伦道夫随便地嚷道。 “No, I ain’t!” Randolph declared at a venture.
“你确实不太像个小孩子了。”他母亲说。“可我们看过一些地方。”她又说，“我觉得那些地方比罗马好很多。”温特伯恩询问有哪些地方，她回答：“有苏黎世。”她下了定论，“我认为苏黎世很可爱，而且我们也没怎么听说过那里。” “You are not much like an infant,” said his mother.“But we have seen places,” she resumed, “that I should put a long way before Rome.”And in reply to Winterbourne’s interrogation, “There’s Zurich,” she concluded, “I think Zurich is lovely; and we hadn’t heard half so much about it.”
“我们见过的最好的地方是里士满城！”伦道夫说。 “The best place we’ve seen is the City of Richmond!” said Randolph.
“他说的是那艘轮船。”他母亲解释，“我们是乘那艘船过海的。伦道夫在里士满城号上玩得很高兴。” “He means the ship,” his mother explained.“We crossed in that ship.Randolph had a good time on the City of Richmond.”
“它是我见过的最好的地方。”孩子重复道，“只是它转向了错误的方向。” “It’s the best place I’ve seen,” the child repeated.“Only it was turned the wrong way.”
“好了，我们在某个时间会转向正确的方向的。”米勒夫人轻声笑着说。温特伯恩希望她的女儿在罗马至少找到了一些满意的地方，她宣称戴西已经太过忘形了。“这是由于社交界——这里的社交界好极了。她到处走动，结交了许多人。当然，她比我走动得多。我必须说他们很爱交际，他们立刻就接纳了她。然后，她认识了许多的绅士。哦，她认为没什么地方像罗马一样好。当然，对于一个年轻小姐来说，如果她认识许多的绅士，那就会愉快得多了。” “Well, we’ve got to turn the right way some time,” said Mrs. Miller with a little laugh.Winterbourne expressed the hope that her daughter at least found some gratification in Rome, and she declared that Daisy was quite carried away.“It’s on account of the society—the society’s splendid.She goes round everywhere; she has made a great number of acquaintances.Of course she goes round more than I do.I must say they have been very sociable; they have taken her right in.And then she knows a great many gentlemen.Oh, she thinks there’s nothing like Rome.Of course, it’s a great deal pleasanter for a young lady if she knows plenty of gentlemen.”
这时，戴西再次将注意力转向温特伯恩。“我刚才一直在和沃克夫人讲你是多么吝啬！”年轻姑娘说。 By this time Daisy had turned her attention again to Winterbourne.“I’ve been telling Mrs. Walker how mean you were!” the young girl announced.
“你提供了什么证据？”温特伯恩问道，他相当恼火米勒小姐没有感激一个倾慕者的热情，这个多情的倾慕者迫不及待地前来罗马，一路上既没有在博洛尼亚，也没有在佛罗伦萨停留。他记得一位酷爱冷嘲热讽的同胞曾经告诉他说，美国女人——那些漂亮女人，这几乎是条公理——是这个世界上最能索取、却最不懂得感恩的。 “And what is the evidence you have offered?” asked Winterbourne, rather annoyed at Miss Miller’s want of appreciation of the zeal of an admirer who on his way down to Rome had stopped neither at Bologna nor at Florence, simply because of a certain sentimental impatience.He remembered that a cynical compatriot had once told him that American women—the pretty ones, and this gave a largeness to the axiom—were at once the most exacting in the world and the least endowed with a sense of indebtedness.
“哎，你在沃韦时非常吝啬。”戴西说，“你不愿意做任何事。我要你留在那里你都不愿意。” “Why, you were awfully mean at Vevey,” said Daisy.“You wouldn’t do anything.You wouldn’t stay there when I asked you.”
“我最亲爱的年轻女士，”温特伯恩以雄辩的口吻喊道，“我一路来到罗马就是为了听你的指责吗？” “My dearest young lady,” cried Winterbourne, with eloquence, “have I come all the way to Rome to encounter your reproaches?”
“听听他说的那话！”戴西和女主人说，捻着这位女士裙子上的一个蝴蝶结。“您听到过这么离奇的话吗？” “Just hear him say that!” said Daisy to her hostess, giving a twist to a bow on this lady’s dress.“Did you ever hear anything so quaint?”
“这么离奇，我亲爱的？”沃克夫人用一种偏袒温特伯恩的语气低声说。 “So quaint, my dear?” murmured Mrs. Walker in the tone of a partisan of Winterbourne.
“哦，我不知道。”戴西边说边用手指拨弄着沃克夫人的缎带。“沃克夫人，我想告诉你点事。” “Well, I don’t know,” said Daisy, fingering Mrs. Walker’s ribbons.“Mrs. Walker, I want to tell you something.”
“妈——妈，”伦道夫插话道，他的话尾音粗声粗气，“我告诉你你该走了。尤金尼奥要生——事了！” “Mother-r,” interposed Randolph, with his rough ends to his words, “I tell you you’ve got to go.Eugenio’ll raise—something!”
“我不害怕尤金尼奥。”戴西仰头说。“听着，沃克夫人。”她继续说，“您知道我要来参加您的聚会吧。” “I’m not afraid of Eugenio,” said Daisy with a toss of her head.“Look here, Mrs. Walker,” she went on, “you know I’m coming to your party.”
“听到这话我很高兴。” “I am delighted to hear it.”
“我买了一件漂亮的裙子！” “I’ve got a lovely dress!”
“我完全相信。” “I am very sure of that.”
“可是我想请求您一件事——允许我带一个朋友来。” “But I want to ask a favor—permission to bring a friend.”
“见到你任何一个朋友我都会高兴。”沃克夫人说，微笑着转向米勒夫人。 “I shall be happy to see any of your friends,” said Mrs. Walker, turning with a smile to Mrs. Miller.
“哦，他们不是我的朋友。”戴西的妈妈答道，以她特有的方式羞涩地微笑着。“我从未和他们说过话。” “Oh, they are not my friends,” answered Daisy’s mamma, smiling shyly in her own fashion.“I never spoke to them.”
“那是我的一个密友——乔万内里先生。”戴西说道，她清晰的小嗓音里没有一丝颤抖，她明丽的小脸上也没有一丝阴影。 “It’s an intimate friend of mine—Mr. Giovanelli,” said Daisy without a tremor in her clear little voice or a shadow on her brilliant little face.
沃克夫人沉默了片刻，迅速瞥了温特伯恩一眼。“我会很高兴见到乔万内里先生。”然后她说。 Mrs. Walker was silent a moment; she gave a rapid glance at Winterbourne.“I shall be glad to see Mr. Giovanelli,” she then said.
“他是个意大利人。”戴西极其沉着地继续说道，“他是我最好的朋友之一；他是世界上最英俊的男人——除了温特伯恩先生。他认识很多意大利人，可是他还想认识一些美国人。他非常喜欢美国人。他无比聪明。他极其可爱！” “He’s an Italian,” Daisy pursued with the prettiest serenity.“He’s a great friend of mine; he’s the handsomest man in the world—except Mr. Winterbourne!He knows plenty of Italians, but he wants to know some Americans.He thinks ever so much of Americans.He’s tremendously clever.He’s perfectly lovely!”
事情定了下来，这个出色的人将被带来参加沃克夫人的聚会，然后米勒夫人准备告别。“我想我们要回旅店了。”她说。 It was settled that this brilliant personage should be brought to Mrs. Walker’s party, and then Mrs. Miller prepared to take her leave.“I guess we’ll go back to the hotel,” she said.
“你们回旅店吧，妈妈，不过我打算散散步。”戴西说。 “You may go back to the hotel, Mother, but I’m going to take a walk,” said Daisy.
“她打算和乔万内里先生散步。”伦道夫声称。 “She’s going to walk with Mr. Giovanelli,” Randolph proclaimed.
“我打算去品奇欧公园。”戴西微笑着说。 “I am going to the Pincio,” said Daisy, smiling.
“一个人吗，我亲爱的——在这个时间？”沃克夫人问道。下午即将结束——这正是车如流水、沉思的行人摩肩接踵的时刻。“我认为现在不太安全，我亲爱的。”沃克夫人说。 “Alone, my dear—at this hour?” Mrs. Walker asked.The afternoon was drawing to a close—it was the hour for the throng of carriages and of contemplative pedestrians.“I don’t think it’s safe, my dear,” said Mrs. Walker.
“我也觉得。”米勒夫人补充说，“你会得热病的，绝对会。记住戴维斯医生告诉你的话吧！” “Neither do I,” subjoined Mrs. Miller.“You’ll get the fever, as sure as you live.Remember what Dr. Davis told you!”
“她去之前给她点药。”伦道夫说。 “Give her some medicine before she goes,” said Randolph.
一行人站了起来，戴西仍然露出漂亮的牙齿，弯腰亲吻女主人。“沃克夫人，您太好了。”她说，“我不会一个人去的，我要去见个朋友。” The company had risen to its feet; Daisy, still showing her pretty teeth, bent over and kissed her hostess.“Mrs. Walker, you are too perfect,” she said.“I’m not going alone; I am going to meet a friend.”
“你的朋友并不会让你远离热病的。”米勒夫人说。 “Your friend won’t keep you from getting the fever,” Mrs. Miller observed.
“他是乔万内里先生吗？”女主人问。 “Is it Mr. Giovanelli?” asked the hostess.
温特伯恩正看着这个年轻的姑娘，听到这个问题，他的注意力集中起来。她站在那里，微笑着，拨弄着她帽子的缎带；她瞥了温特伯恩一眼。然后，在她瞥一眼并微笑的同时，她毫不犹豫地回答道：“是乔万内里先生——英俊的乔万内里先生。” Winterbourne was watching the young girl; at this question his attention quickened.She stood there, smiling and smoothing her bonnet ribbons; she glanced at Winterbourne.Then, while she glanced and smiled, she answered, without a shade of hesitation, “Mr. Giovanelli—the beautiful Giovanelli.”
“我亲爱的年轻朋友,”沃克夫人说，恳切地拉过她的手，“不要在这个时间去往品奇欧公园去见一个英俊的意大利人。” “My dear young friend,” said Mrs. Walker, taking her hand pleadingly, “don’t walk off to the Pincio at this hour to meet a beautiful Italian.”
“哦，他倒是会讲英语。”米勒夫人说。 “Well, he speaks English,” said Mrs. Miller.
“我的天！”戴西叫道，“我不会做任何不恰当的事的。倒是有一个容易的办法能解决这个问题。”她又瞥了温特伯恩一眼。“品奇欧只有一百码远，如果温特伯恩先生像他表现得那么有礼貌的话，他会主动陪我去的吧！” “Gracious me!”Daisy exclaimed, “I don’t to do anything improper.There’s an easy way to settle it.”She continued to glance at Winterbourne.“The Pincio is only a hundred yards distant; and if Mr. Winterbourne were as polite as he pretends, he would offer to walk with me!”
温特伯恩急于证实自己的礼貌，而年轻姑娘也亲切地允许他陪她。他们在米勒夫人之前下了楼，在门口，温特伯恩看到米勒夫人的马车来到了近前，还有那个他在沃韦就结识的衣着光鲜的侍从。“再见，尤金尼奥！”戴西大声说，“我打算去散步。”从维亚·格雷戈里亚娜大街到品奇欧山另一端的美丽花园之间的路程实际上非常短。然而，因为天气很好，车辆、行人十分拥堵，漫步的人也很多，年轻的美国人发现他们的行程被耽搁了。对于这个事实，温特伯恩倒是很乐意，尽管他也意识到了他的尴尬处境。那群行进缓慢、目光悠然的罗马人把注意力集中在这个极为美丽的年轻外国小姐身上，她正挽着他的胳膊穿过人群；他想知道，当戴西提出要无人陪伴，暴露在人群中时，她的脑子里究竟在想什么。在她看来，他自己的任务很明显就是把她交给乔万内里先生；可是温特伯恩立刻感到既恼怒又满足，决定不做这样的事情。 Winterbourne’s politeness hastened to affirm itself, and the young girl gave him gracious leave to accompany her.They passed downstairs before her mother, and at the door Winterbourne perceived Mrs. Miller’s carriage drawn up, with the ornamental courier whose acquaintance he had made at Vevey seated within.“Goodbye, Eugenio!” cried Daisy; “I’m going to take a walk.”The distance from the Via Gregoriana to the beautiful garden at the other end of the Pincian Hill is, in fact, rapidly traversed.As the day was splendid, however, and the concourse of vehicles, walkers, and loungers numerous, the young Americans found their progress much delayed.This fact was highly agreeable to Winterbourne, in spite of his consciousness of his singular situation.The slow-moving, idly gazing Roman crowd bestowed much attention upon the extremely pretty young foreign lady who was passing through it upon his arm; and he wondered what on earth had been in Daisy’s mind when she proposed to expose herself, unattended, to its appreciation.His own mission, to her sense, apparently, was to consign her to the hands of Mr. Giovanelli; but Winterbourne, at once annoyed and gratified, resolved that he would do no such thing.
“为什么你没有来看我？”戴西问道。“你脱不清干系的。” “Why haven’t you been to see me?” asked Daisy.“You can’t get out of that.”
“我曾荣幸地告诉您，我是刚刚下火车。” “I have had the honor of telling you that I have only just stepped out of the train.”
“你肯定是在火车停下来后在车上呆了很久喽！”年轻姑娘轻轻笑着说道。“我猜你是睡着了。你还有时间去看沃克夫人呢。” “You must have stayed in the train a good while after it stopped!” cried the young girl with her little laugh.“I suppose you were asleep.You have had time to go to see Mrs. Walker.”
“我以前就认识沃克夫人——”温特伯恩开始解释。 “I knew Mrs. Walker—” Winterbourne began to explain.
“我知道你在哪里认识她的。你在日内瓦认识她的。她这么告诉我的。嗯，你是在沃韦认识我的。时间差不多。所以你应该来的。”她除了这个问题再没问他别的问题，她开始聊她自己的事情。“我们旅馆的房间很棒，尤金尼奥说那是罗马最好的房间。要是能不死于热病，我们打算呆上整个冬天；我猜我们会呆那么久的。这儿比我想象的要好得多；我以为这儿会安静得可怕；我以为这里肯定会闷得要命。我以为我们会整天四处游走，听那些可怕的老头讲解那些图片和景物。然而我们仅有一个星期是那样过的，现在我正玩得高兴呢。我认识了非常多的朋友，并且，他们全都那么迷人。这儿的社交界非常之好。有各种各样的人——英国人、德国人、意大利人等。我想我最喜欢英国人。我喜欢他们谈话的风格。不过，也有一些可爱的美国人。我没见过这么热情的社交界。每天都有这样那样的事。没有很多舞会，可是我必须说，我从不认为跳舞就是一切。我总是喜欢聊天。我猜在沃克夫人那里我会有许多的聊伴，她的房间太小了。”当他们穿过品奇欧花园的大门时，米勒小姐开始想乔万内里先生会在哪里。“我们最好径直去前面那个地方。”她说，“在那里你可以看看景色。” “I know where you knew her.You knew her at Geneva.She told me so.Well, you knew me at Vevey.That’s just as good.So you ought to have come.”She asked him no other question than this; she began to prattle about her own affairs.“We’ve got splendid rooms at the hotel; Eugenio says they’re the best rooms in Rome.We are going to stay all winter, if we don’t die of the fever; and I guess we’ll stay then.It’s a great deal nicer than I thought; I thought it would be fearfully quiet; I was sure it would be awfully poky.I was sure we should be going round all the time with one of those dreadful old men that explain about the pictures and things.But we only had about a week of that, and now I’m enjoying myself.I know ever so many people, and they are all so charming.The society’s extremely select.There are all kinds—English, and Germans, and Italians.I think I like the English best.I like their style of conversation.But there are some lovely Americans.I never saw anything so hospitable.There’s something or other every day.There’s not much dancing; but I must say I never thought dancing was everything.I was always fond of conversation.I guess I shall have plenty at Mrs. Walker’s, her rooms are so small.”When they had passed the gate of the Pincian Gardens, Miss Miller began to wonder where Mr. Giovanelli might be.“We had better go straight to that place in front,” she said, “where you look at the view.”
“我当然不会帮你找到他。”温特伯恩宣称。 “I certainly shall not help you to find him,” Winterbourne declared.
“那么，没有你我也会找到他。”戴西小姐大喊。 “Then I shall find him without you,” cried Miss Daisy.
“你肯定不会离开我吧！”温特伯恩喊道。 “You certainly won’t leave me!” cried Winterbourne.
她扑哧一声笑了。“你是害怕你会迷路吗？还是害怕被撞了？可是乔万内里就在那里，正倚在那棵树上。他正盯着马车里的女人们看，你见过这么有趣的事吗？” She burst into her little laugh.“Are you afraid you’ll get lost—or run over?But there’s Giovanelli, leaning against that tree.He’s staring at the women in the carriages: did you ever see anything so cool?”
温特伯恩看到不远处一个小个子男人正夹着他的手杖，交叉着手臂站着。他脸很英俊，巧妙地戴着一顶帽子，一只眼睛上有块镜片，纽孔里有一束花。温特伯恩看了他一会儿，然后说：“你想和那个男人说话吗？” Winterbourne perceived at some distance a little man standing with folded arms nursing his cane.He had a handsome face, an artfully poised hat, a glass in one eye, and a nosegay in his buttonhole.Winterbourne looked at him a moment and then said, “Do you mean to speak to that man?”
“我想和他说话吗？怎么，你不会认为我愿意用手势和他交流吧？” “Do I mean to speak to him?Why, you don’t suppose I mean to communicate by signs?”
“那么，请理解，”温特伯恩说，“我想你留下和我在一起。” “Pray understand, then,” said Winterbourne, “that I intend to remain with you.”
戴西停下来看着他，在她脸上没有一丝不安的神情，除了她迷人的眼睛和快乐的酒窝外什么都没有。“哦，她真是个冷酷的女孩！”年轻人想。 Daisy stopped and looked at him, without a sign of troubled consciousness in her face, with nothing but the presence of her charming eyes and her happy dimples.“Well, she’s a cool one!” thought the young man.
“我不喜欢你说那句话的方式。”戴西说，“太傲慢了。” “I don’t like the way you say that,” said Daisy.“It’s too imperious.”
“如果我说错了，我请求你原谅。主要就是让你知道我的意思。” “I beg your pardon if I say it wrong.The main point is to give you an idea of my meaning.”
年轻姑娘更加严肃地望着他，可是眼睛比以往更漂亮了。“我从不允许一个绅士支配我，或者干涉任何我做的事。” The young girl looked at him more gravely, but with eyes that were prettier than ever.“I have never allowed a gentleman to dictate to me, or to interfere with anything I do.”
“我想你犯了一个错误。”温特伯恩说，“你有时应该听绅士的话——听合适的绅士的话。” “I think you have made a mistake,” said Winterbourne.“You should sometimes listen to a gentleman—the right one.”
戴西又大笑起来。“我除了听绅士讲话就没干别的了！”她叫道，“告诉我，乔万内里先生是否是合适的绅士？” Daisy began to laugh again.“I do nothing but listen to gentlemen!” she exclaimed.“Tell me if Mr. Giovanelli is the right one?”
胸前插着花束的那位绅士现在已经看见了我们的两位朋友，他正谄媚地快速走近年轻姑娘。他向温特伯恩，也向后者的同伴分别鞠了躬；他笑容灿烂、目光睿智；温特伯恩寻思，这人长得挺不错的。但是，他还是对戴西说：“他不是合适的绅士。” The gentleman with the nosegay in his bosom had now perceived our two friends, and was approaching the young girl with obsequious rapidity.He bowed to Winterbourne as well as to the latter’s companion; he had a brilliant smile, an intelligent eye; Winterbourne thought him not a bad-looking fellow.But he nevertheless said to Daisy, “No, he’s not the right one.”
戴西明显具有做介绍的天赋；她向她的同伴们提及了彼此的名字。她缓缓地走着，身旁分别是两位同伴；英语说得非常讨巧的乔万内里先生——温特伯恩后来听说他在很多美国女继承人面前练习过这类用语——和她说了大量的客气的废话；他极其地温文尔雅，年轻的美国人什么也没说，却琢磨着意大利式聪明的奥妙，这种聪明能使人在越感到失望的时候表现得越有礼貌。乔万内里当然指望更加亲密一些，他没预料到会是一个三个人的聚会。可是他用一种意味深长的方式克制住了他的脾气。温特伯恩自以为认清了他的把戏。“他不是个绅士，”年轻美国人说，“他只是个聪明的绅士模仿者。他是个音乐教师，或穷文人，或是一个三流艺术家。让他的好相貌见鬼去吧！”当然，乔万内里先生有一副非常漂亮的面孔，但让温特伯恩感到无比愤怒的是他自己可爱的女同胞不知道一个假绅士和一个真绅士之间的差别。乔万内里又说又笑，使自己显得非常容易相处。的确是这样，如果他是个模仿者，他的模仿确实很出色。“然而，”温特伯恩对自己说，“一个好女孩应该知道的！”接着他又回到这个问题：事实上，她是不是个好女孩呢？一个好女孩，就算可以有点美国式的卖弄风骚，会和一个想必下流的外国人约会吗？诚然他们的约会是在大白天罗马最拥挤的角落里进行的，可是说他们选择这里是为了表现极端的玩世不恭也不是不可能。也许看起来很奇怪，温特伯很恼火这个年轻姑娘在会见情人时并没有对他自己的陪伴显得不耐烦，他还对自己的一厢情愿感到很恼火。不可能把她看作是个行为很端正的年轻小姐；她缺少某种不可或缺的优雅气质。要是能够将她当作言情作家所称的“感情上无法无天”的人对待，那问题就会简化了。她要是希望摆脱他，就会让他更加轻视她，要是一旦能轻视她，就会使得她不那么令人困惑了。可是戴西在这一场合的表现还是不可思议地将厚颜无耻和天真无邪融为了一体。 Daisy evidently had a natural talent for performing introductions; she mentioned the name of each of her companions to the other.She strolled alone with one of them on each side of her; Mr. Giovanelli, who spoke English very cleverly—Winterbourne afterward learned that he had practiced the idiom upon a great many American heiresses—addressed her a great deal of very polite nonsense; he was extremely urbane, and the young American, who said nothing, reflected upon that profundity of Italian cleverness which enables people to appear more gracious in proportion as they are more acutely disappointed.Giovanelli, of course, had counted upon something more intimate; he had not bargained for a party of three.But he kept his temper in a manner which suggested far-stretching intentions.Winterbourne flattered himself that he had taken his measure.“He is not a gentleman,” said the young American; “he is only a clever imitation of one.He is a music master, or a penny-a-liner, or a third-rate artist.Damn his good looks!”Mr. Giovanelli had certainly a very pretty face; but Winterbourne felt a superior indignation at his own lovely fellow countrywoman’s not knowing the difference between a spurious gentleman and a real one.Giovanelli chattered and jested and made himself wonderfully agreeable.It was true that, if he was an imitation, the imitation was brilliant.“Nevertheless,” Winterbourne said to himself, “a nice girl ought to know!”And then he came back to the question whether this was, in fact, a nice girl.Would a nice girl, even allowing for her being a little American flirt, make a rendezvous with a presumably low-lived foreigner?The rendezvous in this case, indeed, had been in broad daylight and in the most crowded corner of Rome, but was it not impossible to regard the choice of these circumstances as a proof of extreme cynicism?Singular though it may seem, Winterbourne was vexed that the young girl, in joining her amoroso, should not appear more impatient of his own company, and he was vexed because of his inclination.It was impossible to regard her as a perfectly well-conducted young lady; she was wanting in a certain indispensable delicacy.It would therefore simplify matters greatly to be able to treat her as the object of one of those sentiments which are called by romancers “lawless passions.”That she should seem to wish to get rid of him would help him to think more lightly of her, and to be able to think more lightly of her would make her much less perplexing.But Daisy, on this occasion, continued to present herself as an inscrutable combination of audacity and innocence.
在两位护花使者的陪伴下，她走了大约有一刻钟，在温特伯恩看来，她是在用一种非常幼稚而愉快的语气回应着乔万内里先生的漂亮话。这时，一辆马车离开川流不息的车队向小径驶来。在同一时刻，温特伯恩看到他的朋友沃克夫人——他刚离开她家房子——正坐在车里招呼他呢。他离开米勒小姐身边，匆忙去听候她的吩咐。沃克夫人脸色通红，脸上一副激动的神情。“这真的是太可怕了。”她说。“那个姑娘不可以做这种事情。她不可以和你们两位男士在这里散步。已经有五十个人注意到她了。” She had been walking some quarter of an hour, attended by her two cavaliers, and responding in a tone of very childish gaiety, as it seemed to Winterbourne, to the pretty speeches of Mr. Giovanelli, when a carriage that had detached itself from the revolving train drew up beside the path.At the same moment Winterbourne perceived that his friend Mrs. Walker—the lady whose house he had lately left—was seated in the vehicle and was beckoning to him.Leaving Miss Miller’s side, he hastened to obey her summons.Mrs. Walker was flushed; she wore an excited air.“It is really too dreadful,” she said.“That girl must not do this sort of thing.She must not walk here with you two men.Fifty people have noticed her.”
温特伯恩扬了扬眉毛。“我想，对这事太大惊小怪是件遗憾的事。” Winterbourne raised his eyebrows.“I think it’s a pity to make too much fuss about it.”
“让这个姑娘毁了自己才是件遗憾的事呢！” “It’s a pity to let the girl ruin herself!”
“她是非常天真的。”温特伯恩说。 “She is very innocent,” said Winterbourne.
“她简直是疯了！”沃克夫人喊道，“你见过像她母亲那样愚痴的人吗？刚才你们都离开以后，我一想到这事，就不能安安静静地坐着了。不去尝试着挽救她，似乎太遗憾了。于是我叫了车夫、戴上帽子，尽可能快地赶来了。感谢上天，我找到你们了！” “She’s very crazy!” cried Mrs. Walker.“Did you ever see anything so imbecile as her mother?After you had all left me just now, I could not sit still for thinking of it.It seemed too pitiful, not even to attempt to save her.I ordered the carriage and put on my bonnet, and came here as quickly as possible.Thank Heaven I have found you!”
“您建议我们做什么？”温特伯恩笑着问道。 “What do you propose to do with us?” asked Winterbourne, smiling.
“叫她上车，拉上她在这里四处逛上半个小时，让世人可以看到，她也不是那么疯，然后把她安全地送回家。” “To ask her to get in, to drive her about here for half an hour, so that the world may see she is not running absolutely wild, and then to take her safely home.”
“我认为这不是一个让人高兴的办法。”温特伯恩说，“但是您可以试试。” “I don’t think it’s a very happy thought,” said Winterbourne; “but you can try.”
沃克夫人试了。年轻人追上了米勒小姐，后者只是朝和她谈话的人点了点头并微笑了一下，就和她的同伴走她的路了。戴西一听说沃克夫人希望和她讲话，就特别优雅地返回，乔万内里先生陪在她身旁。她表示她很高兴有机会将这位绅士介绍给沃克夫人。她立刻就作了介绍，并表示她这辈子从未见过像沃克夫人马车里的毯子一样可爱的东西。 Mrs. Walker tried.The young man went in pursuit of Miss Miller, who had simply nodded and smiled at his interlocutor in the carriage and had gone her way with her companion.Daisy, on learning that Mrs. Walker wished to speak to her, retraced her steps with a perfect good grace and with Mr. Giovanelli at her side.She declared that she was delighted to have a chance to present this gentleman to Mrs. Walker.She immediately achieved the introduction, and declared that she had never in her life seen anything so lovely as Mrs. Walker’s carriage rug.
“我很高兴你喜欢它。”这位女士说，甜甜地微笑着。“你愿意上车，让我把它盖在你身上吗？” “I am glad you admire it,” said this lady, smiling sweetly.“Will you get in and let me put it over you?”
“哦，不，谢谢您。”戴西说，“看见您盖着它到处兜风，我会更加喜欢它的。” “Oh, no, thank you,” said Daisy.“I shall admire it much more as I see you driving round with it.”
“上车来吧，和我一起去兜风！”沃克夫人说。 “Do get in and drive with me!” said Mrs. Walker.
“那会很惬意，可是像我现在这样也很迷人呢！”戴西向她两侧的绅士投去灿烂的一瞥。 “That would be charming, but it’s so enchanting just as I am!” and Daisy gave a brilliant glance at the gentlemen on either side of her.
“那也许是很迷人，亲爱的孩子，可那不是这里的习俗。”沃克夫人恳切地说，身体前倾，双手虔诚地交叉在一起。 “It may be enchanting, dear child, but it is not the custom here,” urged Mrs. Walker, leaning forward in her victoria, with her hands devoutly clasped.
“哦，那么，就更应该那么做了！”戴西说，“如果我不散散步，我会喘不上气的。” “Well, it ought to be, then!” said Daisy.“If I didn’t walk I should expire.”
“你应该和你母亲散步，亲爱的。”日内瓦来的女士大声说道，她正在失去耐心。 “You should walk with your mother, dear,” cried the lady from Geneva, losing patience.
“和我母亲，天哪！”年轻姑娘惊呼。温特伯恩看出来她已经察觉到了这干涉的意思。“我母亲一辈子从未一次走过十步。而且，您也知道，”她笑着加了一句，“我也不是五岁的孩子了。” “With my mother dear!” exclaimed the young girl.Winterbourne saw that she scented interference.“My mother never walked ten steps in her life.And then, you know,” she added with a laugh, “I am more than five years old.”
“你是已经长大了，那就应该更理智。你已经长大到会招人议论的年龄了，亲爱的米勒小姐。” “You are old enough to be more reasonable.You are old enough, dear Miss Miller, to be talked about.”
戴西看着沃克夫人，热情地微笑着。“议论？您是什么意思？” Daisy looked at Mrs. Walker, smiling intensely.“Talked about?What do you mean?”
“到我的马车里来，我会告诉你。” “Come into my carriage, and I will tell you.”
戴西又一次迅速瞥了一眼她左右两边的绅士。乔万内里先生正在来回鞠躬，揉搓着他的手套，并非常亲热地笑着；温特伯恩觉得这种场景非常令人不快。“我觉得我并不想知道您的意思。”片刻后，戴西说道。“我觉得我不想知道。” Daisy turned her quickened glance again from one of the gentlemen beside her to the other.Mr. Giovanelli was bowing to and fro, rubbing down his gloves and laughing very agreeably; Winterbourne thought it a most unpleasant scene.“I don’t think I want to know what you mean,” said Daisy presently.“I don’t think I should like it.”
温特伯恩希望沃克夫人盖上她的马车毯子驱车离开，但是这位女士不喜欢遭到蔑视，正如她后来告诉他的那样。“你宁愿被人认为是个非常轻率的姑娘吗？”她问道。 Winterbourne wished that Mrs. Walker would tuck in her carriage rug and drive away, but this lady did not enjoy being defied, as she afterward told him.“Should you prefer being thought a very reckless girl?” she demanded.
“天哪！”戴西喊道。她再次看了看乔万内里先生，然后转向温特伯恩。在她的脸颊上有一丝红晕，真是漂亮极了。“温特伯恩先生认为，”她慢慢地问着，微笑着，把头向后一扬，并从头到脚扫视了他一遍，“为了挽回我的声誉，我应该上马车吗？” “Gracious!” exclaimed Daisy.She looked again at Mr. Giovanelli, then she turned to Winterbourne.There was a little pink flush in her cheek; she was tremendously pretty.“Does Mr. Winterbourne think,” she asked slowly, smiling, throwing back her head, and glancing at him from head to foot, “that, to save my reputation, I ought to get into the carriage?”
温特伯恩脸红了，他犹豫了好一阵子。听她用那种方式提及她的“声誉”似乎很奇怪。而他自己，实际上，说话必须与他的勇敢行动相一致。而在这里，最好的勇敢行动便是告诉她事实；对于温特伯恩来说——正如读者通过我已给出的几处对他的了解那样——事实就是戴西·米勒应该采纳沃克夫人的建议。他望着她精致美丽的面容，然后，非常温和地说：“我想你应该进马车里去。” Winterbourne colored; for an instant he hesitated greatly.It seemed so strange to hear her speak that way of her “reputation.”But he himself, in fact, must speak in accordance with gallantry.The finest gallantry, here, was simply to tell her the truth; and the truth, for Winterbourne, as the few indications I have been able to give have made him known to the reader, was that Daisy Miller should take Mrs. Walker’s advice.He looked at her exquisite prettiness, and then he said, very gently, “I think you should get into the carriage.”
戴西放声大笑起来。“我从未听过如此死板的话！如果这样不合适的话，沃克夫人，”她继续说道，“那么我整个人都是不合适的，您必须得放弃我了。再见吧，我希望您兜风愉快！”她和乔万内里先生——后者以胜利者的姿态向她谄媚地行了个礼——转身走远了。 Daisy gave a violent laugh.“I never heard anything so stiff!If this is improper, Mrs. Walker,” she pursued, “then I am all improper, and you must give me up.Goodbye; I hope you’ll have a lovely ride!” and, with Mr. Giovanelli, who made a triumphantly obsequious salute, she turned away.
沃克夫人坐着，在她身后望着她，眼里含着泪水。“进来吧，先生。”她对温特伯恩说，一边指着她身旁的位子。年轻人回答说，他理应陪伴米勒小姐，而沃克夫人表示，如果他拒绝她的好意，她就再也不和他说话了。她显然是认真的。温特伯恩追上戴西和她的同伴，向这位年轻姑娘伸出手，告诉她沃克夫人迫切要求他的陪伴。他预料，作为回答，她会说出一些相当随便的话，这些话会更加深入地证明她自己是沃克夫人如此好心地、努力地劝阻她成为的那种“放肆的人”。但她却只握了握他的手，看都没有看他，而乔万内里先生则很用力地、极端夸耀地挥了挥帽子向他告别。 Mrs. Walker sat looking after her, and there were tears in Mrs. Walker’s eyes.“Get in here, sir,” she said to Winterbourne, indicating the place beside her.The young man answered that he felt bound to accompany Miss Miller, whereupon Mrs. Walker declared that if he refused her this favor she would never speak to him again.She was evidently in earnest.Winterbourne overtook Daisy and her companion, and, offering the young girl his hand, told her that Mrs. Walker had made an imperious claim upon his society.He expected that in answer she would say something rather free, something to commit herself still further to that “recklessness” from which Mrs. Walker had so charitably endeavored to dissuade her.But she only shook his hand, hardly looking at him, while Mr. Giovanelli bade him farewell with a too emphatic flourish of the hat.
温特伯恩坐上沃克夫人的敞篷马车时，心情并不很好。“您那样很不明智。”当马车再次汇入车流时，他坦率地说。 Winterbourne was not in the best possible humor as he took his seat in Mrs. Walker’s victoria.“That was not clever of you,” he said candidly, while the vehicle mingled again with the throng of carriages.
“在这样的情况下，”他的同伴回答，“我不希望多么明智，我只希望自己很诚恳！” “In such a case,” his companion answered, “I don’t wish to be clever; I wish to be EARNEST!”
“可是，您的诚恳只能触怒她并将她推开。” “Well, your earnestness has only offended her and put her off.”
“这事发生了也好。”沃克夫人说，“如果她断然决定要毁掉自己，我们越早知道越好，这样我们就可以采取相应的行动了。” “It has happened very well,” said Mrs. Walker.“If she is so perfectly determined to compromise herself, the sooner one knows it the better; one can act accordingly.”
“我想她并不想伤害谁。”温特伯恩插话。 “I suspect she meant no harm,” Winterbourne rejoined.
“一个月以前我是这么想的。可是她做得太过分了。” “So I thought a month ago.But she has been going too far.”
“她干了些什么？” “What has she been doing?”
“所有这里的人不做的事。她和碰到的任何一个男人调情；和可疑的意大利人坐在角落里；和同一伙人整晚跳舞；夜里十一点还接待访客。访客们来的时候，她母亲就走开了。 “Everything that is not done here.Flirting with any man she could pick up; sitting in corners with mysterious Italians; dancing all the evening with the same partners; receiving visits at eleven o’clock at night.Her mother goes away when visitors come.”
“可是，她弟弟，”温特伯恩大笑着说，“半夜才睡觉的。” “But her brother,” said Winterbourne, laughing, “sits up till midnight.”
“他肯定是被他看到的东西感染了。”有人告诉我说，在他们的旅店，每个人都在谈论她，当一位绅士来了要见米勒小姐的时候，微笑就会在所有仆人的脸上蔓延。” “He must be edified by what he sees.I’m told that at their hotel everyone is talking about her, and that a smile goes round among all the servants when a gentleman comes and asks for Miss Miller.”
“该死的仆人们！”温特伯恩愤怒地说，“这个可怜的姑娘唯一的缺点，”片刻后，他补充道，“就是她非常没有教养。” “The servants be hanged!” said Winterbourne angrily.“The poor girl’s only fault,” he presently added, “is that she is very uncultivated.”
“她天生粗俗。”沃克夫人说道。 “She is naturally indelicate,” Mrs. Walker declared.
“就拿今天上午的那个例子讲。你在沃韦认识她有多长时间？” “Take that example this morning.How long had you known her at Vevey?”
“几天吧。” “A couple of days.”
“那么，想想她将你离开那个地方当作她自己的事的样子吧！” “Fancy, then, her making it a personal matter that you should have left the place!”
温特伯恩沉默了片刻，然后说道：“我怀疑，沃克夫人，你和我在日内瓦住得太久了！”接着他又问了她一个问题——她让他到马车里来究竟是什么意图。 Winterbourne was silent for some moments; then he said, “I suspect, Mrs. Walker, that you and I have lived too long at Geneva!”And he added a request that she should inform him with what particular design she had made him enter her carriage.
“我希望请求你停止和米勒小姐的关系，不要和她调情，不要再进一步给她机会表现自己，简而言之，不要再理她。” “I wished to beg you to cease your relations with Miss Miller—not to flirt with her—to give her no further opportunity to expose herself—to let her alone, in short.”
“我恐怕做不到。”温特伯恩说，“我特别喜欢她。” “I’m afraid I can’t do that,” said Winterbourne.“I like her extremely.”
“理智一点吧，你不应该再帮她做出丑事。” “All the more reason that you shouldn’t help her to make a scandal.”
“在我和她的交往中没有任何丑事啊。” “There shall be nothing scandalous in my attentions to her.”
“在她和别人的交往过程中肯定会有丑事。不过我已经说出了我的心里话。”沃克夫人继续说，“如果你希望再回到那位年轻小姐那里，我会把你放下。瞧，顺便说一下，你现在就有机会。” “There certainly will be in the way she takes them.But I have said what I had on my conscience,” Mrs. Walker pursued.“If you wish to rejoin the young lady I will put you down.Here, by the way, you have a chance.”
马车正经过品奇欧花园凌驾于罗马城墙之上的部分，从这里可以俯瞰到美丽的博格斯别墅。周围有一道护墙，附近还有几把长椅。不远处的一把长椅上坐着一位绅士和一位小姐，沃克夫人冲他们摇了摇头。与此同时，两人站起身来，向护墙走去。温特伯恩要车夫停下，然后下了马车。他的同伴沉默着看了他一会儿，然后，当他举起帽子向她告别时，她庄严地驱车远去了。温特伯恩站在那里，把目光投向了戴西和她的绅士。他们显然谁也没看见他，两人都沉浸在二人世界之中。他们到达低矮的花园墙壁时，站立了一会儿，远望着博格斯别墅那一片齐刷刷的松树丛，然后乔万内里动作熟练地坐在了宽大的墙沿上。西边的太阳透过几道云柱，在对面的天空中放射出灿烂的光芒，于是，戴西的同伴从她的手里拿过她的阳伞撑开。她稍稍靠近了一点，他把伞撑在她头上，然后，他索性让伞靠在了她的肩上，结果是他们俩的头都从温特伯恩的视线中消失了。这位年轻人徘徊了片刻，然后迈开了步子。不过他并没有朝打着阳伞的那一对走去，而是朝着他姑妈科斯特洛夫人的住所走去。 The carriage was traversing that part of the Pincian Garden that overhangs the wall of Rome and overlooks the beautiful Villa Borghese.It is bordered by a large parapet, near which there are several seats.One of the seats at a distance was occupied by a gentleman and a lady, toward whom Mrs. Walker gave a toss of her head.At the same moment these persons rose and walked toward the parapet.Winterbourne had asked the coachman to stop; he now descended from the carriage.His companion looked at him a moment in silence; then, while he raised his hat, she drove majestically away.Winterbourne stood there; he had turned his eyes toward Daisy and her cavalier.They evidently saw no one; they were too deeply occupied with each other.When they reached the low garden wall, they stood a moment looking off at the great flat-topped pine clusters of the Villa Borghese; then Giovanelli seated himself, familiarly, upon the broad ledge of the wall.The western sun in the opposite sky sent out a brilliant shaft through a couple of cloud bars, whereupon Daisy’s companion took her parasol out of her hands and opened it.She came a little nearer, and he held the parasol over her; then, still holding it, he let it rest upon her shoulder, so that both of their heads were hidden from Winterbourne.This young man lingered a moment, then he began to walk.But he walked—not toward the couple with the parasol; toward the residence of his aunt, Mrs. Costello.
第二天，他很为自己得意，因为当他在米勒夫人的旅店要求见她时，仆人们中间至少没有发笑的。然而，这位女士和她的女儿不在家，第三天，他又来拜访，还是运气不好，没有找到她们。沃克夫人的聚会在第三天晚上举行，尽管温特伯恩上次和女主人会面发生了不快，他还是出现在了宾客中间。沃克夫人属于那样一类美国女士，她们居住在国外时，用她们自己的话说，要研究欧洲的社交界，在这种场合里她已经收集到了几件不同的凡人标本，好像是要做教科书。温特伯恩到达的时候，戴西·米勒并不在那里，过了一会儿，他看见她母亲独自进来了，非常害羞且非常沮丧。米勒夫人暴露在太阳穴上方的头发比以往更卷曲了。当她走近沃克夫人的时候，温特伯恩也凑近了。 He flattered himself on the following day that there was no smiling among the servants when he, at least, asked for Mrs. Miller at her hotel.This lady and her daughter, however, were not at home; and on the next day after, repeating his visit, Winterbourne again had the misfortune not to find them.Mrs. Walker’s party took place on the evening of the third day, and, in spite of the frigidity of his last interview with the hostess, Winterbourne was among the guests.Mrs. Walker was one of those American ladies who, while residing abroad, make a point, in their own phrase, of studying European society, and she had on this occasion collected several specimens of her diversely born fellow mortals to serve, as it were, as textbooks.When Winterbourne arrived, Daisy Miller was not there, but in a few moments he saw her mother come in alone, very shyly and ruefully.Mrs. Miller’s hair above her exposed-looking temples was more frizzled than ever.As she approached Mrs. Walker, Winterbourne also drew near.
“您瞧，我一个人来了。”可怜的米勒夫人说，“我害怕极了，都不知道该做些什么。这是我第一次独自参加聚会，尤其是在这个国家。我本想带伦道夫或尤金尼奥或什么人来，可是戴西非让我一个人来。我不习惯独自一人出来的。” “You see, I’ve come all alone,” said poor Mrs. Miller.“I’m so frightened; I don’t know what to do.It’s the first time I’ve ever been to a party alone, especially in this country.I wanted to bring Randolph or Eugenio, or someone, but Daisy just pushed me off by myself.I ain’t used to going round alone.”
“您女儿不想和她的同伴来给我们捧场吗？”沃克夫人引人注意地问道。 “And does not your daughter intend to favor us with her society?” demanded Mrs. Walker impressively.
“哦，戴西已经都穿戴好了。”米勒夫人说，在描述她女儿近况时，她不是用哲学家就是用历史学家的那种冷静的口吻。“她晚饭前就特意打扮好了。可是，她有一个朋友在那儿，那位绅士，那个她想带来的意大利人。他们正在弹钢琴，看起来他们一时还走不了。乔万内里先生唱得相当好。不过我想他们很快就会来的。”米勒夫人满怀希望地得出结论。 “Well, Daisy’s all dressed,” said Mrs. Miller with that accent of the dispassionate, if not of the philosophic, historian with which she always recorded the current incidents of her daughter’s career.“She got dressed on purpose before dinner.But she’s got a friend of hers there; that gentleman—the Italian—that she wanted to bring.They’ve got going at the piano; it seems as if they couldn’t leave off.Mr. Giovanelli sings splendidly.But I guess they’ll come before very long,” concluded Mrs. Miller hopefully.
“我很遗憾她那样来参加宴会。”沃克夫人说。 “I’m sorry she should come in that way,” said Mrs. Walker.
“哦，我告诉过她了，如果她打算等三个小时再来的话，晚饭前穿戴好是没用的。”戴西的妈妈回答，“我看她也没必要穿那样一件裙子和乔万内里先生坐在一起啊。” “Well, I told her that there was no use in her getting dressed before dinner if she was going to wait three hours,” responded Daisy’s mamma.“I didn’t see the use of her putting on such a dress as that to sit round with Mr. Giovanelli.”
“这太可怕了！”沃克夫人转身向温特伯恩说道，“她故意要出洋相。她是在报复我规劝她。她来了以后，我决不会和她说话。” “This is most horrible!” said Mrs. Walker, turning away and addressing herself to Winterbourne.“Elle s’affiche.It’s her revenge for my having ventured to remonstrate with her.When she comes, I shall not speak to her.”
戴西过了十一点才来，可是，在这样的场合下，她不是一位安静地等别人搭讪的年轻小姐。她走了进来，裙摆沙沙作响；她容光焕发，可爱至极，有说有笑，手中捧着一大束花，由乔万内里先生陪伴着。每个人都停止了讲话，转身望向她。她径直向沃克夫人走来。“我怕您以为我不会再来了，所以我让母亲来告诉您。我想让乔万内里先生在他来之前练习一下，您知道，他唱得好极了，我想让您请他唱歌。这是乔万内里先生，您知道，我曾向您介绍过他，他有着最可爱的嗓音，并且，他会唱最动人的歌曲。我今晚有意让他温习了一遍，我们在旅馆度过了愉快的时光。”戴西用最甜美欢快的声音说了所有这些话，时而望着女主人，时而环视着房间，同时，她还不停地轻拍着自己的肩膀和裙子边缘。“有我认识的人吗？”她问。 Daisy came after eleven o’clock; but she was not, on such an occasion, a young lady to wait to be spoken to.She rustled forward in radiant loveliness, smiling and chattering, carrying a large bouquet, and attended by Mr. Giovanelli.Everyone stopped talking and turned and looked at her.She came straight to Mrs. Walker.“I’m afraid you thought I never was coming, so I sent mother off to tell you.I wanted to make Mr. Giovanelli practice some things before he came; you know he sings beautifully, and I want you to ask him to sing.This is Mr. Giovanelli; you know I introduced him to you; he’s got the most lovely voice, and he knows the most charming set of songs.I made him go over them this evening on purpose; we had the greatest time at the hotel.”Of all this Daisy delivered herself with the sweetest, brightest audibleness, looking now at her hostess and now round the room, while she gave a series of little pats, round her shoulders, to the edges of her dress.“Is there anyone I know?” she asked.
“我想每个人都认识你！”沃克夫人话里有话，匆匆忙忙地向乔万内里先生打了个招呼。这位绅士举止很殷勤。他微笑着鞠躬，露出了洁白的牙齿，他捻捻胡子，转转眼睛，行了一位英俊意大利人在一个晚会上的所有恰当的礼数。他非常动听地唱了六七首歌曲，尽管沃克夫人事后表示她根本不知道是谁要他唱的。显然不是戴西给他下了这个命令。戴西坐在离钢琴很远的地方，尽管她曾公开声称对他的歌唱极度仰慕，但就在他唱歌时，却还能听到她在说话。 “I think every one knows you!” said Mrs. Walker pregnantly, and she gave a very cursory greeting to Mr. Giovanelli.This gentleman bore himself gallantly.He smiled and bowed and showed his white teeth; he curled his mustaches and rolled his eyes and performed all the proper functions of a handsome Italian at an evening party.He sang very prettily half a dozen songs, though Mrs. Walker afterward declared that she had been quite unable to find out who asked him.It was apparently not Daisy who had given him his orders.Daisy sat at a distance from the piano, and though she had publicly, as it were, professed a high admiration for his singing, talked, not inaudibly, while it was going on.
“很遗憾这些房间太小了，我们不能跳舞。”她对温特伯恩说，好像五分钟前才见到他。 “It’s a pity these rooms are so small; we can’t dance,” she said to Winterbourne, as if she had seen him five minutes before.
“我们不能跳舞我并不遗憾。”温特伯恩回答，“我从不跳舞。” “I am not sorry we can’t dance,” Winterbourne answered; “I don’t dance.”
“你当然不跳舞了，你太死板了。”戴西小姐说，“我希望你和沃克夫人坐车兜风玩得很开心！” “Of course you don’t dance; you’re too stiff,” said Miss Daisy.“I hope you enjoyed your drive with Mrs. Walker!”
“不，我并不开心，我更愿意和你散步。” “No. I didn’t enjoy it; I preferred walking with you.”
“我们分成对儿了，那样好得多。”戴西说，“可是你听说过如此冷酷的事吗？沃克夫人想让我上她的马车，丢下可怜的乔万内里先生，还以此作为合乎体统的借口。人们的观念不同嘛！那样做太不仁慈了，他一直说要散步，都有十天了。” “We paired off: that was much better,” said Daisy.“But did you ever hear anything so cool as Mrs. Walker’s wanting me to get into her carriage and drop poor Mr. Giovanelli, and under the pretext that it was proper?People have different ideas!It would have been most unkind; he had been talking about that walk for ten days.”
“他根本就不该提这事。”温特伯恩说，“他是绝对不会向这个国家的年轻女士提议陪他到处逛街的。” “He should not have talked about it at all,” said Winterbourne; “he would never have proposed to a young lady of this country to walk about the streets with him.”
“逛街？”戴西瞪着漂亮的眼睛喊道，“那么，他能向她提议去哪儿散步呢？品奇欧也不是大街啊，并且，感谢上帝，我不是这个国家的年轻女士。据我所知，这个国家的年轻女士过得非常无聊，我看不出什么要因为她们而改变我的习惯。” “About the streets?” cried Daisy with her pretty stare.“Where, then, would he have proposed to her to walk?The Pincio is not the streets, either; and I, thank goodness, am not a young lady of this country.The young ladies of this country have a dreadfully poky time of it, so far as I can learn; I don’t see why I should change my habits for THEM.”
“恐怕你的习惯是一个打情骂俏的人才有的。”温特伯恩严肃地说。 “I am afraid your habits are those of a flirt,” said Winterbourne gravely.
“当然是。”她喊道，再次微笑着瞪了他一眼。“我是个可怕的、讨厌的、喜欢打情骂俏的人！你听说过哪一位漂亮姑娘不喜欢卖弄风情吗？但是，我想你现在会告诉我说我不是个漂亮的姑娘。” “Of course they are,” she cried, giving him her little smiling stare again.“I’m a fearful, frightful flirt!Did you ever hear of a nice girl that was not?But I suppose you will tell me now that I am not a nice girl.”
“你是个非常漂亮的姑娘，可是我希望你和我打情骂俏，并且只和我一个人。”温特伯恩说。 “You’re a very nice girl; but I wish you would flirt with me, and me only,” said Winterbourne.
“啊！谢谢你！非常感谢你。你是我最不愿意打情骂俏的人。正如我曾有幸告诉你的那样，你太死板了。” “Ah!thank you—thank you very much; you are the last man I should think of flirting with.As I have had the pleasure of informing you, you are too stiff.”
“你老是这么说。”温特伯恩说。 “You say that too often,” said Winterbourne.
戴西开心地笑了。“如果我真的想让你生气，那我就会再说一遍。” Daisy gave a delighted laugh.“If I could have the sweet hope of making you angry, I should say it again.”
“不要那么做，我生气的时候，会比以往更死板的。不过，如果你不和我调情，至少也一定不要和钢琴旁边的你的朋友调情，在这里，人们不理解那样的事情。” “Don’t do that; when I am angry I’m stiffer than ever.But if you won’t flirt with me, do cease, at least, to flirt with your friend at the piano; they don’t understand that sort of thing here.”
“我觉着他们什么都理解不了！”戴西叫道。 “I thought they understood nothing else!” exclaimed Daisy.
“年轻的未婚女子不会这样的。” “Not in young unmarried women.”
“在我看来年轻的未婚女子这样做比成熟的已婚女子更合适。”戴西宣称。 “It seems to me much more proper in young unmarried women than in old married ones,” Daisy declared.
“哦。”温特伯恩说，“你和本地人打交道的时候，就必须合乎这个地方的风俗。打情骂俏是纯粹的美国风俗，但在这里是没有的。所以，当你和乔万内里先生在公共场合露面时，并且没有你母亲在场——” “Well,” said Winterbourne, “when you deal with natives you must go by the custom of the place.Flirting is a purely American custom; it doesn’t exist here.So when you show yourself in public with Mr. Giovanelli, and without your mother—”
“天哪！可怜的妈妈！”戴西插话。 “Gracious!poor Mother!” interposed Daisy.
“尽管你或许是在调情，可乔万内里先生不是，他会有别的意图。” “Though you may be flirting, Mr. Giovanelli is not; he means something else.”
“不管怎么说，他没有跟我讲道理。”戴西欢快地说。“要是你非常想知道，我就告诉你，我们都没有在调情，我们是非常要好的朋友，用不着那么做了，我们是非常亲密的朋友。” “He isn’t preaching, at any rate,” said Daisy with vivacity.“And if you want very much to know, we are neither of us flirting; we are too good friends for that: we are very intimate friends.”
“啊！”温特伯恩接着说，“如果你们彼此相爱，那就是另一回事了。” “Ah!” rejoined Winterbourne, “if you are in love with each other, it is another affair.”
到目前为止，她一直都允许他坦诚地说话，以至于他没有料到这句突然说出的话会使她如此震惊。她立刻站了起来，满脸通红，他心想，这个喜欢打情骂俏的美国小妞真是这世界上最奇怪的生物。“至少，乔万内里先生，”她瞥了她的对话者一眼说，“从未对我说过这么讨厌的话。” She had allowed him up to this point to talk so frankly that he had no expectation of shocking her by this ejaculation; but she immediately got up, blushing visibly, and leaving him to exclaim mentally that little American flirts were the queerest creatures in the world.“Mr. Giovanelli, at least,” she said, giving her interlocutor a single glance, “never says such very disagreeable things to me.”
温特伯恩迷惑了，他站在那儿瞪大眼睛望着。乔万内里先生结束了演唱。他离开钢琴来到了戴西身边。“你不愿来另一个房间喝点茶吗？”他问道，在她面前鞠了一躬，满脸堆笑。 Winterbourne was bewildered; he stood, staring.Mr. Giovanelli had finished singing.He left the piano and came over to Daisy.“Won’t you come into the other room and have some tea?” he asked, bending before her with his ornamental smile.
戴西转向温特伯恩，又微笑起来。他越发迷惑了，因为这不合情理的微笑什么也说明不了，尽管它似乎可以证明，她甜美而温和，能宽容地原谅别人的冒犯。“温特伯恩先生从未提过要请我喝茶。”她带着些许的嘲弄说。 Daisy turned to Winterbourne, beginning to smile again.He was still more perplexed, for this inconsequent smile made nothing clear, though it seemed to prove, indeed, that she had a sweetness and softness that reverted instinctively to the pardon of offenses.“It has never occurred to Mr. Winterbourne to offer me any tea,” she said with her little tormenting manner.
“但我给你提过建议。”温特伯恩说。 “I have offered you advice,” Winterbourne rejoined.
“我更喜欢淡茶！”戴西喊了一声，和引人注目的乔万内里一起走开了。当晚剩余的时间，她一直和他坐在相邻房间的窗口旁。有一场有趣的钢琴表演，可是这两个年轻人都没有留意。当戴西来向沃克夫人道别的时候，这位女士有意识地弥补了一下自己的软弱——在姑娘到来那一刻，她曾感到内疚。她转过身去，用背直对着米勒小姐，让她离开时没有任何的面子。温特伯恩正站在大门旁边，他看到了一切。戴西脸色变得很苍白，她望着她的母亲，但是米勒夫人很谦卑，根本没有意识到任何违背常规社交礼仪的行为。事实上，她却似乎感觉到一种与谦卑不相称的冲动，要将人们的注意力引向她自己那些极其遵守礼仪的行为上。“晚安，沃克夫人。”她说，“我们度过了一个美好的夜晚。您瞧，如果戴西来参加聚会的时候我没有要陪着她，我可不想让她走的时候也没有陪她。”戴西转过身去，脸色苍白，严肃地看着大门附近的一圈人，温特伯恩看到她开始时太震惊、太迷惑了，甚至都没有感到气愤。他站在自己的立场上看也极其受触动。 “I prefer weak tea!” cried Daisy, and she went off with the brilliant Giovanelli.She sat with him in the adjoining room, in the embrasure of the window, for the rest of the evening.There was an interesting performance at the piano, but neither of these young people gave heed to it.When Daisy came to take leave of Mrs. Walker, this lady conscientiously repaired the weakness of which she had been guilty at the moment of the young girl’s arrival.She turned her back straight upon Miss Miller and left her to depart with what grace she might.Winterbourne was standing near the door; he saw it all.Daisy turned very pale and looked at her mother, but Mrs. Miller was humbly unconscious of any violation of the usual social forms.She appeared, indeed, to have felt an incongruous impulse to draw attention to her own striking observance of them.“Good night, Mrs. Walker,” she said; “we’ve had a beautiful evening.You see, if I let Daisy come to parties without me, I don’t want her to go away without me.”Daisy turned away, looking with a pale, grave face at the circle near the door; Winterbourne saw that, for the first moment, she was too much shocked and puzzled even for indignation.He on his side was greatly touched.
“您那样太残忍了。”他对沃克夫人说。 “That was very cruel,” he said to Mrs. Walker.
“她别想再进入我的会客室！”女主人说道。 “She never enters my drawing room again!” replied his hostess.
既然温特伯恩不会在沃克夫人的会客室见到她，他就尽可能经常地去米勒夫人的旅店见她。女士们很少在家，可每当他找到她们的时候，那位忠诚的乔万内里总是在场。相貌出众的小个子罗马人经常独自和戴西坐在会客室里。米勒夫人显然持有这样的观点:任其自然就是最好的监视。起初，温特伯恩惊讶地注意到，在这样的场合下，戴西对他的到来既不尴尬也不恼火，但是他很快便见怪不怪了，她出乎意料的行为举止才是唯一可以预料的事。和乔万内里面对面的聊天被打断时，她也没有表现出不悦，她可以精神饱满地、自由自在地和两位绅士聊天，就像在面对一个人，她的谈话将厚颜无耻和天真无邪融为一体。温特伯恩对自己说，如果她真的对乔万内里感兴趣，她竟毫不注意保护他们会面的神圣性，这点很奇怪，他越来越喜欢她那看似天真的冷淡和她那永远开朗的好性情了。他说不出为什么，可是在他看来她似乎是个永远不会嫉妒的姑娘。虽然恐怕会引发读者的嘲笑，不过我可以断言，就目前为止引起温特伯恩兴趣的女士而言，除去某些偶然性，他似乎经常会对这些女士感到害怕——真的害怕；然而他却很开心地感到自己永远不会害怕戴西·米勒。必须补充的是，这种感觉并不完全是对戴西的奉承，而是因为他确信，或者说他担心她其实是个非常轻浮的人。 Since Winterbourne was not to meet her in Mrs. Walker’s drawing room, he went as often as possible to Mrs. Miller’s hotel.The ladies were rarely at home, but when he found them, the devoted Giovanelli was always present.Very often the brilliant little Roman was in the drawing room with Daisy alone, Mrs. Miller being apparently constantly of the opinion that discretion is the better part of surveillance.Winterbourne noted, at first with surprise, that Daisy on these occasions was never embarrassed or annoyed by his own entrance; but he very presently began to feel that she had no more surprises for him; the unexpected in her behavior was the only thing to expect.She showed no displeasure at her tete-a-tete with Giovanelli being interrupted; she could chatter as freshly and freely with two gentlemen as with one; there was always, in her conversation, the same odd mixture of audacity and puerility.Winterbourne remarked to himself that if she was seriously interested in Giovanelli, it was very singular that she should not take more trouble to preserve the sanctity of their interviews; and he liked her the more for her innocent-looking indifference and her apparently inexhaustible good humor.He could hardly have said why, but she seemed to him a girl who would never be jealous.At the risk of exciting a somewhat derisive smile on the reader’s part, I may affirm that with regard to the women who had hitherto interested him, it very often seemed to Winterbourne among the possibilities that, given certain contingencies, he should be afraid—literally afraid—of these ladies; he had a pleasant sense that he should never be afraid of Daisy Miller.It must be added that this sentiment was not altogether flattering to Daisy; it was part of his conviction, or rather of his apprehension, that she would prove a very light young person.
但是她明显对乔万内里非常感兴趣。每当他说话时，她都看着他；她不断地告诉他去做这做那；她时常打趣地谩骂他。她似乎完全忘记了在沃克夫人的小型聚会上温特伯恩说过的任何使她不悦的话。一个星期日的下午，温特伯恩和他的姑妈去了圣彼得教堂，他看到戴西在那位必然会出现的乔万内里的陪伴下，正在大教堂周边散步。他立刻将这位年轻姑娘和她的绅士指给科斯特洛夫人。这位女士透过她的眼镜看了他们一会儿，然后说道： But she was evidently very much interested in Giovanelli.She looked at him whenever he spoke; she was perpetually telling him to do this and to do that; she was constantly “chaffing” and abusing him.She appeared completely to have forgotten that Winterbourne had said anything to displease her at Mrs. Walker’s little party.One Sunday afternoon, having gone to St. Peter’s with his aunt, Winterbourne perceived Daisy strolling about the great church in company with the inevitable Giovanelli.Presently he pointed out the young girl and her cavalier to Mrs. Costello.This lady looked at them a moment through her eyeglass, and then she said:
“那就是让你这些天闷闷不乐的原因吧，嗯？” “That’s what makes you so pensive in these days, eh?”
“我一点也没发现我闷闷不乐啊。”年轻人说。 “I had not the least idea I was pensive,” said the young man.
“你心事重重，肯定是在想着什么事。” “You are very much preoccupied; you are thinking of something.”
“那您认为，”他问道，“我在想什么事呢？” “And what is it,” he asked, “that you accuse me of thinking of?”
“就是那位年轻女士——贝克小姐？钱德勒小姐？——她叫什么名字来着？——米勒小姐和那个小理发匠的私通。” “Of that young lady’s—Miss Baker’s, Miss Chandler’s—what’s her name?—Miss Miller’s intrigue with that little barber’s block.”
“您把它称为私通？”温特伯恩问道，“那是这么明显公开发展的关系呀。” “Do you call it an intrigue,” Winterbourne asked—“an affair that goes on with such peculiar publicity?”
“那是他们的愚蠢，”科斯特洛夫人说，“不是他们的美德。” “That’s their folly,” said Mrs. Costello; “it’s not their merit.”
“不。”温特反驳道，带着他姑妈影射他心事重重时的语气。“我不相信他们之间有什么私通的事情。” “No,” rejoined Winterbourne, with something of that pensiveness to which his aunt had alluded.“I don’t believe that there is anything to be called an intrigue.”
“我已听许多人说起过，他们说她完全被他迷住了。” “I have heard a dozen people speak of it; they say she is quite carried away by him.”
“他们当然非常亲密。”温特伯恩说。 “They are certainly very intimate,” said Winterbourne.
科斯特洛夫人再次用她的镜片察看了一下这对年轻人。“他非常英俊。很容易就能看出这是怎么一回事。她认为他是世界上最优雅的男子，最优秀的绅士。她从未见过像他一样的人，他甚至比那个侍从还要好。可能是那个侍从介绍他的，如果他成功地娶到了这位年轻小姐，侍从将会得到一大笔酬金。” Mrs. Costello inspected the young couple again with her optical instrument.“He is very handsome.One easily sees how it is.She thinks him the most elegant man in the world, the finest gentleman.She has never seen anything like him; he is better, even, than the courier.It was the courier probably who introduced him; and if he succeeds in marrying the young lady, the courier will come in for a magnificent commission.”
“我不相信她会考虑嫁给他。”温特伯恩说，“我不相信他会希望娶到她。” “I don’t believe she thinks of marrying him,” said Winterbourne, “and I don’t believe he hopes to marry her.”
“你可以肯定她什么都不想。她一天一天，一小时一小时地混日子，就像黄金时代人们所做的那样。我想象不出更庸俗的事了。并且，同时，”科斯特洛夫人补充说，“可以肯定，她可能随时会告诉你说她‘订婚’了。” “You may be very sure she thinks of nothing.She goes on from day to day, from hour to hour, as they did in the Golden Age.I can imagine nothing more vulgar.And at the same time,” added Mrs. Costello, “depend upon it that she may tell you any moment that she is ‘engaged.’”
“我想那超出了乔万内里的期望。”温特伯恩说。 “I think that is more than Giovanelli expects,” said Winterbourne.
“谁是乔万内里？” “Who is Giovanelli?”
“那个小个子意大利人。我问了一些关于他的问题并获悉了一些情况。他显然是个非常令人尊重的小个子男人。我相信他是个小本经营的律师大人。但是他没有进入所谓的一流社交圈。我认为那个侍从介绍他绝对是不可能的。他显然是被米勒小姐迷倒了。如果她认为他是世界上最优秀的绅士，那么在他看来，他也还从未亲自接触过像这位这么美丽、这么富裕、这么有钱的年轻女士。并且，在他看来，她肯定非常地美丽风趣。我怀疑他连做梦都没梦到过能娶她。那对于他来说肯定是不可能的运气。他除了他的英俊脸蛋什么都给不了她，而那片神奇的美元大地上却有一位殷实的米勒先生。乔万内里先生知道他没有任何头衔。就算他是个伯爵或侯爵也好啊！他肯定对他的运气惊叹不已，他们竟然会接受他。” “The little Italian.I have asked questions about him and learned something.He is apparently a perfectly respectable little man.I believe he is, in a small way, a cavaliere avvocato.But he doesn’t move in what are called the first circles.I think it is really not absolutely impossible that the courier introduced him.He is evidently immensely charmed with Miss Miller.If she thinks him the finest gentleman in the world, he, on his side, has never found himself in personal contact with such splendor, such opulence, such expensiveness as this young lady’s.And then she must seem to him wonderfully pretty and interesting.I rather doubt that he dreams of marrying her.That must appear to him too impossible a piece of luck.He has nothing but his handsome face to offer, and there is a substantial Mr. Miller in that mysterious land of dollars.Giovanelli knows that he hasn’t a title to offer.If he were only a count or a marchese!He must wonder at his luck, at the way they have taken him up.”
“他对此的解释是他有张英俊的脸蛋，并认为米勒小姐是个轻佻任性的姑娘！”科斯特洛夫人说。 “He accounts for it by his handsome face and thinks Miss Miller a young lady qui se passe ses fantaisies!” said Mrs. Costello.
“这非常正确。”温特伯恩继续说，“戴西和她的母亲还没有上升到那个阶段——我该称作什么呢？——那个文化修养，根本没有伯爵或侯爵的概念。我想她们在智力上还理解不了这个概念。” “It is very true,” Winterbourne pursued, “that Daisy and her mamma have not yet risen to that stage of—what shall I call it?—of culture at which the idea of catching a count or a marchese begins.I believe that they are intellectually incapable of that conception.”
“哦！但是那位律师大人不这样认为。”科斯特洛夫人说。 “Ah! but the avvocato can’t believe it,” said Mrs. Costello.
那天在圣彼得教堂，温特伯恩搜集到了许多的戴西“私通”的证言。罗马的一群美国移民上前来和坐在巨大壁柱下的一张便携凳上的科斯特洛夫人说话。晚祷在优美的风琴声和邻近诗班的圣歌声中进行；与此同时，科斯特洛夫人和她的朋友们正在谈论可怜的小米勒小姐，说她实在做得“太过分了”。温特伯恩对他听到的话感到不悦，可是，当他走出到教堂巨大的台阶上时，他看见戴西出现在了他的前面，和她的同谋者进了一辆敞篷马车，穿过罗马那些玩世不恭的街道走远了，他不能否认，她真的做得太过分了。他为她感到很遗憾，并不是因为他相信她彻底失去了理智，而是因为听到那么多话将美丽、不设防、自然同无序世界中的庸俗不堪相提并论，令人着实心痛。在这之后他曾试图给米勒夫人一个暗示。一天，他在科索大街遇到一个朋友，是一个像他一样的游客，刚游览了美丽的画廊，从多丽亚宫出来。他的朋友谈论了一会儿委拉斯开兹的杰出作品——挂在宫殿陈列室之一内的教皇英诺森十世的肖像，然后说：“顺便说一下，就在同一个陈列室里，我有幸注意到一幅不同类型的画面——上周你指给我的那个漂亮的美国姑娘。”为了回答温特伯恩的追问，他的朋友叙述说，那个漂亮姑娘比平时更漂亮了，和一个同伴坐在珍藏大教皇肖像的隐蔽角落里。 Of the observation excited by Daisy’s “intrigue,” Winterbourne gathered that day at St. Peter’s sufficient evidence.A dozen of the American colonists in Rome came to talk with Mrs. Costello, who sat on a little portable stool at the base of one of the great pilasters.The vesper service was going forward in splendid chants and organ tones in the adjacent choir, and meanwhile, between Mrs. Costello and her friends, there was a great deal said about poor little Miss Miller’s going really “too far.”Winterbourne was not pleased with what he heard, but when, coming out upon the great steps of the to a vulgar place among the categories of disorder.He made an attempt after this to give a hint to Mrs. Miller.He met one day in the Corso a friend, a tourist like himself, who had just come out of 他与她母亲简短会面的几天后，他在被称为凯撒宫的废墟前与她相遇，那里鲜花盛开，景色迷人。罗马的早春让空气中充满了花的芳香，崎岖不平的巴勒登山表面蒙上了一层柔和的翠绿。戴西正沿着一座巨大的废墟堆顶部漫步，四周绕着长满青苔的大理石的围墙、路砖上刻满了碑文。在他看来似乎罗马从没像此刻那么可爱。他站在那儿，眺望着远处环绕着城市的迷人而和谐的线条和色彩，呼吸着微微湿润的香味，感受到岁月的新鲜与此地的古老再次神秘地混合起来。在他看来戴西也似乎从未如此美丽，但是，无论什么时候遇见她，他都会这么觉着。乔万内里在她的身边，就连他也有了不寻常的神采奕奕的一面。 A few days after his brief interview with her mother, he encountered her in that beautiful abode of flowering desolation known as the Palace of the Caesars.The early Roman spring had filled the air with bloom and perfume, and the rugged surface of the Palatine was muffled with tender verdure.Daisy was strolling along the top of one of those great mounds of ruin that are embanked with mossy marble and paved with monumental inscriptions.It seemed to him that Rome had never been so lovely as just then.He stood, looking off at the enchanting harmony of line and color that remotely encircles the city, inhaling the softly humid odors, and feeling the freshness of the year and the antiquity of the place reaffirm themselves in mysterious interfusion.It seemed to him also that Daisy had never looked so pretty, but this had been an observation of his whenever he met her.Giovanelli was at her side, and Giovanelli, too, wore an aspect of even unwonted brilliancy.

“寂寞？”温特伯恩问。 “Lonesome?” asked Winterbourne.

“你会发现我不像其他一些人那么呆板。”温特伯恩微笑着说。 “You will find I am not so stiff as several others,” said Winterbourne, smiling.

“是啊，她是这么认为。”戴西很坦白地说。 “Well, she does,” said Daisy very simply.

乔万内里扬了扬他有型的眉毛，露出他闪亮的牙齿。但是他温顺地接受了温特伯恩的指责。“我告诉过小姐这么做太轻率了，可是小姐什么时候小心过呢？” Giovanelli lifted his well-shaped eyebrows and showed his brilliant teeth.But he took Winterbourne’s rebuke with docility.“I told the signorina it was a grave indiscretion, but when was the signorina ever prudent?”
“我从未病过，也不想生病！”这位小姐宣称，“我看上去也不像是会生病的，我可是很健康的！我一定要看看月光下的大剧场，我不应该不看看就回家，并且我们过得非常开心，不是吗，乔万内里先生？如果有什么危险的话，尤金尼奥会给我一些药片的。他弄到了一些很好的药片。” “I never was sick, and I don’t mean to be!” the signorina declared.“I don’t look like much, but I’m healthy!I was bound to see the Colosseum by moonlight; I shouldn’t have wanted to go home without that; and we have had the most beautiful time, haven’t we, Mr. Giovanelli?If there has been any danger, Eugenio can give me some pills.He has got some splendid pills.”
“我建议你，”温特伯恩说，“赶紧回家吃上一片药。” “I should advise you,” said Winterbourne, “to drive home as fast as possible and take one!”
“你的话很明智。”乔万内里附和道，“我这就去确认马车预备好了。”他迅速走开了。 “What you say is very wise,” Giovanelli rejoined.“I will go and make sure the carriage is at hand.”And he went forward rapidly.
戴西和温特伯恩跟在后边。他一直望着她，她似乎一点也不尴尬。温特伯恩什么也不说，而戴西则聊着这地方的美景。“噢，我真的看见了月光下的大剧场了！”她惊呼，“这真是件好事。”随后，注意到温特伯恩的沉默后，她问他为何不说话。他没有回答，只是笑了起来。他们走过一道黑暗的拱廊，看到乔万内里和马车正等在前面。戴西在这里停留了片刻，望着这位年轻的美国人。“那天，你真的相信我订婚了吗？”她问。 Daisy followed with Winterbourne.He kept looking at her; she seemed not in the least embarrassed.Winterbourne said nothing; Daisy chattered about the beauty of the place.“Well, I HAVE seen the Colosseum by moonlight!” she exclaimed.“That’s one good thing.”Then, noticing Winterbourne’s silence, she asked him why he didn’t speak.He made no answer; he only began to laugh.They passed under one of the dark archways; Giovanelli was in front with the carriage.Here Daisy stopped a moment, looking at the young American.“DID you believe I was engaged, the other day?” she asked.
“那天我相信什么并不重要。”温特伯恩说，还在笑着。 “It doesn’t matter what I believed the other day,” said Winterbourne, still laughing.
“那么，你现在觉得呢？” “Well, what do you believe now?”
“我想你订婚与否区别不大啦！” “I believe that it makes very little difference whether you are engaged or not!”
他感觉到年轻姑娘漂亮的眼睛正透过拱廊浓浓的幽暗盯着他，她显然要答话。可是乔万内里催促她走。“快点！快点！”他说，“如果我们在午夜前回去就非常安全了。” He felt the young girl’s pretty eyes fixed upon him through the thick gloom of the archway; she was apparently going to answer.But Giovanelli hurried her forward.“Quick!quick!” he said; “if we get in by midnight we are quite safe.”
戴西在马车里坐下，那个幸运的意大利人坐在她身旁。“别忘了尤金尼奥的药片！”温特伯恩举起帽子时说。 Daisy took her seat in the carriage, and the fortunate Italian placed himself beside her.“Don’t forget Eugenio’s pills!” said Winterbourne as he lifted his hat.
“我一点儿也不在乎，”戴西用有点奇怪的语气说，“得不得罗马热病！”说话间，马车夫抽响了鞭子，他们沿着古道坎坷不平的修补路面向前方驶去。 “I don’t care,” said Daisy in a little strange tone, “whether I have Roman fever or not!”Upon this the cab driver cracked his whip, and they rolled away over the desultory patches of the antique pavement.
公道地说，温特伯恩向谁都没有提起过他半夜在大剧场遇到戴西和一位绅士的事；然而，一两天后，戴西在那种情况下去过那里的事却被小小的美国圈里的每个成员都知道了，大家都评论着这件事。温特伯恩寻思着他们当然是在旅馆里知道的，戴西回来后，门卫和车夫之间肯定互相说了些什么。但是，与此同时，这个年轻人也意识到，对于他来说，这个打情骂俏的美国小姐被下等仆人议论已不再是一件很遗憾的事了。一两天后，这些人传出了严重的消息：那位打情骂俏的美国小姐生病了，病得很严重。温特伯恩听到传言后，便立刻去旅馆了解更多的消息。他发现两三个好心的朋友已经先于他到了，他们正在米勒夫人的客厅里接受伦道夫的招待。 Winterbourne, to do him justice, as it were, mentioned to no one that he had encountered Miss Miller, at midnight, in the Colosseum with a gentleman; but nevertheless, a couple of days later, the fact of her having been there under these circumstances was known to every member of the little American circle, and commented accordingly.Winterbourne reflected that they had of course known it at the hotel, and that, after Daisy’s return, there had been an exchange of remarks between the porter and the cab driver.But the young man was conscious, at the same moment, that it had ceased to be a matter of serious regret to him that the little American flirt should be “talked about” by low-minded menials.These people, a day or two later, had serious information to give: the little American flirt was alarmingly ill.Winterbourne, when the rumor came to him, immediately went to the hotel for more news.He found that two or three charitable friends had preceded him, and that they were being entertained in Mrs. Miller’s salon by Randolph.
“就是因为晚上到处乱跑，”伦道夫说，“这就是她生病的原因。她总是在晚上到处乱逛。我觉得她不该去，天黑得要命。在这里，晚上你什么都看不见，除非有月亮的时候。在美国总是有月亮的！”没有看见米勒夫人，她现在至少也该是在陪伴着她的女儿。很明显，戴西病得很严重。 “It’s going round at night,” said Randolph—“that’s what made her sick.She’s always going round at night.I shouldn’t think she’d want to, it’s so plaguy dark.You can’t see anything here at night, except when there’s a moon.In America there’s always a moon!”Mrs. Miller was invisible; she was now, at least, giving her daughter the advantage of her society.It was evident that Daisy was dangerously ill.
温特伯恩经常去问询她的消息，有一次他见到了米勒夫人，让他惊讶的是，她虽然十分担忧，却极为镇静，而且看上去还是位高效、干练的护士。她讲了很多有关戴维斯医生的话，可是温特伯恩还是向她表示了问候，并对自己说，毕竟她不是个荒谬的傻瓜。“戴西有一天说起你了。”她对他说，“有半数时间她不知道她在说什么，可是那次，我想她知道。她要我给你捎个口信。她要我告诉你说，她从未和那个英俊的意大利人订过婚。我肯定我非常高兴；自从她生病以来，乔万内里先生从没来过我们这儿。我原以为他真是个绅士；可是我觉得那样非常不礼貌！一位女士告诉我说，他害怕我会因为他带戴西晚上出去而生他的气。唉，我是生气，可是我想他知道我是位有身份的女士呀。我才不屑于去责骂他。无论怎样，她说她没有订婚。我不知道她为什么想让你知道，但是她和我说了三次：‘你一定告诉温特伯恩先生。’然后，她让我问问你，你是否记得在瑞士的那次去古堡的事。可是我说，我不愿传达任何这样的口信。只是，如果她没有订婚的话，我确信我很高兴知道这事。” Winterbourne went often to ask for news of her, and once he saw Mrs. Miller, who, though deeply alarmed, was, rather to his surprise, perfectly composed, and, as it appeared, a most efficient and judicious nurse.She talked a good deal about Dr. Davis, but Winterbourne paid her the compliment of saying to himself that she was not, after all, such a monstrous goose.“Daisy spoke of you the other day,” she said to him.“Half the time she doesn’t know what she’s saying, but that time I think she did.She gave me a message she told me to tell you.She told me to tell you that she never was engaged to that handsome Italian.I am sure I am very glad; Mr. Giovanelli hasn’t been near us since she was taken ill.I thought he was so much of a gentleman; but I don’t call that very polite!A lady told me that he was afraid I was angry with him for taking Daisy round at night.Well, so I am, but I suppose he knows I’m a lady.I would scorn to scold him.Anyway, she says she’s not engaged.I don’t know why she wanted you to know, but she said to me three times, ‘Mind you tell Mr. Winterbourne.’And then she told me to ask if you remembered the time you went to that castle in Switzerland.But I said I wouldn’t give any such messages as that.Only, if she is not engaged, I’m sure I’m glad to know it.”
可是，正如温特伯恩说的，这已经不重要了。此后一个星期，这个可怜的姑娘死了，那是一场可怕的热病。戴西的坟墓在小小的新教墓地里，在罗马皇宫城墙的一个角落里，在柏树和密集的迎春花下面。温特伯恩和很多其他送葬者站在墓旁，送葬者的数量超过你的预想，虽然这只是一个一生都传出流言蜚语的年轻姑娘。他的身旁站着乔万内里，在温特伯恩转身离开前，他凑了过来。乔万内里脸色很苍白，在这个场合他的钮孔里没插鲜花，他似乎想说点什么。最后，他说：“她是我见过的最漂亮的姑娘，而且是最亲切的姑娘。”之后，他又加上了一句，“她还是最天真的姑娘。” But, as Winterbourne had said, it mattered very little.A week after this, the poor girl died; it had been a terrible case of the fever.Daisy’s grave was in the little Protestant cemetery, in an angle of the wall of imperial Rome, beneath the cypresses and the thick spring flowers.Winterbourne stood there beside it, with a number of other mourners, a number larger than the scandal excited by the young lady’s career would have led you to expect.Near him stood Giovanelli, who came nearer still before Winterbourne turned away.Giovanelli was very pale: on this occasion he had no flower in his buttonhole; he seemed to wish to say something.At last he said, “She was the most beautiful young lady I ever saw, and the most amiable”; and then he added in a moment, “and she was the most innocent.”
温特伯恩望着他，不一会儿就重复道：“还是最天真的？” Winterbourne looked at him and presently repeated his words, “And the most innocent?”
“最天真的！” “The most innocent!”
温特伯恩感到极度愤怒。“究竟为什么，”他问，“你要带她去那个要命的地方呢？” Winterbourne felt sore and angry.“Why the devil,” he asked, “did you take her to that fatal place?”
乔万内里先生显然镇定，还是一样的温文尔雅。他望了一会儿地面，然后说：“我并不怕我自己，是她非想去的。” Mr. Giovanelli’s urbanity was apparently imperturbable.He looked on the ground a moment, and then he said, “For myself I had no fear; and she wanted to go.”
“那不是理由！”温特伯恩严肃地说。 “That was no reason!” Winterbourne declared.
狡猾的罗马人再次垂下他的眼睛。“如果她活着，我也什么都得不到。她决不会嫁给我的，我肯定。” The subtle Roman again dropped his eyes.“If she had lived, I should have got nothing.She would never have married me, I am sure.”
“她决不会嫁给你？” “She would never have married you?”
“我曾一度希望如此。可是不会的。我肯定。” “For a moment I hoped so.But no.I am sure.”
温特伯恩听着他的话，站在那儿盯着四月雏菊中的新坟。当他再次转过身时，乔万内里先生已迈着轻柔缓慢的步伐退去了。 Winterbourne listened to him: he stood staring at the raw protuberance among the April daisies.When he turned away again, Mr. Giovanelli, with his light, slow step, had retired.
温特伯恩几乎立刻就离开了罗马，不过第二年夏天，他又再次来到沃韦，看望他的姑妈科斯特洛夫人。科斯特洛夫人喜欢沃韦。温特伯恩会不时地想起戴西·米勒和她神秘莫测的行为举止。一天，他向他的姑妈说起她，说他总觉得，对她有些不公正。 Winterbourne almost immediately left Rome; but the following summer he again met his aunt, Mrs. Costello at Vevey.Mrs. Costello was fond of Vevey.In the interval Winterbourne had often thought of Daisy Miller and her mystifying manners.One day he spoke of her to his aunt—said it was on his conscience that he had done her injustice.
“我真是不明白，”科斯特洛夫人说，“你的不公正怎么影响到她了？” “I am sure I don’t know,” said Mrs. Costello.“How did your injustice affect her?”
“她死前给我捎了口信，当时我不明白，可是后来我明白了。她很希望得到别人的尊重。” “She sent me a message before her death which I didn’t understand at the time; but I have understood it since.She would have appreciated one’s esteem.”
“那是一种婉转的说法吗，”科斯特洛夫人问，“是说她会报答别人的喜爱吗？” “Is that a modest way,” asked Mrs. Costello, “of saying that she would have reciprocated one’s affection?”
温特伯恩没有回答这个问题，可是他过了一会儿却说：“去年夏天您说的那句话是对的。我注定会犯错。我在国外住得太久了。” Winterbourne offered no answer to this question; but he presently said, “You were right in that remark that you made last summer.I was booked to make a mistake.I have lived too long in foreign parts.”
然而，他还是回到了日内瓦生活，关于他留下的动机，有两种非常矛盾的传言：一种是说他在努力“学习”；另一种则是暗示他对一位非常聪明的外国小姐很感兴趣。 Nevertheless, he went back to live at Geneva, whence there continue to come the most contradictory accounts of his motives of sojourn: a report that he is “studying” hard—an intimation that he is much interested in a very clever foreign lady.
