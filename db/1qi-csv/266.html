葡萄树与山羊 The Vine and the Goat
在葡萄收获的时节，有一株葡萄藤枝繁叶茂，长满了葡萄。一只山羊路过，一点点地啃食着它新生的藤叶和卷须。葡萄藤对山羊说道：“你为什么要这样无缘无故伤害我，啃食我的叶子呢？难道没有余下的嫩草可吃了吗？不过，等不了多久，我就可以名正言顺地复仇了，因为如果你现在啃食我的叶子，咬断我的根茎，那么，当你成为祭品的时候，我将把酿成的葡萄酒泼在你的身上。” A Vine was luxuriant in the time of vintage with leaves and grapes.A Goat, passing by, nibbled its young tendrils and its leaves.The Vine addressed him and said: “Why do you thus injure me without a cause, and crop my leaves?Is there no young grass left?But I shall not have to wait long for my just revenge; for if you now should crop my leaves, and cut me down to my root, I shall provide the wine to pour over you when you are led as a victim to the sacrifice.”
朱庇特和猴子 Jupiter and the Monkey
朱庇特向森林里所有的动物发布了一份公告，承诺如果谁的孩子被公认为是最美的，它将得到一份大大的奖赏。一只猴子也跟着其他动物一起来了，带着一个母亲的所有慈爱，呈献出一只塌鼻子、没有毛、丑陋的小猴子作为候选者，竞争那份承诺的奖赏。当它的儿子出场时，迎接猴妈妈的是一片嘲笑声。猴妈妈坚定地说道：“我不知道朱庇特会不会把这个奖分给我的儿子，但我知道一点，那就是至少在我——它的母亲眼里，它是最标致、最英俊、最美丽的。 Jupite issued a proclamation to all the beasts of the forest and promised a royal reward to the one whose offspring should be deemed the handsomest.The Monkey came with the rest and presented, with all a mother’s tenderness, a flat nosed, hairless, ill featured young Monkey as a candidate for the promised reward.A general laugh saluted her on the presentation of her son.She resolutely said, “I know not whether Jupiter will allot the prize to my son, but this I do know, that he is at least in the eyes of me his mother, the dearest, handsomest, and most beautiful of all.”
寡妇和她的小女仆们 The Widow and Her Little Maidens
一个爱干净的寡妇有两个小女仆服侍她。她习惯在清晨公鸡啼叫的时候就把她们叫醒。女仆们因过度辛劳而感到很恼火，于是决定杀掉那只早早就唤醒女主人起来的公鸡。等把那只公鸡杀了以后，她们才发现这样做只是给自己带来了更大的麻烦。因为她们的女主人不再听公鸡啼叫报时了，而是在半夜就把她们叫醒去工作。 A Widow who was fond of cleaning had two little maidens to wait on her.She was in the habit of waking them early in the morning, at cockcrow.The maidens, aggravated by such excessive labor, resolved to kill the cock who roused their mistress so early.When they had done this, they found that they had only prepared for themselves greater troubles, for their mistress, no longer hearing the hour from the cock, woke them up to their work in the middle of the night.
牧童和狼 The Shepherd’s Boy and the Wolf
有个牧童在村子附近照看一群羊。他大喊“狼来了！狼来了！”把村民们引了出来，而当他的邻居们过来帮忙时，他却嘲笑他们的苦心，这样来来回回发生了三四次。可最后，狼真的来了。牧童这次真慌了，痛苦地大叫道：“求求你们啦，来帮帮我吧，狼在吃羊啊”，但是没人理会他的呼救，也没人给他任何帮助。狼没有理由害怕，便不慌不忙地把所有的羊都撕烂吃掉了。 A Shepherd-boy, who watched a flock of sheep near a village, brought out the villagers three or four times by crying out, “Wolf! Wolf!” and when his neighbors came to help him, laughed at them for their pains.The Wolf, however, did truly come at last.The Shepherd boy, now really alarmed, shouted in an agony of terror: “Pray, do come and help me; the Wolf is killing the sheep”; but no one paid any heed to his cries, nor rendered any assistance.The Wolf, having no cause of fear, at his leisure lacerated or destroyed the whole flock.
一个爱说谎的人即便说真话也不会有人相信。 There is no believing a liar, even when he speaks the truth.
猫和鸟儿们 The Cat and the Birds
一只猫听说有一间鸟舍里的鸟儿们生病了，于是就把自己乔装打扮成医生，还带上了自己的手杖和一袋子的工具去拜访鸟儿们。猫敲了敲门，询问鸟舍里的鸟儿们是否都好，还说如果他们生病了，他很乐意给他们开处方，给他们治病。鸟儿们回答道：“我们都很好，只要你行行好离开这儿，让我们像现在这样呆着，我们就会一直这样好下去。” A Cat, hearing that the Birds in a certain aviary, were ailing dressed himself up as a physician, and, taking his cane and a bag of instruments becoming his profession, went to call on them.He knocked at the door and inquired of the inmates how they all did, saying that if they were ill, he would be happy to prescribe for them and cure them.They replied, “We are all very well, and shall continue so, if you will only be good enough to go away, and leave us as we are.”
小孩与狼 The Kid and the Wolf
一个小孩站在屋顶上的安全地带，看见一只狼经过，便立即开始嘲弄和辱骂起狼来。狼抬头向上看，说道：“小子！我听见你说的话了，但是嘲讽我的并不是你，而是你站着的屋顶。” A Kid standing on the roof of a house, out of harm’s way, saw a Wolf passing by and immediately began to taunt and revile him.The Wolf, looking up, said, “Sirrah!I hear thee: yet it is not thou who mockest me, but the roof on which thou art standing.”
时间与地点经常可以让弱者比强者更具有优势。 Time and place often give the advantage to the weak over the strong.
公牛与青蛙 The Ox and the Frog
一头公牛在水塘边喝水，踩到一群小青蛙，并把其中一只踩死了。青蛙妈妈跳了过来，没找到她的这个孩子，便询问其他小青蛙发生了什么事。“他死了，亲爱的妈妈；他被刚才到水塘边的一头非常巨大的长着四只大脚的野兽用偶蹄踩死了。”青蛙妈妈使劲地鼓气，胀大肚子，问道：“巨兽有没有这么大。”“停下来吧，妈妈，不要鼓气了，”一只小青蛙说道，“也不要再生气了；因为我确信，就算您胀破了肚皮，也模仿不出巨兽那巨大的样子。” An Ox drinking at a pool trod on a brood of young frogs and crushed one of them to death.The Mother coming up, and missing one of her sons, inquired of his brothers what had become of him.“He is dead, dear Mother; for just now a very huge beast with four great feet came to the pool and crushed him to death with his cloven heel.”The Frog, puffing herself out, inquired, “if the beast was as big as that in size.”“Cease, Mother, to puff yourself out,” said her son, “and do not be angry; for you would, I assure you, sooner burst than successfully imitate the hugeness of that monster.”
牧羊人和狼 The Shepherd and the Wolf
从前，有个牧羊人发现了一只小狼崽，并把它喂养长大。过了不久，牧羊人便教狼偷附近羊群里的羊羔。狼学偷羊学得很好，便对牧羊人说：“既然你教会了我偷羊，你就必须小心提防，否则你也会失去你羊群中的一些羊。” A Shepherd once found the whelp of a Wolf and brought it up, and after a while taught it to steal lambs from the neighboring flocks.The Wolf, having shown himself an apt pupil, said to the Shepherd, “Since you have taught me to steal, you must keep a sharp lookout, or you will lose some of your own flock.”
父亲和他的两个女儿 The Father and His Two Daughters
一个男人有两个女儿，一个嫁给了一名花匠，另一个嫁给了一名瓦匠。过了一段时间，他去探望嫁给花匠的女儿，并询问她好不好，一切是否都顺利。她说：“一切都很顺利，我只有一个愿望，那就是能下场大雨。那样的话，植物们就能很好地被灌溉了。”没过多久，父亲去探望嫁给瓦匠的女儿。同样地，他询问她过得怎样。她回答道：“我什么都不奢望，只有一个愿望，那就是干燥的天气能一直持续下去，并且阳光能炽热、灿烂，那样的话，砖块就可以被晒干了。”父亲对她说：“如果你的姐姐希望下雨，而你希望天气干燥，那么我是该祈求下雨还是不下雨呢？” A man had two daughters, the one married to a gardener, and the other to a tile maker.After a time he went to the daughter who had married the gardener, and inquired how she was and how all things went with her. She said, “All things are prospering with me, and I have only one wish, that there may be a heavy fall of rain, in order that the plants may be well watered.”Not long after, he went to the daughter who had married the tile maker, and likewise inquired of her how she fared; she replied, “I want for nothing, and have only one wish, that the dry weather may continue, and the sun shine hot and bright, so that the bricks might be dried.”He said to her, “If your sister wishes for rain, and you for dry weather, with which of the two am I to join my wishes?”
农夫和他的儿子们 The Farmer and His Sons
一位父亲在临死之际，想要确保他的儿子们也能像他一样重视自己的农场。于是，他把他们叫到自己的病榻前，说道：“我的儿子们啊，在我的其中一个葡萄园里藏有一大笔宝藏。”父亲死后，儿子们便拿起铁锹和鹤嘴锄，认真地刨遍了农场的每一块土地。他们没找到什么宝藏，但是丰硕的收成回报了他们的劳动。 A Father, being on the point of death, wished to be sure that his sons would give the same attention to his farm as he himself had given it.He called them to his bedside and said, “My sons, there is a great treasure hid in one of my vineyards.”The sons, after his death, took their spades and mattocks and carefully dug over every portion of their land.They found no treasure, but the vines repaid their labor by an extraordinary and superabundant crop.
小螃蟹和它的妈妈 The Crab and Its Mother
螃蟹妈妈对儿子说：“我的孩子，为什么你要这么横着走呢？直着走要好看多了。”小螃蟹答道：“一点儿没错，亲爱的妈妈。如果您能教我如何直着走的话，我一定这么走。”螃蟹妈妈试着直着走，但没有成功。于是，她没有再提出异议，接受了孩子的指摘。 A Crab said to her son, “Why do you walk so one-sided, my child? It is far more becoming to go straight forward.”The young Crab replied: “Quite true, dear Mother; and if you will show me the straight way, I will promise to walk in it.”The Mother tried in vain, and submitted without remonstrance to the reproof of her child.
身教胜于言传。 Example is more powerful than precept.
母牛和公牛 The Heifer and the Ox
一头母牛看到一头公牛被套在犁上辛苦地干活，便贬损他不得不劳动的悲惨命运，让公牛很烦恼。不久之后，到了收获感恩节，主人卸去了公牛的轭，却用绳索绑住了母牛，把它领往祭坛，准备杀了它，以庆祝这一盛事。公牛看着这一切，微笑着对母牛说：“之前你可以闲着不干活，原来是因为你很快就要被献祭啊。” A Heifer saw an Ox hard at work harnessed to a plow, and tormented him with reflections on his unhappy fate in being compelled to labor.Shortly afterwards, at the harvest festival, the owner released the Ox from his yoke, but bound the Heifer with cords and led her away to the altar to be slain in honor of the occasion. The Ox saw what was being done, and said with a smile to the Heifer: “For this you were allowed to live in idleness, because you were presently to be sacrificed.”
燕子、蛇和法院 The Swallow, the Serpent, and the Court of Justice
有一只从国外回来的燕子，它特别喜欢跟人类住在一起，于是便在法院的墙上筑了一个巢，并在那儿孵了七只小燕子。蛇从墙上的洞里溜进了鸟巢，吃光了这些羽翼未丰的雏鸟。燕子发现巢里空空如也，伤心不已，大叫道：“是谁使我如此不幸！在这种地方，其他人的权利都得到了保护，却只有我遭此横祸。” A Swallow, returning from abroad and especially fond of dwelling with men, built herself a nest in the wall of a Court of Justice and there hatched seven young birds.A Serpent gliding past the nest from its hole in the wall ate up the young unfledged nestlings.The Swallow, finding her nest empty, lamented greatly and exclaimed: “Woe to me a stranger! that in this place where all others’ rights are protected, I alone should suffer wrong.”
小偷和他的母亲 The Thief and His Mother
一个小男孩偷了同学一本课本，把它带回了家，交给了他的母亲。母亲不仅没有打他，还鼓励他这么做。第二次，男孩偷了一件斗篷带回来给母亲，她又表扬了他。小男孩长大成人了。他继续偷更为贵重的东西。最后，他在偷窃时被当场抓获，于是被双手绑在身后押赴了刑场。他的母亲跟在人群之中，伤心地捶胸顿足。于是，这位年轻人说道：“我想在我母亲耳边说几句话。”母亲走近他时，他立刻用牙咬住她的耳朵，并且给咬掉了。母亲大骂他是个不孝子，他却反驳道：“哼！如果我第一次偷那本教科书时你打了我，我也不会落得今天这个下场，死得如此不光彩。” A boy stole a lesson book from one of his schoolfellows and took it home to his Mother.She not only abstained from beating him, but encouraged him.He next time stole a cloak and brought it to her, and she again commended him.The Youth, advanced to adulthood, proceeded to steal things of still greater value.At last he was caught in the very act, and having his hands bound behind him, was led away to the place of public execution.His Mother followed in the crowd and violently beat her breast in sorrow, whereupon the young man said, “I wish to say something to my Mother in her ear.”She came close to him, and he quickly seized her ear with his teeth and bit it off.The Mother upbraided him as an unnatural child, whereon he replied, “Ah! if you had beaten me when I first stole and brought to you that lesson book, I should not have come to this, nor have been thus led to a disgraceful death.”
老人和死神 The Old Man and Death
有一个老人，他以砍伐林木为生。一天，他扛着木柴去城里卖，漫长的路程使他疲惫不堪。他坐在路边，扔下担子，祈求着“死神”降临。“死神”回应了他的召唤，立即出现了，并问老人为什么叫他。老人忙回答道：“呃，请您把担子举起来，再放回到我肩膀上吧。” An Old man was employed in cutting wood in the forest, and, in carrying the faggots to the city for sale one day, became very wearied with his long journey.He sat down by the wayside, and throwing down his load, besought “Death” to come.“Death” immediately appeared in answer to his summons and asked for what reason he had called him.The Old Man hurriedly replied, “That, lifting up the load, you may place it again upon my shoulders.”
杉树与荆棘 The Fir Tree and the Bramble
杉树对荆棘炫耀道：“你一点儿用处都没有，而屋顶和房屋却处处都要用上我。”荆棘答道：“可怜的东西，光想想那些将要把你砍倒的斧头和锯子，你就会庆幸自己是荆棘而不是杉树了。” A Fir-Tree said boastingly to the Bramble, “You are useful for nothing at all; while I am everywhere used for roofs and houses.”The Bramble answered: “You poor creature, if you would only call to mind the axes and saws which are about to hew you down, you would have reason to wish that you had grown up a Bramble, not a Fir-Tree.”
穷而无忧胜于富而有虑。 Better poverty without care, than riches with.
老鼠、青蛙和老鹰 The Mouse, the Frog, and the Hawk
老鼠一直在陆地上生活，而青蛙大部分时间生活在水里。不幸的是，他们凑巧成了亲密的朋友。有一天，青蛙一心要搞个恶作剧，便把老鼠的腿和自己的腿紧紧地系在了一起。系好之后，起先，青蛙带他的老鼠朋友去了他们经常觅食的草地上。然后，青蛙渐渐地把老鼠领到了他住的池塘。等走到池塘边时，青蛙突然跳入水中，将老鼠一并拽了下去。青蛙在水中极为享受，边四处畅游，边呱呱大叫，就像他做了一件好事似的。而不幸的老鼠不一会儿就被水淹死了，尸体浮到了水面上，但仍然和青蛙的腿系在一起。一只老鹰看到了这一幕，便用爪子一把抓住老鼠，带着飞上了天。而青蛙因为还系在老鼠的腿上，也被老鹰作为猎物抓去吃掉了。 A Mouse who always lived on the land, by an unlucky chance formed an intimate acquaintance with a Frog, who lived for the most part in the water.The Frog, one day intent on mischief, bound the foot of the Mouse tightly to his own.Thus joined together, the Frog first of all led his friend the Mouse to the meadow where they were accustomed to find their food.After this, he gradually led him towards the pool in which he lived, until reaching the very brink, he suddenly jumped in, dragging the Mouse with him.The Frog enjoyed the water amazingly, and swam croaking about, as if he had done a good deed.The unhappy Mouse was soon suffocated by the water, and his dead body floated about on the surface, tied to the foot of the Frog.A Hawk observed it, and, pouncing upon it with his talons, carried it aloft.The Frog, being still fastened to the leg of the Mouse, was also carried off as a prisoner, and was eaten by the Hawk.
害人反害己。 Harm hatch, harm catch.
被狗咬了的人 The Man Bitten by a Dog
一个人被狗咬了，四处寻找可以治愈他的人。一个朋友遇见他，得知他的需求之后说道：“如果你想痊愈，就拿一片面包，蘸一点儿你伤口上的血，去喂咬你的那条狗吧。”被狗咬了的人对这个建议嗤之以鼻，说道：“为什么？如果我这么做的话，就像是在乞求镇上的每条狗都来咬我一样。” A Man who had been bitten by a Dog went about in quest of someone who might heal him.A friend, meeting him and learning what he wanted, said, “If you would be cured, take a piece of bread, and dip it in the blood from your wound, and go and give it to the Dog that bit you.”The Man who had been bitten laughed at this advice and said, “Why? If I should do so, it would be as if I should beg every Dog in the town to bite me.”
对作恶之人施善会让他们对你的伤害变本加厉。 Benefits bestowed upon the evil-disposed increase their means of injuring you.
两只罐子 The Two Pots
从小河的上游漂下来两只罐子，一只是用陶瓷做的，一只是用黄铜做的。陶罐子对铜罐子说：“请和我保持距离，不要靠近我。因为即使你非常轻地碰我一下，我也会变成碎片的。更何况，我也根本不想靠近你。” A river carried down in its stream two Pots, one made of earthenware and the other of brass.The Earthen Pot said to the Brass Pot, “Pray keep at a distance and do not come near me, for if you touch me ever so slightly, I shall be broken in pieces, and besides, I by no means wish to come near you.”
彼此平等才能做好朋友。 Equals make the best friends.
狼和羊 The Wolf and the Sheep
一匹狼被猎狗咬伤了，伤势极其严重。他拖着伤残的身体，病怏怏地躺在自己的窝里。因为需要吃东西，狼喊住了正路过的一只羊，问羊能不能去附近的那条小溪里取点水来。“因为，”狼说，“如果你弄点水给我喝，我就能想办法自己去找肉吃了。”“是呀，”羊说，“如果我把水送来给你喝，你肯定会吃了我的肉。” A Wolf, sorely wounded and bitten by dogs, lay sick and maimed in his lair.Being in want of food, he called to a Sheep who was passing, and asked him to fetch some water from a stream flowing close beside him.“For,” he said, “if you will bring me drink, I will find means to provide myself with meat.”“Yes,” said the Sheep, “if I should bring you the draught, you would doubtless make me provide the meat also.”
伪善的谎言是很容易被看穿的。 Hypocritical speeches are easily seen through.
黑仆人 The Aethiop
有个人听说一名黑仆人的肤色之所以黑，是因为以前的主人们忽视他们，把他们弄得满身污垢。他信以为真，就把黑仆人买了下来。一回到家，他就用各种清洁方法不停地给仆人擦洗。仆人患了重感冒，但肤色和脸色却没有丝毫改变。 The purchaser of a black servant was persuaded that the color of his skin arose from dirt contracted through the neglect of his former masters.On bringing him home he resorted to every means of cleaning, and subjected the man to incessant scrubbings.The servant caught a severe cold, but he never changed his color or complexion.
江山易改，本性难移。 What’s bred in the bone will stick to the flesh.
渔夫和渔网 The Fisherman and His Nets
渔夫正在捕鱼，他非常成功地撒了一次网，捕到了一大网鱼。渔夫技艺高超，用渔网逮住了所有的大鱼，并拖上了岸，但他却无法阻止小鱼从网眼中又漏回到海里去。 A Fisherman, engaged in his calling, made a very successful cast and captured a great haul of fish.He managed by a skillful handling of his net to retain all the large fish and to draw them to the shore; but he could not prevent the smaller fish from falling back through the meshes of the net into the sea.
渔夫和猎人 The Huntsman and the Fisherman

河流们联合起来向海抱怨说：“为什么汇入你的潮水中时，我们的水如此甘甜可口，而你却改变了我们，让我们变得咸而不可饮用了呢？”海觉察到这些河流是有意来责难他的，便说：“那你们就祈祷别再流到我这里来，这样，我就不会把你们变咸了。” The rivers joined together to complain to the Sea, saying, “Why is it that when we flow into your tides so potable and sweet, you work in us such a change, and make us salty and unfit to drink?” The Sea, perceiving that they intended to throw the blame on him, said, “Pray cease to flow into me, and then you will not be made briny.”
顽皮的驴 The Playful Ass
一头驴爬到房顶上，在那儿又蹦又跳，把上面的砖瓦踩得粉碎。他的主人追到房顶上，很快将它赶了下来，并用粗木棍狠狠地打了它一顿。驴子说：“为什么呀？我昨天看到猴子也这么做了，你们都开怀大笑，好像他的表演给你们带来了无穷的乐趣。” An ass climbed up to the roof of a building, and frisking about there, broke in the tiling.The owner went up after him and quickly drove him down, beating him severely with a thick wooden cudgel.The Ass said, “Why, I saw the Monkey do this very thing yesterday, and you all laughed heartily, as if it afforded you very great amusement.”
三个手艺人 The Three Tradesmen
一座很大的城池被包围了，城中居民被召集在一起，来商议最好的抗敌方法。一个砌砖工迫不及待地推荐说，砖块是有效护城的最好材料。一个木匠也同样热忱地建议道，用木材防御敌人要更可取一些。这时，一个皮匠站起来说道：“先生们，我的想法跟你们的都不一样：在抵御材料上，没有什么能跟兽皮相比，也没有什么跟皮革一样好了。” A great city was besieged, and its inhabitants were called together to consider the best means of protecting it from the enemy.A Bricklayer earnestly recommended bricks as affording the best material for an effective resistance.A Carpenter, with equal enthusiasm, proposed timber as a preferable method of defense.Upon which a Currier stood up and said, “Sirs, I differ from you altogether: there is no material for resistance equal to a covering of hides; and nothing so good as leather.”
每个人都认为自己的是最好的。 Every man for himself.
主人和狗 The Master and His Dogs
一个人被暴风雨困在了他乡下的房子中，为了维持生活，他先杀了自己的绵羊，然后又杀了山羊。暴风雨还在继续，他被迫屠宰了一对公牛，以获得食物。看到这些后，他的狗聚在一起商量道：“该是我们离开的时候了，主人连辛勤劳作、给他带来财富的耕牛都宰了，我们又怎能奢望他不宰我们呢？” A certain man, detained by a storm in his country house, first of all killed his sheep, and then his goats, for the maintenance of his household.The storm still continuing, he was obliged to slaughter his yoke oxen for food.On seeing this, his Dogs took counsel together, and said, “It is time for us to be off, for if the master spare not his oxen, who work for his gain, how can we expect him to spare us?”
不善待自己的家人的人，也不是一个值得信任的朋友。 He is not to be trusted as a friend who mistreats his own family.
狼和牧羊人 The Wolf and the Shepherds
一只过路的狼看见几个牧羊人正在棚舍里用餐，他们在啃羊腿。狼走近他们，说道：“如果我也啃羊腿，你们早该大呼小叫了！” A wolf, passing by, saw some Shepherds in a hut eating a haunch of mutton for their dinner.Approaching them, he said, “What a clamor you would raise if I were to do as you are doing!”
海豚、鲸和西鲱鱼 The Dolphins, the Whales, and the Sprat
一群海豚和一群鲸展开了激烈的交战。当他们的争斗进入白热化时，一只西鲱鱼从海浪中伸出头来说，如果海豚和鲸愿意让他当裁判，他就可以调和他们之间的矛盾。其中一只海豚回答说：“我们宁可打得两败俱伤，也不愿让你干涉我们的事情。” The Dolphins and Whales waged a fierce war with each other.When the battle was at its height, a Sprat lifted its head out of the waves and said that he would reconcile their differences if they would accept him as an umpire.One of the Dolphins replied, “We would far rather be destroyed in our battle with each other than admit any interference from you in our affairs.”
运神像的驴子 The Ass Carrying the Image
从前，一头驴子驮着一尊有名的木质神像在城市的街道中穿梭，这尊神像要被放置在城中的一座寺庙里。驴子所到之处，人们都对神像顶礼膜拜。然而，驴子却以为人们鞠躬是向它致敬，便毛发直立，得意起来，它趾高气扬，一步也不肯往前走。赶驴的人见驴子就这样停下来不走了，便狠狠地在驴背上抽了几鞭，并斥责道：“唉，你这又倔又蠢的驴子，人们还不至于崇拜一头驴。” The Ass once carried through the streets of a city a famous wooden Image, to be placed in one of its Temples.As he passed along, the crowd made lowly prostration before the Image.The Ass, thinking that they bowed their heads in token of respect for himself, bristled up with pride, gave himself airs, and refused to move another step.The driver, seeing him thus stop, laid his whip lustily about his shoulders and said, “O you perverse dull head! It is not yet come to this, that men pay worship to an Ass.”
那些把别人的荣誉放在自己身上的人真是太不明智了。 They are not wise who give to themselves the credit due to others.
两个旅行者和斧子 The Two Travelers and the Axe
有两个人一起赶路。其中一个人在小路上捡到了一把斧子，他对另一个人说：“我捡到了一把斧子。”“不，朋友，”另一个人说道，“不要说‘我’捡到了，而应该说‘我们’捡到了一把斧子。”他们走了没多远，就看到那把斧子的主人在追赶他们，捡到斧子的那个人说道：“我们完了。”“不，”另一个人回答道，“你还是保持你最开始的说话方式吧，我的朋友，你先前是怎么想的，现在就怎么想。所以，你要说‘我，而不是‘我们’完了。” Two men were journeying together. One of them picked up an axe that lay upon the path, and said, “I have found an axe.”“Nay, my friend,” replied the other, “do not say ‘I,’ but ‘We’ have found an axe.”They had not gone far before they saw the owner of the axe pursuing them, and he who had picked up the axe said, “We are undone.”“Nay,” replied the other, “keep to your first mode of speech, my friend; what you thought right then, think right now.Say ‘I,’ not ‘We’ are undone.”
有难同担者，才能有福同享。 He who shares the danger ought to share the prize.
老狮子 The Old Lion
一头年老体衰的狮子病得有气无力，躺在地上，奄奄一息。一头野猪冲到狮子跟前，用长牙顶狮子，以此来报复他。狮子曾给他造成的伤害，他久久不能忘怀。过了一小会儿，一头公牛也用牛角来顶狮子，好像狮子是他的敌人一样。驴子看到野猪和公牛攻击了这头庞大的狮子却没安然无恙，于是，他也用脚后跟踢狮子的头部。这头快要断气的狮子说：“勇敢的动物侮辱我，我还能勉强忍受，但你是大自然的耻辱，竟也这样对待我，真是让我死两次啊。” A lion, worn out with years and powerless from disease, lay on the ground at the point of death.A Boar rushed upon him, and avenged with a stroke of his tusks a long remembered injury.Shortly afterwards the Bull with his horns gored him as if he were an enemy. When the Ass saw that the huge beast could be assailed with impunity, he let drive at his forehead with his heels.The expiring Lion said, “I have reluctantly brooked the insults of the brave, but to be compelled to endure such treatment from thee, a disgrace to Nature, is indeed to die a double death.”
老猎狗 The Old Hound
有一条老猎狗，年轻力壮之时从未向森林中的任何野兽屈服过。年老后，在一次狩猎中，它遇到了一头野猪。它勇敢地扑上去，咬住了野猪的耳朵，但由于它的牙齿已老化，它无法一直咬着，结果，野猪逃脱了。很快，它的主人赶了上来，对此大失所望，还狠狠地责骂了他一顿。老猎狗抬起头来，说道：“主人啊，这不是我的错。我的精神头跟年轻时一样好，可我却对衰老无能为力啊。你应该表扬我年轻时的勇敢行为，但却不能责备我现在的衰老无力啊！” A hound, who in the days of his youth and strength had never yielded to any beast of the forest, encountered in his old age a boar in the chase.He seized him boldly by the ear, but could not retain his hold because of the decay of his teeth, so that the boar escaped.His master, quickly coming up, was very much disappointed, and fiercely abused the dog.The Hound looked up and said, “It was not my fault. master: my spirit was as good as ever, but I could not help my infirmities.I rather deserve to be praised for what I have been, than to be blamed for what I am.”
蜜蜂和朱庇特 The Bee and Jupiter
一只来自海美塔斯山的蜜蜂飞上了奥林匹斯山。她是蜂巢中的蜂后，打算将蜂巢中的一些新鲜蜂蜜献给朱庇特。朱庇特收到了蜂蜜非常高兴，他承诺蜂后将满足她的任何请求。于是，蜂后恳求道：“我恳求您，赐给我一根刺吧，如果有人类想要过来偷我的蜂蜜，我便可以刺死他。”朱庇特非常不悦，因为他爱人类，但由于他许下的诺言，他又不能拒绝这个请求。于是他回复蜜蜂道：“我可以答应你的请求，但那要以你自己的生命为代价。因为如果你使用了你的刺，它就会留在你造成的伤口中，然后你会因为失去它而死去。” A bee from Mount Hymettus, the queen of the hive, ascended to Olympus to present Jupiter some honey fresh from her combs.Jupiter, delighted with the offering of honey, promised to give whatever she should ask.She therefore besought him, saying, “Give me, I pray thee, a sting, that if any mortal shall approach to take my honey, I may kill him.”Jupiter was much displeased, for he loved the race of man, but could not refuse the request because of his promise.He thus answered the Bee: “You shall have your request, but it will be at the peril of your own life.For if you use your sting, it shall remain in the wound you make, and then you will die from the loss of it.”
有邪恶愿望的人终将自食恶果。 Evil wishes, like chickens, come home to roost.
挤牛奶的姑娘和她的奶桶 The Milk-Woman and Her Pail
一位农夫的女儿提着一桶牛奶走在从牧场回农舍的路上，当时她满心欢喜。“将来卖牛奶的钱至少可以买三百个鸡蛋。这些鸡蛋，就算有不小心破掉的，也还可以孵出两百五十只小鸡来。当家禽的价格达到最高峰时，那些小鸡也正好可以拿到市场上卖了，如此一来，到今年年底，我所得的那份钱就足够买一件新礼服了。我将穿着这件裙子参加圣诞舞会，所有的年轻小伙子都会邀请我跳舞，但我会高昂着头将他们一一拒绝。”就在此刻，她真的随着自己的幻想昂起了头，彼时奶桶翻倒在地，顷刻间她想象中的所有计划都破灭了。 A farmer’s daughter was carrying her pail of milk from the field to the farmhouse, when she fell amusing.“The money for which this milk will be sold, will buy at least three hundred eggs.The eggs, allowing for all mishaps, will produce two hundred and fifty chickens.The chickens will become ready for the market when poultry will fetch the highest price, so that by the end of the year I shall have money enough from my share to buy a new gown.In this dress I will go to the Christmas parties, where all the young fellows will propose to me, but I will toss my head and refuse them every one.”At this moment she tossed her head in unison with her thoughts, when down fell the milk pail to the ground, and all her imaginary schemes perished in a moment.
海滨旅行者 The Seaside Travelers
一些沿着海岸旅行的游客，登上了高高的悬崖顶端，在那里俯瞰大海，他们看见远处有一艘好像是巨轮的物体在行驶。他们满怀希望地等待着看它驶入海港，但当他们观看的那个物体迎着风越来越靠近海岸时，他们才发现它充其量只能算是只小舟，而不是什么大船。然而当它到达海滩时，他们才发现它只是一大捆树枝而已，于是其中一人对他的同伴们说：“我们的等待毫无意义，因为我们最终看到的只不过是一堆木头。” Some travelers, journeying along the seashore, climbed to the summit of a tall cliff, and looking over the sea, saw in the distance what they thought was a large ship.They waited in the hope of seeing it enter the harbor, but as the object on which they looked was driven nearer to shore by the wind, they found that it could at the most be a small boat, and not a ship.When however it reached the beach, they discovered that it was only a large faggot of sticks, and one of them said to his companions, “We have waited for no purpose, for after all there is nothing to see but a load of wood.”
我们对生活的纯粹期待往往超出了现实。 Our mere anticipations of life outrun its realities.
铜匠和他的狗 The Brazier and His Dog
铜匠有一只小狗，它是主人的最爱，一直以来陪伴着铜匠。每当铜匠埋头敲打他的铜器时，狗就会在一旁睡觉；而当他去吃饭时，小狗就会醒来，摇摇尾巴，好像在索要它的那份食物一样。一天，小狗的主人佯装生气，在它面前挥动着棍子，说道：“你这个讨厌的小懒虫！我该拿你怎么办呢？我炼铜时，你就在垫子上睡觉；我辛苦工作完准备吃饭了，你就醒来摇着尾巴要东西吃。难道你不知道劳动是一切恩惠的源泉，只有那些劳动的人才有权利吃饭吗？” A brazier had a little dog, which was a great favorite with his master, and his constant companion.While he hammered away at his metals the Dog slept; but when, on the other hand, he went to dinner and began to eat, the Dog woke up and wagged his tail, as if he would ask for a share of his meal.His master one day, pretending to be angry and shaking his stick at him, said, “You wretched little sluggard!what shall I do to you?While I am hammering on the anvil, you sleep on the mat; and when I begin to eat after my toil, you wake up and wag your tail for food.Do you not know that larbor is the source of every blessing, and that none but those who work are entitled to eat?”
驴子和它的影子 The Ass and His Shadow
一个旅行者雇了一头驴载他到一个遥远的地方。这天的天气十分炎热，太阳使劲地照射着。旅行者停下来休息，跑到了驴子的影子下面躲避太阳。由于影子里只能容得下一个人，而旅行者和驴子的主人都想要进去躲着，于是他们之间爆发了一场激烈的争吵，争论到底谁有权利占用这个影子。驴子主人坚持认为他出租的只是驴子本身，并不是它的影子。旅行者则宣称在他雇用驴子的同时，他也雇用了它的影子。争辩从口角发展到了厮打，然而就在他们打斗时，驴子疾驰而去。 A traveler hired an Ass to convey him to a distant place.The day being intensely hot, and the sun shining in its strength, the Traveler stopped to rest, and sought shelter from the heat under the Shadow of the Ass.As this afforded only protection for one, and as the Traveler and the owner of the Ass both claimed it, a violent dispute arose between them as to which of them had the right to the Shadow.The owner maintained that he had let the Ass only, and not his Shadow.The Traveler asserted that he had, with the hire of the Ass, hired his Shadow also.The quarrel proceeded from words to blows, and while the men fought, the Ass galloped off.
在为影子争吵的时候，我们往往失去了事物本身。 In quarreling about the shadow we often lose the substance.
驴子和它的主人 The Ass and His Masters
有一头驴，它的主人是一位卖草药的，主人总是给它很少的食物却让它做繁重的工作，于是它恳求朱庇特帮它解除目前的服役，给它换一个主人。在告诫了驴子可能会为它的请求感到后悔之后，朱庇特让它卖给了一个瓦匠。不久之后，它发现它要承载的负担更重了，砖厂的工作也更加艰苦，于是，它乞求再换一个主人。朱庇特告诉它这会是他最后一次答应它的请求，然后下令把它卖给了一个制革工。驴子发现它陷入了更糟糕的境地，想着这位主人的职业，他叹息道：“我还不如跟着之前第一个主人挨饿，或跟着之前第二个主人做苦工，这些都比被卖给现在的主人好，因为我死后他将会把我的皮做成皮革，让我为他所用。” An ass, belonging to an herb-seller who gave him too little food and too much work made a petition to Jupiter to be released from his present service and provided with another master.Jupiter, after warning him that he would repent his request, caused him to be sold to a tile-maker.Shortly afterwards, finding that he had heavier loads to carry and harder work in the brickfield, he petitioned for another change of master. Jupiter, telling him that it would be the last time that he could grant his request, ordained that he be sold to a tanner. The Ass found that he had fallen into worse hands, and noting his master’s occupation, said, groaning: “It would have been better for me to have been either starved by the one, or to have been overworked by the other of my former masters, than to have been bought by my present owner, who will even after I am dead tan my hide, and make me useful to him.”
橡树和芦苇 The Oak and the Reeds
一棵巨大的橡树被风连根拔起,并被抛到了溪流的另一边。它落到了一片芦苇丛中，于是对芦苇说道：“我很好奇，你们又轻又弱，怎么没有被那些大风彻底碾碎？”它们回答道：“你与风正面对抗，结果就是你被摧毁；相反地，我们在一点微风面前就低头，因此能够免受伤害，从而幸免于难。” A very large oak was uprooted by the wind and thrown across a stream.It fell among some Reeds, which it thus addressed: “I wonder how you, who are so light and weak, are not entirely crushed by these strong winds.”They replied, “You fight and contend with the wind, and consequently you are destroyed; while we on the contrary bend before the least breath of air, and therefore remain unbroken, and escape.”
忍辱负重方能取胜。 Stoop to conquer.
渔夫和小鱼 The Fisherman and the Little Fish
有个渔夫以捕鱼为生，有一天，他捕捞了一整天只捕到了一条小鱼。小鱼抽搐地喘息着，乞求渔夫能放它一条生路：“哦，先生，我对您有什么好处呢？我才有多大点儿的价值？我甚至还没有长大呢。求您饶我一命，把我放回海里吧。我很快就可以长成大鱼，可以上有钱人的餐桌了，那时您再来捉我，就能从我身上赚一大笔钱了。”渔夫回答道：“如果为了可能会得到的更大的不确定利益而放弃了眼前必然的所得，那我一定是个头脑简单的人。” A fishman who lived on the produce of his nets, one day caught a single small Fish as the result of his day’s labor.The Fish, panting convulsively, thus entreated for his life: “O Sir, what good can I be to you, and how little am I worth?I am not yet come to my full size.Pray spare my life, and put me back into the sea.I shall soon become a large fish fit for the tables of the rich, and then you can catch me again, and make a handsome profit of me.”The Fisherman replied, “I should indeed be a very simple fellow if, for the chance of a greater uncertain profit, I were to forego my present certain gain.”
猎人和樵夫 The Hunter and the Woodman
一位并不是很勇敢的猎人，一直在寻找一只狮子的踪迹。他向一位正在森林里砍伐橡树的樵夫打听，问他是否看到过狮子的任何足迹，或者是否知道它巢穴的位置。“我马上告诉你狮子在哪儿。”樵夫说。猎人的脸色立即变得非常苍白，牙齿也因为恐惧而打颤，他回答道：“不用了，谢谢。我没有问你那个，我没想找狮子，只想寻找它的踪迹而已。” A hunter, not very bold, was searching for the tracks of a Lion.He asked a man felling oaks in the forest if he had seen any marks of his footsteps or knew where his lair was.“I will,” said the man, “at once show you the Lion himself.”The Hunter, turning very pale and chattering with his teeth from fear, replied, “No, thank you.I did not ask that; it is his track only I am in search of, not the Lion himself.”
英雄的勇敢不只表现在言语上，更应该表现在行动上。 The hero is brave in deeds as well as words.
野猪和狐狸 The Wild Boar and the Fox
一头野猪站在树下，并不断在树干上磨着他的獠牙。一只狐狸走过，问他为什么在没有猎人或猎犬威胁的情况下还将牙磨得那样锋利。他回答道：“我是经过深思熟虑后才这么做的，因为如果等到我需要使用它们的时候，再去磨利我的武器，那样就会来不及了。” A wild boar stood under a tree and rubbed his tusks against the trunk.A Fox passing by asked him why he thus sharpened his teeth when there was no danger threatening from either huntsman or hound.He replied, “I do it advisedly; for it would never do to have to sharpen my weapons just at the time I ought to be using them.”
农场里的狮子 The Lion in a Farmyard
有一只狮子闯进了一座农场里。农场主关上大门想要抓住它。当这只狮子发现自己无路可逃时，它便向农场里的绵羊扑去，并将它们杀死，然后又去攻击公牛。这个农场主开始担心自己也会有生命危险，于是就打开大门放走了狮子。狮子走后，农场主对损失的绵羊和公牛感到无比痛心。他的妻子目睹了事情发生的全过程，对他说道：“照我说啊，你这是罪有应得。你明明知道即使是远远地听到狮子的吼叫，你都会吓得两腿直哆嗦，那一刻你怎么会想要把一只狮子和你一起关在自己的农场里呢？” A lion entered a farmyard.The Farmer, wishing to catch him, shut the gate.When the Lion found that he could not escape, he flew upon the sheep and killed them, and then attacked the oxen.The Farmer, beginning to be alarmed for his own safety, opened the gate and released the Lion.On his departure the Farmer grievously lamented the destruction of his sheep and oxen, but his wife, who had been a spectator to all that took place, said, “On my word, you are rightly served, for how could you for a moment think of shutting up a Lion along with you in your farmyard when you know that you shake in your shoes if you only hear his roar at a distance?’
墨丘利和雕刻家 Mercury and the Sculptor
一次，墨丘利决定去了解一下世人对他有多尊敬。为此，他化作一个凡人，并以此装扮来到了一个雕刻家的工作室里，看过各种各样的雕像之后，他问了一下朱庇特和朱诺的雕像的价钱。得知他们的价钱后，他指着自己的那尊雕像对雕刻家说：“你一定对这尊雕像要价更高吧，因为它既是众神的信使，也是你们的财神。”雕刻家回答道：“嗯，如果你买了那两个的话，我就把这个白送给你。” Mercury once determined to learn in what esteem he was held among mortals.For this purpose he assumed the character of a man and visited in this disguise a Sculptor’s studio having looked at various statues, he demanded the price of two figures of Jupiter and Juno.When the sum at which they were valued was named, he pointed to a figure of himself, saying to the Sculptor, “You will certainly want much more for this, as it is the statue of the Messenger of the Gods, and author of all your gain.”The Sculptor replied, “Well, if you will buy these, I’ll fling you that into the bargain.”
天鹅与家鹅 The Swan and the Goose
一个有钱人从市场上买回了一只家鹅和一只天鹅。他养家鹅是为了吃它的肉，而养天鹅是为了听它唱歌。到了该杀家鹅的时候了，厨子夜里来捉家鹅，但由于天太黑了，他根本分不清哪只是天鹅哪只是家鹅。于是厨子错把天鹅当成家鹅捉走了。在这性命攸关的时刻，天鹅突然唱起歌来，用歌声表明了自己的身份，最终它用自己悦耳的声音保全了性命。 A certain rich man bought in the market a Goose and a Swan.He fed the one for his table and kept the other for the sake of its song.When the time came for killing the Goose, the cook went to get him at night, when it was dark, and he was not able to distinguish one bird from the other.By mistake he caught the Swan instead of the Goose.The Swan, threatened with death, burst forth into song and thus made himself known by his voice, and preserved his life by his melody.
肚胀的狐狸 The Swollen Fox
有一只饥肠辘辘的狐狸，看见一棵橡树的树洞里有一些牧羊人留下来的肉和面包，便爬进洞里美美地饱餐了一顿。当它吃完后，才发现自己竟然撑得爬不出来了，于是它开始唉声叹气，为自己的命运悲叹起来。另一只狐狸恰巧从那里经过，听到了它的啼哭，便走过去询问它悲叹的缘由。当它听明白是怎么回事后，便对树洞里的狐狸说：“哎，你就老老实实地呆在里面吧，我的朋友，等你恢复到你钻进去时的样子，自然很容易就能出来了。” A very hungry fox, seeing some bread and meat left by shepherds in the hollow of an oak, crept into the hole and made a hearty meal.When he finished, he was so full that he was not able to get out, and began to groan and lament his fate.Another Fox passing by heard his cries, and coming up, inquired the cause of his complaining.On learning what had happened, he said to him, “Ah, you will have to remain there, my friend, until you become such as you were when you crept in, and then you will easily get out.”
狐狸和樵夫 The Fox and the Woodcutter
一只狐狸被一群猎狗追赶，这时它碰到了一个正在砍伐橡树的樵夫，于是它请求樵夫给自己指一个安全的藏身之处。樵夫建议狐狸去他的小屋里躲着，于是狐狸爬进屋子里找了个角落藏了起来。不一会儿，猎人就带着猎狗追赶了上来，他询问樵夫是否看到过一只狐狸。樵夫大声说他没有看见，然而他说话时却一直用手指着狐狸藏身的小屋。猎人并没有注意到樵夫的手势，而是相信了他说的话，于是匆忙向前追赶去了。猎人刚一走远，狐狸招呼也没打一声就要离开，樵夫叫住了它，并指责道：“你真是个忘恩负义的家伙，我救了你的命，你却连个谢字都没说就要离开。”狐狸回答道：“确实，如果你言行一致的话，我是应该要好好感谢你，可是你的手势背叛了你的言语。” A fox, running before the hounds, came across a Woodcutter felling an oak and begged him to show him a safe hiding place.The Woodcutter advised him to take shelter in his own hut, so the Fox crept in and hid himself in a corner.The huntsman soon came up with his hounds and inquired of the Woodcutter if he had seen the Fox.He declared that he had not seen him, and yet pointed, all the time he was speaking, to the hut where the Fox lay hidden.The huntsman took no notice of the signs, but believing his word, hastened forward in the chase.As soon as they were well away, the Fox departed without taking any notice of the Woodcutter: whereon he called to him and reproached him, saying, “You ungrateful fellow, you owe your life to me, and yet you leave me without a word of thanks.”The Fox replied, “Indeed, I should have thanked you fervently if your deeds had been as good as your words, and if your hands had not been traitors to your speech.”
捕鸟人、山鹑和公鸡 The Bird catcher, the Partridge, and the Cock
一位捕鸟人刚准备坐下来吃晚餐时，他的一位朋友意外前来造访，可他的晚餐全是些素菜。可捕鸟网空空如也，因为他还什么也没有捕到。他只好决定把那只花斑山鹑杀了，那可是他驯养用来诱捕鸟兽的诱饵。这只山鹑诚挚地向捕鸟人恳求道：“没有了我，您下次去捕鸟时可怎么办呢？谁唧唧地鸣唱催您入眠，谁来提醒您鸟儿入网？”于是捕鸟人就饶了它，决定去选一只刚刚长出鸡冠的上好的小公鸡来杀了吃。这只公鸡在它的栖木上可怜巴巴地劝捕鸟人说：“如果您杀了我，以后谁为您清晨打鸣啊？谁每天把您叫醒去工作，谁在早晨提醒您去查看捕鸟网？”捕鸟人回答道：“你说得很对。你是一只很有用处的报时鸟。但是我的朋友和我现在必须要吃晚餐。” A bird catcher was about to sit down to a dinner of herbs when a friend unexpectedly came in.The bird trap was quite empty, as he had caught nothing, and he had to kill a pied Partridge, which he had tamed for a decoy.The bird entreated earnestly for his life: “What would you do without me when next you spread your nets?Who would chirp you to sleep, or call for you the covey of answering birds?”The Bird catcher spared his life, and determined to pick out a fine young Cock just attaining to his comb.But the Cock expostulated in piteous tones from his perch: “If you kill me, who will announce to you the appearance of the dawn?Who will wake you to your daily tasks or tell you when it is time to visit the bird trap in the morning?”He replied, “What you say is true.You are a capital bird at telling the time of day.But my friend and I must have our dinners.”

猴子和渔夫 The Monkey and the Fishermen
一只猴子坐在一棵高高的树上，它看见渔夫们正在往河里撒网，便仔细地观察他们的动作。过了一会儿，渔夫们停止了捕鱼，将网留在河岸上回家吃饭去了。作为模仿能力最强的动物，猴子从树顶上爬了下来，并竭力模仿渔夫们的动作开始捕鱼。它拿起渔网，本想把其扔进河里，但自己却被网缠住，随网一起没入了河里。在它奄奄一息时，它对自己说道：“我真是活该，连网都从没用过，还试着捕什么鱼呢？” A monkey perched upon a lofty tree saw some Fishermen casting their nets into a river, and narrowly watched their proceedings.The Fishermen after a while gave up fishing, and on going home to dinner left their nets upon the bank.The Monkey, who is the most imitative of animals, descended from the treetop and endeavored to do as they had done.Having handled the net, he threw it into the river, but became tangled in the meshes and drowned.With his last breath he said to himself, “I am rightly served; for what business had I who had never handled a net to try and catch fish?”
跳蚤与摔跤运动员 The Flea and the Wrestler
一只跳蚤跳到一名摔跤运动员的赤脚上，咬了他一口，弄得他大声向大力神赫拉克勒斯求助。当跳蚤又一次跳到他的脚上时，他呻吟道：“哦，赫拉克勒斯！如果你连一只跳蚤都不帮我对付，我又怎么指望你去帮我对付更强大的对手呢？” A flea settled upon the bare foot of a Wrestler and bit him, causing the man to call loudly upon Hercules for help.When the Flea a second time hopped upon his foot, he groaned and said, “O Hercules! if you will not help me against a Flea, how can I hope for your assistance against greater antagonists?”
两只青蛙 The Two Frogs
有两只青蛙住在同一个水塘里。当水塘在酷暑的高温下变得枯竭时，他们离开了这里，并开始一起去寻找新的住所。他们在行进过程中碰巧经过一口深井，井里水资源很充足。当他们发现这口井时，其中一只青蛙对另一只说：“咱们跳下去，就把这口井当作咱们的住所吧：它既能遮蔽风雨又能为咱们提供食物。”另一只青蛙却更为谨慎地回答道：“但万一这里的水也干了，我们怎么能从这么深的井里再次爬上来呢？” Two frogs dwelt in the same pool.When the pool dried up under the summer’s heat, they left it and setout together for another home.As they went along they chanced to pass a deep well, amply supplied with water, and when they saw it, one of the Frogs said to the other, “Let us descend and make our abode in this well: it will furnish us with shelter and food.”The other replied with greater caution, “But suppose the water should fail us.How can we get out again from so great a depth?”

猫和老鼠 The Cat and the Mice
有一幢房子，里面老鼠泛滥成灾。一只猫发现了这一情况后，便来到了那幢房子里，开始一只一只地把他们抓起来吃掉。由于害怕丢掉性命，老鼠们都躲在他们的洞里不敢出来。猫无法再抓到老鼠了，于是她意识到，她必须要用个计谋把老鼠们引出来才行。为了达到这一目的，她跳上了一根木桩，把自己吊在上边装死。一只老鼠从洞内向外偷窥，看到她后说道：“啊，我亲爱的女士，即使你变成一个食品袋子，我们也绝不会靠近你的。” A certain house was overrun with Mice.A Cat, discovering this, made her way into it and began to catch and eat them one by one.Fearing for their lives, the Mice kept themselves close in their holes.The Cat was no longer able to get at them and perceived that she must tempt them forth by some device.For this purpose she jumped upon a peg, and suspending herself from it, pretended to be dead.One of the Mice, peeping stealthily out, saw her and said, “Ah, my good madam, even though you should turn into a meal-bag, we will not come near you.”
