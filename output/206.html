纳姆－博克，不诚实的人 Nam-Bok, the Unveracious
作者：杰克·伦敦 by Jack London
“一个海豹皮舟，难道不是吗？看！一个海豹皮舟，还有一个握着一只桨在笨拙地划船的人！” “A bidarka, is it not so?Look! a bidarka, and one man who drives clumsily with a paddle!”
老巴斯克－华－沃恩跪起身，因虚弱和急切而浑身颤抖，凝望着海面。 Old Bask-Wah-Wan rose to her knees, trembling with weakness and eagerness, and gazed out over the sea.
“纳姆－博克划起桨来总是笨手笨脚的。”她回忆地唠叨，一边遮着眼睛免受阳光照射，一边凝视着洒满银灰色的水面，“纳姆－博克总是笨手笨脚的。我记得……” “Nam-Bok was ever clumsy at the paddle,” she maundered reminiscently, shading the sun from her eyes and staring across the silver-spilled water.“Nam-Bok was ever clumsy.I remember....”
但女人和孩子大声地笑了，他们的笑声中带有轻微的嘲笑，她的声音越来越小，后来她的嘴唇只是无声地蠕动着。 But the women and children laughed loudly, and there was a gentle mockery in their laughter, and her voice dwindled till her lips moved without sound.
库加从骨雕中抬起发丝斑白的头，沿着她的视线望去。除了偏离航线时以外，这海豹皮舟正朝着沙滩驶来。船上的人划桨与其说凭的是灵巧，倒不如说靠的就是力气，他沿着阻力最大的曲线划船靠近。库加又低下头去忙活起来，在膝盖间的象牙上他刻了一条鱼的背鳍，像这样的鱼从来没在海里游过。 Koogah lifted his grizzled head from his bone-carving and followed the path of her eyes.Except when wide yaws took it off its course, a bidarka was heading in for the beach.Its occupant was paddling with more strength than dexterity, and made his approach along the zigzag line of most resistance.Koogah’s head dropped to his work again, and on the ivory tusk between his knees he scratched the dorsal fin of a fish the like of which never swam in the sea.
“这无疑是个来自邻村的人，”他最后说道，“来请教我在骨头上打标记的事情。而且这个人笨手笨脚的。他永远都不会知道怎么做。” “It is doubtless the man from the next village,” he said finally, “come to consult with me about the marking of things on bone.And the man is a clumsy man.He will never know how.”
“那是纳姆－博克。”老巴斯克－华－沃恩重复着。“我难道不认识自己的儿子吗？”她尖声反问道，“我说，我再说一遍，那是纳姆－博克。” “It is Nam-Bok,” old Bask-Wah-Wan repeated.“Should I not know my son?” she demanded shrilly.“I say, and I say again, it is Nam-Bok.”
“你都这样说了好几个夏天了。”其中一个女人轻声责备道，“每当海面上的冰雪融化，你就坐着凝望一整天，偶然发现一条独木舟，就会说，‘这是纳姆－博克。’纳姆－博克死了，老巴斯克－华－沃恩，死去的人不会回来了。死去的人是不可能回来的。” “And so thou hast said these many summers,” one of the women chided softly.“Ever when the ice passed out of the sea hast thou sat and watched through the long day, saying at each chance canoe, ‘This is Nam-Bok.’ Nam-Bok is dead, O Bask-Wah-Wan, and the dead do not come back.It cannot be that the dead come back.”
“纳姆－博克！”那个老女妇人哭喊道，声音如此响亮，如此清晰，整个村子的人都震惊了，看着她。 “Nam-Bok!” the old woman cried, so loud and clear that the whole village was startled and looked at her.
她挣扎着站起来，在沙滩上蹒跚前进。她被一个躺着晒太阳的婴儿绊倒了，婴儿哭了起来，婴儿的母亲哄着婴儿让他停止哭泣，并愤慨地咒骂那个老妇人，可她根本没在意。孩子们在她前面沿着海滩跑着，海豹皮舟上的那个人逐渐靠近，一下子没划好桨，方向偏差，几乎翻了船，这会儿，女人们也跟了上来。库加放下他的海象牙，也走了过去，沉重地倚靠在他的拐杖上，跟在他后面的人，三三两两慢悠悠地走着。 She struggled to her feet and tottered down the sand.She stumbled over a baby lying in the sun, and the mother hushed its crying and hurled harsh words after the old woman, who took no notice.The children ran down the beach in advance of her, and as the man in the bidarka drew closer, nearly capsizing with one of his ill-directed strokes, the women followed.Koogah dropped his walrus tusk and went also, leaning heavily upon his staff, and after him loitered the men in twos and threes.
那个海豹皮舟翻向舷侧，海浪的微波几乎要将它沉没，只有一个光着身子的小男孩跑进水里，将船首高高地拉到沙滩上。那个人站起身，用探求的眼光看着那排村民。一件五彩缤纷的运动衫，脏兮兮的，已被穿得破烂不堪，松松垮垮地贴在他宽阔的肩膀上，一条红色棉手帕在他的喉咙处打着水手式的那种平结。他头发短削，戴着一顶渔民的苏格兰式便帽，穿着粗蓝布裤子和笨重的劳动靴，这构成了他的全套装束。 The bidarka turned broadside and the ripple of surf threatened to swamp it, only a naked boy ran into the water and pulled the bow high up on the sand.The man stood up and sent a questing glance along the line of villagers.A rainbow sweater, dirty and the worse for wear, clung loosely to his broad shoulders, and a red cotton handkerchief was knotted in sailor fashion about his throat.A fisherman’s tam-o’-shanter on his close-clipped head, and dungaree trousers and heavy brogans, completed his outfit.
然而对大育空河三角洲地区的这些单纯的渔民来说，他依然是个罕见的人物，他们终生在白令海上向外凝望，在那时仅仅看见过两个白人——一个人口普查员和一个迷路的耶稣会牧师。他们是一个可怜的民族，土里没有金子，手里也没有贵重的毛皮，所以白人们远远超越了他们。并且，经过数千年，阿拉斯加的碎石渐渐使育空河那部分海域变浅，直到船舶在看不见陆地的地方搁浅。所以湿漉漉的海岸，以及它那长长的内部河段和巨大的泥地群岛，被很多行船的人避开了，渔民们不知道这类事情的存在。 But he was none the less a striking personage to these simple fisherfolk of the great Yukon Delta, who, all their lives, had stared out on Bering Sea and in that time seen but two white men,—the census enumerator and a lost Jesuit priest.They were a poor people, with neither gold in the ground nor valuable furs in hand, so the whites had passed them afar.Also, the Yukon, through the thousands of years, had shoaled that portion of the sea with the detritus of Alaska till vessels grounded out of sight of land.So the sodden coast, with its long inside reaches and huge mud-land archipelagoes, was avoided by the ships of men, and the fisherfolk knew not that such things were.
库加，那个雕刻骨头的人，仓皇之间向后退，被他的拐杖绊倒在地上。“纳姆－博克！”他一边叫喊着，一边疯狂地争着走开，“纳姆－博克，被吹飞到海上的人，回来了！” Koogah, the Bone-Scratcher, retreated backward in sudden haste, tripping over his staff and falling to the ground.“Nam-Bok!” he cried, as he scrambled wildly for footing.“Nam-Bok, who was blown off to sea, come back!”
男男女女都往后退缩，而孩子们在他们腿间急促地奔来跑去。只有欧匹－关是勇敢的，这与他作为村子里的首领很配。他大步走向前，长久认真地注视着这个新来的人。 The men and women shrank away, and the children scuttled off between their legs.Only Opee-Kwan was brave, as befitted the head man of the village.He strode forward and gazed long and earnestly at the new-comer.
“是纳姆－博克。”他最后说，听他的话里透出确信无疑，女人们恐惧地恸哭起来，往后挪得更远了。 “It is Nam-Bok,” he said at last, and at the conviction in his voice the women wailed apprehensively and drew farther away.
这个陌生人的嘴唇犹豫地动着，他棕色的脖子扭动着，挣扎着想说出话来。 The lips of the stranger moved indecisively, and his brown throat writhed and wrestled with unspoken words.
“啦啦，这是纳姆－博克。”巴斯克－华－沃恩用嘶哑的声音说着，凝视着他的脸，“我曾经说过纳姆－博克会回来的。” “La la, it is Nam-Bok,” Bask-Wah-Wan croaked, peering up into his face.“Ever did I say Nam-Bok would come back.”
“哎，是纳姆－博克回来了。”这次纳姆－博克自己说话了，他的一条腿跨过海豹皮舟的一侧，一只脚踏在船上，另一只脚踏在岸上。他再次扭动脖子，像是要努力回想起已经忘却的话语。当话说出口时，伴随着喉音出现的是奇怪的声音和双唇的唾液飞溅。“大家好，老伙计们，”他说，“我被海风吹走前，我往日的兄弟们。” “Ay, it is Nam-Bok come back.”This time it was Nam-Bok himself who spoke, putting a leg over the side of the bidarka and standing with one foot afloat and one ashore.Again his throat writhed and wrestled as he grappled after forgotten words.And when the words came forth they were strange of sound and a spluttering of the lips accompanied the gutturals.“Greeting, O brothers,” he said, “brothers of old time before I went away with the off-shore wind.”
他走出来，双脚踩在沙滩上，欧匹－关向他挥手示意让他回去。 He stepped out with both feet on the sand, and Opee-Kwan waved him back.
“你死了，纳姆－博克。”他说。 “Thou art dead, Nam-Bok,” he said.
纳姆－博克笑了：“我胖了。” Nam-Bok laughed. “I am fat.”
“死人是不会胖的。”欧匹－关坦白说，“你过得很好，但这事儿很奇怪。没有人能敌得过海上风，而且紧接着几年以后还回来了。” “Dead men are not fat,” Opee-Kwan confessed.“Thou hast fared well, but it is strange.No man may mate with the off-shore wind and come back on the heels of the years.”
“我回来了。”纳姆－博克简单地回答。 “I have come back,” Nam-Bok answered simply.
“但愿你是一个幽灵，那么，你就是过去的纳姆－博克的一个转瞬即逝的幽灵。幽灵是可以回来的。” “Mayhap thou art a shadow, then, a passing shadow of the Nam-Bok that was.Shadows come back.”
“我饿了。幽灵是不吃东西的。” “I am hungry.Shadows do not eat.”
但欧匹－关心下怀疑，他手抹过额头，感到非常困惑。纳姆－博克也感到困惑不解，他上下打量着那一排渔民，发现他们眼中没有丝毫欢迎的意思。男男女女们都在窃窃私语。孩子们怯生生地偷偷溜回大人们中间，毛发竖立的狗儿向他摇着尾巴，在他身边怀疑地嗅来嗅去。 But Opee-Kwan doubted, and brushed his hand across his brow in sore puzzlement.Nam-Bok was likewise puzzled, and as he looked up and down the line found no welcome in the eyes of the fisherfolk.The men and women whispered together.The children stole timidly back among their elders, and bristling dogs fawned up to him and sniffed suspiciously.
“我生了你，纳姆－博克，你还小的时候，我给你喂奶，”巴斯克－华－沃恩呜咽着说，靠得更近了；“无论你是幽灵，或者不是，我现在都会给你吃的。” “I bore thee, Nam-Bok, and I gave thee suck when thou wast little,” Bask-Wah-Wan whimpered, drawing closer; “and shadow though thou be, or no shadow, I will give thee to eat now.”
纳姆－博克走近她，但一声恐惧和威胁的咆哮声警告他后退。他用一种奇怪的语言说了听起来像什么“该死的”的话，并补充道：“我不是幽灵，而是一个人。” Nam-Bok made to come to her, but a growl of fear and menace warned him back.He said something in a strange tongue which sounded like “Goddam,” and added, “No shadow am I, but a man.”
“谁可能了解有关神秘的万物呢？”欧匹－关问道，是问自己，也是问他的部落成员。“我们是，但瞬间后我们又都不是幽灵。如果人类可以变为幽灵，那幽灵不可以变成人吗？纳姆－博克过去是，但现在不是。这个我们知道，但不知道这个是纳姆－博克还是纳姆－博克的幽灵。” “Who may know concerning the things of mystery?”Opee-Kwan demanded, half of himself and half of his tribespeople.“We are, and in a breath we are not.If the man may become shadow, may not the shadow become man?Nam-Bok was, but is not.This we know, but we do not know if this be Nam-Bok or the shadow of Nam-Bok.”
纳姆－博克清清了嗓子，做出了回答。“很久以前，欧匹－关，你父亲的父亲离开了，也在几年后回来了。人们在火炉旁给他留了个地方。据说……”他意味深长地停顿了一下，他们在认真地听他所说的话。“据说，”他重复着，深慎地将他的意思讲清楚，“他的女人，希普希普，在他回来后给他生了两个儿子。” Nam-Bok cleared his throat and made answer.“In the old time long ago, thy father’s father, Opee-Kwan, went away and came back on the heels of the years.Nor was a place by the fire denied him.It is said...”He paused significantly, and they hung on his utterance.“It is said,” he repeated, driving his point home with deliberation, “that Sipsip, his klooch, bore him two sons after he came back.”
“但他跟海上风无关。”欧匹－关反驳道，“他去了陆地中心，理所当然地，一个男人要继续前行到达陆地。” “But he had no doings with the off-shore wind,” Opee-Kwan retorted.“He went away into the heart of the land, and it is in the nature of things that a man may go on and on into the land.”
“海洋也一样。但那不重要。据说……你父亲的父亲讲述了他所见到的奇怪故事。” “And likewise the sea.But that is neither here nor there.It is said...that thy father’s father told strange tales of the things he saw.”
“哎，他讲述的奇怪故事。” “Ay, strange tales he told.”
“我也有奇怪的故事要讲，”纳姆－博克狡诈地说，而且当他们犹豫不决的时候，“也有礼物。” “I, too, have strange tales to tell,” Nam-Bok stated insidiously.And, as they wavered, “And presents likewise.”
他从海豹皮舟上拉出一条披肩，质地和颜色棒极了，将它搭在妈妈的肩膀上。女人们集体发出羡慕的赞叹声，老巴斯克－华－沃恩拨弄着色彩鲜艳的织物，轻拍着它，孩子般快乐地低声哼唱着。 He pulled from the bidarka a shawl, marvellous of texture and color, and flung it about his mother’s shoulders.The women voiced a collective sigh of admiration, and old Bask-Wah-Wan ruffled the gay material and patted it and crooned in childish joy.
“他有故事要讲。”库加喃喃低语。“而且有礼物。”一个女人附和道。 “He has tales to tell,” Koogah muttered.“And presents,” a woman seconded.
欧匹－关知道他的人民很急切，而且，他意识到对于那些没听过的故事，他自已也有一种渴望的好奇心。“捕鱼一直不错，”他明智地说道，“我们有充足的油。所以来吧，纳姆－博克，让我们尽情地吃。” And Opee-Kwan knew that his people were eager, and further, he was aware himself of an itching curiosity concerning those untold tales.“The fishing has been good,” he said judiciously, “and we have oil in plenty.So come, Nam-Bok, let us feast.”
其中两个人肩扛海豹皮舟，将它抬到火旁。纳姆－博克走在欧匹－关的旁边，村民紧随其后，只有那些女人拖延了一会儿，用手指抚摸着披肩。 Two of the men hoisted the bidarka on their shoulders and carried it up to the fire.Nam-Bok walked by the side of Opee-Kwan, and the villagers followed after, save those of the women who lingered a moment to lay caressing fingers on the shawl.
筵席进行期间很少有人谈话，尽管很多人好奇地偷瞄巴斯克－华－沃恩的儿子。这使他感到尴尬——然而，不是因为他生性羞怯，而是因为海豹油的恶臭让他没了胃口，而且他强烈渴望隐藏他对这一话题的感受。 There was little talk while the feast went on, though many and curious were the glances stolen at the son of Bask-Wah-Wan.This embarrassed him—not because he was modest of spirit, however, but for the fact that the stench of the seal-oil had robbed him of his appetite, and that he keenly desired to conceal his feelings on the subject.
“吃吧，你饿了。”欧匹－关命令道，纳姆－博克闭上双眼，将手放入一大锅腐臭的鱼里。 “Eat; thou art hungry,” Opee-Kwan commanded, and Nam-Bok shut both his eyes and shoved his fist into the big pot of putrid fish.
“啦啦，不要不好意思。今年海豹很多，强壮的人永远都是饥饿的。”巴斯克－华－沃恩将一大块极其令人作呕的鲑鱼浸到油里，将滴着油的鲑鱼温柔地递给儿子。 “La la, be not ashamed.The seal were many this year, and strong men are ever hungry.”And Bask-Wah-Wan sopped a particularly offensive chunk of salmon into the oil and passed it fondly and dripping to her son.
绝望中，前驱症状提醒着他，他的胃口没有老人们的那么好，他填满烟斗开始吸烟。人们吵闹着边吃边看。尽管可以从到北部进行贸易的爱斯基摩人那不时得到少量劣质的烟草，但他们当中很少有人能吹嘘自己非常熟悉珍贵的烟草。坐在他旁边的库加，表示他不反对抽烟，抽了几口后，嘴上厚厚的一层油，在琥珀色的烟斗柄上被吸掉了。随即纳姆－博克用只颤抖的手捂着肚子，拒绝了再次给他的食物。他说库加可以保留烟斗，因为从一开始他就打算把这个孝敬给他。人们舔舔手指，对他的慷慨表示赞同。 In despair, when premonitory symptoms warned him that his stomach was not so strong as of old, he filled his pipe and struck up a smoke.The people fed on noisily and watched.Few of them could boast of intimate acquaintance with the precious weed, though now and again small quantities and abominable qualities were obtained in trade from the Eskimos to the northward.Koogah, sitting next to him, indicated that he was not averse to taking a draw, and between two mouthfuls, with the oil thick on his lips, sucked away at the amber stem.And thereupon Nam-Bok held his stomach with a shaky hand and declined the proffered return.Koogah could keep the pipe, he said, for he had intended so to honor him from the first.And the people licked their fingers and approved of his liberality.
欧匹－关站起身，“老纳姆－博克，现在，筵席结束了，我们要听有关你见过的那些奇怪的事情。” Opee-Kwan rose to his feet “And now, O Nam-Bok, the feast is ended, and we would listen concerning the strange things you have seen.”
渔民们双手鼓掌，把活计拿到自己身边，准备倾听。男人们忙于制造长矛和雕刻象牙，而女人们刮掉粗毛海皮上的脂肪，并将它们捋顺或者用兽筋缝制软靴。 The fisherfolk applauded with their hands, and gathering about them their work, prepared to listen.The men were busy fashioning spears and carving on ivory, while the women scraped the fat from the hides of the hair seal and made them pliable or sewed muclucs with threads of sinew.
纳姆－博克扫视着这一切，但它却并不像他回忆中的那么有吸引力。流浪的那些年，他向往的就是这个场景，而现在这个场景出现了，他却感到失望。他认为，这种空无内容清贫的生活，跟他已经习惯的那种生活没法比。尽管如此，他会使他们的视野开阔一点儿，一想到这里他的眼睛就闪闪发光。 Nam-Bok’s eyes roved over the scene, but there was not the charm about it that his recollection had warranted him to expect.During the years of his wandering he had looked forward to just this scene, and now that it had come he was disappointed.It was a bare and meagre life, he deemed, and not to be compared to the one to which he had become used.Still, he would open their eyes a bit, and his own eyes sparkled at the thought.
“兄弟们，”他开始了，自以为是地叙述他做过的大事，“那是多年前的夏末，我离开的时候，可能差不多是这样的天气。你们都记得那一天，海鸥低飞，来自陆地的风猛烈地吹着，逆着风我都无法控制我的海豹皮舟。我将海豹皮舟的盖子绑在身上，那样水就进不来了，整晚我都在跟暴风雨作斗争。早上看不到陆地——只有海洋——海上风紧紧地拥我入怀，一直向前。这样过了３个晚上，拂晓时分我还是没看到陆地，海上风不肯放过我。 “Brothers,” he began, with the smug complacency of a man about to relate the big things he has done, “it was late summer of many summers back, with much such weather as this promises to be, when I went away.You all remember the day, when the gulls flew low, and the wind blew strong from the land, and I could not hold my bidarka against it.I tied the covering of the bidarka about me so that no water could get in, and all of the night I fought with the storm.And in the morning there was no land,—only the sea,—and the off-shore wind held me close in its arms and bore me along.Three such nights whitened into dawn and showed me no land, and the off-shore wind would not let me go.
当第四天来临，我跟疯子似的了。因缺乏食物我无力浸桨拨水，晕头转向，口很渴。但是海不再狂躁了，温和的南风吹来，环视四周的时候，我看到的景象让我以为自己真的疯了。” And when the fourth day came, I was as a madman.I could not dip my paddle for want of food; and my head went round and round, what of the thirst that was upon me.But the sea was no longer angry, and the soft south wind was blowing, and as I looked about me I saw a sight that made me think I was indeed mad.”
纳姆－博克停下来，剔去留在牙缝上的一片鲑鱼，男人和女人们的手闲了下来，头伸向前，等待着。 Nam-Bok paused to pick away a sliver of salmon lodged between his teeth, and the men and women, with idle hands and heads craned forward, waited.
“那是一个独木舟，一个大的独木舟。即使把我见过的所有独木舟用来制成一个，都没有那么大。” “It was a canoe, a big canoe.If all the canoes I have ever seen were made into one canoe, it would not be so large.”
人们发出怀疑的惊叹声，而经历过很多沧桑的库加摇摇头。 There were exclamations of doubt, and Koogah, whose years were many, shook his head.
“如果每个海豹皮舟是一粒沙子那么大，”纳姆－博克不服地继续说道，“即使海豹皮舟有这个沙滩上的沙子这么多，他们仍然不能制造出一个像我第四天早上看到的那么大的独木舟。那是一个很大的独木舟，它被称作纵帆船。我看见这个奇迹般的东西，这个大纵帆船，跟着我，我看到上面有人——” “If each bidarka were as a grain of sand,” Nam-Bok defiantly continued, “and if there were as many bidarkas as there be grains of sand in this beach, still would they not make so big a canoe as this I saw on the morning of the fourth day.It was a very big canoe, and it was called a schooner.I saw this thing of wonder, this great schooner, coming after me, and on it I saw men—”
“等等，哦　纳姆－博克！”欧匹－关插话道，“他们是什么样的人？——高个子？” “Hold, O Nam-Bok!”Opee-Kwan broke in.“What manner of men were they?—big men?”
“不，仅仅是像你和我这样的人。” “Nay, mere men like you and me.”
“那个大独木舟开得快吗？” “Did the big canoe come fast?”
“哎。” “Ay.”
“船帮高，人矮。”欧匹－关坚信不疑地叙述着前提，“这些人用长桨划船吗？” “The sides were tall, the men short.”Opee-Kwan stated the premises with conviction.“And did these men dip with long paddles?”
纳姆－博克露齿而笑。“没有桨。”他说。 Nam-Bok grinned.“There were no paddles,” he said.
人们张大了嘴，接下来是一阵长久的沉默。欧匹－关借库加的烟斗，抽了两口，沉思着。一个较年轻的女人怯生生地咯咯笑了，引来了愤怒的目光。 Mouths remained open, and a long silence dropped down.Opee-Kwan borrowed Koogah’s pipe for a couple of contemplative sucks.One of the younger women giggled nervously and drew upon herself angry eyes.
“没有桨？”欧匹－关柔声问道，还回了烟斗。 “There were no paddles?”Opee-Kwan asked softly, returning the pipe.
“顺着南风，”纳姆－博克解释道， “The south wind was behind,” Nam-Bok explained.
“但随风漂流很慢。” “But the wind-drift is slow.”
“纵帆船有翅膀——像这样。”他在沙地上画了一个桅杆和帆的简图，人们聚集在周围研究它。风猛烈地吹着，为了更形象的解释，他抓住妈妈披肩的边角，将它们摊开，直到它像一个帆那样张满。巴斯克－华－沃恩咒骂着艰难行进，但在几英尺远处被吹倒在海滩上，困在一堆浮木上喘不过气来。人们嘟哝着说貌似理解了，但库加突然向后甩了甩发丝灰白的头。 “The schooner had wings—thus.”He sketched a diagram of masts and sails in the sand, and the men crowded around and studied it.The wind was blowing briskly, and for more graphic elucidation he seized the corners of his mother’s shawl and spread them out till it bellied like a sail.Bask-Wah-Wan scolded and struggled, but was blown down the beach for a score of feet and left breathless and stranded in a heap of driftwood.The men uttered sage grunts of comprehension, but Koogah suddenly tossed back his hoary head.
“呵！呵！”他笑了，“这个大的独木舟是一个愚蠢的东西！非常愚蠢的一个东西！风的玩物！风去哪，它就得去哪。那艘船上旅行的人没有谁能说出登陆的海滩的名字，因为它总是跟着风走，风无处不去，但没人知道是去哪儿。” “Ho!Ho!” he laughed.“A foolish thing, this big canoe!A most foolish thing!The plaything of the wind!Wheresoever the wind goes, it goes too.No man who journeys therein may name the landing beach, for always he goes with the wind, and the wind goes everywhere, but no man knows where.”
“是这样的，”欧匹－关严肃地补充道，“顺着风行进很容易，但逆着风一个人就要费劲了；因为这些大独木舟上的人没有桨，他们一点儿也不努力。” “It is so,” Opee-Kwan supplemented gravely.“With the wind the going is easy, but against the wind a man striveth hard; and for that they had no paddles these men on the big canoe did not strive at all.”
“几乎不需要努力，”纳姆－博克生气地叫喊着，“纵帆船同样逆着风走。” “Small need to strive,” Nam-Bok cried angrily.“The schooner went likewise against the wind.”
“你说什么使纵——纵——纵帆船行进？”库加问道，狡猾地在陌生词汇上出差错。 “And what said you made the sch—sch—schooner go?”Koogah asked, tripping craftily over the strange word.
“是风。”回答很不耐烦。 “The wind,” was the impatient response.
“那么风使纵——纵——纵帆船逆着风走。”老库加公然斜着眼看着欧匹－关，他周围的笑声越来越大，库加接着说：“风从南方吹来，将纵帆船吹向南方。风逆着风吹。风同时向两个相反的方向吹。这非常简单。我们明白了，纳姆－博克。我们非常清楚了。” “Then the wind made the sch—sch—schooner go against the wind.”Old Koogah dropped an open leer to Opee-Kwan, and, the laughter growing around him, continued: “The wind blows from the south and blows the schooner south.The wind blows against the wind.The wind blows one way and the other at the same time.It is very simple.We understand, Nam-Bok.We clearly understand.”
“你是一个傻瓜！” “Thou art a fool!”
“真理从你的嘴里说出，”库加温顺地回答，“我相当明白了，事情很简单。” “Truth falls from thy lips,” Koogah answered meekly.“I was over-long in understanding, and the thing was simple.”
但纳姆－博克的脸阴沉下来，他语速很快，说了一些他们以前从未听过的话。骨雕和剥皮重新开始了，但他紧闭双唇，不再说不被相信的话。 But Nam-Bok’s face was dark, and he said rapid words which they had never heard before.Bone-scratching and skin-scraping were resumed, but he shut his lips tightly on the tongue that could not be believed.
“这个纵——纵——纵帆船，”库加冷静地问道，“是用大树做成的吗？” “This sch—sch—schooner,” Koogah imperturbably asked; “it was made of a big tree?”
“它是用很多树做成的，”纳姆－博克立刻怒声说，“它非常大。” “It was made of many trees,” Nam-Bok snapped shortly.“It was very big.”
他再次愠怒地陷入沉默，而欧匹－关用肘轻推库加，带着迟钝的惊异，他摇摇头，呐呐说道，“这非常奇怪。” He lapsed into sullen silence again, and Opee-Kwan nudged Koogah, who shook his head with slow amazement and murmured, “It is very strange.”
纳姆－博克陷入了圈套。“那没什么，”他轻快地说，“你应该看看那艘轮船。纵帆船对于那艘轮船来说，正如这粒沙子之于那个海豹皮舟，海豹皮舟之于那个纵帆船。而且，轮船是用铁制成的。它全身上下都是铁。” Nam-bok took the bait.“That is nothing,” he said airily; “you should see the steamer.As the grain of sand is to the bidarka, as the bidarka is to the schooner, so the schooner is to the steamer.Further, the steamer is made of iron.It is all iron.”
“不，不，纳姆－博克，”首领叫喊着，“那怎么可能？铁总是沉底的。瞧，在与邻村首领的贸易中，我得到一个铁刀子，昨天那个铁刀子从我的手里滑落到了海里。所有事物都有法则的。从来没有一样事物存在于法则之外。这个我知道。此外，我们知道每种事物都有一个法则，而且所有铁都有同一个法则。所以收回你的话，纳姆－博克，我们可能还尊敬你。” “Nay, nay, Nam-Bok,” cried the head man; “how can that be?Always iron goes to the bottom.For behold, I received an iron knife in trade from the head man of the next village, and yesterday the iron knife slipped from my fingers and went down, down, into the sea.To all things there be law.Never was there one thing outside the law.This we know.And, moreover, we know that things of a kind have the one law, and that all iron has the one law.So unsay thy words, Nam-Bok, that we may yet honor thee.”
“就是这样的，”纳姆－博克坚持说，“轮船全都是铁而且没有下沉。” “It is so,” Nam-Bok persisted.“The steamer is all iron and does not sink.”
“不，不；这不可能。” “Nay, nay; this cannot be.”
“我亲眼看到了它。” “With my own eyes I saw it.”
“这不符合道理。” “It is not in the nature of things.”
“那告诉我，纳姆－博克,”库加打断了，唯恐这个故事不会继续下去，“告诉我，没有陆地引导行驶时，这些人寻找渡海路径的方式。” “But tell me, Nam-Bok,” Koogah interrupted, for fear the tale would go no farther, “tell me the manner of these men in finding their way across the sea when there is no land by which to steer.”
“太阳指明路径。” “The sun points out the path.”
“但是怎么指？” “But how?”
“正午，纵帆船的船长拿着一种东西，他的眼睛透过它望向太阳，然后他使太阳爬下了天空，到达地球的边缘。” “At midday the head man of the schooner takes a thing through which his eye looks at the sun, and then he makes the sun climb down out of the sky to the edge of the earth.”
“现在这是邪恶的巫术！”欧匹－关叫喊着，被悖理逆天的行为惊呆了。男人们惊恐地举起手，而女人们悲叹着。“这是邪恶的巫术。误导伟大的太阳是没有好处的，它赶走了黑夜给了我们海豹、鲑鱼和温暖的天气。” “Now this be evil medicine!” cried Opee-Kwan, aghast at the sacrilege.The men held up their hands in horror, and the women moaned.“This be evil medicine.It is not good to misdirect the great sun which drives away the night and gives us the seal, the salmon, and warm weather.”
“如果那是邪恶的巫术将会怎样？”纳姆－博克尖刻地问道，“我也透过那个东西看过太阳，使太阳爬下了天空。” “What if it be evil medicine?”Nam-Bok demanded truculently.“I, too, have looked through the thing at the sun and made the sun climb down out of the sky.”
那些离他最近的人都迅速远离了他，一个女人将孩子的脸捂在胸前，那样他的目光就不会落在他身上了。 Those who were nearest drew away from him hurriedly, and a woman covered the face of a child at her breast so that his eye might not fall upon it.
“但在第四天早上，纳姆－博克。”库加暗示说，“第四天早上，纵——纵——纵帆船跟着你？” “But on the morning of the fourth day, O Nam-Bok,” Koogah suggested; “on the morning of the fourth day when the sch—sch—schooner came after thee?”
“我几乎没有力气了，无法逃跑。所以我被带到了船上，他们给我水喝，给我东西吃。我的兄弟们，你们见过白种人两次。这些都是白人，跟我的手指和脚趾一样多。当我见到他们充满善意时，我鼓起勇气，下决心带走我看到的所有记录。他们教我做他们的工作，提供给我好的食物和一个睡觉的地方。 “I had little strength left in me and could not run away.So I was taken on board and water was poured down my throat and good food given me.Twice, my brothers, you have seen a white man.These men were all white and as many as have I fingers and toes.And when I saw they were full of kindness, I took heart, and I resolved to bring away with me report of all that I saw.And they taught me the work they did, and gave me good food and a place to sleep.
日复一日我们在海面上行进，每天船长牵引太阳出了天空，让它说出我们在哪里。风平浪静时，我们猎取海狗，而他们总是扔掉肉和脂肪，只留下兽皮，因此我感到非常惊奇。” And day after day we went over the sea, and each day the head man drew the sun down out of the sky and made it tell where we were.And when the waves were kind, we hunted the fur seal and I marvelled much, for always did they fling the meat and the fat away and save only the skin.”
欧匹－关的嘴猛烈地抽搐着，他刚要谴责这样的浪费时，库加踢了踢他，让他安静下来。 Opee-Kwan’s mouth was twitching violently, and he was about to make denunciation of such waste when Koogah kicked him to be still.
“一段疲倦的时间后，当太阳消失，霜冻来临时，船长将纵帆船的船头指向南方。我们向南方和东方旅行了很多天，从没看到过陆地，我们靠近一个村子，村子里的人欢呼着——” “After a weary time, when the sun was gone and the bite of the frost come into the air, the head man pointed the nose of the schooner south.South and east we travelled for days upon days, with never the land in sight, and we were near to the village from which hailed the men—”
“他们怎么知道他们靠近了？”欧匹－关再也控制不住自己了，问道。“那又看不到陆地。” “How did they know they were near?”Opee-Kwan, unable to contain himself longer, demanded.“There was no land to see.”
纳姆－博克愤怒地瞪着他。“我没有说船长将太阳赶下天空吗？” Nam-Bok glowered on him wrathfully.“Did I not say the head man brought the sun down out of the sky?”
库加插话，而纳姆－博克继续着。 Koogah interposed, and Nam-Bok went on.
“正如我所说，当我们靠近那个村子时，一场巨大的暴风雨爆发了，晚上我们感到很无助，也不知道我们在哪儿——” “As I say, when we were near to that village a great storm blew up, and in the night we were helpless and knew not where we were—”
“你刚刚说船长知道——” “Thou hast just said the head man knew—”
“哦，安静，欧匹－关！你是一个傻瓜，理解不了。正如我所说，夜里我们感到很无助，这时我听到了海滩上的海涛声，盖过了暴风雨的咆哮声。接着我们受到了强大的撞击，我掉进了水里，眩晕过去。那是一个被岩石包围的海岸，有一片好几英里的海滩，法则就是我应该把手伸进沙子，将自己远离海浪。其他人一定撞在了岩石上，因为除了船长没人上岸，单凭他手指上的戒指，我就认出了他。 “Oh, peace, Opee-Kwan!Thou art a fool and cannot understand.As I say, we were helpless in the night, when I heard, above the roar of the storm, the sound of the sea on the beach.And next we struck with a mighty crash and I was in the water, swimming.It was a rock-bound coast, with one patch of beach in many miles, and the law was that I should dig my hands into the sand and draw myself clear of the surf.The other men must have pounded against the rocks, for none of them came ashore but the head man, and him I knew only by the ring on his finger.
天亮时，纵帆船没了踪影，我转脸望向陆地，往里走去，我可能在那里找到食物，见到人。当我来到一所房子时，有人将我带进去并给我吃的，因为我学会了他们的语言，这些白人总是很友好。那是一所大房子，比我们和我们的父辈建造的房子都大。” When day came, there being nothing of the schooner, I turned my face to the land and journeyed into it that I might get food and look upon the faces of the people.And when I came to a house I was taken in and given to eat, for I had learned their speech, and the white men are ever kind.And it was a house bigger than all the houses built by us and our fathers before us.”
“那是一所巨大的房子。”库加说道，用惊愕掩盖他的不信任。 “It was a mighty house,” Koogah said, masking his unbelief with wonder.
“要很多树才能造出一所这样的房子。”欧匹－关得到了启示补充道。 “And many trees went into the making of such a house,” Opee-Kwan added, taking the cue.
“那没什么。”纳姆－博克轻蔑地耸耸肩。“我们的房子跟那所房子比起来，就像那所房子之于我尚未看到的房子。” “That is nothing.”Nam-Bok shrugged his shoulders in belittling fashion.“As our houses are to that house, so that house was to the houses I was yet to see.”
“他们不是大个子的人？” “And they are not big men?”
“不；仅仅是像你和我这样的人。”纳姆－博克回答道，“为了能走得舒服些，我砍了一根棍子，想着我要将这些报告给你们，我的兄弟们，我就在那根棍子上给住在那所房子里的每个人都刻下一个凹口。我在那里呆了很多天，还工作，为此他们付给我工钱——关于这个东西你们一无所知，但它非常好。 “Nay; mere men like you and me,” Nam-Bok answered.“I had cut a stick that I might walk in comfort, and remembering that I was to bring report to you, my brothers, I cut a notch in the stick for each person who lived in that house.And I stayed there many days, and worked, for which they gave me money—a thing of which you know nothing, but which is very good.
一天我离开了那个地方，去了更远的陆地。我走着，见到了很多人，我就在棍子上砍更小的凹口，这样所有人就都有地方了。那时我遇到一种奇怪的东西。我前方的地面上有一个铁条，厚度跟我的手臂一样，在一步远的地方有另一个铁条——” And one day I departed from that place to go farther into the land.And as I walked I met many people, and I cut smaller notches in the stick, that there might be room for all.Then I came upon a strange thing.On the ground before me was a bar of iron, as big in thickness as my arm, and a long step away was another bar of iron—”
“那么你就是一个富人了，”欧匹－关肯定地说，“因为铁比世界上任何其他东西都有价值。它可以制成很多刀子。” “Then wert thou a rich man,” Opee-Kwan asserted; “for iron be worth more than anything else in the world.It would have made many knives.”
“不，那不是我的。” “Nay, it was not mine.”
“那是捡到的，捡到的就是合法的。” “It was a find, and a find be lawful.”
“不是这样的；白人把它放在那里的，此外，这些铁条太长了，长得我都看不到它们的尽头，没人能够带走它们。” “Not so; the white men had placed it there And further, these bars were so long that no man could carry them away—so long that as far as I could see there was no end to them.”
“纳姆－博克，铁非常多。”欧匹－关劝道。 “Nam-Bok, that is very much iron,” Opee-Kwan cautioned.
“哎，我亲眼看到都很难让人相信；但我不能否定我的眼睛。我看的时候听到……”他突然转向首领。“欧匹－关，你听过海狮愤怒的吼叫声。在你的脑海里清楚地想象一下有海浪那么多的海狮，想象所有这些海狮被组成了一头海狮，我听到的那个东西的吼叫声就像这头海狮吼叫的声音。” “Ay, it was hard to believe with my own eyes upon it; but I could not gainsay my eyes.And as I looked I heard....”He turned abruptly upon the head man.“Opee-Kwan, thou hast heard the sea-lion bellow in his anger.Make it plain in thy mind of as many sea-lions as there be waves to the sea, and make it plain that all these sea-lions be made into one sea-lion, and as that one sea-lion would bellow so bellowed the thing I heard.”
渔民惊奇地叫喊着，而欧匹－关的下巴一沉，张口结舌。 The fisherfolk cried aloud in astonishment, and Opee-Kwan’s jaw lowered and remained lowered.
“而在远处，我看到了一头怪物，有一千条鲸鱼那么大。它只有一只眼，吐着烟，发出的哼声非常响亮。我害怕极了，就沿着铁条间的小路，颤抖着双腿跑开了。但它以风的速度跟过来，这个怪物，我越过铁条时，脸上带着它喷出热热的气息，……” “And in the distance I saw a monster like unto a thousand whales.It was one-eyed, and vomited smoke, and it snorted with exceeding loudness.I was afraid and ran with shaking legs along the path between the bars.But it came with the speed of the wind, this monster, and I leaped the iron bars with its breath hot on my face....”
欧匹－关再次管住了他的嘴。“那么——然后呢，纳姆－博克？” Opee-Kwan gained control of his jaw again.“And—and then, O Nam-Bok?”
“然后它来到铁条上，没有伤害我；当我的双腿再也支撑不住时，它就消失不见了。在那个国家，这是非常普通的事情。甚至女人和孩子都不会害怕。男人让这些怪物们劳动。” “Then it came by on the bars, and harmed me not; and when my legs could hold me up again it was gone from sight.And it is a very common thing in that country.Even the women and children are not afraid.Men make them to do work, these monsters.”
“就像我们让狗劳动一样？”库加问道，眼中闪着怀疑的光。 “As we make our dogs do work?”Koogah asked, with sceptic twinkle in his eye.
“哎，就像我们让狗劳动那样。” “Ay, as we make our dogs do work.”
“那它们怎么繁殖这些——这些东西？”欧匹－关问道。 “And how do they breed these—these things?” Opee-Kwan questioned.
“它们从不繁殖。人们用铁巧妙地将它们制成，喂它们吃石头，给它们水喝。石头变成了火，水变成了蒸汽，水蒸汽就是它们鼻孔的气息，而且——” “They breed not at all.Men fashion them cunningly of iron, and feed them with stone, and give them water to drink.The stone becomes fire, and the water becomes steam, and the steam of the water is the breath of their nostrils, and—”
“好啦，好啦，哦　纳姆－博克。”欧匹－关打断了他，“告诉我们一些其他的奇观。我们对这个我们不能理解的东西厌烦了。” “There, there, O Nam-Bok,” Opee-Kwan interrupted.“Tell us of other wonders.We grow tired of this which we may not understand.”
“你们不理解？”纳姆－博克绝望地问道。 “You do not understand?” Nam-Bok asked despairingly.
“是，我们不理解，”男人和女人们悲叹着回应道，“我们无法理解。” “Nay, we do not understand,” the men and women wailed back.“We cannot understand.”
纳姆－博克想到了一台联合收割机，想到了看到的机器里的活人，想到了机器里传来的人声，他知道他的人民永远不会理解。 Nam-Bok thought of a combined harvester, and of the machines wherein visions of living men were to be seen, and of the machines from which came the voices of men, and he knew his people could never understand.
“我敢说我骑着铁怪物穿过了陆地？”他痛苦地问道。 “Dare I say I rode this iron monster through the land?” he asked bitterly.
欧匹－关扬起手，手掌朝外，公然表示不信。“继续说；说点儿什么。我们听着呢。” Opee-Kwan threw up his hands, palms outward, in open incredulity.“Say on; say anything.We listen.”
“那时我的确骑上了铁怪物，我还为此付了钱——” “Then did I ride the iron monster, for which I gave money—”
“你说过它们吃石头。” “Thou saidst it was fed with stone.”
“你个傻瓜，我同样说过钱是一种你们不知道的东西。正如我说的，我骑着怪物穿过陆地，穿过很多村子，直到我来到一个在咸水海湾处的大村子。房顶耸入星空，周围漂浮着云朵，到处都有很多烟雾。那个村子的喧闹声就像暴风雨中海洋的咆哮声，人太多了，所以我扔掉了棍子，不再记它上面的凹口了。” “And likewise, thou fool, I said money was a thing of which you know nothing.As I say, I rode the monster through the land, and through many villages, until I came to a big village on a salt arm of the sea.And the houses shoved their roofs among the stars in the sky, and the clouds drifted by them, and everywhere was much smoke.And the roar of that village was like the roar of the sea in storm, and the people were so many that I flung away my stick and no longer remembered the notches upon it.”
“你做过小凹口。”库加责骂道，“你本可以带来记录的。” “Hadst thou made small notches,” Koogah reproved, “thou mightst have brought report.”
纳姆－博克愤怒地转向他。“我做过小凹口！听着，库加，你这个刻骨头的人！如果我做小凹口，那根棍子不能，二十根棍子也不能，记下他们——不，这个村子和邻村之间所有海滩上的所有浮木都不能记下。即使你们所有人，女人和孩子在内，再多二十倍的人，即使你们每人有二十只手，每只手上有一根棍子和一把刀子，仍然不能砍出跟我看到的人一样多的凹口，他们的人数众多，而且他们来去如此之快。” Nam-Bok whirled upon him in anger.“Had I made small notches!Listen, Koogah, thou scratcher of bone!If I had made small notches, neither the stick, nor twenty sticks, could have borne them—nay, not all the driftwood of all the beaches between this village and the next.And if all of you, the women and children as well, were twenty times as many, and if you had twenty hands each, and in each hand a stick and a knife, still the notches could not be cut for the people I saw, so many were they and so fast did they come and go.”
“全世界不可能有那么多人。”欧匹－关反驳道，因为他被吓到了，而且他的头脑无法想象如此巨大的数字。 “There cannot be so many people in all the world,” Opee-Kwan objected, for he was stunned and his mind could not grasp such magnitude of numbers.
“对于全世界，它有多大，你知道什么？”纳姆－博克问道。 “What dost thou know of all the world and how large it is?” Nam-Bok demanded.
“但一个地方不可能有这么多人。” “But there cannot be so many people in one place.”
“你有什么权利说什么是可能的，什么是不可能的？” “Who art thou to say what can be and what cannot be?”
“一个地方不可能有这么多人，这合情合理。他们的独木舟会填满海洋，直到占满所有空间。每天他们能够捕完海里的鱼，而且不是所有人都会得到食物。” “It stands to reason there cannot be so many people in one place.Their canoes would clutter the sea till there was no room.And they could empty the sea each day of its fish, and they would not all be fed.”
“所以它貌似是不可能的,”纳姆－博克做出最后的回答，“但它就是这样的。我亲眼看到了，于是扔掉了我的棍子。”他沉闷地打着呵欠，站起来。“我划了很远。漫长的一天，我累了。现在我要睡了，明天我们继续谈我看到的事情。” “So it would seem,” Nam-Bok made final answer; “yet it was so.With my own eyes I saw, and flung my stick away.”He yawned heavily and rose to his feet.“I have paddled far.The day has been long, and I am tired.Now I will sleep, and to-morrow we will have further talk upon the things I have seen.”
巴斯克－华－沃恩，害怕地跛着脚走在前面，固然自豪，但却对她杰出的儿子充满敬畏，领着他到了她的圆顶小屋，并将他安排在油腻的、难闻的皮毛间。但男人们逗留在火旁，举行了一个会议，其间有窃窃私语和低声讨论。 Bask-Wah-Wan, hobbling fearfully in advance, proud indeed, yet awed by her wonderful son, led him to her igloo and stowed him away among the greasy, ill-smelling furs.But the men lingered by the fire, and a council was held wherein was there much whispering and low-voiced discussion.
一个小时过去了，又过了一个小时，纳姆－博克睡着了，讨论继续着。傍晚的太阳向西北方向落下，晚上十一点钟时接近正北方。然后首领和雕骨的人散会，叫醒了纳姆－博克。他眨着眼望向他们，翻了个身再次睡下。欧匹－关抓住他的胳膊，友好却坚定地摇醒了他。 An hour passed, and a second, and Nam-Bok slept, and the talk went on.The evening sun dipped toward the northwest, and at eleven at night was nearly due north.Then it was that the head man and the bone-scratcher separated themselves from the council and aroused Nam-Bok.He blinked up into their faces and turned on his side to sleep again.Opee-Kwan gripped him by the arm and kindly but firmly shook his senses back into him.
“来，纳姆－博克，醒醒！”他命令道，“时间到了。” “Come, Nam-Bok, arise!” he commanded.“It be time.”
“又一个筵席？”纳姆－博克叫喊着，“不，我不饿。你们继续去吃吧，让我睡觉。” “Another feast?” Nam-Bok cried.“Nay, I am not hungry.Go on with the eating and let me sleep.”
“离开的时间到了！”库加怒喝道。 “Time to be gone!” Koogah thundered.
但欧匹－关说话更柔和了。“我们还小的时候，你跟我是划海豹皮舟的伙伴。”他说，“我们一起第一次追海豹，从陷阱中拉出鲑鱼。你确实救了我一命，纳姆－博克，当海洋吞没我时，我被卷到了黑岩石处。我们一起忍饥挨饿，忍受霜冻严寒，一起爬在同一张毛皮下面，靠近彼此躺着。由于这些事，且出于好意我站在你这边，而你回来后竟是这样一个大骗子，这使我感到极度伤心。我们理解不了，我们的头脑因你讲过的事情而混乱。这不好，会议期间我们谈论了很多。因此我们会把你送走，我们的头脑就会保持清醒而强壮，不会被不可理解的事情所烦扰。” But Opee-Kwan spoke more softly.“Thou wast bidarka-mate with me when we were boys,” he said.“Together we first chased the seal and drew the salmon from the traps.And thou didst drag me back to life, Nam-Bok, when the sea closed over me and I was sucked down to the black rocks.Together we hungered and bore the chill of the frost, and together we crawled beneath the one fur and lay close to each other.And because of these things, and the kindness in which I stood to thee, it grieves me sore that thou shouldst return such a remarkable liar.We cannot understand, and our heads be dizzy with the things thou hast spoken.It is not good, and there has been much talk in the council.Wherefore we send thee away, that our heads may remain clear and strong and be not troubled by the unaccountable things.”
“你作为幽灵说过的这些事，”库加开始紧张，“你从幽灵的世界将它们带来，你必须将它们带回幽灵世界。你的海豹皮舟准备好了，部落成员等着呢。你走了，他们才会去睡。” “These things thou speakest of be shadows,” Koogah took up the strain.“From the shadow-world thou hast brought them, and to the shadow-world thou must return them.Thy bidarka be ready, and the tribespeople wait.They may not sleep until thou art gone.”
纳姆－博克迷惘了，但听明白了首领的话。 Nam-Bok was perplexed, but hearkened to the voice of the head man.
“如果你是纳姆－博克，”欧匹－关说　，“你是一个可怕的且非常杰出的骗子；如果你是纳姆－博克的幽灵，那么关于你作为幽灵说出的话，活着的人知道了是没有好处的。你提到的那个大村子，我们认为是幽灵的村子。那里飘动着死人的灵魂；因为死人有很多，而活人几乎没有。死人没有回来。死人从来不会回来——除了带着惊奇故事的你。这不是死人回来的集会，如果我们允许它，我们就会有很大的麻烦。” “If thou art Nam-Bok,” Opee-Kwan was saying, “thou art a fearful and most wonderful liar; if thou art the shadow of Nam-Bok, then thou speakest of shadows, concerning which it is not good that living men have knowledge.This great village thou hast spoken of we deem the village of shadows.Therein flutter the souls of the dead; for the dead be many and the living few.The dead do not come back.Never have the dead come back—save thou with thy wonder-tales.It is not meet that the dead come back, and should we permit it, great trouble may be our portion.”
纳姆－博克很了解他的人民，知道会议的决定是至高无上的。所以他任人把自己带到水边，在那里他被扔近他的海豹皮舟里，手里塞进了一只桨。一只迷途的野禽在朝海的某处鸣叫着，海浪软绵绵地冲上沙地留下凹面。暗淡的暮光笼罩着陆地和海面，太阳在北面闷烧着，朦胧混乱，笼罩着血红的迷雾。海鸥低飞。海上风猛烈刺骨地吹着，顺风方向大量的阴云预示着恶劣的天气。 Nam-Bok knew his people well and was aware that the voice of the council was supreme.So he allowed himself to be led down to the water’s edge, where he was put aboard his bidarka and a paddle thrust into his hand.A stray wild-fowl honked somewhere to seaward, and the surf broke limply and hollowly on the sand.A dim twilight brooded over land and water, and in the north the sun smouldered, vague and troubled, and draped about with blood-red mists.The gulls were flying low.The off-shore wind blew keen and chill, and the black-massed clouds behind it gave promise of bitter weather.
“你正式出海，”欧匹－关似神谕地唱着，“回到海上。因此获得平衡，而且所有事物都恢复法则。” “Out of the sea thou earnest,” Opee-Kwan chanted oracularly, “and back into the sea thou goest.Thus is balance achieved and all things brought to law.”
巴斯克－华－沃恩跛着走到泡痕并叫喊着，“我祝福你，纳姆－博克，为此你会记住我。” Bask-Wah-Wan limped to the froth-mark and cried, “I bless thee, Nam-Bok, for that thou remembered me.”
而库加将纳姆－博克推离了海滩，扯下肩膀上的披肩，将它扔进了海豹皮舟。 But Koogah, shoving Nam-Bok clear of the beach, tore the shawl from her shoulders and flung it into the bidarka.
“长夜寒冷，”她哭号着，“霜冻容易冻伤老骨头。” “It is cold in the long nights,” she wailed; “and the frost is prone to nip old bones.”
“那个东西是一个幽灵，”雕刻骨头的人回答道，“幽灵不能帮你保暖。” “The thing is a shadow,” the bone-scratcher answered, “and shadows cannot keep thee warm.”
纳姆－博克站起来，那样他的声音会传到。“老巴斯克－华－沃恩，生下我的妈妈！”他喊道，“听着纳姆－博克，你儿子的话。他的海豹皮舟上有两个人的地方，他想要你跟他一起走。因为他要旅行去有充足的鱼和油的地方。那里不会有霜冻，生活轻松，铁物为人们劳作。你要来吗，老巴斯克－华－沃恩？” Nam-Bok stood up that his voice might carry.“O Bask-Wah-Wan, mother that bore me!” he called.“Listen to the words of Nam-Bok, thy son.There be room in his bidarka for two, and he would that thou camest with him.For his journey is to where there are fish and oil in plenty.There the frost comes not, and life is easy, and the things of iron do the work of men.Wilt thou come, O Bask-Wah-Wan?”
她思考了一会儿，当海豹皮舟迅速漂离时，她的声音提高到颤抖的最高音。“我老了，纳姆－博克，很快我就会加入到幽灵中去。但我不希望在死前离开。我老了，纳姆－博克，我害怕。” She debated a moment, while the bidarka drifted swiftly from her, then raised her voice to a quavering treble.“I am old, Nam-Bok, and soon I shall pass down among the shadows.But I have no wish to go before my time.I am old, Nam-Bok, and I am afraid.”
一束光掠过昏暗的海面，将船和人裹在红色和金色的光彩中。此刻，渔民鸦雀无声，只听到海上风的哀怨声和在空中低飞的海鸥的叫声。 A shaft of light shot across the dim-lit sea and wrapped boat and man in a splendor of red and gold.Then a hush fell upon the fisherfolk, and only was heard the moan of the off-shore wind and the cries of the gulls flying low in the air.
