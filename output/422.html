我生命的转折点 The Turning-Point of My Life
作者：马克·吐温 by Mark Twain
一 I
如果我没理解错的话，《集市》杂志想邀请我们几个针对上面这个标题写些东西。 If I understand the idea, the BAZAR invites several of us to write upon the above text.
这个题目指的是在我的生命历程中，哪个环境我认为对我职业生涯最为重要。 It means the change in my life’s course which introduced what must be regarded by me as the most IMPORTANT condition of my career.
但它也暗示着——或许不是有意的——那个转折点本身创造了新环境。 But it also implies—without intention, perhaps—that that turning-point ITSELF was the creator of the new condition.
这使它太与众不同，赋予了它太大的重要性，让它承载了太多的功勋。 This gives it too much distinction, too much prominence, too much credit.
它只是这条由许多转折点构成的长链上的最后一个环节，而这条长链的形成是为了产生那个最重要的结果。它的重要性并不亚于它前面一万个环节中最不起眼的那一个。 It is only the LAST link in a very long chain of turning-points commissioned to produce the cardinal result; it is not any more important than the humblest of its ten thousand predecessors.
这一万个环节，每一个都在指定的日子为推动进程贡献了自己被指定的那一份力量，它们都是必不可少的。抹杀其中的任何一环都可能毁掉整个进程，产生其他的结果。 Each of the ten thousand did its appointed share, on its appointed date, in forwarding the scheme, and they were all necessary; to have left out any one of them would have defeated the scheme and brought about SOME OTHER result.
我知道，我们总爱说“某某事件是我人生的一个转折点”，但我们不应该这样说。 I know we have a fashion of saying “such and such an event was the turning-point in my life,” but we shouldn’t say it.
我们只能承认：它处于长链最后一环的位置使它成为最突出的那一环；但就真正的重要性而言，它并不比它前面的任何一个更重要。 We should merely grant that its place as LAST link in the chain makes it the most CONSPICUOUS link; in real importance it has no advantage over any one of its predecessors.
也许史上记载的最著名的转折点要数渡过鲁比肯河了。 Perhaps the most celebrated turning-point recorded in history was the crossing of the Rubicon.
苏维托尼乌斯这样写道： Suetonius says:
他和他的军队来到了鲁比肯河河岸，他停了一会儿，脑子里反复想着他将要走的这一步具有的重要意义，于是他转过身对身边的士兵说道：“现在我们还可以撤退，可一旦我们跨过这座小桥，除了决一死战，我们别无选择。” Coming up with his troops on the banks of the Rubicon, he halted for a while, and, revolving in his mind the importance of the step he was on the point of taking, he turned to those about him and said, “We may still retreat; but if we pass this little bridge, nothing is left for us but to fight it out in arms.”
这是一个极其重要的时刻。 This was a stupendously important moment.
恺撒在这之前的生命中所经历的大大小小的事件，一个阶段接着一个阶段，一环扣着一环，促成了这一刻。 And all the incidents, big and little, of Caesar’s previous life had been leading up to it, stage by stage, link by link.
这是最后的一个环节——仅仅只是最后的那一个，并没有比其他的那些环节更重要。但是当我们回顾历史时，想象的薄雾却将它放大了，巨大无比，就像海王星的轨道一样大。 This was the LAST link—merely the last one, and no bigger than the others; but as we gaze back at it through the inflating mists of our imagination, it looks as big as the orbit of Neptune.
亲爱的读者，这一环节牵涉到你的个人利益，对我也是，对其他所有的人类也如此。 You, the reader, have a PERSONAL interest in that link, and so have I; so has the rest of the human race.
它是你生命链中的一环，也是我生命链中的一环。 It was one of the links in your life-chain, and it was one of the links in mine.
现在，恺撒在思索，我们不妨屏住呼吸等待着。 We may wait, now, with bated breath, while Caesar reflects.
他的决定牵涉到你我的命运。 Your fate and mine are involved in his decision.
就在他犹豫不决的时候，下面的事情发生了。 While he was thus hesitating, the following incident occurred.
一个举止优雅、气质高贵的人走到附近，坐下来吹起了风笛。 A person remarked for his noble mien and graceful aspect appeared close at hand, sitting and playing upon a pipe.
不仅仅是牧羊人，还有许多士兵都涌过去听他吹笛子，其中还有一些号兵。这个吹笛子的人从一个号兵夺过军号，一边跑向鲁比肯河一边吹，嘹亮的冲锋号声响了起来，传向河对岸。 When not only the shepherds, but a number of soldiers also, flocked to listen to him, and some trumpeters among them, he snatched a trumpet from one of them, ran to the river with it, and, sounding the advance with a piercing blast, crossed to the other side.
一听到号声，恺撒便大声喊道：“让我们冲向那个众神预示的地方，让我们冲向邪恶敌人召唤我们去的地方。 Upon this, Caesar exclaimed: “Let us go whither the omens of the gods and the iniquity of our enemies call us.
我们只能拼死一战了。” THE DIE IS CAST.”
于是他过了河——永远改变了整个人类的未来。 So he crossed—and changed the future of the whole human race, for all time.
但那个路人也是恺撒生命链中的一环，必不可少的一环。 But that stranger was a link in Caesar’s life-chain, too; and a necessary one.
我们不知道他的名字，也没有再听说过他；他是偶然出现在那里的。他看上去像一个偶然事件，但他又不纯粹是一个偶然，他受他自己的生命链的驱使而来，吹响了那振奋人心的号角。那号角促使恺撒下定决心，从此这号声回荡在历史的长廊，不曾消逝。 We don’t know his name, we never hear of him again; he was very casual; he acts like an accident; but he was no accident, he was there by compulsion of HIS life-chain, to blow the electrifying blast that was to make up Caesar’s mind for him, and thence go piping down the aisles of history forever.
假如这个过路的人没有出现在那里，历史会变成什么样！ If the stranger hadn’t been there!
但是他确确实实出现在了那里。 But he WAS.
而且恺撒也过了河。 And Caesar crossed.
于是带来了这样的结果。 With such results!
浩瀚如烟的历史事件——每件事都是人类的生命链上的一环。每一件事都能引发下一个，下一个又引发了下下个，这样继续下去：共和国的毁灭、帝国的建立、帝国的瓦解、基督教在帝国废墟上的崛起、基督教向其他地方的传播——如此等等。这样一环一环在指定时间和指定地点依次发生，美洲的发现便是其中一环，英国移民以及其他移民流入美洲也是一环，他们的西进（我的祖先也在他们中间）又是一环，他们中的一些人定居在密苏里，结果是，我出现在了那里。 Such vast events—each a link in the HUMAN RACE’S life-chain; each event producing the next one, and that one the next one, and so on: the destruction of the republic; the founding of the empire; the breaking up of the empire; the rise of Christianity upon its ruins; the spread of the religion to other lands—and so on; link by link took its appointed place at its appointed time, the discovery of America being one of them; our Revolution another; the inflow of English and other immigrants another; their drift westward (my ancestors among them) another; the settlement of certain of them in Missouri, which resulted in ME.
我也是渡过鲁比肯河后的必然结果。 For I was one of the unavoidable results of the crossing of the Rubicon.
如果这个陌生人以及他的号声没有存在过（他不可能这样，因为他是既定的环节），恺撒也没有过河。 If the stranger, with his trumpet blast, had stayed away (which he COULDN’T, for he was the appointed link) Caesar would not have crossed.
如果那样，后面会发生什么事情，我们无法猜测。 What would have happened, in that case, we can never guess.
我们只知道那些发生过了的事就不会发生了。 We only know that the things that did happen would not have happened.
他们也会被同样的惊人的事件所取代，但它们的性质以及结果会是怎样，谁也不知道。 They might have been replaced by equally prodigious things, of course, but their nature and results are beyond our guessing.
但我个人感兴趣的是，我现在将不在这里了，而是在其他的地方，也许我会是黑人——谁也说不准。 But the matter that interests me personally is that I would not be HERE now, but somewhere else; and probably black—there is no telling.
非常好，我很高兴他过了河。 Very well, I am glad he crossed.
我是发自内心的由衷的感激，尽管我以前对此一点儿也不关心。 And very really and thankfully glad, too, though I never cared anything about it before.
二 II
就我而言，文学是我生命中最重要的一个特点。 To me, the most important feature of my life is its literary feature.
我成为职业文人已经四十多年了。 I have been professionally literary something more than forty years.
我的生命中有许许多多的转折点，但是，那个注定将我引入文学殿堂的一环是链条中最突出的一环。 There have been many turning-points in my life, but the one that was the link in the chain appointed to conduct me to the literary guild is the most CONSPICUOUS link in that chain.
因为它是最后的一环。 BECAUSE it was the last one.
它并不比它前面的环节更重要。 It was not any more important than its predecessors.
除了渡过鲁比肯河，其他所有环节都不起眼；但作为促使我成为文人的因素，它们具有同等的重要性，也包括渡过鲁比肯河。 All the other links have an inconspicuous look, except the crossing of the Rubicon; but as factors in making me literary they are all of the one size, the crossing of the Rubicon included.
我很清楚自己是怎样步入文学界的，下面我会告诉你们导致并引起这个结果的步骤。 I know how I came to be literary, and I will tell the steps that lead up to it and brought it about.
渡过鲁比肯河并不是第一步，甚至算不上最近的那一步。为了找到第一环，我本应该追溯到恺撒时代以前。 The crossing of the Rubicon was not the first one, it was hardly even a recent one; I should have to go back ages before Caesar’s day to find the first one.
但为了节省篇幅，我仅仅追溯到几代以前，就从我的童年时代的一件事情说起吧。 To save space I will go back only a couple of generations and start with an incident of my boyhood.
就在我十二岁半的时候，我的父亲死了。 When I was twelve and a half years old, my father died.
那时正值春天。 It was in the spring.
夏天到来了，流行性麻疹也随之而来。 The summer came, and brought with it an epidemic of measles.
那阵子，几乎每天都有孩子死掉。 For a time a child died almost every day.
村子里充斥着恐惧、悲痛、绝望，陷入了瘫痪状态。 The village was paralyzed with fright, distress, despair.
那些没有染上麻疹的孩子被关在家里以免他们被传染。 Children that were not smitten with the disease were imprisoned in their homes to save them from the infection.
家里没有了笑脸，除了庄严的圣歌外没有别的歌声，除了虔诚的祈祷外没有别的声音，没有嬉闹，没有嘈杂，没有笑声，家里人轻手轻脚地走着，仿佛幽灵一般。 In the homes there were no cheerful faces, there was no music, there was no singing but of solemn hymns, no voice but of prayer, no romping was allowed, no noise, no laughter, the family moved spectrally about on tiptoe, in a ghostly hush.
我仿佛成了囚犯。 I was a prisoner.
我的灵魂沉浸在这可怕的沉寂之中——我感到十分恐惧。 My soul was steeped in this awful dreariness—and in fear.
每天每夜，或者在某个时刻，一种突如其来的战栗就会深入我的骨髓，这个时候我对自己说：“看吧，我被传染了，我要死了。” At some time or other every day and every night a sudden shiver shook me to the marrow, and I said to myself, “There, I’ve got it! and I shall die.”
生活在这样悲伤的环境下还有什么意思，最后我决定想办法染上这种病，不管怎样，我要结束这种日子。 Life on these miserable terms was not worth living, and at last I made up my mind to get the disease and have it over, one way or the other.
我从家里逃了出来，跑到邻居家里去，他们家里有我的一个玩伴，他也染上了麻疹，身体极度虚弱。 I escaped from the house and went to the house of a neighbor where a playmate of mine was very ill with the malady.
趁着这次机会，我溜进他的房间，和他躺在一起。 When the chance offered I crept into his room and got into bed with him.
后来，他的母亲发现了我，把我送回去关了起来。 I was discovered by his mother and sent back into captivity.
但我还是染上了麻疹，他们没办法改变这个事实。 But I had the disease; they could not take that from me.
我快死了。 I came near to dying.
整个村子都对我的行为十分感兴趣，他们十分好奇，每天都派人打听我的消息，一天不止一次，而是好几次。 The whole village was interested, and anxious, and sent for news of me every day; and not only once a day, but several times.
所有人都认为我会死，但在第十四天的时候，我转危为安，让他们失望了。 Everybody believed I would die; but on the fourteenth day a change came for the worse and they were disappointed.
这是我生命的一个转折点。 This was a turning-point of my life.
（第一个环节） (Link number one.)
因为我康复后，母亲终止了我的学业，让我给一个印刷工当学徒。 For when I got well my mother closed my school career and apprenticed me to a printer.
母亲一直想让我不再淘气，但她被弄烦了，在我染病的惊险事件后，她决定把我交给一个比她更能管住我的人。 She was tired of trying to keep me out of mischief, and the adventure of the measles decided her to put me into more masterful hands than hers.
我成了印刷工，开始为我以文学为职业的生命链上又添上了一环。 I became a printer, and began to add one link after another to the chain which was to lead me into the literary profession.
这是一条漫长的道路，但我那时是不可能知道这些的。我不知道这条道路通往一个目的地，甚至我根本不知道应该有一个目的地，所以我是漠不关心的。 A long road, but I could not know that; and as I did not know what its goal was, or even that it had one, I was indifferent.
我还有些满足。 Also contented.
年轻的印刷工漫无目的地寻觅工作，必要时还要再找一份工作。 A young printer wanders around a good deal, seeking and finding work; and seeking again, when necessity commands.
注： N. B.
必要性是一种环境，环境主宰着你——当环境发出指令时，你唯有服从；你也许可以争辩——那是你的特权，但就像一个正在垂落的人争辩引力的存在一样，不会有任何的好处，你只能服从。 Necessity is a CIRCUMSTANCE; Circumstance is man’s master—and when Circumstance commands, he must obey; he may argue the matter—that is his privilege, just as it is the honorable privilege of a falling body to argue with the attraction of gravitation—but it won’t do any good, he must OBEY.
在环境的指引和支配下，我游荡了十年，最后来到了艾奥瓦州的一个城市，在那里工作了几个月。 I wandered for ten years, under the guidance and dictatorship of Circumstance, and finally arrived in a city of Iowa, where I worked several months.
那时，这些书中我感兴趣的是有关亚马逊河的一本书。 Among the books that interested me in those days was one about the Amazon.
旅行者讲述了他沿着这条大河进行漫长旅行中发生的诱人故事。他从帕拉走到马代拉河的发源地，穿越了一片充满魔力的腹地。那里充满了丰富的热带奇观：有各种各样的花、鸟和动物，好像一个博物馆；那里有短吻鳄、鳄鱼和猴子，自由自在，好像在动物园中一样。 The traveler told an alluring tale of his long voyage up the great river from Para to the sources of the Madeira, through the heart of an enchanted land, a land wastefully rich in tropical wonders, a romantic land where all the birds and flowers and animals were of the museum varieties, and where the alligator and the crocodile and the monkey seemed as much at home as if they were in the Zoo.
他还讲了一个关于“古柯”的令人惊奇的故事。古柯是一种拥有超自然力量的蔬菜，它十分有营养，补充人的力量，马代拉山区的土著居民只要吃一点儿古柯粉，就可以上山下山一整天，不需要再补充别的东西了。 Also, he told an astonishing tale about COCA, a vegetable product of miraculous powers, asserting that it was so nourishing and so strength-giving that the native of the mountains of the Madeira region would tramp up hill and down all day on a pinch of powdered coca and require no other sustenance.
我渴望到亚马逊河去。 I was fired with a longing to ascend the Amazon.
我也渴望在全世界打开古柯市场。 Also with a longing to open up a trade in coca with all the world.
几个月中，我都一直那样梦想着，并尝试着寻找办法到达帕拉，好让这桩生意在坚实的基础上突见成效。 During months I dreamed that dream, and tried to contrive ways to get to Para and spring that splendid enterprise upon an unsuspecting planet.
但都失败了。 But all in vain.
一个人或许可以随心所欲地谋划，但是只要环境这个魔术师不插手其中，不亲自过问这件事情，任何计划都不太可能会有结果。 A person may PLAN as much as he wants to, but nothing of consequence is likely to come of it until the magician CIRCUMSTANCE steps in and takes the matter off his hands.
最后环境帮助了我。 At last Circumstance came to my help.
帮我的方式是这样的： It was in this way.
环境为了帮助或伤害另一个人，让他在街上丢了五十美元；环境为了帮助或伤害我，让我捡到了这五十美元。 Circumstance, to help or hurt another man, made him lose a fifty-dollar bill in the street; and to help or hurt me, made me find it.
我充分利用捡到的那五十美元，当天，我就离开，去了亚马逊河。 I advertised the find, and left for the Amazon the same day.
这是另一个转折点，也就是另一个环节。 This was another turning-point, another link.
环境能安排那个镇上的另外一个人去亚马逊河，让他以五十美元作为启动资金，打开古柯的世界贸易市场，并让他服从吗？ Could Circumstance have ordered another dweller in that town to go to the Amazon and open up a world-trade in coca on a fifty-dollar basis and been obeyed?
不，只有我一个。 No, I was the only one.
那里还有其他的愚人——很多很多——但他们不同于我。 There were other fools there—shoals and shoals of them—but they were not of my kind.
对我这种类型而言，我是唯一的。 I was the only one of my kind.
环境是强大的，但它不能单独工作，他需要一个搭档。 Circumstance is powerful, but it cannot work alone; it has to have a partner.
它的搭档是人的性情，即人的天性。 Its partner is man’s TEMPERAMENT—his natural disposition.
人的性情不是自己创造的，而是人天生就有的，人无法控制它，也不用对性情做出的行为负责。 His temperament is not his invention, it is BORN in him, and he has no authority over it, neither is he responsible for its acts.
人不能改变性情，没有什么能改变它，没什么能改变它——除非这改变是暂时的。 He cannot change it, nothing can change it, nothing can modify it—except temporarily.
但还是不能永久性地改变它。 But it won’t stay modified.
性情是永久性的，就像人眼睛的颜色和耳朵的形状。 It is permanent, like the color of the man’s eyes and the shape of his ears.
蓝眼睛在某些特殊的光线下可能会变灰，可是一旦光线恢复正常后，又恢复了原来的颜色。 Blue eyes are gray in certain unusual lights; but they resume their natural color when that stress is removed.
可能对一个人起支配作用的一种环境，但对另一个人却没有影响。 A Circumstance that will coerce one man will have no effect upon a man of a different temperament.
如果环境将钞票扔在恺撒的路上，他的性情也不会促使他去亚马逊河的。 If Circumstance had thrown the bank-note in Caesar’s way, his temperament would not have made him start for the Amazon.
他的性情会迫使他用钱做些事，但不是去亚马逊河。 His temperament would have compelled him to do something with the money, but not that.
也许会让他看见这些钞票——然后等待。 It might have made him advertise the note—and WAIT.
我们不知道等待的结果是什么。 We can’t tell.
也许环境会使他去纽约，买下政府，结果就是轮到特威德上台时什么也捞不到。 Also, it might have made him go to New York and buy into the Government, with results that would leave Tweed nothing to learn when it came his turn.
好极了，环境为我带来了资本，而我的性情告诉我该用这些钱做些什么。 Very well, Circumstance furnished the capital, and my temperament told me what to do with it.
有时候性情就是一头笨驴。 Sometimes a temperament is an ass.
如果那头笨驴的主人正巧也是个蠢驴，那他以后也会继续如此。 When that is the case of the owner of it is an ass, too, and is going to remain one.
训练、经验和社交可以暂时打磨他、改善他、提高他，以至于人们认为他是一只骡子，但他们错了。 Training, experience, association, can temporarily so polish him, improve him, exalt him that people will think he is a mule, but they will be mistaken.
他暂时地、人为地成了个骡子，但骨子里他仍是头笨驴，而且会一直如此。 Artificially he IS a mule, for the time being, but at bottom he is an ass yet, and will remain one.
性情上，我属于实干型的人。 By temperament I was the kind of person that DOES things.
先做事情，然后再考虑。 Does them, and reflects afterward.
因此没多想，也没多问，我启程去了亚马逊河。 So I started for the Amazon without reflecting and without asking any questions.
那是五十多年以前。 That was more than fifty years ago.
整个那段时间里，我的性情一直都没有变，一点儿都没有。 In all that time my temperament has not changed, by even a shade.
因为先做事情后思考，我被重重地惩罚了很多次。但这些折磨对我来说没有影响，我仍然做着环境和性情支配我做的事情，然后再思考。 I have been punished many and many a time, and bitterly, for doing things and reflecting afterward, but these tortures have been of no value to me; I still do the thing commanded by Circumstance and Temperament, and reflect afterward.
我的反思总是十分激烈。 Always violently.
当我反思着以前干的事情时，即使是聋子都能听到我在思考。 When I am reflecting, on these occasions, even deaf persons can hear me think.
我经过辛辛那提市，然后到了俄亥俄州和密西西比河。 I went by the way of Cincinnati, and down the Ohio and Mississippi.
我想从新奥尔良乘船到帕拉。 My idea was to take ship, at New Orleans, for Para.
在新奥尔良经过打听，我发现没有到帕拉的船。 In New Orleans I inquired, and found there was no ship leaving for Para.
而且，一直以来都没有去帕拉的船。 Also, that there never had BEEN one leaving for Para.
我思考着。 I reflected.
一个警察来了，问我在干什么，我告诉了他。 A policeman came and asked me what I was doing, and I told him.
他赶我走，告诉我如果再让他发现我在公众的大街上思考，他就抓我去警察局。 He made me move on, and said if he caught me reflecting in the public street again he would run me in.
几天后，我的钱用完了。 After a few days I was out of money.
环境出现了，带来了我生命中的另一个转折点——新的一环。 Then Circumstance arrived, with another turning-point of my life—a new link.
当我走下坡路时，我认识了一个引航员。 On my way down, I had made the acquaintance of a pilot.
我请他教我开船，他同意了。 I begged him to teach me the river, and he consented.
我成了一个引航员。 I became a pilot.
不久，为了将我的文学生涯推向另一个阶段，环境又来了——这次带来了内战。 By and by Circumstance came again—introducing the Civil War, this time, in order to push me ahead another stage or two toward the literary profession.
船只停运了，我又没办法谋生了。 The boats stopped running, my livelihood was gone.
环境带来了新的转折点和新的环节，帮助了我。 Circumstance came to the rescue with a new turning-point and a fresh link.
我的兄弟被派往内华达州新成立的土地局工作。他请我一起去帮他办公。 My brother was appointed secretary to the new Territory of Nevada, and he invited me to go with him and help him in his office.
我同意了。 I accepted.
在内华达州，环境为我提供了那次“白银热”，我去了矿井，料想能够发财；但事实不是那么回事。 In Nevada, Circumstance furnished me the silver fever and I went into the mines to make a fortune, as I supposed; but that was not the idea.
这想法让我向文学又迈进了一步。 The idea was to advance me another step toward literature.
出于兴趣，我给弗吉尼亚州的《企业报》胡乱地写了点儿东西。 For amusement I scribbled things for the Virginia City ENTERPRISE.
一个人要是当了十年的印刷工，却没拼凑过许多好文章和差文章，没有学会区分优劣文章，起初是无意地学，后来则是有意识地学，同时也没有于无意识中形成自己文章的所谓的“风格”，那他就算是白干了。 One isn’t a printer ten years without setting up acres of good and bad literature, and learning—unconsciously at first, consciously later—to discriminate between the two, within his mental limitations; and meantime he is unconsciously acquiring what is called a “style.”
我的一篇稿子让人注意到了，《企业报》把我叫去，让我去做那里的员工。 One of my efforts attracted attention, and the ENTERPRISE sent for me and put me on its staff.
因此我成了记者——又是一个环节。 And so I became a journalist—another link.
不久之后，环境和萨克拉门托的《联合报》派我去桑威奇群岛五六个月，详细报道白糖的事情。 By and by Circumstance and the Sacramento UNION sent me to the Sandwich Islands for five or six months, to write up sugar.
我去了，写了稿子，还添上了许多与糖没什么关联的、不相干的事。 I did it; and threw in a good deal of extraneous matter that hadn’t anything to do with sugar.
但是正是这些不相干的事帮我进入了下一个环节。 But it was this extraneous matter that helped me to another link.
这些稿子让我出名了，于是旧金山邀请我去演讲。 It made me notorious, and San Francisco invited me to lecture.
我去了。 Which I did.
并且赚了不少。 And profitably.
我想环游世界已经很久了，现在环境友好而出人意外地将我推向舞台，并为我提供了一些收入。 I had long had a desire to travel and see the world, and now Circumstance had most kindly and unexpectedly hurled me upon the platform and furnished me the means.
因此我加入了“震教城旅行团”。 So I joined the “Quaker City Excursion.”
当我回到美国时，环境在码头等我——带来最后一个环节——那个引人注目的、完美的、胜利的环节：有人请我写书，我写了，书名叫《傻子在国外》。 When I returned to America, Circumstance was waiting on the pier—with the LAST link—the conspicuous, the consummating, the victorious link: I was asked to WRITE A BOOK, and I did it, and called it THE INNOCENTS ABROAD.
因此我终于进入了文学这个行业。 Thus I became at last a member of the literary guild.
那是四十二年前，从那之后我一直是文人。 That was forty-two years ago, and I have been a member ever since.
不算鲁比肯河事件，我能真心地说，我之所以走上文学之路是因为十二岁时我得了麻疹。 Leaving the Rubicon incident away back where it belongs, I can say with truth that the reason I am in the literary profession is because I had the measles when I was twelve years old.
三 III
现在看待这些细节时，吸引我的不是细节本身，而是它们中没有一个是我预知到的，也不是我计划的，我不是它们的创造者。 Now what interests me, as regards these details, is not the details themselves, but the fact that none of them was foreseen by me, none of them was planned by me, I was the author of none of them.
环境与我的性情一起合作，创造和支配了这些细节。 Circumstance, working in harness with my temperament, created them all and compelled them all.
我经常怀着良好的用意予以帮助，但被拒绝了——一般是毫无礼貌地拒绝了。 I often offered help, and with the best intentions, but it was rejected—as a rule, uncourteously.
我不曾计划过某事，然后让它按照我的计划发生。 I could never plan a thing and get it to come out the way I planned it.
而它则以其他的方式出现——以我不曾想过的方式。 It came out some other way—some way I had not counted upon.
对于人类——作为智力的一种奇迹——我并不像年轻时那样钦佩了，我从书本中认识人类，但却和活人没什么私交。 And so I do not admire the human being—as an intellectual marvel—as much as I did when I was young, and got him out of books, and did not know him personally.
以前读到某某将军做了某一件聪明的事，我就相信。 When I used to read that such and such a general did a certain brilliant thing, I believed it.
然而事情却不是那样的。 Whereas it was not so.
是环境在他的性情的帮助下做到这些。 Circumstance did it by help of his temperament.
环境也许会因为将军具有别的性情而失败，因为环境也许看得到机会，但却因为将军的慢性子、急性子或生性多疑而错失有利条件。 The circumstances would have failed of effect with a general of another temperament: he might see the chance, but lose the advantage by being by nature too slow or too quick or too doubtful.
公众和报纸曾热烈讨论过一个事件，一次有人就此事向格兰特将军请教他的看法；他毫不犹豫地回答了。 Once General Grant was asked a question about a matter which had been much debated by the public and the newspapers; he answered the question without any hesitancy.
“将军，谁策划了向佐治亚州进军？” “General, who planned the the march through Georgia?”
“敌人！” “The enemy!”
他还说敌人通常为你制定了计划。 He added that the enemy usually makes your plans for you.
他的意思是敌人可能因为疏忽，或者因为环境的力量为你打开了一扇窗，然后你发现了它并加以利用。 He meant that the enemy by neglect or through force of circumstances leaves an opening for you, and you see your chance and take advantage of it.
毫无疑问，在性情的帮助下，环境为我们计划了一切。 Circumstances do the planning for us all, no doubt, by help of our temperaments.
我认为人和手表之间没什么不同，除了人是有意识的，而手表没有；人试着计划事情，而手表没有。 I see no great difference between a man and a watch, except that the man is conscious and the watch isn’t, and the man TRIES to plan things and the watch doesn’t.
手表不能给自己上弦，也不能给自己对时——这些都是来自手表外部的力量。 The watch doesn’t wind itself and doesn’t regulate itself—these things are done exteriorly.
外部影响和外在环境给人上弦，给人对时。 Outside influences, outside circumstances, wind the MAN and regulate him.
只靠人自己，他根本无法给自己对时，而他自己给自己计算的时间没有价值。 Left to himself, he wouldn’t get regulated at all, and the sort of time he would keep would not be valuable.
少数的人是极好的手表，有金壳，有很好的对时装置，还有一些人仅仅是简单的、走时准确的、便宜的沃特伯里手表。 Some rare men are wonderful watches, with gold case, compensation balance, and all those things, and some men are only simple and sweet and humble Waterburys.
我就是一块沃特伯里手表。 I am a Waterbury.
就是有的人说的那种沃特伯里手表。 A Waterbury of that kind, some say.
一个国家仅仅是个人的乘积。 A nation is only an individual multiplied.
国家制定计划，环境来了后，破坏了计划——或者是扩大了计划。 It makes plans and Circumstances comes and upsets them—or enlarges them.
一些爱国者把茶叶从船上丢掉；另一些爱国者摧毁了巴士底狱。 Some patriots throw the tea overboard; some other patriots destroy a Bastille.
这些计划在那里停止；然后环境意外地出现了，将这些不大的暴乱演变成一场革命。 The PLANS stop there; then Circumstance comes in, quite unexpectedly, and turns these modest riots into a revolution.
所以有了不幸的哥伦布。 And there was poor Columbus.
他为开辟一条通往古老国家的新路线详细制订了一份周密的计划。 He elaborated a deep plan to find a new route to an old country.
环境修改了他的计划，让他发现了一块新大陆。 Circumstance revised his plan for him, and he found a new WORLD.
至今他因此受到赞扬。 And HE gets the credit of it to this day.
他与这件事其实没什么关系。 He hadn’t anything to do with it.
我的（以及各位读者的）生命中真正发生转折的地方是在伊甸园。 Necessarily the scene of the real turning-point of my life (and of yours) was the Garden of Eden.
第一个环节在那里形成了，最终导致我两手空空，只能投身文学。 It was there that the first link was forged of the chain that was ultimately to lead to the emptying of me into the literary guild.
亚当的性情是神在地球上向人类发布的第一个命令。 Adam’s TEMPERAMENT was the first command the Deity ever issued to a human being on this planet.
也是唯一一个亚当绝不能违背的命令。 And it was the only command Adam would NEVER be able to disobey.
它说：“人要柔弱，像水一样，没有主见，极容易被说服。” It said, “Be weak, be water, be characterless, be cheaply persuadable.”
最后那条命令造成了那个结果，它注定要被违背。 The latter command, to let the fruit alone, was certain to be disobeyed.
不是由于亚当自身，而是由于他的性情决定的——那性情是他无法创造的，也无法掌控的。 Not by Adam himself, but by his TEMPERAMENT—which he did not create and had no authority over.
因为性情才是人，而披着衣服、被命名为“人”的那种东西仅仅是它的影子，仅此而已。 For the TEMPERAMENT is the man; the thing tricked out with clothes and named Man is merely its Shadow, nothing more.
老虎的性情法则是“你要杀戮”；羊的性情法则是“你不应该杀戮”。 The law of the tiger’s temperament is, Thou shalt kill; the law of the sheep’s temperament is Thou shalt not kill.
为了遵循后一个命令，要老虎将陌生的肥羊搁置一旁，要羊的蹄子沾满狮子的鲜血是白费工夫，因为这些命令不可能被遵守的。 To issue later commands requiring the tiger to let the fat stranger alone, and requiring the sheep to imbue its hands in the blood of the lion is not worth while, for those commands CAN’T be obeyed.
他们会违背性情的法则，而性情的法则是至高无上的，凌驾于一切权威之上。 They would invite to violations of the law of TEMPERAMENT, which is supreme, and take precedence of all other authorities.
我不禁对亚当和夏娃感到失望。 I cannot help feeling disappointed in Adam and Eve.
也就是，对他们的性情感到失望。 That is, in their temperaments.
我不是对他们本人失望，他们是可怜的、无助的年轻人——受到性情的支配，他们的性情就像是黄油做的，一旦奉命与火接触就会融化。 Not in THEM, poor helpless young creatures—afflicted with temperaments made out of butter; which butter was commanded to get into contact with fire and BE MELTED.
我不由得希望，当初伊甸园里的不是亚当，而是由马丁·路德和贞德——他们是绝佳的一对，拥有石棉做的性情，而不是黄油做的性情。 What I cannot help wishing is, that Adam had been postponed, and Martin Luther and Joan of Arc put in their place—that splendid pair equipped with temperaments not made of butter, but of asbestos.
不论是诱人的劝说还是地狱之火，撒旦都无法欺骗他们，让他们吃下苹果。 By neither sugary persuasions nor by hell fire could Satan have beguiled THEM to eat the apple.
结果会大不一样！ There would have been results!
是的，确实。 Indeed, yes.
至今苹果还会完整无损；而地球上将不会有人类，不会有你，也不会有我。 The apple would be intact today; there would be no human race; there would be no YOU; there would be no ME.
这样的话，那个使我最终成为文人的、极为古老的初始规划也会失败。 And the old, old creation-dawn scheme of ultimately launching me into the literary guild would have been defeated.
