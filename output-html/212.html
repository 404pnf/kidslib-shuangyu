像古代的阿格斯 Like Argus of the Ancient Times
作者：杰克·伦敦 by Jack London
那是1897年的夏天，塔尔沃特家遇到了麻烦。在十分压抑且伤心地度过了安静的十年之后，塔尔沃特爷爷又爆发了。这次是克朗代克热。他这种病最先出现且一直不变的一个症状就是唱歌。他只唱一段歌曲，尽管他仅仅记得第一节，还只是这一节的三行歌词。当他提高他那沙哑的嗓音时，家人就知道他的脚有些痒痒了，脑子里也全是以前的疯狂往事。他现在就在用假音唱： It was the summer of 1897, and there was trouble in the Tarwater family.Grandfather Tarwater, after remaining properly subdued and crushed for a quiet decade, had broken out again.This time it was the Klondike fever.His first and one unvarying symptom of such attacks was song.One chant only he raised, though he remembered no more than the first stanza and but three lines of that.And the family knew his feet were itching and his brain was tingling with the old madness, when he lifted his hoarse-cracked voice, now falsetto-cracked, in:
就像古时候的阿格斯，我们离开了这个现代的希腊，嗒—嗒，嗒—嗒，嗒，嗒，嗒—嗒，来剪金羊毛。 Like Argus of the ancient times,We leave this modern Greece,Tum-tum, tum-tum, tum, tum, tum-tum,To shear the Golden Fleece.
早在十年前，配着“颂歌”的旋律，他就唱过这一段，那时候他正沉湎于去巴塔哥尼亚淘金的狂热中。这个人口众多的一家子曾开会讨论过如何对付他的这种病，但实施起来还是很困难。当其他所有人都不能动摇他的决心时，他们为他申请了律师，威胁他说，要拿出监护文件把他关在收纳精神失常者的州收容所里——这样对他也不是没有道理，因为在二十五年前，他因为投机买卖失败，只剩下了加利福尼亚州十英亩贫瘠的土地，从此以后，他再也没有展示出更好的生意头脑。 Ten years earlier he had lifted the chant, sung to the air of the “Doxology,” when afflicted with the fever to go gold-mining in Patagonia.The multitudinous family had sat upon him, but had had a hard time doing it.When all else had failed to shake his resolution, they had applied lawyers to him, with the threat of getting out guardianship papers and of confining him in the state asylum for the insane—which was reasonable for a man who had, a quarter of a century before, speculated away all but ten meagre acres of a California principality, and who had displayed no better business acumen ever since.
为约翰·塔尔沃特申请律师，就像给他涂抹芥末膏药一样，令他心有余悸。因为，在他看来，他们是贵族，比任何人都要高贵，他们只会把他赶出辽阔的塔尔沃特庄园。所以，当他沉浸于巴塔哥尼亚狂热时，那个烈性药方的想法就足以治好他。很快他就放弃了那种狂热，同意不去巴塔哥尼亚，以此表明他不是疯子。 The application of lawyers to John Tarwater was like the application of a mustard plaster.For, in his judgment, they were the gentry, more than any other, who had skinned him out of the broad Tarwater acres.So, at the time of his Patagonian fever, the very thought of so drastic a remedy was sufficient to cure him.He quickly demonstrated he was not crazy by shaking the fever from him and agreeing not to go to Patagonia.
然后，他又表明了他实际有多么疯狂，因为他主动立契约把私人财产转给他的家人，包括塔尔沃特套房的十英亩土地、房子、谷仓、附属建筑物以及水权。而且他还把银行里的八百美元也交了出来，那可是他从失事财富中抢救出的存了很长时间的钱。但正是因为这一点，他的家人就没理由把他送进收容所了，因为一旦把他送进收容所，一定会让他所做的一切都无效。 Next, he demonstrated how crazy he really was, by deeding over to his family, unsolicited, the ten acres on Tarwater Flat, the house, barn, outbuildings, and water-rights.Also did he turn over the eight hundred dollars in bank that was the long-saved salvage of his wrecked fortune.But for this the family found no cause for committal to the asylum, since such committal would necessarily invalidate what he had done.
“爷爷一定是不高兴了。”玛丽说。玛丽是他的大女儿，当她父亲戒烟的时候，她已经当奶奶了。 “Grandfather is sure peeved,” said Mary, his oldest daughter, herself a grandmother, when her father quit smoking.
他留给自己的所有东西就是几匹老马、一辆山地四轮马车，和拥挤房屋中的一间房。而且，他说过自己对他们没有义务，除了递送美国邮件的合同义务，每星期两次，得从凯尔特维尔翻过塔尔沃特山到达旧阿尔马登——那是一个在高地养牛区里间歇性开发过的水银矿。他就跟他的老马，每周两次地往返于这段路程。而且十年来，不论晴雨，他从没耽误过一次。而且他也从来没有落下每周给玛丽的伙食费用。这个伙食费是他坚持要给的，即使是在巴塔哥尼亚狂热症恢复期间，他都按时照付，虽然为了付这笔钱他得戒烟。 All he had retained for himself was a span of old horses, a mountain buckboard, and his one room in the crowded house.Further, having affirmed that he would be beholden to none of them, he got the contract to carry the United States mail, twice a week, from Kelterville up over Tarwater Mountain to Old Almaden—which was a sporadically worked quick-silver mine in the upland cattle country.With his old horses it took all his time to make the two weekly round trips.And for ten years, rain or shine, he had never missed a trip.Nor had he failed once to pay his week’s board into Mary’s hand.This board he had insisted on, in the convalescence from his Patagonian fever, and he had paid it strictly, though he had given up tobacco in order to be able to do it.
“哈！”他向这台老旧的塔尔沃特磨坊里毁坏的水车倾诉过心事。这座旧磨坊是他用建筑用材做的，而且还给第一批定居者磨过面粉。“哈！只要我还能养活自己，他们永远不会把我放到这个贫瘠的农场上的。而且我名下没有任何钱财，没有哪个律师会来帮我打官司。” “Huh!” he confided to the ruined water wheel of the old Tarwater Mill, which he had built from the standing timber and which had ground wheat for the first settlers.“Huh!They’ll never put me in the poor farm so long as I support myself.And without a penny to my name it ain’t likely any lawyer fellows’ll come snoopin’ around after me.”
但是，恰恰因为这些高度合理的行为，人们认为约翰·塔尔沃特有些疯癫了！ And yet, precisely because of these highly rational acts, it was held that John Tarwater was mildly crazy!
他第一次唱“像古代的阿格斯”这段，是在1849年，那时他二十二岁，正狂热地追逐着想去加利福尼亚的念头。他卖掉了在密歇根的二百四十英亩土地，其中有四十英亩是开发过的，总共只卖得了四头带轭的牛和一驾四轮马车，然后他就开始驾车穿越大平原。 The first time he had lifted the chant of “Like Argus of the Ancient Times,” had been in 1849, when, twenty-two years’ of age, violently attacked by the Californian fever, he had sold two hundred and forty Michigan acres, forty of it cleared, for the price of four yoke of oxen, and a wagon, and had started across the Plains.
“然后我们在霍尔堡改变了方向，在那儿俄勒冈的移民往北走，我们则向南驶往加利福尼亚。”他讲述那段艰苦旅程时总结道。而比尔·平和我过去常常在萨克拉门托山谷里，用绳索捕猎藏在卡什沼矮灌木丛中的灰熊。” “And we turned off at Fort Hall, where the Oregon emigration went north’ard, and swung south for Californy,” was his way of concluding the narrative of that arduous journey.And Bill Ping and me used to rope grizzlies out of the underbrush of Cache Slough in the Sacramento Valley.”
接下来的几年他一直运送货物和挖矿山，用从默塞德矿区得到的资金，他满足了自己所在种族占有土地的欲望，并在索诺马县定居下来。 Years of freighting and mining had followed, and, with a stake gleaned from the Merced placers, he satisfied the land-hunger of his race and time by settling in Sonoma County.
在送信的十年间，他的脚步遍及塔尔沃特镇、塔尔沃特山谷和塔尔沃特山，而这其中的大部分土地都曾属于他，他也一直梦想着在他离世之前，再赢回这些土地。他高大、憔悴的身躯要比几年前更为挺拔，一双小眼睛间距很窄，里面闪耀着蓝色的光芒。此刻，他又唱起了那段古老的歌曲。 During the ten years of carrying the mail across Tarwater Township, up Tarwater Valley, and over Tarwater Mountain, most all of which land had once been his, he had spent his time dreaming of winning back that land before he died.And now, his huge gaunt form more erect than it had been for years, with a glinting of blue fires in his small and close-set eyes, he was lifting his ancient chant again.
“又来了——听他唱。”威廉·塔尔沃特说道。 “There he goes now—listen to him,” said William Tarwater.
“家里没人。”哈里斯·托平笑道，他是家里的临时工、安妮·塔尔沃特的丈夫和她九个孩子的父亲。 “Nobody at home,” laughed Harris Topping, day labourer, husband of Annie Tarwater, and father of her nine children.
厨房的门开了，老人走进来，他刚喂完马回来。歌声已经停止；但是玛丽开始生气了，因为她烧伤了手，而孙子也把稀释过的牛奶吐了一地。 The kitchen door opened to admit the old man, returning from feeding his horses.The song had ceased from his lips; but Mary was irritable from a burnt hand and a grandchild whose stomach refused to digest properly diluted cows’ milk.
“现在你继续这样已经没用了，父亲，”她说他，“再去克朗代克那样的地方，对你已经不合适了，而且唱歌不会给你带来任何东西。” “Now there ain’t no use you carryin’ on that way, father,” she tackled him.“The time’s past for you to cut and run for a place like the Klondike, and singing won’t buy you nothing.”
“是一样的，”他平静地答道，“我打赌，我能再到克朗代克那块地方，淘到足够的金子买回塔尔沃特的土地。” “Just the same,” he answered quietly.“I bet I could go to that Klondike place and pick up enough gold to buy back the Tarwater lands.”
“老糊涂！”安妮说道。 “Old fool!” Annie contributed.
“少于三十万你是买不回那些土地的。”威廉努力让他冷静下来。 “You couldn’t buy them back for less’n three hundred thousand and then some,” was William’s effort at squelching him.
“但只要我能到那儿，就至少可以挣到三十万。”老人平静地反驳道。 “Then I could pick up three hundred thousand, and then some, if I was only there,” the old man retorted placidly.
“感谢老天你不可能徒步走到那儿，否则你真会这么干，我知道的。”玛丽喊道，“海上旅行是要花钱的。” “Thank God you can’t walk there, or you’d be startin’, I know,” Mary cried.“Ocean travel costs money.”
“我过去有钱。”她父亲低声下气地说道。 “I used to have money,” her father said humbly.
“但你现在一点儿钱都没有了——所以忘了这件事吧。”威廉建议道，“都是过去的事了，就像曾经和比尔·平用绳索捉熊一样。现在也没熊了。” “Well, you ain’t got any now—so forget it,” William advised.“Them times is past, like roping bear with Bill Ping.There ain’t no more bear.”
“都是一样的——” “Just the same—”
但是玛丽打断了他。她从厨房的桌子上抓过今天的报纸，粗鲁地在她年迈的父亲鼻子底下挥舞着。 But Mary cut him off.Seizing the day’s paper from the kitchen table, she flourished it savagely under her aged progenitor’s nose.
“那些克朗代克人说什么了？这上面清清楚楚地写着呢。只有血气方刚的年轻人才能受得了克朗代克地区的天气。那里的天气比北极的还恶劣。他们把大批死去的同伴留在了那里。看看他们的照片。你比他们当中年纪最大的都要大上四十岁。” “What do those Klondikers say?There it is in cold print.Only the young and robust can stand the Klondike.It’s worse than the north pole.And they’ve left their dead a-plenty there themselves.Look at their pictures.You’re forty years older ’n the oldest of them.”
约翰·塔尔沃特还真看了起来，但是他的目光游移到了引人入胜的头版照片上。 John Tarwater did look, but his eyes strayed to other photographs on the highly sensational front page.
“看看他们敲落金砖的照片，”他说，“我对金子了解得很。我不是从默塞德挣过两万吗？如果不是那场突如其来的大暴雨冲毁了我的翼堤，我会挣不到十万吗？现在，只要我在克朗代克——” “And look at the photys of them nuggets they brought down,” he said.“I know gold.Didn’t I gopher twenty thousand outa the Merced?And wouldn’t it a-ben a hundred thousand if that cloudburst hadn’t busted my wing-dam?Now if I was only in the Klondike—”
“完全疯了。”威廉对着其余的人公开嘲笑道。 “Crazy as a loon,” William sneered in open aside to the rest.
“跟你父亲说话要礼貌些，”老塔尔沃特温和地责备说，“如果我那样跟我父亲说话，他会用木棍打得我鼻青脸肿。” “A nice way to talk to your father,” Old Man Tarwater censured mildly.“My father’d have walloped the tar out of me with a single-tree if I’d spoke to him that way.”
“但是你真的疯了，父亲——”威廉开始说道。 “But you ARE crazy, father—” William began.
“估计你是对的，儿子。这点倒是我父亲还明白的地方。他肯定会揍你。” “Reckon you’re right, son.And that’s where my father wasn’t crazy.He’d a-done it.”
“老家伙正在读一些杂志上的文章，说的是四十岁以后的成功男人。”安妮嘲笑道。 “The old man’s been reading some of them magazine articles about men who succeeded after forty,” Annie jibed.
“为什么不可以呢，安妮？”老人问道，“为什么一个男人在他七十岁之后，就不能成功呢？我今年才七十岁。而且如果我可以去克朗代克的话，也许我可以成功——” “And why not, daughter?” he asked.“And why can’t a man succeed after he’s seventy?I was only seventy this year.And mebbe I could succeed if only I could get to the Klondike—”
“但是你不可以去。”玛丽打断了他。 “Which you ain’t going to get to,” Mary shut him off.
“哦，嗯，那么，”他叹气道，“既然我不能，我也只能去睡觉了。” “Oh, well, then,” he sighed, “seein’s I ain’t, I might just as well go to bed.”
他站了起来，身子高大憔悴，骨骼硬朗，而且关节突出，饱经人间沧桑。他参差不齐的头发和胡须不再是灰白，而是雪白了，就跟长在他宽大瘦削的手指背上的一撮撮毛发一样。他走向门口，打开门，叹了口气，又停下来回头看了一眼。 He stood up, tall, gaunt, great-boned and gnarled, a splendid ruin of a man.His ragged hair and whiskers were not grey but snowy white, as were the tufts of hair that stood out on the backs of his huge bony fingers.He moved toward the door, opened it, sighed, and paused with a backward look.
“还是一样的，”他哀伤地低语道，“我的脚底板还是痒得很。” “Just the same,” he murmured plaintively, “the bottoms of my feet is itching something terrible.”
早在第二天全家人热闹起来之前，老塔尔沃特就已经打着灯笼喂了马，整理好了马具，做好了早饭。在油灯下吃过饭后他就走了，经过塔尔沃特山谷，前往凯尔特维尔。自从签下邮差合同后，这段寻常的路程他已经走过一千零四十次了，但是这回有两点不寻常。他没有驾车前往凯尔特维尔，而是在主路换了方向，往南去了圣罗莎。甚至比这更值得注意的是，放在他两脚间用纸包着的包裹。包裹里有一套体面的黑色套装，玛丽有很长时间都不愿再看他穿这套衣服了，不是因为衣服破旧，而是因为，就如他猜的那样，玛丽在内心深处认为他下葬时穿这套衣服才够体面。 Long before the family stirred next morning, his horses fed and harnessed by lantern light, breakfast cooked and eaten by lamp fight, Old Man Tarwater was off and away down Tarwater Valley on the road to Kelterville.Two things were unusual about this usual trip which he had made a thousand and forty times since taking the mail contract.He did not drive to Kelterville, but turned off on the main road south to Santa Rosa.Even more remarkable than this was the paper-wrapped parcel between his feet.It contained his one decent black suit, which Mary had been long reluctant to see him wear any more, not because it was shabby, but because, as he guessed what was at the back of her mind, it was decent enough to bury him in.
在圣罗莎一个二手衣服商店，他立马就卖了这套衣服，得到二点五美元。同样从这个乐于助人的店主手里，他卖了他过世很久的妻子的婚戒，得了四美元。他还处理掉了那些马匹跟那辆四轮马车，卖了七十五美元，但只拿到了二十五美元的现金。他在街上偶然碰到了奥尔顿·格兰杰，之前约翰从没向奥尔顿提过在1874年借给他的十美元，这次他跟奥尔顿提起了这件小事，奥尔顿立马就还给了他。而且，在最不可能将来会阔绰的人中，他找到了镇上的酒鬼，约翰在过去阔绰时曾经给他买过很多酒。约翰·塔尔沃特从酒鬼那里借了一美元。最终，约翰坐上了下午去往旧金山的火车。 And at Santa Rosa, in a second-hand clothes shop, he sold the suit outright for two dollars and a half.From the same obliging shopman he received four dollars for the wedding ring of his long- dead wife.The span of horses and the wagon he disposed of for seventy-five dollars, although twenty-five was all he received down in cash.Chancing to meet Alton Granger on the street, to whom never before had he mentioned the ten dollars loaned him in ’74, he reminded Alton Granger of the little affair, and was promptly paid.Also, of all unbelievable men to be in funds, he so found the town drunkard for whom he had bought many a drink in the old and palmy days.And from him John Tarwater borrowed a dollar.Finally, he took the afternoon train to San Francisco.
十二天之后，带着一个半空的帆布包，包里装着毯子和旧衣服，他登上了戴依海滩，此时正是克朗代克淘金潮最火热的时刻。海滩上叫喊声撕心裂肺，一片混乱。万吨重的设备散乱地堆在海滩上，由两万人操作着，场面喧嚣热闹。货物由印度航船运过来，越过奇尔库特到达莱克林德曼后，其价钱就从每磅十六美分涨到了三十美分，这样就是每吨六百美元。而亚寒带的冬天马上就要来了。所有人都知道这点，也都知道他们两万人中，只有很少人能跨过这个关口，剩下的只能留在这里，等待晚春来临时的冰雪消融。 A dozen days later, carrying a half-empty canvas sack of blankets and old clothes, he landed on the beach of Dyea in the thick of the great Klondike Rush.The beach was screaming bedlam.Ten thousand tons of outfit lay heaped and scattered, and twice ten thousand men struggled with it and clamoured about it.Freight, by Indian-back, over Chilcoot to Lake Linderman, had jumped from sixteen to thirty cents a pound, which latter was a rate of six hundred dollars a ton.And the sub-arctic winter gloomed near at hand.All knew it, and all knew that of the twenty thousand of them very few would get across the passes, leaving the rest to winter and wait for the late spring thaw.
这就是老约翰·塔尔沃特踏上的沙滩。他跨过这个沙滩，沿着一条通向奇尔库特的小路向前走着，高声笑唱起了那段古老的歌谣，他自己就像一位阿格斯爷爷，不用担心这世上装备的问题，因为他也没有任何装备。那晚，他就睡在离戴依海滩五英里远的平原上，就在导航独木舟的前方。戴依河从冰山上的黑暗山谷中喷涌而出，在这里变成了一条汹涌的山洪，远处的冰山正是山洪的来源。 Such the beach old John Tarwater stepped upon; and straight across the beach and up the trail toward Chilcoot he headed, cackling his ancient chant, a very Grandfather Argus himself, with no outfit worry in the world, for he did not possess any outfit.That night he slept on the flats, five miles above Dyea, at the head of canoe navigation.Here the Dyea River became a rushing mountain torrent, plunging out of a dark canyon from the glaciers that fed it far above.
第二天一大早，他在这里看到了一个体重不过百的矮个子男人，蹒跚地走在一条只有脚那么宽的木头上，背上背着一百多磅重的面粉。而且，他还看到这个矮个子男人在木头桥上绊倒了，脸朝下掉进一个静静的涡流中，涡流的水有两英尺深，而他眼看就要无声无息地被淹死了。他并不想就这么死去，但是背上的面粉跟他差不多重，让他无法起来。 And here, early next morning, he beheld a little man weighing no more than a hundred, staggering along a foot-log under all of a hundred pounds of flour strapped on his back.Also, he beheld the little man stumble off the log and fall face-downward in a quiet eddy where the water was two feet deep and proceed quietly to drown.It was no desire of his to take death so easily, but the flour on his back weighed as much as he and would not let him up.
“谢谢您，老先生。”当塔尔沃特把他从水里拖上岸后，他对塔尔沃特说。 “Thank you, old man,” he said to Tarwater, when the latter had dragged him up into the air and ashore.
在他解开鞋带，把水倒出时，他们攀谈了起来。接着，他摸出一枚十美元的金币，给他的救命恩人。 While he unlaced his shoes and ran the water out, they had further talk.Next, he fished out a ten-dollar gold-piece and offered it to his rescuer.
老塔尔沃特摇了摇头，打了个冷颤，因为刚才冰冷的河水一直浸湿到了他的膝盖。 Old Tarwater shook his head and shivered, for the ice-water had wet him to his knees.
“但是我想，我是不会拒绝坐下来和您共进友好一餐的。 “But I reckon I wouldn’t object to settin’ down to a friendly meal with you.”
“您还没用过早餐吗？”这个矮个子真诚而好奇地瞟了他一眼问道。他年纪在四十岁以上，并说自己叫安森。 “Ain’t had breakfast?” the little man, who was past forty and who had said his name was Anson, queried with a glance frankly curious.
“一口也没吃。”约翰·塔尔沃特回答道。 “Nary bite,” John Tarwater answered.
“您的装备呢？在前面吗？” “Where’s your outfit?Ahead?”
“没有装备。” “Nary outfit.”
“难道您想在内陆买吃的？” “Expect to buy your grub on the Inside?”
“朋友，我一美元也没有，买不了吃的。所以现在没有什么比吃一口热腾腾的早餐更重要的了。” “Nary a dollar to buy it with, friend.Which ain’t so important as a warm bite of breakfast right now.”
再走四分之一英里，在安森的营地里，塔尔沃特发现了一个三十多岁的瘦瘦的、红胡须的青年人，这人正对着火中的湿柳木诅咒。矮个子男人介绍说这人叫查尔斯后，查尔斯就把自己的愤怒转到了塔尔沃特身上，而和蔼又健忘的塔尔沃特只专心烤火。他借着早上寒冷的微风，生起一股气流，因为之前另一个人愚蠢地用石头把空气堵死了。很快，烟少了，火焰更旺了。这个团体的第三个成员叫比尔·威尔逊，而他们叫他大比尔，他带着一个一百四十磅的包裹回来了；而塔尔沃特认为很难吃的早饭，都给查尔斯吃得精光。蘑菇半生不熟，大部分都糊了,熏肉也烤糊了，而咖啡难喝得更是无法用语言形容。 In Anson’s camp, a quarter of a mile on, Tarwater found a slender, red-whiskered young man of thirty cursing over a fire of wet willow wood.Introduced as Charles, he transferred his scowl and wrath to Tarwater, who, genially oblivious, devoted himself to the fire, took advantage of the chill morning breeze to create a draught which the other had left stupidly blocked by stones, and soon developed less smoke and more flame.The third member of the party, Bill Wilson, or Big Bill as they called him, came in with a hundred-and-forty-pound pack; and what Tarwater esteemed to be a very rotten breakfast was dished out by Charles.The mush was half cooked and mostly burnt, the bacon was charred carbon, and the coffee was unspeakable.
这顿饭还是很快就给狼吞虎咽地吃完了。然后这三个伙伴就带上他们的空包朝着下一个地方前进，那是一英里外的最后一个露营地，有他们剩余的装备。老塔尔沃特也变得忙碌起来。他洗盘子，搜寻干木头，修补一个破的背包带，磨快了砍肉刀和营地的斧头，重新把凿子和铁铲装进一个更方便携带的包裹。 Immediately the meal was wolfed down the three partners took their empty pack-straps and headed down trail to where the remainder of their outfit lay at the last camp a mile away.And old Tarwater became busy.He washed the dishes, foraged dry wood, mended a broken pack-strap, put an edge on the butcher-knife and camp-axe, and repacked the picks and shovels into a more carryable parcel.
这顿简单的早餐留给他最深刻的印象，就是安森和大比尔对查尔斯所表现出的敬畏。某个早上，安森又背了一袋一百磅重的包进来，在他喘气的间隙，塔尔沃特巧妙地向安森暗示了他的这种印象。 What had impressed him during the brief breakfast was the sort of awe in which Anson and Big Bill stood of Charles.Once, during the morning, while Anson took a breathing spell after bringing in another hundred-pound pack, Tarwater delicately hinted his impression.
“你看，是这样的，”安森说，“我们做了领导工作的划分。我们各有专长。我是一个木工。当我们到莱克林德曼之后，等树都砍了，锯成厚木板后，我就要指挥如何建造这艘船。大比尔是个伐木工兼矿工。所以，他就指挥怎样弄出这些木头和所有的采矿作业。我们的大部分装备都在前面。在付钱给印第安人，让他们把大部分装备运到奇尔库特尽头后，我们就没钱了。我们最后一个伙伴跟装备在一起，独自带着它们从另一边前行。他叫利物浦，是个水手。所以，当船建好之后，他就是队伍的头，负责在湖上和急流中指挥航行直到克朗代克。 “You see, it’s this way,” Anson said.“We’ve divided our leadership.We’ve got specialities.Now I’m a carpenter.When we get to Lake Linderman, and the trees are chopped and whipsawed into planks, I’ll boss the building of the boat.Big Bill is a logger and miner.So he’ll boss getting out the logs and all mining operations.Most of our outfit’s ahead.We went broke paying the Indians to pack that much of it to the top of Chilcoot.Our last partner is up there with it, moving it along by himself down the other side.His name’s Liverpool, and he’s a sailor.So, when the boat’s built, he’s the boss of the outfit to navigate the lakes and rapids to Klondike.
“而查尔斯——这位克雷顿先生——他有什么专长呢？”塔尔沃特问道。 “And Charles—this Mr. Crayton—what might his speciality be?”Tarwater asked.
“他是商人。谈到生意和组织时，就由他做主。” “He’s the business man.When it comes to business and organization he’s boss.”
“嗯。”塔尔沃特沉思道，“可以把这么多有专长的人聚到一起，真是太幸运了。” “Hum,” Tarwater pondered.“Very lucky to get such a bunch of specialities into one outfit.”
“不仅仅是幸运。”安森同意道，“这也全是巧合。我们每个人都是独自开始的。我们是在去旧金山的船上遇到的，然后就组成了这个团体。——好了，我得走了。查尔斯有揍人的倾向，因为就像我不能打包自己的包裹一样，你不能期待一个一百磅重的人来背一个一百六十磅的包裹。” “More than luck,” Anson agreed.“It was all accident, too.Each of us started alone.We met on the steamer coming up from San Francisco, and formed the party.—Well, I got to be goin’.Charles is liable to get kicking because I ain’t packin’ my share’ just the same, you can’t expect a hundred-pound man to pack as much as a hundred-and-sixty-pounder.”
“留在这儿，给我们做点儿晚饭吧。”查尔斯在背下一袋包裹时对塔尔沃特说，他注意到了这个老人是个干活的好手。 “Stick around and cook us something for dinner,” Charles, on his next load in and noting the effects of the old man’s handiness, told Tarwater.
而塔尔沃特做了顿像样的晚饭，他先洗了盘子，晚饭准备了像样的猪肉和豆子，在煎锅上烤的面包十分可口，令那三个伙伴吃得几乎贴了在煎锅上了。洗完盘子后，他剪了胡须，点着了早饭要用的火，还给安森表演了一个用鞋袜玩的花招，这鞋袜对于任何一个徒步旅行者来说都是无价之宝，然后又唱起了“像古代的阿格斯”这首歌，并给他们讲述1849年横跨这块平原的大迁徙。 And Tarwater cooked a dinner that was a dinner, washed the dishes, had real pork and beans for supper, and bread baked in a frying-pan that was so delectable than the three partners nearly foundered themselves on it.Supper dishes washed, he cut shavings and kindling for a quick and certain breakfast fire, showed Anson a trick with foot-gear that was invaluable to any hiker, sang his “Like Argus of the Ancient Times,” and told them of the great emigration across the Plains in Forty-nine.
“天哪，自从我们到达这个海滩之后，这还是第一次令人高兴又轻松的露营。”大比尔敲了敲他的烟斗说道，并开始脱鞋准备睡觉。 “My goodness, the first cheerful and hearty-like camp since we hit the beach,” Big Bill remarked as he knocked out his pipe and began pulling off his shoes for bed.
“孩子们，是不是我活跃了气氛？”塔尔沃特和蔼地询问道。 “Kind of made things easy, boys, eh?”Tarwater queried genially.
所有人都点了点头。“那我有一个提议，孩子们。你们可以采纳也可以拒绝，但请好好地把它听完。你们得赶在冰封期前到达。你们中总会有一个人把一半时间浪费在做饭上，原本他也许能把时间花在整理装备上的。如果我给你们做饭，你们所有人的进度都会更快。而且，吃得越好，也会让你们打包打得更好。而且有时候我也可以一起打包干活，很大量，嗯，一定，很大量。” All nodded.“Well, then, I got a proposition, boys.You can take it or leave it, but just listen kindly to it.You’re in a hurry to get in before the freeze-up.Half the time is wasted over the cooking by one of you that he might be puttin’ in packin’ outfit.If I do the cookin’ for you, you all’ll get on that much faster.Also, the cookin’ ’ll be better, and that’ll make you pack better.And I can pack quite a bit myself in between times, quite a bit, yes, sir, quite a bit.”
大比尔和安森正要点头同意，但是查尔斯阻止了他们。 Big Bill and Anson were just beginning to nod their heads in agreement, when Charles stopped them.
“那你希望我们给你什么回报呢？”查尔斯问这位老者。 “What do you expect of us in return?” he demanded of the old man.
“哦，这个我就让你们决定。” “Oh, I leave it up to the boys.”
“那不成。”查尔斯厉声责难道，“你提的建议。现在就由你了结。” “That ain’t business,” Charles reprimanded sharply.“You made the proposition.Now finish it.”
“嗯，是这样的——” “Well, it’s this way—”
“你期望我们整个冬天都供你吃，嗯？”查尔斯打断说。 “You expect us to feed you all winter, eh?”Charles interrupted.
“不，先生，我不是那么想的。我所打算的，就是能搭你们的船去克朗代克，这对你们很合适吧。” “No, siree, I don’t.All I reckon is a passage to Klondike in your boat would be mighty square of you.”
“老头，你连一盎司吃的都没有。等你到了那里，你会饿死的。” “You haven’t an ounce of grub, old man.You’ll starve to death when you get there.”
“我已经很顺当地吃了一段时间了。”老塔尔沃特回答道，眼里闪烁着一道古怪的光芒，“我七十岁了，还从没饿死过。” “I’ve been feedin’ some long time pretty successful,” Old Tarwater replied, a whimsical light in his eyes.“I’m seventy, and ain’t starved to death never yet.”
“你愿意签一份协议，说你一到道森就自谋生计吗？”这个生意人要求道。 “Will you sign a paper to the effect that you shift for yourself as soon as you get to Dawson?” the business one demanded.
“哦，当然。”老者回答。 “Oh, sure,” was the response.
查尔斯看了看他另外两个伙伴，他俩对这个安排都露出了满意的表情。 Again Charles checked his two partners’ expressions of satisfaction with the arrangement.
“还有一件事，老头。我们是个四人团体，在类似的问题上我们都有投票权。扬·利物浦已经带着主要的装备在前面了。所以他也有发言权，但是他不在这里，没法发言。” “One other thing, old man.We’re a party of four, and we all have a vote on questions like this.Young Liverpool is ahead with the main outfit.He’s got a say so, and he isn’t here to say it.”
“他是个怎样的搭档？”塔尔沃特问道。 “What kind of a party might he be?” Tarwater inquired.
“他是个蛮横的水手，性急，脾气不好。” “He’s a rough-neck sailor, and he’s got a quick, bad temper.”
“有些狂躁。”安森补充道。 “Some turbulent,” Anson contributed.
“而且他要诅咒起来是相当的恐怖。”大比尔也跟着证实。 “And the way he can cuss is simply God-awful,” Big Bill testified.
“但他很正直。”大比尔又说。 “But he’s square,” Big Bill added.
对这个评价，安森起劲地点了点头。 Anson nodded heartily to this appraisal.
“孩子们，”塔尔沃特总结道，“我动身到加利福尼亚，最后也到了。我也会到克朗代克的。任何东西都不能阻止我，任何东西都不能。我还打算在那片土地上挣个三十万美元。任何东西都不能阻止我，任何东西都不能，因为我非常需要钱。只要这个孩子正直，我就不介意什么坏脾气。我会碰碰运气的，也会跟你们一起干活，直到我们赶上利物浦。然后，如果他不同意这个建议，那我就输了。但是不管怎样，我是不会看到他说不的，因为那时离冰冻期很近了，对我来说再找到这样的机会也太晚了。而且，就像我一定要到克朗代克一样，他绝不可能不同意的。” “Well, boys,” Tarwater summed up, “I set out for Californy and I got there.And I’m going to get to Klondike.Ain’t a thing can stop me, ain’t a thing.I’m going to get three hundred thousand outa the ground, too.Ain’t a thing can stop me, ain’t a thing, because I just naturally need the money.I don’t mind a bad temper so long’s the boy is square.I’ll take my chance, an’ I’ll work along with you till we catch up with him.Then, if he says no to the proposition, I reckon I’ll lose.But somehow I just can’t see ’m sayin’ no, because that’d mean too close up to freeze-up and too late for me to find another chance like this.And, as I’m sure going to get to Klondike, it’s just plumb impossible for him to say no.”
老约翰·塔尔沃特在一条不常有突出人物的小道上，成了一个突出的人物。数千人一起，每人身上都背有半吨重的装备，他们在这条路上往返折回有二十次了，所有人都认识他，都叫他“圣诞老人。”而且，在他工作的时候，他经常用他苍老的假音唱那段歌曲。他加入的那个三人团体里，没有一个人能抱怨他的工作。确实，他的关节有些僵硬——他承认有点儿风湿。他行动缓慢，当他行动起来时，关节似乎咯吱作响，要裂开了一样；但是他仍然继续走着。夜里，他是最后一个进毯子睡觉的，早上又是第一个起来的，所以另外三个人能在吃早餐工作前喝上热咖啡。而在早餐和正餐之间，以及在正餐和晚饭之间，他总能够打几个回程的包裹。然而，他的负重极限是六十磅。他是能负重七十五磅，但坚持不了。有一次，他试着负重九十磅，但在路上摔倒了，而且在之后的几天走路都是摇摇晃晃的。 Old John Tarwater became a striking figure on a trail unusually replete with striking figures.With thousands of men, each back- tripping half a ton of outfit, retracing every mile of the trail twenty times, all came to know him and to hail him as “Father Christmas.”And, as he worked, ever he raised his chant with his age-falsetto voice.None of the three men he had joined could complain about his work.True, his joints were stiff—he admitted to a trifle of rheumatism.He moved slowly, and seemed to creak and crackle when he moved; but he kept on moving.Last into the blankets at night, he was first out in the morning, so that the other three had hot coffee before their one before-breakfast pack.And, between breakfast and dinner and between dinner and supper, he always managed to back-trip for several packs himself.Sixty pounds was the limit of his burden, however.He could manage seventy-five, but he could not keep it up.Once, he tried ninety, but collapsed on the trail and was seriously shaky for a couple of days afterward.
干活！在这条路上，努力工作的人第一次知道了什么叫干活，按体力比较起来，没有比老塔尔沃特更卖力工作的了。一方面被即将到来的寒冬逼得几近绝望，另一方面被淘金梦狂热地吸引着，他们拼尽最后一丝力气工作,然后中途退出。其他人在失败已成定局时，脑子都爆裂了。有些人疯了，其他人则在足以摧毁人的压力之下，与同伙解除了伙伴关系，结束了一生的友谊，这些伙伴同他们自己一样优秀，一样紧张疯狂。 Work!On a trail where hard-working men learned for the first time what work was, no man worked harder in proportion to his strength than Old Tarwater.Driven desperately on by the near-thrust of winter, and lured madly on by the dream of gold, they worked to their last ounce of strength and fell by the way.Others, when failure made certain, blew out their brains.Some went mad, and still others, under the irk of the man-destroying strain, broke partnerships and dissolved life-time friendships with fellows just as good as themselves and just as strained and mad.
干活！老塔尔沃特可以让他们所有人感到羞愧，尽管他的关节似乎要裂开了，尽管他有令人不快的干咳。不论早晚，不论是在路上还是在路边的营地里，他总是很显眼，总是忙于某件事情，总是热情地回应别人对他“圣诞老人”的招呼。疲惫的背包客把他们的包放在塔尔沃特包裹旁边的木头上或石头上，然后说：“给我们唱你的那首歌吧，老伙计，关于1849年的那首。”而当他气喘吁吁地满足他们的要求后，他们就会背着自己的包站起来，评论说歌曲真是鼓舞人心，然后又重新上路。 Work!Old Tarwater could shame them all, despite his creaking and crackling and the nasty hacking cough he had developed.Early and late, on trail or in camp beside the trail he was ever in evidence, ever busy at something, ever responsive to the hail of “Father Christmas.”Weary back-trippers would rest their packs on a log or rock alongside of where he rested his, and would say: “Sing us that song of yourn, dad, about Forty-Nine.”And, when he had wheezingly complied, they would arise under their loads, remark that it was real heartening, and hit the forward trail again.
“要是有一个人为他的船票干活，并且成功地挣到了，”大比尔向他的两个搭档坦白说过，“那么那个人就是我们的老废物。” “If ever a man worked his passage and earned it,” Big Bill confided to his two partners, “that man’s our old Skeezicks.”
“当然。”安森同意道，“他对这个团体来说是有价值的，而我，作为团体的一员，非常同意把他纳为一名正式伙伴——” “You bet,” Anson confirmed.“He’s a valuable addition to the party, and I, for one, ain’t at all disagreeable to the notion of making him a regular partner—”
“想都别想！”查尔斯·克雷顿插了一句，“我们到了道森后就甩了他——这可是商定好了的。如果让他一直跟着我们，我们只有埋了他。而且，我们可能会遇上一次饥荒，到时每一盎司的食物都很关键。要记得，我们这一路上都在用我们自己的食物供着他。而且如果我们在下一年拮据的时候吃完了食物，你就知道为什么它们消耗得这么快了。汽船要到六月中旬之后，才能运粮食到道森，而现在到那时还有九个月呢。” “None of that!”Charles Crayton cut in.“When we get to Dawson we’re quit of him—that’s the agreement.We’d only have to bury him if we let him stay on with us.Besides, there’s going to be a famine, and every ounce of grub’ll count.Remember, we’re feeding him out of our own supply all the way in.And if we run short in the pinch next year, you’ll know the reason.Steamboats can’t get up grub to Dawson till the middle of June, and that’s nine months away.”
“嗯，你带的钱和装备跟我们剩下的人带的一样多，”大比尔让步道，“你当然有发言权。” “Well, you put as much money and outfit in as the rest of us,” Big Bill conceded, “and you’ve a say according.”
“我以后也会有发言权。”查尔斯宣称道，显得越来越烦躁。“对于你们两个蠢人而言，真是幸亏能有个人为你们事先考虑，否则你们非得饿死。我跟你们说，饥荒快来了。我一直都在研究行情。面粉会变成两美元一磅，或者是十美元，或者根本没得卖。你们记住我说的话。” “And I’m going to have my say,” Charles asserted with increasing irritability.“And it’s lucky for you with your fool sentiments that you’ve got somebody to think ahead for you, else you’d all starve to death.I tell you that famine’s coming.I’ve been studying the situation.Flour will be two dollars a pound, or ten, and no sellers.You mark my words.”
穿过满是碎石的平原，走上黑暗的峡谷到达希普营地，经过挂在头上的危险冰川到达斯凯尔斯，他们又手脚并用从斯凯尔斯爬上陡峭的冰蚀岩石，老塔尔沃特依然在营地做饭，打包，唱歌。他的歌声在第一场秋雪中，穿过奇尔库特通道，越过树带界限上空。那些在下面的人没有柴火，在火山口湖寒冷的边缘地带，他们听到从上方凛冽的风雪中传来一个奇特的声音，那声音唱到： Across the rubble-covered flats, up the dark canyon to Sheep Camp, past the over-hanging and ever-threatening glaciers to the Scales, and from the Scales up the steep pitches of ice-scoured rock where packers climbed with hands and feet, Old Tarwater camp-cooked and packed and sang.He blew across Chilcoot Pass, above timberline, in the first swirl of autumn snow.Those below, without firewood, on the bitter rim of Crater Lake, heard from the driving obscurity above them a weird voice chanting:
“像古代的阿格斯，我们离开这个现代的希腊，嗒—嗒，嗒—嗒，嗒，嗒，嗒—嗒，来剪金羊毛。” “Like Argus of the ancient times,We leave this modern Greece,Tum-tum, tum-tum, tum, tum, tum-tum,To shear the Golden Fleece.”
从这场疾风小雪中，他们看到一个高瘦的身影，雪白的胡子跟暴风雪混在一起飞舞着，背上压着一个六十磅的露营行李大包。 And out of the snow flurries they saw appear a tall, gaunt form, with whiskers of flying white that blended with the storm, bending under a sixty-pound pack of camp dunnage.
“圣诞老人！”他们招呼着。然后就是：“为圣诞老人喝彩三声！” “Father Christmas!” was the hail.And then: “Three rousing cheers for Father Christmas!”
离火山口湖两英里远的地方就是快乐营地——之所以这样叫，是因为那是树带界限的最边缘，在那儿他们又可以烤火取暖了。它不能叫做树带，因为它只是一株矮小的岩地云杉，最高的枝叶从来没长到比苔藓高出一英尺的高度，而且在苔藓下的部分扭曲匍匐得像小猪一样。在通往快乐营地的路上，沐浴着六天以来的第一缕阳光，老塔尔沃特把包裹靠在一块巨大的岩石上，喘着气。小路围绕着这块巨石，负重累累的人缓缓地艰难前行着，而背着空包的人则快速地跛行回去背新的货物。有两次老塔尔沃特都试图站起来继续走，但每次，他摇晃的身形都给他发出警告，于是他便坐回去再积攒些力气。在石头附近，他听到了问候的声音，并听出这是查尔斯·克雷顿的声音，而且意识到他们终于跟扬·利物浦碰头了。很快，查尔斯就直奔主题，他的每一个词都清晰地传入了塔尔沃特耳中，他坦率无误地描述了塔尔沃特，以及他提出的搭船去道森的建议。 Two miles beyond Crater Lake lay Happy Camp—so named because here was found the uppermost fringe of the timber line, where men might warm themselves by fire again.Scarcely could it be called timber, for it was a dwarf rock-spruce that never raised its loftiest branches higher than a foot above the moss, and that twisted and grovelled like a pig-vegetable under the moss.Here, on the trail leading into Happy Camp, in the first sunshine of half a dozen days, Old Tarwater rested his pack against a huge boulder and caught his breath.Around this boulder the trail passed, laden men toiling slowly forward and men with empty pack-straps limping rapidly back for fresh loads.Twice Old Tarwater essayed to rise and go on, and each time, warned by his shakiness, sank back to recover more strength.From around the boulder he heard voices in greeting, recognized Charles Crayton’s voice, and realized that at last they had met up with Young Liverpool.Quickly, Charles plunged into business, and Tarwater heard with great distinctness every word of Charles’ unflattering description of him and the proposition to give him passage to Dawson.
“多么愚蠢的提议。”查尔斯说完后，利物浦如是评断道，“一位七十岁的老爷爷！如果他快死了，那你究竟为什么还要搭上他？如果到时有饥荒，而且看起来也会有，我们每一盎司的粮食都不能少。我们只准备了四个人的，不是五个人的。” “A dam fool proposition,” was Liverpool’s judgment, when Charles had concluded.“An old granddad of seventy!If he’s on his last legs, why in hell did you hook up with him?If there’s going to be a famine, and it looks like it, we need every ounce of grub for ourselves.We only out-fitted for four, not five.”
“好了。”塔尔沃特听到查尔斯在安抚他那位伙伴，“别激动。这个老古怪同意等我们追上你的时候，把最后的决定权留给你。你所要做的，就是坚持你的意见，然后说不。” “It’s all right,” Tarwater heard Charles assuring the other.“Don’t get excited.The old codger agreed to leave the final decision to you when we caught up with you.All you’ve got to do is put your foot down and say no.”
“你的意思是，在你鼓励利用他一路从戴依干活到这里后，就由我来拒绝这个老头？” “You mean it’s up to me to turn the old one down, after your encouraging him and taking advantage of his work clear from Dyea here?”
“这是一条艰苦的路，利物浦，只有硬汉才能走过。”查尔斯努力辩解道。 “It’s a hard trail, Liverpool, and only the men that are hard will get through,” Charles strove to palliate.
“那就要我干这肮脏的活？”利物浦抱怨道，同时塔尔沃特的心也沉了下去。 “And I’m to do the dirty work?”Liverpool complained, while Tarwater’s heart sank.
“是这么回事。”查尔斯说道，“决定权在你。” “That’s just about the size of it,” Charles said.“You’ve got the deciding.”
然后，老塔尔沃特的心又提了起来，因为随着空气给这股不敬的言语飓风撕裂开来，其中蹦出了几句话，像是：——“脏臭鼬……先看你下地狱……我意已决……地狱之火和堕落……这个老古怪跟着我们一起沿育空河走，就这么定了，我的朋友们……难吗？除非我展示给你们看，你们根本就不知道困难是什么……如果你们试图要赶他走的话，我就会把这些装备扔到地狱去，然后离开……赶他试试，就这样，你们就要想想最后的审判日，想想上帝的爆发会如何摧毁这个营地！” Then old Tarwater’s heart uprose again as the air was rent by a cyclone of profanity, from the midst of which crackled sentences like:—“Dirty skunks!...See you in hell first!...My mind’s made up!...Hell’s fire and corruption!...The old codger goes down the Yukon with us, stack on that, my hearty!...Hard?You don’t know what hard is unless I show you!...I’ll bust the whole outfit to hell and gone if any of you try to side-track him!...Just try to side-track him, that is all, and you’ll think the Day of Judgment and all God’s blastingness has hit the camp in one chunk!”
利物浦的一连串演讲如此激励人心，毫不费力，这位身负重担的老人听了后轻松地站起来，大踏步地朝着快乐营地进发。 Such was the invigoratingness of Liverpool’s flow of speech that, quite without consciousness of effort, the old man arose easily under his load and strode on toward Happy Camp.
从快乐营地到长湖，从长湖到深湖，从深湖往上走越过一个巨大的拱形物，再下到莱克林德曼，这场激烈的天人大战仍在继续。人们心力交瘁，在路边痛哭流涕。但是冬天却从未停下脚步。秋日狂风肆虐，迎着刺骨的滂沱大雨和越来越急的雪阵，塔尔沃特和他所在的团队把他们最后的一套装备堆在了沙滩上。 From Happy Camp to Long Lake, from Long Lake to Deep Lake, and from Deep Lake up over the enormous hog-back and down to Linderman, the man-killing race against winter kept on.Men broke their hearts and backs and wept beside the trail in sheer exhaustion.But winter never faltered.The fall gales blew, and amid bitter soaking rains and ever-increasing snow flurries, Tarwater and the party to which he was attached piled the last of their outfit on the beach.
根本没时间休息。湖的那边，在奔涌洪流上方一英里处，他们驻扎在一片云杉树下面，挖了锯木坑。在这里，他们用双手和一把不够锋利的双人锯，把云杉树干锯成了木材。他们没日没夜地工作着。在值夜班的时候，老塔尔沃特第三次晕倒在锯木坑下面。但白天他还是做饭，而且，在闲暇之时，当厚木板从河里漂下来时，他还帮安森在急流边上造船。 There was no rest.Across the lake, a mile above a roaring torrent, they located a patch of spruce and built their saw-pit.Here, by hand, with an inadequate whipsaw, they sawed the spruce-trunks into lumber.They worked night and day.Thrice, on the night-shift, underneath in the saw-pit, Old Tarwater fainted.By day he cooked as well, and, in the betweenwhiles, helped Anson in the building of the boat beside the torrent as the green planks came down.
白天变得越来越短。风从北面吹来，刮个不停。早上，他们困倦地从毯子里钻出来，穿上袜子，在塔尔沃特早已为他们生好的火堆边上，烘烤他们已经冻结了的鞋子。在内陆，饥荒的传言越来越多。从白令海来的最后一批食物运送汽船停在离道森北部几百英里远的育空平原的起点处，因为低水位而拖延了。实际上，它们是位于旧哈德逊海湾公司在北极圈内的育空边界贸易站。道森的面粉涨到了两美元一磅，但是没人愿意卖。富矿带和理想黄金国的国王们，赚足了钱就动身前往外面的世界，因为他们在这里买不到食物。矿工委员会没收了所有的食物，然后严格地按照份额发放给工人们。谁私藏一盎司食物，就会被无情地打死。有二十个人已经被这样处死了。 The days grew shorter.The wind shifted into the north and blew unending gales.In the mornings the weary men crawled from their blankets and in their socks thawed out their frozen shoes by the fire Tarwater always had burning for them.Ever arose the increasing tale of famine on the Inside.The last grub steamboats up from Bering Sea were stalled by low water at the beginning of the Yukon Flats hundreds of miles north of Dawson.In fact, they lay at the old Hudson Bay Company’s post at Fort Yukon inside the Arctic Circle.Flour in Dawson was up to two dollars a pound, but no one would sell.Bonanza and Eldorado Kings, with money to burn, were leaving for the Outside because they could buy no grub.Miners’ Committees were confiscating all grub and putting the population on strict rations.A man who held out an ounce of grub was shot like a dog.A score had been so executed already.
而且，在这种早已使诸多年轻人崩溃的压力之下，老塔尔沃特也快要倒下了。他咳得越来越严重，如果不是他的同伴睡得跟死人一样，他准得让他们整个晚上都醒着。而且，他开始打冷颤了，所以得穿着衣服睡觉。这么一来，他的衣袋里就一件不剩了。所有衣服都穿在身上，紧紧裹住他枯瘦老迈的躯体。 And, under a strain which had broken so many younger men, Old Tarwater began to break.His cough had become terrible, and had not his exhausted comrades slept like the dead, he would have kept them awake nights.Also, he began to take chills, so that he dressed up to go to bed.When he had finished so dressing, not a rag of garment remained in his clothes bag.All he possessed was on his back and swathed around his gaunt old form.
“呀！”大比尔说，“他现在就穿上了所有衣服，气温还没到零下二十度呢，等温度达到零下五六十度，他可怎么办？” “Gee!” said Big Bill.“If he puts all he’s got on now, when it ain’t lower than twenty above, what’ll he do later on when it goes down to fifty and sixty below?”
他们把粗制的船放入山中急流，有十几次都差点儿失手丢了船，在暴风雪最激烈的时候，他们划船穿过了莱克林德曼的南端。第二天早上，他们计划装上货物再启航，直接深入到最北部，穿越湖泊、激流和箱形峡谷开始这次长达五百英里的危险之旅。但是在那晚睡觉之前，扬·利物浦去了营地外面。回去之后，他看到伙伴们都睡着了。他叫醒塔尔沃特，低声跟他交谈起来。 They lined the rough-made boat down the mountain torrent, nearly losing it a dozen times, and rowed across the south end of Lake Linderman in the thick of a fall blizzard.Next morning they planned to load and start, squarely into the teeth of the north, on their perilous traverse of half a thousand miles of lakes and rapids and box canyons.But before he went to bed that night, Young Liverpool was out over the camp.He returned to find his whole party asleep.Rousing Tarwater, he talked with him in low tones.
“听着，老爹，”他说，“你在我们的船上有位置，而且这也是你自己挣来的。但是你自己也知道，几年来你都很好，但你现在的健康状况并不乐观。如果你继续跟着我们，你一定会死掉的——现在等我把话说完，老爹。一张船票的价格已经涨到了五百美元。我一直在奔波，而且找到了一个乘客。他是阿拉斯加商会的一个官员，也想加入进来。他出价六百美元同我一起坐船。但现在这船票在你手上。你可以卖给他，把这六百美元收归囊中，趁天气还好，启程向南前往加利福尼亚。两天后你就会到戴依，再过一个多星期就可以到加利福尼亚。你觉得怎么样？” “Listen, dad,” he said.—“You’ve got a passage in our boat, and if ever a man earned a passage you have.But you know yourself you’re pretty well along in years, and your health right now ain’t exciting.If you go on with us you’ll croak surer’n hell.—Now wait till I finish, dad.The price for a passage has jumped to five hundred dollars.I’ve been throwing my feet and I’ve hustled a passenger.He’s an official of the Alaska Commercial and just has to get in.He’s bid up to six hundred to go with me in our boat.Now the passage is yours.You sell it to him, poke the six hundred into your jeans, and pull South for California while the goin’s good.You can be in Dyea in two days, and in California in a week more.What d’ye say?”
在他能自由喘气说话前，塔尔沃特咳了咳，并颤抖了好一会儿。 Tarwater coughed and shivered for a space, ere he could get freedom of breath for speech.
“孩子，”他说，“我告诉你一件事。我在1849年赶着四轭牛车跨过大平原，最后一头都没丢。我直接把它们赶到加利福尼亚，然后用它们从萨特要塞运货到了美国关卡。现在，我要去克朗代克。任何东西都不能阻止我，任何东西都不行。我要搭这条船，和你一起掌舵，进军到克朗代克，而且我要从苔原下挖出三十万。所以，卖掉我的船票，这是有悖于理智、常识的。但是，我真诚地感谢你，孩子，我真诚地感谢你。” “Son,” he said, “I just want to tell you one thing.I drove my four yoke of oxen across the Plains in Forty-nine and lost nary a one.I drove them plumb to Californy, and I freighted with them afterward out of Sutter’s Fort to American Bar.Now I’m going to Klondike.Ain’t nothing can stop me, ain’t nothing at all.I’m going to ride that boat, with you at the steering sweep, clean to Klondike, and I’m going to shake three hundred thousand out of the moss-roots.That being so, it’s contrary to reason and common sense for me to sell out my passage.But I thank you kindly, son, I thank you kindly.”
这个年轻水手冲动地伸出手，一把抓住了老人家。 The young sailor shot out his hand impulsively and gripped the old man’s.
“看在上帝的份上，老爹！”他喊道，“那你就是确定要去了。你是真正的男子汉。”他带着毫不掩饰的蔑视扫视这些熟睡的人，其中查尔斯·克雷顿在他的红胡子堆里打呼噜。“老爹，他们看起来不再跟你站一边了。” “By God, dad!” he cried.“You’re sure going to go then.You’re the real stuff.”He looked with undisguised contempt across the sleepers to where Charles Crayton snored in his red beard.“They don’t seem to make your kind any more, dad.”
尽管出来的老前辈都摇头，还预言他们会在湖上冻死，他们仍然往北方奋勇前行。很明显，冰冻期随时可能来临，他们也不再愿意出于安全考虑而耽搁时间了。基于这个原因，利物浦决定开着这艘满载货物的船，冒险穿过连接莱克林德曼和本尼特湖的急流。依惯例，必须把空船沿河流下，然后把货物转成陆路运输。即使这样，仍有许多空船出了事。但此时采取预防措施已经为时过晚。 Into the north they fought their way, although old-timers, coming out, shook their heads and prophesied they would be frozen in on the lakes.That the freeze-up might come any day was patent, and delays of safety were no longer considered.For this reason, Liverpool decided to shoot the rapid stream connecting Linderman to Lake Bennett with the fully loaded boat.It was the custom to line the empty boats down and to portage the cargoes across.Even then many empty boats had been wrecked.But the time was past for such precaution.
“爬出来，老爹。”利物浦一边指挥塔尔沃特，一边准备从河畔上荡过去，进入急流中。 “Climb out, dad,” Liverpool commanded as he prepared to swing from the bank and enter the rapids.
老塔尔沃特摇了摇他花白的头。 Old Tarwater shook his white head.
“我就抓紧这套装备。”他说道，“这是唯一可以渡过的方法。你知道的，孩子，我要去克朗代克。如果我抓紧这艘船，它也就自然地漂到克朗代克了。如果我出去了，那很有可能你们会失去这条船。” “I’m sticking to the outfit,” he declared.“It’s the only way to get through.You see, son, I’m going to Klondike.If I stick by the boat, then the boat just naturally goes to Klondike, too.If I get out, then most likely you’ll lose the boat.”
“嗯，超载可没什么好处。”查尔斯喊着，船的缆绳解开时，他突然跳到岸上。 “Well, there’s no use in overloading,” Charles announced, springing abruptly out on the bank as the boat cast off.
“下次你等我的命令！”利物浦在岸上喊着，水流紧紧吃住了这条船，“而且不要再在这股急流里打转，我们没有时间浪费在救你上！” “Next time you wait for my orders!”Liverpool shouted ashore as the current gripped the boat.“And there won’t be any more walking around rapids and losing time waiting to pick you up!”
他们在河里花了十分钟的时间，查尔斯在陆地上则花了半个小时。当他们在本尼特湖头等他的时候，他们和几个衣衫褴褛要往外走的老前辈一起打发了白天的时光。饥荒的消息比以前都要严重。西北骑警驻扎在马什湖脚下，淘金者就从这里进入到了加拿大的领土。如果一个人没有带够七百磅的食物，骑警是不会让他过去的。在道森城，带着狗队的一千人正等着冰冻期的来临。贸易公司不能履行他们的粮食合同，而合伙人正在签牌，看谁应该走，谁应该留下来处理这些要求。 What took them ten minutes by river, took Charles half an hour by land, and while they waited for him at the head of Lake Bennett they passed the time of day with several dilapidated old-timers on their way out.The famine news was graver than ever.The North-west Mounted Police, stationed at the foot of Lake Marsh where the gold-rushers entered Canadian territory, were refusing to let a man past who did not carry with him seven hundred pounds of grub.In Dawson City a thousand men, with dog-teams, were waiting the freeze-up to come out over the ice.The trading companies could not fill their grub-contracts, and partners were cutting the cards to see which should go and which should stay and work the claims.
“那就解决了。”当他知道在边界处的骑警们的举动之后，查尔斯说道，“老人家，你不妨现在回去吧。” “That settles it,” Charles announced, when he learned of the action of the mounted police on the boundary.“Old Man, you might as well start back now.”
“爬到船上去！”利物浦命令道，“我们要去克朗代克，而且老爹要跟着一起去。” “Climb aboard!” Liverpool commanded.“We’re going to Klondike, and old dad is going along.”
大风转南，让他们得以顺风从本尼特湖而下，在这之前，他们靠利物浦做的一面大帆航行。这套沉重的装备可以视为压舱物，所以在关键时刻，他可以放手一搏，满帆前进。风向西南偏了四个点，而且来得恰是时候，那时他们刚好进入到了卡里布交叉口，风就带着他们顺流直下，到了塔吉什湖和马什湖的连接处。在这个暴风雨的黄昏——他们穿过了危险的大风湾，在那里他们看到另外两条淘金者用来装货物的船只翻覆了，淹没在水中。 A shift of gale to the south gave them a fair wind down Lake Bennett, before which they ran under a huge sail made by Liverpool.The heavy weight of outfit gave such ballast that he cracked on as a daring sailor should when moments counted.A shift of four points into the south-west, coming just at the right time as they entered upon Caribou Crossing, drove them down that connecting link to lakes Tagish and Marsh.In stormy sunset and twilight—they made the dangerous crossing of Great Windy Arm, wherein they beheld two other boat-loads of gold-rushers capsize and drown.
查尔斯说晚上要在沙滩上过夜，但是利物浦坚持航行，通过浅滩上海浪的声音，以及岸上不时出现的火堆（表明有船失事或是有胆小的探险者），他们必须继续航行驶向塔吉什。凌晨四点钟，他叫起了查尔斯。老塔尔沃特颤抖着醒来了，他听到利物浦命令船尾的克雷顿来到舵盘旁，呆在他身边，而且还听到一场只有一个人说话的对话。 Charles was for beaching for the night, but Liverpool held on, steering down Tagish by the sound of the surf on the shoals and by the occasional shore-fires that advertised wrecked or timid argonauts.At four in the morning, he aroused Charles.Old Tarwater, shiveringly awake, heard Liverpool order Crayton aft beside him at the steering-sweep, and also heard the one-sided conversation.
“听着，朋友，查尔斯，闭上你的嘴，”利物浦开始说了，“我要你脑袋里牢牢记着，并且要永远记着：老爹要通过警察这关。懂了吗？他要通过。当他们检查我们的装备的时候，老爹有五分之一的份，知道了吗？那样会降低我们的份额，但是我们可以蒙混过关。现在要明白这点，牢牢地记住：在这件事上谁也别掉链子——” “Just listen, friend Charles, and keep your own mouth shut,” Liverpool began.“I want you to get one thing into your head and keep it there: OLD DAD’S GOING BY THE POLICE.UNDERSTAND?HE’S GOING BY.When they examine our outfit, old dad’s got a fifth share in it, savvee?That’ll put us all ‘way under what we ought to have, but we can bluff it through.Now get this, and get it hard: THERE AIN’T GOING TO BE ANY FALL-DOWN ON THIS BLUFF—”
“如果你认为我会出卖那个老古怪——”查尔斯开始愤怒地说道。 “If you think I’d give away on the old codger—” Charles began indignantly.
“这可是你说的，”利物浦阻止了他，“我可没说。现在——听我说，好好听着：我不关心你在想什么。而是你要想什么。我们今天下午就要经过警察局，我们要准备好顺利地蒙混过去，聪明人一点就明白。” “You thought that,” Liverpool checked him, “because I never mentioned any such thing.Now—get me and get me hard: I don’t care what you’ve been thinking.It’s what you’re going to think.We’ll make the police post some time this afternoon, and we’ve got to get ready to pull the bluff without a hitch, and a word to the wise is plenty.”
“如果你认为我早就想好了——”查尔斯又开始了。 “If you think I’ve got it in my mind—” Charles began again.
“听着。”利物浦打断了他，“我不知道你在想什么。我也不想知道。我想让你知道我在想什么。如果有一丝疏忽，如果老爹被警察送回去，我就挑选一块最安静的风景区把你送上岸。然后把你狠狠地揍一顿。听明白了，一定要听明白了。不会是打你两下就完事，而是把你大卸八块。我不期望会杀了你，但是会揍得你半死。” “Look here,” Liverpool shut him off.“I don’t know what’s in your mind.I don’t want to know.I want you to know what’s in my mind.If there’s any slip-up, if old dad gets turned back by the police, I’m going to pick out the first quiet bit of landscape and take you ashore on it.And then I’m going to beat you up to the Queen’s taste.Get me, and get me hard.It ain’t going to be any half-way beating, but a real, two-legged, two-fisted, he-man beating.I don’t expect I’ll kill you, but I’ll come damn near to half-killing you.”
“但是我还能做什么呢？”查尔斯几乎要呜咽了。 “But what can I do?”Charles almost whimpered.
“就一件事，”这是利物浦的最后一句话，“你就祈祷吧。你就努力祈祷老爹过得了警察这一关吧，他一定得通过。就这样。缩回你的毯子里吧。” “Just one thing,” was Liverpool’s final word.“You just pray.You pray so hard that old dad gets by the police that he does get by.That’s all.Go back to your blankets.”
在他们到达勒巴尔日湖之前，这块土地覆盖着半年不化的皑皑白雪。他们也不能随心所欲地靠岸停船，因为河岸边缘已经结冰了。进入河口，还没到勒巴尔日湖，他们发现了一百艘被暴风雨困住的探险者之船。从北方来的暴风雪毫不停歇，刮过广阔的湖面。连续三个早上，他们都出航与暴风雪搏击，航行在翻滚的波涛中，海水落到船上就结成了冰。当其他人拼命划桨时，老塔尔沃特也奋力保持充足的运动量以生存下去，他挫去船上的冰，然后把它们扔到船外。 Before they gained Lake Le Barge, the land was sheeted with snow that would not melt for half a year.Nor could they lay their boat at will against the bank, for the rim-ice was already forming.Inside the mouth of the river, just ere it entered Lake Le Barge, they found a hundred storm-bound boats of the argonauts.Out of the north, across the full sweep of the great lake, blew an unending snow gale.Three mornings they put out and fought it and the cresting seas it drove that turned to ice as they fell in-board.While the others broke their hearts at the oars, Old Tarwater managed to keep up just sufficient circulation to survive by chopping ice and throwing it overboard.
出航的这三天，他们每天都被折腾得很无助，只能掉头逃跑，躲进避风港湾。到第四天，这一百艘船增加到了三百艘，而船上两千个探险者知道，大风就是勒巴尔日湖冰封期的前兆。在远处，急流还会继续奔流几天，但是，除非他们能立刻走出这里，否则在接下来的六个月里，他们注定会被冻僵的。 Each day for three days, beaten to helplessness, they turned tail on the battle and ran back into the sheltering river.By the fourth day, the hundred boats had increased to three hundred, and the two thousand argonauts on board knew that the great gale heralded the freeze-up of Le Barge.Beyond, the rapid rivers would continue to run for days, but unless they got beyond, and immediately, they were doomed to be frozen in for six months to come.
“今天我们就得过去。”利物浦宣布道，“我们回去一切就白费了。而且我们当中在桨边死去的人，会得到重生，会继续拉船。” “This day we go through,” Liverpool announced.“We turn back for nothing.And those of us that dies at the oars will live again and go on pulling.”
他们渡过了这个难关，在黄昏之前已经过了半个湖，风慢慢减弱，他们整个晚上都在向前拉着，如果有人在船桨边睡着了，利物浦会敲醒他们，然后又得继续辛苦工作，就像要熬过一生的噩梦一样。星星出来了，湖面平静得像一张纸，当他们的桨叶打碎冻结了的薄冰时，那些冰像是破裂的玻璃，发出叮叮声。 And they went through, winning half the length of the lake by nightfall and pulling on through all the night hours as the wind went down, falling asleep at the oars and being rapped awake by Liverpool, toiling on through an age-long nightmare while the stars came out and the surface of the lake turned to the unruffledness of a sheet of paper and froze skin-ice that tinkled like broken glass as their oar-blades shattered it.
天空破晓时十分寒冷，他们进入了河道，身后的海水都结冰了。利物浦查看了一下他年迈的乘客，发现他很虚弱，几乎要死去了。他让船绕行至边缘有冰的地方，要生火让老塔尔沃特里里外外都暖和起来，但查尔斯反对这样浪费时间。 As day broke clear and cold, they entered the river, with behind them a sea of ice.Liverpool examined his aged passenger and found him helpless and almost gone.When he rounded the boat to against the rim-ice to build a fire and warm up Tarwater inside and out, Charles protested against such loss of time.
“这不是生意买卖，所以你不要来干涉，”利物浦警告他，“现在由我负责这次航行。所以你就给我爬出去，劈柴火，多劈点儿。我来照顾好老爹。你，安森，在岸上生堆火。还有你，比尔，在船里点着育空火炉。老爹没有我们这些人年轻，在剩下的旅途中，他得在甲板上的火堆旁烤火。” “This ain’t business, so don’t you come horning in,” Liverpool informed him.“I’m running the boat trip.So you just climb out and chop firewood, and plenty of it.I’ll take care of dad.You, Anson, make a fire on the bank.And you, Bill, set up the Yukon stove in the boat.Old dad ain’t as young as the rest of us, and for the rest of this voyage he’s going to have a fire on board to sit by.”
所有的都过去了，船仍被水流控制着，就像河里的汽船一样，烟从烟囱的两个连接处冒出来，它被分开的水流搁浅在沙滩上。随着向北部严冬地带的深入，船还得跟急流和峡谷对抗。当他们经过的时候，大小萨蒙河的浮冰正流进干流，而且在急流下面，锚冰从河的底部升上来，随之而来的透明的冰渣覆盖了整个水面。这些边缘地区的冰日以继夜地增多，而静止的地方已经延伸到离岸边有一百码远了。老塔尔沃特穿着他所有衣服，坐在火炉旁边，让火一直烧着。他们日以继夜地前行，丝毫不敢停留，因为害怕冰封期会突然来临，他们只有加快速度，同越来越多糊状的冰赛跑。 All of which came to pass; and the boat, in the grip of the current, like a river steamer with smoke rising from the two joints of stove-pipe, grounded on shoals, hung up on split currents, and charged rapids and canyons, as it drove deeper into the Northland winter.The Big and Little Salmon rivers were throwing mush-ice into the main river as they passed, and, below the riffles, anchor- ice arose from the river bottom and coated the surface with crystal scum.Night and day the rim-ice grew, till, in quiet places, it extended out a hundred yards from shore.And Old Tarwater, with all his clothes on, sat by the stove and kept the fire going.Night and day, not daring to stop for fear of the imminent freeze-up, they dared to run, an increasing mushiness of ice running with them.
“怎么了，老朋友？”利物浦会不时地叫叫他。 “What ho, old hearty?”Liverpool would call out at times.
“加油啊。”老塔尔沃特学会了怎么回应。 “Cheer O,” Old Tarwater had learned to respond.
“孩子，作为报答，我能为你做些什么呢？”在烧火的同时，塔尔沃特会不时地问利物浦，而利物浦则在冰冻的舵盘那里，为了血液流通不时地双手交替敲打着。 “What can I ever do for you, son, in payment?”Tarwater, stoking the fire, would sometimes ask Liverpool, beating now one released hand and now the other as he fought for circulation where he steered in the freezing stern-sheets.
“就唱唱你平时总唱的那首歌吧，那个1849年。”他总是这样回复道。 “Just break out that regular song of yours, old Forty-Niner,” was the invariable reply.
而塔尔沃特会提高他的嗓门，咯咯咯地唱这一段，快唱完时他会提高音量，而船也摇摆着进入到有蛋糕那么厚的冰区里，停泊在道森城的岸边了，所有道森海滨区域的人们都竖起了耳朵，听这首胜利的颂歌： And Tarwater would lift his voice in the cackling chant, as he lifted it at the end, when the boat swung in through driving cake-ice and moored to the Dawson City bank, and all waterfront Dawson pricked its ears to hear the triumphant paean:
像古代的阿格斯，我们离开这个现代的希腊，嗒—嗒，嗒—嗒，嗒，嗒，嗒—嗒，来剪金羊毛。 Like Argus of the ancient times,We leave this modern Greece,Tum-tum, tum-tum, tum, tum, tum-tum,To shear the Golden Fleece,
查尔斯也唱起来了，但他唱得很小声，以至于他的团队里没有一个人听到，更别说所有水手了。 Charles did it, but he did it so discreetly that none of his party, least of all the sailor, ever learned of it.
