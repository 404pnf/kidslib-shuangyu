The Six Hungry Beasts 六只饥肠辘辘的动物
by Andrew Lang 作者：安德鲁·朗格
Once upon a time there lived a man who dwelt with his wife in a little hut, far away from any neighbors. 从前，有一对夫妻住在一间小木屋里，附近一个邻居都没有。
But they did not mind being alone, and would have been quite happy, if it had not been for a marten, who came every night to their poultry yard, and carried off one of their fowls. 然而他们并不介意独住，但是有一只貂每天晚上都会溜进他们的禽舍，偷走一只家禽。要是没有这只貂，他们会生活得非常幸福。
The man laid all sorts of traps to catch the thief, but instead of capturing the foe, it happened that one day he got caught himself, and falling down, struck his head against a stone, and was killed. 男人设下各种陷阱来抓贼，非但没抓到贼，有一天他自己反倒掉进了陷阱里，他倒在地上，头撞到石头，就这样一命呜呼了。
Not long after the marten came by on the look out for his supper. 不久，貂来到这里寻觅晚餐。
Seeing the dead man lying there, he said to himself: ‘That is a prize, this time I have done well’; and dragging the body with great difficulty to the sledge which was waiting for him, drove off with his booty. 看到男人躺在地上，已经死了，它自言自语道：“这是对我的奖赏，这次我做得很不错。”貂费了很大的力气把尸体拖到旁边的雪橇上，带着战利品离开了。
He had not driven far when he met a squirrel, who bowed and said: ‘Good-morning, godfather! what have you got behind you?’ 没走多远，它就遇到了一只松鼠，松鼠给它鞠了个躬，说：“貂大哥，早上好！您后面拉的是什么东西呀？”
The marten laughed and answered: ‘Did you ever hear anything so strange? 貂大笑起来，回答说：“你听过这么奇怪的事情吗？
The old man that you see here set traps about his hen-house, thinking to catch me but he fell into his own trap, and broke his own neck. 你看到的这个老头，他想抓住我，就在他家鸡舍周围设下陷阱，不料自己掉进陷阱里，摔断了脖子。
He is very heavy; I wish you would help me to draw the sledge.’ 他太重了，我希望你能帮我一起拉雪橇。”
The squirrel did as he was asked, and the sledge moved slowly along. 松鼠同意了他的要求，和貂一起拉着雪橇慢慢地向前走。
By-and-by a hare came running across a field, but stopped to see what wonderful thing was coming. 不一会儿，从田地里跑过来一只野兔，它停下来，看看发生了什么好事。
‘What have you got there?’ she asked, and the marten told his story and begged the hare to help them pull. “你们拉的是什么呀？”它问道。貂把事情的经过告诉了野兔，并请野兔帮忙一起拉。
The hare pulled her hardest, and after a while they were joined by a fox, and then by a wolf, and at length a bear was added to the company, and he was of more use than all the other five beasts put together. 野兔使劲地拉雪橇。过了一会儿，一只狐狸、一只狼，最后还有一只熊陆续加入了它们的队伍。其中熊的力气最大，比另外五只动物的力气加起来还要大。
Besides, when the whole six had supped off the man he was not so heavy to draw. 而且六只动物吃了男人之后，雪橇拉起来就没有那么重了。
The worst of it was that they soon began to get hungry again, and the wolf, who was the hungriest of all, said to the rest: 可最糟糕的是，它们很快又开始饿了。狼最饿，它对另外五只动物说：
‘What shall we eat now, my friends, as there is no more man?’ “朋友们，没有人可以吃了，我们现在该吃什么呢？”
‘I suppose we shall have to eat the smallest of us,’ replied the bear, and the marten turned round to seize the squirrel who was much smaller than any of the rest. 熊回答说：“我觉得，应该吃我们中间最小的动物。”松鼠比另外五只动物都小得多，于是貂转身去抓松鼠。
But the squirrel ran up a tree like lightning, and the marten remembering, just in time, that he was the next in size, slipped quick as thought into a hole in the rocks. 但松鼠如闪电般上了树。貂立马意识到，现在它是最小的了，便飞快地溜进石洞里。
‘What shall we eat now?’ asked the wolf again, when he had recovered from his surprise. 狼惊呆了，回过神来时又问了一遍：“我们现在该吃什么呢？”
‘We must eat the smallest of us,’ repeated the bear, stretching out a paw towards the hare; but the hare was not a hare for nothing, and before the paw had touched her, she had darted deep into the wood. “我们必须吃最小的那只。”熊一边重复道，一边伸出熊掌去抓野兔。但野兔不愧是野兔，熊掌还没碰到它，它就飞一般地钻进了树林深处。
Now that the squirrel, the marten, and the hare had all gone, the fox was the smallest of the three who were left, and the wolf and the bear explained that they were very sorry, but they would have to eat him. 由于松鼠、貂和野兔都跑掉了，那么剩下的三只动物中数狐狸最小。于是狼和熊对狐狸说，它们非常抱歉，但它们只好吃它了。
Michael, the fox, did not run away as the others had done, but smiled in a friendly manner, and remarked: ‘Things taste so stale in a valley; one’s appetite is so much better up on a mountain.’ 这只名叫迈克尔的狐狸，并没有像前几只动物那样逃跑，反而报以友好的微笑，说道：“在山谷里吃东西，味道不新鲜；到山上吃呢，胃口就会大好。”
The wolf and the bear agreed, and they turned out of the hollow where they had been walking, and chose a path that led up the mountain side. 狼和熊都赞成，它们一起走出山谷，选了一条通往山顶的路。
The fox trotted cheerfully by his two big companions, but on the way he managed to whisper to the wolf: ‘Tell me, Peter, when I am eaten, what will you have for your next dinner?’ 狐狸在两只大动物之间欢快地小跑，但在路上，它想办法悄悄地对狼说：“彼得，告诉我，吃了我以后，你们下顿吃什么呢？”
This simple question seemed to put out the wolf very much. 这个简单的问题似乎让狼十分生气。
What would they have for their next dinner, and, what was more important still, who would there be to eat it? 它们下顿要吃什么呢？更重要的是，谁来吃呢？
They had made a rule always to dine off the smallest of the party, and when the fox was gone, why of course, he was smaller than the bear. 它们有约在先，总是吃最小的动物。狐狸被吃完了之后，只剩下狼和熊了，显然，它要比熊小得多。
These thoughts flashed quickly through his head, and he said hastily: 这些想法在它脑子里快速闪现，它急忙说道：
‘Dear brothers, would it not be better for us to live together as comrades, and everyone to hunt for the common dinner? “好兄弟，如果我们三个结伴儿，一起生活，合伙觅食，食物共享，岂不更好？
Is not my plan a good one?’ 我的主意不错吧？”
‘It is the best thing I have ever heard,’ answered the fox; and as they were two to one the bear had to be content, though in his heart he would much have preferred a good dinner at once to any friendship. “这是我听过的最好的主意。”狐狸回答。由于它们三个当中已有两个同意了，熊也只好同意。尽管在熊心中，友情跟立刻饱餐一顿相比，根本不算什么。
For a few days all went well; there was plenty of game in the forest, and even the wolf had as much to eat as he could wish. 刚开始的几天，一切都很顺利。森林里有很多猎物，就连狼都可以想吃多少就吃多少。
One morning the fox as usual was going his rounds when he noticed a tall, slender tree, with a magpie’s nest in one of the top branches. 一天早上，狐狸像往常一样四处转悠，这时，它注意到一棵又高又细的树，最上面的一根树枝上有个喜鹊窝。
Now the fox was particularly fond of young magpies, and he set about making a plan by which he could have one for dinner. 因为狐狸特别喜欢吃小喜鹊，于是它开始想办法抓一只当饭吃。
At last he hit upon something which he thought would do, and accordingly he sat down near the tree and began to stare hard at it. 终于，它灵光一现，有了认为可行的点子。于是，它在这棵树附近坐了下来，紧盯着这棵树。
‘What are you looking at, Michael?’ asked the magpie, who was watching him from a bough. “迈克尔，你在看什么呢？”树枝上的喜鹊看到了狐狸，问道。
‘I’m looking at this tree. “我在看这棵树。
It has just struck me what a good tree it would be to cut my new snow-shoes out of.’ 这棵树不错，我刚刚想到，正好可以用它来做一双新雪鞋。”
But at this answer the magpie screeched loudly, and exclaimed: ‘Oh, not this tree, dear brother, I implore you! 但喜鹊听了立刻尖叫起来，大喊：“噢，不要挑这棵树，狐狸大哥，我求你了！
I have built my nest on it, and my young ones are not yet old enough to fly.’ 我在这棵树上搭了窝，我的孩子们都还小，还不会飞。”
‘It will not be easy to find another tree that would make such good snow-shoes,’ answered the fox, cocking his head on one side, and gazing at the tree thoughtfully; ‘but I do not like to be ill-natured, so if you will give me one of your young ones I will seek my snow-shoes elsewhere.’ “要另找一棵能做这么好的雪鞋的树，可不容易啊。”狐狸一边回答，一边把头歪向一边，若有所思地盯着这棵树，“不过我不想当恶人，因此，如果你愿把你的一个孩子给我的话，我就会到别处找棵树做雪鞋。”
Not knowing what to do the poor magpie had to agree, and flying back, with a heavy heart, he threw one of his young ones out of the nest. 可怜的喜鹊不知所措，只好同意。它心情沉重地飞回窝里，把它的一个孩子从窝里扔了出来。
The fox seized it in his mouth and ran off in triumph, while the magpie, though deeply grieved for the loss of his little one, found some comfort in the thought that only a bird of extraordinary wisdom would have dreamed of saving the rest by the sacrifice of the one. 狐狸叼住小喜鹊，得意地跑开了。而喜鹊尽管因为失去一个孩子而感到非常痛苦，但想到只有智慧出众的鸟儿才会想到牺牲一只小喜鹊保全其他几只，它又找到了些许安慰。
But what do you think happened? 但是，你猜随后怎样？
Why, a few days later, Michael the fox might have been seen sitting under the very same tree, and a dreadful pang shot through the heart of the magpie as he peeped at him from a hole in the nest. 唉，几天之后，你可能会看到狐狸迈克尔恰好坐在同一棵树下。喜鹊透过窝里的孔隙瞥见了狐狸，一阵剧痛猛地袭上心头。
‘What are you looking at?’ he asked in a trembling voice. “你在看什么？”它声音颤抖地问。
‘At this tree. “看这棵树呀。
I was just thinking what good snowshoes it would make,’ answered the fox in an absent voice, as if he was not thinking of what he was saying. 我刚刚在想，正好可以用它做一双多么好的雪鞋啊。”狐狸心不在焉地回答道，似乎说这些话时并没过多思考。
‘Oh, my brother, my dear little brother, don’t do that,’ cried the magpie, hopping about in his anguish. “噢，我的大哥，我亲爱的大哥，别这么做。”喜鹊哭喊道，痛苦地跳来跳去。
‘You know you promised only a few days ago that you would get your snow-shoes elsewhere.’ “几天前，你刚许诺说会到别处找棵树做雪鞋的。”
‘So I did; but though I have searched through the whole forest, there is not a single tree that is as good as this. “我是许诺过，但我找遍了整个森林，没有一棵树能比这棵树更合适了。
I am very sorry to put you out, but really it is not my fault. 实在抱歉，让你失望了，但这真的不是我的错。
The only thing I can do for you is to offer to give up my snow-shoes altogether if you will throw me down one of your young ones in exchange.’ 如果你肯把你的小喜鹊扔下来一只跟我作交换的话，我会彻底放弃做雪鞋的念头，这是我唯一能为你做的事情了。”
And the poor magpie, in spite of his wisdom, was obliged to throw another of his little ones out of the nest; and this time he was not able to console himself with the thought that he had been much cleverer than other people. 可怜的喜鹊，尽管聪慧，只好把自己的另一个小孩子扔出窝。这一次，它再也无法用自以为比别人更聪明这种想法来安慰自己了。
He sat on the edge of his nest, his head drooping and his feathers all ruffled, looking the picture of misery. 它坐在窝边，耷拉着脑袋，羽毛蓬乱，看起来万分痛苦。
Indeed he was so different from the gay, jaunty magpie whom every creature in the forest knew, that a crow who was flying past, stopped to inquire what was the matter. 的确，它跟森林里的动物们所熟悉的那只欢快、活泼的喜鹊大不相同，以至于一只飞过的乌鸦停了下来，询问发生了什么事情。
‘Where are the two young ones who are not in the nest?’ asked he. “你的那两个孩子呢？怎么不在窝里呀？”乌鸦问道。
‘I had to give them to the fox,’ replied the magpie in a quivering voice; ‘he has been here twice in the last week, and wanted to cut down my tree for the purpose of making snow-shoes out of it, and the only way I could buy him off was by giving him two of my young ones.’ “我不得不把它们给了狐狸。”喜鹊声音颤抖地回答，“上周，它来了两次，说想砍了这棵树，要用它做雪鞋。我只能把两个孩子送给它，才买通了它。”
Oh, you fool,’ cried the crow, ‘the fox was only trying to frighten you. “唉，你这个傻瓜。”乌鸦喊道，“狐狸只是来吓吓你而已。
He could not have cut down the tree, for he has neither axe nor knife. 它既没有斧子也没有刀，是不能把树砍倒的。
Dear me, to think that you have sacrificed your young ones for nothing! 天哪，你居然白白牺牲了两个孩子啊！
Dear, dear! how could you be so very foolish!’ 天哪，天哪！你怎么能这么笨呢！”
And the crow flew away, leaving the magpie overcome with shame and sorrow. 乌鸦飞走了，留下满心羞愧与痛苦的喜鹊。
The next morning the fox came to his usual place in front of the tree, for he was hungry, and a nice young magpie would have suited him very well for dinner. 第二天早上，狐狸又来到老地方，来到了这棵树前，因为它饿了，一只美味的小喜鹊是再合适不过的美餐了。
But this time there was no cowering, timid magpie to do his bidding, but a bird with his head erect and a determined voice. 但这次喜鹊不再胆小地蜷缩成一团，对狐狸言听计从了，反而昂着头，声音坚定。
‘My good fox,’ said the magpie putting his head on one side and looking very wise—‘my good fox, if you take my advice, you will go home as fast as you can. “亲爱的狐狸，”喜鹊边说边把头歪到一边，看起来很聪明的样子，“亲爱的狐狸，如果你肯听我的话，就赶紧滚回家去吧。
There is no use your talking about making snow-shoes out of this tree, when you have neither knife nor axe to cut it down with!’ 你既没有砍树用的刀，也没有斧子，还说要用这棵树来做雪鞋，根本就是胡说！
‘Who has been teaching you wisdom?’ asked the fox, forgetting his manners in his surprise at this new turn of affairs. “谁把你教得这么聪明？”狐狸对事情的新变化感到意外，都没顾得上风度，忙问道。
‘The crow, who paid me a visit yesterday,’ answered the magpie. “乌鸦，它昨天来看我了。”喜鹊答道。
‘The crow was it?’ said the fox, ‘well, the crow had better not meet me for the future, or it may be the worse for him.’ “乌鸦？”狐狸说，“好吧，从今以后，最好别让乌鸦碰到我，否则更有它好受的。”
As Michael, the cunning beast, had no desire to continue the conversation, he left the forest; but when he came to the high road he laid himself at full length on the ground, stretching himself out, just as if he was dead. 狡猾的狐狸迈克尔无心再与喜鹊说话，就离开了森林。但一到大路上，它就伸直四肢，平躺在地上，仿佛死了一般。
Very soon he noticed, out of the corner of his eye, that the crow was flying towards him, and he kept stiller and stiffer than ever, with his tongue hanging out of his mouth. 不久，它从眼角瞄到乌鸦正向自己飞过来，就越发安静，越发僵直，舌头也挂在嘴外。
The crow, who wanted her supper very badly, hopped quickly towards him, and was stooping forward to peck at his tongue when the fox gave a snap, and caught him by the wing. 乌鸦正急切地寻觅晚餐，便飞快地向狐狸飞过来。它俯下身子正要啄狐狸的舌头时，狐狸猛然一咬，咬住了乌鸦的翅膀。
The crow knew that it was of no use struggling, so he said: 乌鸦知道挣扎是没用的，于是它说：
‘Ah, brother, if you are really going to eat me, do it, I beg of you, in good style. “啊，兄弟，如果你真要吃掉我，就吃吧，我求你了，吃得体面一点儿。
Throw me first over this precipice, so that my feathers may be strewn here and there, and that all who see them may know that your cunning is greater than mine.’ 先把我从悬崖上扔下去，这样我的羽毛就会到处散落，无论谁看到我的羽毛，都会知道你比我聪明多了。”
This idea pleased the fox, for he had not yet forgiven the crow for depriving him of the young magpies, so he carried the crow to the edge of the precipice and threw him over, intending to go round by a path he knew and pick him up at the bottom. 听了这个主意，狐狸非常开心，因为它对乌鸦害自己没能吃到小喜鹊的事一直记恨在心，于是它把乌鸦带到悬崖边，把乌鸦扔了出去，打算要沿自己熟悉的一条路到崖底去捡乌鸦的尸体。
But no sooner had the fox let the crow go than he soared up into the air, and hovering just out of touch of his enemy’s jaws, he cried with a laugh: ‘Ah, fox! you know well how to catch, but you cannot keep.’ 但狐狸刚把乌鸦扔出去，乌鸦就飞上了天空，在敌人嘴巴够不到的地方盘旋，一边大笑一边喊道：“啊，狐狸！你抓动物的本领很强，但守住动物的本领却不行。”
With his tail between his legs, the fox slunk into the forest. 狐狸夹着尾巴溜进了森林。
He did not know where to look for a dinner, as he guessed that the crow would have flown back before him, and put everyone on their guard. 它不知道要到哪儿觅食，因为它猜想乌鸦会先于它飞回来，一定会让每个动物都提防自己。
The notion of going to bed supperless was very unpleasant to him, and he was wondering what in the world he should do, when he chanced to meet with his old friend the bear. 一想到要饿着肚子睡觉，狐狸就觉得很不高兴。它正在想在到底该做什么的时候，碰巧遇到了它的老朋友——熊。
This poor animal had just lost his wife, and was going to get someone to mourn over her, for he felt her loss greatly. 可怜的熊刚失去自己的妻子，感到万分悲恸，正要找人为妻子哭丧。
He had hardly left his comfortable cave when he had come across the wolf, who inquired where he was going. 它刚离开舒适的洞穴，就碰到了狼，狼问熊要去哪儿。
‘I am going to find a mourner,’ answered the bear, and told his story. “我要去找一个哭丧的。”熊回答道，并告诉狼事情的原委。
‘Oh, let me mourn for you,’ cried the wolf. “哦，让我来哭吧。”狼大声说。
‘Do you understand how to howl?’ said the bear. “你知道怎么哭丧吗？”熊问。
‘Oh, certainly, godfather, certainly,’ replied the wolf; but the bear said he should like to have a specimen of his howling, to make sure that he knew his business. “哦，当然，大哥，我当然知道。”狼回答。但熊说它想让狼先试着哭一下，以确信狼知道怎么哭丧。
So the wolf broke forth in his song of lament: ‘Hu, hu, hu, hum, hoh,’ he shouted, and he made such a noise that the bear put up his paws to his ears, and begged him to stop. 于是，狼突然放声哀嚎：“呜——呜——呜——呣——嗷。”狼大叫起来，声音非常吵，以至于熊用爪子捂着耳朵，恳求狼停下来。
‘You have no idea how it is done. “你根本不知道怎样哭丧。
Be off with you,’ said he angrily. 快滚。”熊气呼呼地说。
A little further down the road the hare was resting in a ditch, but when she saw the bear, she came out and spoke to him, and inquired why he looked so sad. 沿着路往前再走一段，野兔正在沟里休息。但它看到了熊后，便出来跟熊打招呼，问熊为什么看起来那么悲伤。
The bear told her of the loss of his wife, and of his search after a mourner that could lament over her in the proper style. 熊告诉野兔，自己刚失去了妻子，想找个哭丧的，用合适的方式来哀悼妻子。
The hare instantly offered her services, but the bear took care to ask her to give him a proof of her talents, before he accepted them. 野兔立刻说自己愿意帮忙，但熊留了心，在答应之前，它让野兔来向它证明自己的才能。
‘Pu, pu, pu, pum, poh,’ piped the hare; but this time her voice was so small that the bear could hardly hear her. “噗——噗——噗——噗——呸。”野兔尖叫起来，但它的声音太小，熊几乎听不到。
‘That is not what I want,’ he said, ‘I will bid you good morning.’ “这不是我想要的。”熊说，“再见吧。”
It was after this that the fox came up, and he also was struck with the bear’s altered looks, and stopped. 在这之后，狐狸走了过来。它看到熊脸色异常，吓了一跳，就停下来。
‘What is the matter with you, godfather?’ asked he, ‘and where are you going?’ “大哥，你怎么了？”它问道，“你要去哪儿？”
‘I am going to find a mourner for my wife,’ answered the bear. “我要为我的妻子找个哭丧的。”熊答道。
‘Oh, do choose me,’ cried the fox, and the bear looked at him thoughtfully. “哦，那就找我吧。”狐狸大声说，熊若有所思地看了看它。
‘Can you howl well?’ he said. “你哭得好听吗？”它说。
‘Yes, beautifully, just listen,’ and the fox lifted up his voice and sang weeping: ‘Lou, lou, lou! the famous spinner, the baker of good cakes, the prudent housekeeper is torn from her husband! “好听，好听极了，听着。”狐狸提高了嗓门哭了起来，“咕——咕——咕，它远近闻名，织得一手好布，做得一手好饭，它勤俭持家，它就这么离开了它的丈夫！
Lou, lou, lou! she is gone! she is gone!’ 咕——咕——咕！它已离开了！它已离开了！”
‘Now at last I have found someone who knows the art of lamentation,’ exclaimed the bear, quite delighted; and he led the fox back to his cave, and bade him begin his lament over the dead wife who was lying stretched out on her bed of grey moss. “现在，我终于找到一个会哭丧的了。”熊高兴得大叫起来。于是，它把狐狸领回洞穴。去世的妻子直挺挺地平躺在床上，上面满是灰色藓苔，熊叫狐狸开始为妻子哭丧。
But this did not suit the fox at all. 但这一点儿都不合狐狸的意。
‘One cannot wail properly in this cave,’ he said, ‘it is much too damp. “在这个洞穴里，是不能好好哭丧的。”它说，“这里太潮湿了。
You had better take the body to the storehouse. 你最好把尸体放在仓库里。
It will sound much finer there.’ 在那里，哭丧的声音会好听得多。”
So the bear carried his wife’s body to the storehouse, while he himself went back to the cave to cook some pap for the mourner. 于是，熊把妻子的尸体抱到了仓库，自己则回到洞穴去给哭丧者煮点粥。
From time to time he paused and listened for the sound of wailing, but he heard nothing. 它不时地停下来听听哭丧的声音，但什么都没听到。
At last he went to the door of the storehouse, and called to the fox: 最后，它来到仓库门前，对狐狸喊道：
‘Why don’t you howl, godfather? “你为什么不哭啊，兄弟？
What are you about?’ 你在干什么呢？”
And the fox, who, instead of weeping over the dead bear, had been quietly eating her, answered: 狐狸并没有为死去的熊哭丧，反而正悄悄地吃着尸体，它回答说：
‘There only remain now her legs and the soles of her feet. “现在只剩下它的腿和脚掌了。
Give me five minutes more and they will be gone also!’ 再给我五分钟，我会把它们也都吃完的！”
When the bear heard that he ran back for the kitchen ladle, to give the traitor the beating he deserved. 熊听后，跑回厨房拿了长柄勺，要给这个叛徒它应得的毒打。
But as he opened the door of the storehouse, Michael was ready for him, and slipping between his legs, dashed straight off into the forest. 但它刚打开仓库门，迈克尔已做好了准备，从熊的两腿之间溜走，立即匆忙飞奔到森林中。
The bear, seeing that the traitor had escaped, flung the ladle after him, and it just caught the tip of his tail, and that is how there comes to be a spot of white on the tails of all foxes. 看到这个叛徒逃走了，熊把长柄勺朝狐狸扔了过去，却只打到了狐狸的尾巴尖。这就是所有的狐狸尾巴上都有一个白点的原因。
[From Finnische Mahrchen.] ——选自《芬兰童话故事》
