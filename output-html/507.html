CHAPTER I. 第一章
When Death is present in a household on a Christmas Day, the very contrast between the time as it now is, and the day as it has often been, gives a poignancy to sorrow—a more utter blankness to the desolation. 当死神在圣诞日降临，对于一个家庭而言，与平日形成鲜明对比的是，此时此刻的悲伤中又平添了一股辛酸——就好比荒凉之中多了一片更彻底的空白。
James Leigh died just as the far-away bells of Rochdale Church were ringing for morning service on Christmas Day, 1836. 1836年，圣诞日，罗奇代尔教堂那遥远的早礼拜钟声刚刚敲响，詹姆斯·利去世了。
A few minutes before his death, he opened his already glazing eyes, and made a sign to his wife, by the faint motion of his lips, that he had yet something to say. 去世前几分钟，他睁开了那双已经呆滞无神的眼睛，嘴唇微微颤动，向妻子示意他还有话要交代。
She stooped close down, and caught the broken whisper, “I forgive her, Annie! 她俯下身靠近他，听到了那断断续续的耳语：“我原谅她，安妮！
May God forgive me!” 愿上帝原谅我！”
“Oh, my love, my dear! only get well, and I will never cease showing my thanks for those words. “哦，我的爱人，我亲爱的！赶快好起来吧，我会永远感激你说的这些话的。
May God in heaven bless thee for saying them. 愿上帝因你说的这些话保佑你！
Thou’rt not so restless, my lad! may be—Oh, God!” 安息吧，我的爱人！也许——哦，上帝啊！”
For even while she spoke he died. 她的话尚未说完，他便撒手人寰。
They had been two-and-twenty years man and wife; for nineteen of those years their life had been as calm and happy as the most perfect uprightness on the one side, and the most complete confidence and loving submission on the other, could make it. 他们结为夫妇已有二十二年了，其中的十九年中，他们一方是正气满身，另一方则是自信与谦恭兼备，这样的结合使得他们的生活过得平静而且幸福。
Milton’s famous line might have been framed and hung up as the rule of their married life, for he was truly the interpreter, who stood between God and her; she would have considered herself wicked if she had ever dared even to think him austere, though as certainly as he was an upright man, so surely was he hard, stern, and inflexible. 弥尔顿的名言可能会被镶框并被挂起来作为他们婚姻生活的准则，他是上帝和她之间真正的阐释者；她甚至都不敢把他想成严厉的人，那样她会觉得自己是邪恶的，但正如他肯定是一个正直的人那样，他也无疑是一个冷酷、严厉和顽固的人。
But for three years the moan and the murmur had never been out of her heart; she had rebelled against her husband as against a tyrant, with a hidden, sullen rebellion, which tore up the old landmarks of wifely duty and affection, and poisoned the fountains whence gentlest love and reverence had once been for ever springing. 但是近三年来，她心中的不满和抱怨从来就没有消散过；她像反抗一个暴君那样，以一种隐忍、乖戾的方式来对抗她的丈夫，这摧毁了那过去象征妻子责任和感情的标志，也毒化了那曾经不断涌出温柔的爱意和敬畏的喷泉。
But those last blessed words replaced him on his throne in her heart, and called out penitent anguish for all the bitter estrangement of later years. 可是他最后那些祝福的话又把他重新放回了她心目中的王座上，并且唤起了由于过去几年那所有辛酸的隔阂带来的忏悔的痛苦。
It was this which made her refuse all the entreaties of her sons, that she would see the kind-hearted neighbours, who called on their way from church, to sympathize and condole. 正因如此，当她的儿子们恳求她出来会见那些好心从教堂归来后登门拜访表示同情和慰问的邻居们时，她断然拒绝。
No! she would stay with the dead husband that had spoken tenderly at last, if for three years he had kept silence; who knew but what, if she had only been more gentle and less angrily reserved he might have relented earlier—and in time? 不！她要守在她那已经过世的丈夫身边，虽然三年来他一直保持沉默，但他最后的话语却是那么体贴；如果她之前能够表现得更加温柔一点儿，没那么愤怒和冷淡，他的态度或许会早一点儿，甚至及时得到缓和，这又有谁知道呢？
She sat rocking herself to and fro by the side of the bed, while the footsteps below went in and out; she had been in sorrow too long to have any violent burst of deep grief now; the furrows were well worn in her cheeks, and the tears flowed quietly, if incessantly, all the day long. 她坐在床边，来回摆动着身体，楼下脚步声进出不断；她已经悲伤得太久，现在不会再有任何沉痛的激烈爆发；她的脸颊布满泪痕，眼泪无声地滑落，一整天来从未间断。
But when the winter’s night drew on, and the neighbours had gone away to their homes, she stole to the window, and gazed out, long and wistfully, over the dark grey moors. 但是随着冬日夜晚的临近，邻居们也各自回家了，她悄悄地靠近窗口，长久地凝视着深灰色的荒野，若有所思。
She did not hear her son’s voice, as he spoke to her from the door, nor his footstep as he drew nearer. 她没有听到儿子在门口跟她说话的声音，也没有听到他走近时的脚步声。
She started when he touched her. 当他碰到她时，她吓了一跳。
“Mother! come down to us. “妈妈！和我们一起到楼下来。
There’s no one but Will and me. 那儿只有我和威尔。
Dearest mother, we do so want you.” 最亲爱的妈妈，我们很需要你。”
The poor lad’s voice trembled, and he began to cry. 这可怜的孩子声音发颤，哭了起来。
It appeared to require an effort on Mrs. Leigh’s part to tear herself away from the window, but with a sigh she complied with his request. 利太太看起来似乎并不太愿意离开窗前，但她只是叹了口气，还是答应了孩子的请求。
The two boys (for though Will was nearly twenty-one, she still thought of him as a lad) had done everything in their power to make the house-place comfortable for her. 两个孩子（尽管威尔已经快二十一岁了，她仍然把他当作孩子来看待）已经做了他们能做的一切，尽可能使她在房子里感到舒适。
She herself, in the old days before her sorrow, had never made a brighter fire or a cleaner hearth, ready for her husband’s return home, than now awaited her. 就连她自己，在经历这些悲痛之前的日子里，也从来没有像现在孩子们等待她这样，把火烧得那么旺，把炉子收拾得那么干净，来等待她的丈夫回家。
The tea-things were all put out, and the kettle was boiling; and the boys had calmed their grief down into a kind of sober cheerfulness. 茶具已经全部摆好，水壶也在沸腾；两个孩子已经冷静下来，不再悲伤，并且有了一丝审慎的喜悦。
They paid her every attention they could think of, but received little notice on her part; she did not resist, she rather submitted to all their arrangements; but they did not seem to touch her heart. 他们想尽一切办法来关注她，而她却对此不以为然；她没有反抗，而是宁愿顺从他们所有的安排，但是他们的所作所为似乎并没有触及她的内心世界。
When tea was ended—it was merely the form of tea that had been gone through—Will moved the things away to the dresser. 喝完茶——其实只不过是过去喝茶的那种形式而已——威尔把茶具收回到了餐具柜中。
His mother leant back languidly in her chair. 他的母亲则懒懒地向后靠在椅子里。
“Mother, shall Tom read you a chapter? “母亲，让汤姆给你读一段文章吧？
He’s a better scholar than I.” 他的学问比我高。”
“Ay, lad!” said she, almost eagerly. “好的，孩子！”她回答道，几乎是迫不及待，
“That’s it. “这样吧。
Read me the Prodigal Son. 给我读下浪子回头的故事吧。
Ay, ay, lad. 对，对，孩子。
Thank thee.” 谢谢你。”
Tom found the chapter, and read it in the high-pitched voice which is customary in village schools. 汤姆找到了那一章，以一种在乡村学校里惯用的高音来诵读。
His mother bent forward, her lips parted, her eyes dilated; her whole body instinct with eager attention. 他的母亲身体前倾，嘴巴张开，双眼圆睁，她的整个身体充满了热切的关注。
Will sat with his head depressed and hung down. 威尔则垂头丧气地坐在一边。
He knew why that chapter had been chosen; and to him it recalled the family’s disgrace. 他清楚母亲为什么选择那一章，这让他想起了他们家庭的耻辱。
When the reading was ended, he still hung down his head in gloomy silence. 诵读结束时，他仍然低垂着脑袋，闷不作声。
But her face was brighter than it had been before for the day. 但是他母亲的脸色比这天早些时候要开朗了许多。
Her eyes looked dreamy, as if she saw a vision; and by-and-by she pulled the Bible towards her, and, putting her finger underneath each word, began to read them aloud in a low voice to herself; she read again the words of bitter sorrow and deep humiliation; but most of all, she paused and brightened over the father’s tender reception of the repentant prodigal. 她的双眼开始朦胧，仿佛看到了一个幻影；她把圣经慢慢地拉向自己，手指放在每一行字下面，开始以一种低沉的声音大声地读给自己听；她再一次品读这些充满了苦涩的伤痛和深深的羞辱的文字；但是最重要的是，她在读到浪子回头中父亲以温柔的情怀迎回悔改的浪子时，她停顿了下来，人也变得活泼了许多。
So passed the Christmas evening in the Upclose Farm. 阿普克洛斯农场的圣诞夜晚就这样过去了。
The snow had fallen heavily over the dark waving moorland before the day of the funeral. 葬礼前一天，大雪在黑暗起伏的荒野里肆虐。
The black storm-laden dome of heaven lay very still and close upon the white earth, as they carried the body forth out of the house which had known his presence so long as its ruling power. 风暴充斥的黑色苍穹静静地覆盖在皑皑的大地上，人们把詹姆斯·利的尸体从他一直居住的房子里抬了出来。
Two and two the mourners followed, making a black procession, in their winding march over the unbeaten snow, to Milne Row Church; now lost in some hollow of the bleak moors, now slowly climbing the heaving ascents. 送葬者们成双结对地跟在后面，形成了一条黑色的队伍，蜿蜒前行在人迹罕至的雪地上，向米尔恩·罗教堂走去；一会儿消失在某处空旷荒凉的沼泽地里，一会儿又缓慢地攀登在起伏的山坡上。
There was no long tarrying after the funeral, for many of the neighbours who accompanied the body to the grave had far to go, and the great white flakes which came slowly down were the boding forerunners of a heavy storm. 葬礼后，许多陪送遗体来送葬的邻居并没有耽搁太久，他们还有很长的一段路要走回去，缓慢飘落的大片雪花也向人们预告着一场大风暴的来临。
One old friend alone accompanied the widow and her sons to their home. 最后陪伴这可怜的寡妇和她的孩子回家的，是一个老朋友。
The Upclose Farm had belonged for generations to the Leighs; and yet its possession hardly raised them above the rank of labourers. 利家人拥有阿普克洛斯农场已经有好几代了，但这却并没有使他们的身份比普通的劳动者高出多少。
There was the house and out-buildings, all of an old-fashioned kind, and about seven acres of barren unproductive land, which they had never possessed capital enough to improve; indeed, they could hardly rely upon it for subsistence; and it had been customary to bring up the sons to some trade, such as a wheelwright’s or blacksmith’s. 农场的房子及其附属建筑都很老式，还有大约七英亩的荒芜贫瘠的土地一直缺乏足够的资金来改善；事实上，他们很难依靠这片土地来维持生计，因此，两个男孩很早就习惯了去学习掌握一些其他的手艺，比如车轮修造或者锻工。
James Leigh had left a will in the possession of the old man who accompanied them home. 詹姆斯·利留下的一封遗嘱由陪他们一起回家的老人保管。
He read it aloud. 他大声地宣读遗嘱。
James had bequeathed the farm to his faithful wife, Anne Leigh, for her lifetime, and afterwards to his son William. 詹姆斯把农场留给了他忠贞的妻子，安妮·利终身所有，在她之后是他的儿子威廉。
The hundred and odd pounds in the savings bank was to accumulate for Thomas. 而储蓄账户中的一百多英镑则作为积累财富留给了托马斯。
After the reading was ended, Anne Leigh sat silent for a time and then she asked to speak to Samuel Orme alone. 遗嘱宣读完毕后，安妮·利默不作声地坐了一会儿，然后她要求和塞缪尔·奥姆单独谈谈。
The sons went into the back kitchen, and thence strolled out into the fields regardless of the driving snow. 两个孩子走进了后厨房，从那里溜达了出去，冒着大雪，来到了田间。
The brothers were dearly fond of each other, although they were very different in character. 兄弟两个尽管在性格上非常不同，他们之间的感情却非常深厚。
Will, the elder, was like his father, stern, reserved, and scrupulously upright. 威尔，作为大哥，和他的父亲一样，严肃，缄默，是一个满身正气的人。
Tom (who was ten years younger) was gentle and delicate as a girl, both in appearance and character. 而比威尔小十岁的汤姆则无论从外貌上还是性格上都表现得非常温和、娇弱，像个女孩子一样。
He had always clung to his mother and dreaded his father. 一直以来，他总是害怕父亲，而粘在母亲的身边。
They did not speak as they walked, for they were only in the habit of talking about facts, and hardly knew the more sophisticated language applied to the description of feelings. 他们默不作声地走着，因为他们习惯了只在讨论事情时说话，很少会用复杂的语言来描述内心的感受。
Meanwhile their mother had taken hold of Samuel Orme’s arm with her trembling hand. 此时，他们的母亲用颤抖的手紧紧抓住塞缪尔·奥姆的手臂。
“Samuel, I must let the farm—I must.” “塞缪尔，我必须得把农场租出去——我必须得这么做。”
“Let the farm! “把农场租出去！
What’s come o’er the woman?” 你怎么了？”
“Oh, Samuel!” said she, her eyes swimming in tears, “I’m just fain to go and live in Manchester. “噢，塞缪尔！”她双眼满含热泪，“我想搬去曼彻斯特住。
I mun let the farm.” 我必须得把农场租出去。”
Samuel looked, and pondered, but did not speak for some time. 塞缪尔看着她，深思着，有一段时间没有说话。
At last he said— 最后他说道：
“If thou hast made up thy mind, there’s no speaking again it; and thou must e’en go. “如果你已经下定了决心，那就没有争论的必要了，你一定要去的。
Thou’lt be sadly pottered wi’ Manchester ways; but that’s not my look out. 你会受不了曼彻斯特的生活方式的，但这并不是我应该担心的。
Why, thou’lt have to buy potatoes, a thing thou hast never done afore in all thy born life. 唉，你将不得不去买土豆，这是你从出生到现在都没有做过的事情。
Well! it’s not my look out. 好啦！这也不是我应该担心的。
It’s rather for me than again me. 实际上你这样做对我也是有好处的。
Our Jenny is going to be married to Tom Higginbotham, and he was speaking of wanting a bit of land to begin upon. 我的女儿珍妮马上要和汤姆·希金博特姆结婚了，他说想要一片土地来耕种。　
His father will be dying sometime, I reckon, and then he’ll step into the Croft Farm. 他的父亲早晚会过世的，我估计到那时他将会继承克罗夫特农场。
But meanwhile—” 但是，在那之前——”
“Then, thou’lt let the farm,” said she, still as eagerly as ever. “那么说，你会租下农场。”她说道，和之前一样迫切。
“Ay, ay, he’ll take it fast enough, I’ve a notion. “当然，当然，我想他会很快租下农场的。
But I’ll not drive a bargain with thee just now; it would not be right; we’ll wait a bit.” 但是我不会现在就和你讨价还价的，现在还不太合适，我们可以等一下。”
“No; I cannot wait; settle it out at once.” “不，我不能等了，现在就定下来吧。”
“Well, well; I’ll speak to Will about it. “好吧，好吧，我会和威尔谈这件事的。
I see him out yonder. 我看见他在那边。
I’ll step to him and talk it over.” 我现在就过去和他讨论这件事。”
Accordingly he went and joined the two lads, and, without more ado, began the subject to them. 于是他走过去加入到两个小伙子当中，开门见山地和他们提起了这件事情。
“Will, thy mother is fain to go live in Manchester, and covets to let the farm. “威尔，你的母亲想去曼彻斯特居住并且渴望把农场租出去。
Now, I’m willing to take it for Tom Higginbotham; but I like to drive a keen bargain, and there would be no fun chaffering with thy mother just now. 因此，我想把它租给汤姆·希金博特姆，但是我希望能快点儿成交，现在和你母亲讨价还价并不好受。
Let thee and me buckle to, my lad! and try and cheat each other; it will warm us this cold day.” 让我们一起努力吧，孩子们！试着去哄骗一下彼此，会让我们在这寒冷日子感到温暖的。”
“Let the farm!” said both the lads at once, with infinite surprise. “出租农场？”两个孩子大吃一惊，齐声嚷道，
“Go live in Manchester!” “去曼彻斯特居住？”
When Samuel Orme found that the plan had never before been named to either Will or Tom, he would have nothing to do with it, he said, until they had spoken to their mother. 当塞缪尔·奥姆发现无论是威尔还是汤姆都对这个计划事先一无所知时，他表示，在孩子们和他们的母亲没有讨论这件事之前，他什么也不会做的。
Likely she was “dazed” by her husband’s death; he would wait a day or two, and not name it to any one; not to Tom Higginbotham himself, or may be he would set his heart upon it. 她很可能是因为丈夫的去世才“鬼迷心窍”，他会等上一两天，在这之前他不会向任何人提起此事，尤其是汤姆·希金博特姆，不然他可能会为此打定主意的。
The lads had better go in and talk it over with their mother. 孩子们则最好回去和他们的母亲讨论下这件事情。
He bade them good-day, and left them. 他告别了他们，离开了。
Will looked very gloomy, but he did not speak till they got near the house. 威尔看上去非常忧郁，但是直到他们靠近房子时，他才开口说话。
Then he said— 他说道：
“Tom, go to th’ shippon, and supper the cows. “汤姆，去牛舍看看，喂一下奶牛。
I want to speak to mother alone.” 我想和母亲单独谈谈。”
When he entered the house-place, she was sitting before the fire, looking into its embers. 当他走进房间的时候，她正坐在火炉边，眼睛盯着里面的炭火。
She did not hear him come in: for some time she had lost her quick perception of outward things. 她没有听到他进来的声音：她对外界事物的感知已经有一段时间不像以前那样快速了。
“Mother! what’s this about going to Manchester?” asked he. “妈妈！为什么要去曼彻斯特？”他问道。
“Oh, lad!” said she, turning round, and speaking in a beseeching tone, “I must go and seek our Lizzie. “哦，孩子！”她说道，转过身来，带着一种近乎恳求的语气，“我必须去寻找我们的莉齐。
I cannot rest here for thinking on her. 呆在这儿想她，我无法安心。
Many’s the time I’ve left thy father sleeping in bed, and stole to th’ window, and looked and looked my heart out towards Manchester, till I thought I must just set out and tramp over moor and moss straight away till I got there, and then lift up every downcast face till I came to our Lizzie. 很多次我在你父亲睡着的时候离开床畔，悄悄地来到窗前，就这么望着，望着，我的心也飘向了曼彻斯特，直到我认为我必须立刻启程，越过荒野和沼泽，一路到那儿，然后抬起一张张低垂的脸，直到找到我们的莉齐。
And often, when the south wind was blowing soft among the hollows, I’ve fancied (it could but be fancy, thou knowest) I heard her crying upon me; and I’ve thought the voice came closer and closer, till at last it was sobbing out, ‘Mother!’ close to the door; and I’ve stolen down, and undone the latch before now, and looked out into the still, black night, thinking to see her—and turned sick and sorrowful when I heard no living sound but the sough of the wind dying away. 时常，当南风轻拂在山谷中时，我想象（也只能是想象，你知道的）我听到了她在恳求我，我感到她的声音越来越近，到最后成了在门前喊‘妈妈’的哭诉，于是我悄悄地下楼，打开门闩，向外面寂静、漆黑的夜晚望去，想着能看到她——结果是让人失望和伤心的，我听不到一丝活着的声音，只有逐渐逝去的飒飒的风声。

I’m no reader, but I learnt off them texts to comfort me a bit, and I’ve said them many a time a day to myself. 我不读书，但是我把它们背下来了，这样能给我一点点安慰，并且我每天都念给自己听很多次。
Lizzie, lass, don’t hide thy head so; it’s thy mother as is speaking to thee. 莉齐，我的姑娘，别再这样蒙着头了，和你说话的是你的母亲。
Thy little child clung to me only yesterday; and if it’s gone to be an angel, it will speak to God for thee. 你的孩子昨天才扑向我的怀抱，如果她成为天使，她会对上帝提起你的。
Nay, don’t sob a that ’as; thou shalt have it again in heaven; I know thou’lt strive to get there, for thy little Nancy’s sake—and listen! 别，别这么哭了，你会在天堂再次拥有她的，为了你的小南希，我知道你会努力到达那儿的——听着！
I’ll tell thee God’s promises to them that are penitent—only doan’t be afeard.” 我会告诉你上帝对那些忏悔者的承诺——只是不要再害怕了。”
Mrs. Leigh folded her hands, and strove to speak very clearly, while she repeated every tender and merciful text she could remember. 利太太双手合十，重复诵念着她能记起来的每一个温柔慈悲的章节，努力使自己的声音非常清晰。
She could tell from the breathing that her daughter was listening; but she was so dizzy and sick herself when she had ended, that she could not go on speaking. 她从女儿的呼吸中可以判断出她在听，但是在诵读完完毕后她自己是那么头晕和难受，她无法再继续说下去。
It was all she could do to keep from crying aloud. 只有这样做，她才不会大声哭出来。
At last she heard her daughter’s voice. 终于她听到了女儿的声音。
“Where have they taken her to?” she asked. “他们把她带哪儿去了？”她问道。
“She is downstairs. “她在楼下。
So quiet, and peaceful, and happy she looks.” 她看起来是那么的安详、平静和幸福。”
“Could she speak! “要是她能说话多好！
Oh, if God—if I might but have heard her little voice! 哦，如果上帝——如果我可以听到她那小小的声音！
Mother, I used to dream of it. 妈妈，我过去常常梦到她。
May I see her once again? 我还会再见到她吗？
Oh, mother, if I strive very hard and God is very merciful, and I go to heaven, I shall not know her—I shall not know my own again: she will shun me as a stranger, and chug to Susan Palmer and to you. 哦，妈妈，如果我非常努力，并且上帝也非常仁慈，我去了天堂，我会认不出她——我会连自己都认不出来；她会像躲避生人一样躲开我，扑到苏珊·帕尔默和你的怀里。
Oh, woe! 哦，呜呜！
Oh, woe!” 哦，呜呜！”
She shook with exceeding sorrow. 她极度悲哀地摇了摇头。
In her earnestness of speech she had uncovered her face, and tried to read Mrs. Leigh’s thoughts through her looks. 在她说出这些诚挚的话时，双手已经从脸上拿开，试图从利太太的表情读出她心里面的想法。
And when she saw those aged eyes brimming full of tears, and marked the quivering lips, she threw her arms round the faithful mother’s neck, and wept there, as she had done in many a childish sorrow, but with a deeper, a more wretched grief. 当她看到那双老迈的双眼噙满了泪水，留意到那颤抖着的嘴唇时，她伸出双臂环绕住她忠贞的母亲的脖颈，哭了起来，就像以前多次经历的儿时哀伤一样，只是这次的哀伤更加深沉，更加悲惨。
Her mother hushed her on her breast; and lulled her as if she were a baby; and she grew still and quiet. 母亲把她抱在怀里，像哄小孩一样哄着她，她逐渐安静了下来。
They sat thus for a long, long time. 她们就那样坐了很长、很长的时间。
At last, Susan Palmer came up with some tea and bread and butter for Mrs. Leigh. 最后，苏珊·帕尔默上楼来给利太太带了一些茶、面包以及黄油。
She watched the mother feed her sick, unwilling child, with every fond inducement to eat which she could devise; they neither of them took notice of Susan’s presence. 她看着这位母亲喂她那生病的、不情愿的孩子吃东西，每一口她都费尽心机地诱导；她们两个谁也没有注意到苏珊的存在。
That night they lay in each other’s arms; but Susan slept on the ground beside them. 那一夜她们躺在彼此的怀抱里，而苏珊则睡在她们身旁的地面上。
They took the little corpse (the little unconscious sacrifice, whose early calling-home had reclaimed her poor wandering mother) to the hills, which in her lifetime she had never seen. 他们把孩子那小小的遗体（这幼小无辜的牺牲者啊，她的夭折感化了她那可怜的流浪母亲）带到了她一生都没有见过的山上。
They dared not lay her by the stern grandfather in Milne Row churchyard, but they bore her to a lone moorland graveyard, where, long ago, the Quakers used to bury their dead. 他们不敢把她葬在米尔恩·罗教堂墓地里她严厉的祖父身边，而是把她带到了一片人迹罕至的荒野墓地，很久以前，那儿曾经是贵格会教徒的埋葬地点。
They laid her there on the sunny slope, where the earliest spring flowers blow. 他们把她葬在了向阳坡上，最早的春花会在那里开放。
Will and Susan live at the Upclose Farm. 威尔和苏珊住在阿普克洛斯农场。
Mrs. Leigh and Lizzie dwell in a cottage so secluded that, until you drop into the very hollow where it is placed, you do not see it. 利太太和莉齐住在山谷里一个僻静的茅屋里，只有走进这条山谷，你才能看到它的存在。
Tom is a schoolmaster in Rochdale, and he and Will help to support their mother. 汤姆现在是罗奇代尔的一名男教师，他和威尔一起赡养他们的母亲。
I only know that, if the cottage be hidden in a green hollow of the hills, every sound of sorrow in the whole upland is heard there—every call of suffering or of sickness for help is listened to by a sad, gentle-looking woman, who rarely smiles (and when she does her smile is more sad than other people’s tears), but who comes out of her seclusion whenever there is a shadow in any household. 我知道的只有这些，如果茅屋隐藏在绿色的山谷中，那么在整个高地的每一个悲伤的声音都会被听到——每一声痛苦或疾病的呼唤都会被一个伤心的、相貌温和的女人听到，她很少有笑容出现（当她笑的时候，她的笑容比别人的眼泪还要悲伤），每当有家庭出现阴影的时候，她就会从她的幽居中走出来。
Many hearts bless Lizzie Leigh, but she—she prays always and ever for forgiveness—such forgiveness as may enable her to see her child once more. 有许多颗心在祝福莉齐·利，可是她——她总是不停地在祈求宽恕——只有宽恕才可能让她再次见到她的孩子。
Mrs. Leigh is quiet and happy. 利太太的生活安静而幸福。
Lizzie is, to her eyes, something precious—as the lost piece of silver—found once more. 在她的眼里，莉齐就是她的宝贝——好比丢失的银元——失而复得。
Susan is the bright one who brings sunshine to all. 苏珊是那个给大家带来阳光的欢快的人。
Children grow around her and call her blessed. 孩子们在她身边长大并且都称她有福。
One is called Nanny; her Lizzie often takes to the sunny graveyard in the uplands, and while the little creature gathers the daisies, and makes chains, Lizzie sits by a little grave and weeps bitterly. 其中一个孩子叫南妮，莉齐经常带着她来到阳光明媚的高地墓地，当小家伙忙着采摘雏菊编织项圈时，莉齐则坐在一个小小的坟前，伤心地流泪。
