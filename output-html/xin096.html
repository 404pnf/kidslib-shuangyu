The Brave Little Tailor 勇敢的小裁缝
by Andrew Lang 作者：安德鲁·朗格
One summer’s day a little tailor sat on his table by the window in the best of spirits, and sewed for dear life. 夏日的某一天，有个小裁缝精神抖擞地坐在靠窗的桌子旁，勤劳地工作着。
As he was sitting thus a peasant woman came down the street, calling out: “Good jam to sell, good jam to sell.” 正当他坐着，突然传来了“好果酱，卖果酱啦！”的叫卖声。一个乡下女人在街上边走边吆喝着。
This sounded sweetly in the tailor’s ears; he put his frail little head out of the window, and shouted: “up here, my good woman, and you’ll find a willing customer.” 这叫卖声在小裁缝的耳里简直是福音。于是，他探出他那脆弱的小脑袋，叫道：“上来吧，我的好大娘，我想买你的果酱。”
The woman climbed up the three flights of stairs with her heavy basket to the tailor’s room, and he made her spread out all the pots in a row before him. 卖果酱的女人提着沉甸甸的篮子，爬了三层楼来到了小裁缝的房间。小裁缝让她把果酱在他面前一溜排开。
He examined them all, lifted them up and smelled them, and said at last: “This jam seems good, weigh me four ounces of it, my good woman; and even if it’s a quarter of a pound I won’t stick at it.” 他仔细地检查了一遍，掂量掂量，又闻了闻，最后才说：“这果酱不错，给我称四盎司吧，如果是四分之一磅，也是可以的。”
The woman, who had hoped to find a good market, gave him what he wanted, but went away grumbling wrathfully. 这个乡下女人原以为能做一笔好生意，只好给他称了他要的果酱，然后愤愤不平、唠唠叨叨地离开了。
“Now heaven shall bless this jam for my use,” cried the little tailor, “and it shall sustain and strengthen me.” “现在愿上帝保佑，这些果酱可以够我用，够我生活，让我变得强壮起来。”小裁缝叫嚷着说。
He fetched some bread out of a cupboard, cut a round off the loaf, and spread the jam on it. 他从橱柜里取出一些面包，切了一圈，把果酱涂在上面。
“That won’t taste amiss,” he said; “but I’ll finish that waistcoat first before I take a bite.” “尝起来应该不错，”他说，“但是我要先完成那件背心才能吃。”
He placed the bread beside him, went on sewing, and out of the lightness of his heart kept on making his stitches bigger and bigger. 于是他把面包放一边，继续缝他的衣服。由于没那么用心了，他的针脚越来越大。
In the meantime the smell of the sweet jam rose to the ceiling, where heaps of flies were sitting, and attracted them to such an extent that they swarmed on to it in masses. 与此同时，扎堆在天花板上的苍蝇嗅到了果酱的甜蜜味道，于是，它们一窝蜂地涌到了面包上。
“Ha! who invited you?” said the tailor, and chased the unwelcome guests away. “嘿！谁请你们来的？”小裁缝嚷道，一边驱赶这些不速之客。
But the flies, who didn’t understand English, refused to let themselves be warned off, and returned again in even greater numbers. 但是这些苍蝇，哪里听得懂英语，才不会一赶就离开。飞走了后又飞了回来，数量反而更多。
At last the little tailor, losing all patience, reached out of his chimney corner for a duster, and exclaiming: “Wait, and I’ll give it to you,” he beat them mercilessly with it. 最后，小裁缝所有耐性都耗尽了，冲到壁炉角，拿出一把掸子，叫道：“等着，我要给你们几掸子。”于是，他毫不手软地打死了它们。
When he left off he counted the slain, and no fewer than seven lay dead before him with outstretched legs. 离开的时候，他数了数被打死的苍蝇，正好七只，张着腿摆在他面前。
“What a desperate fellow I am!” said he, and was filled with admiration at his own courage. “我是一个多么有胆有识的家伙呀！”他说道，对自己的勇气深感佩服。
“The whole town must know about this”; and in great haste the little tailor cut out a girdle, hemmed it, and embroidered on it in big letters, “Seven at a blow.” “整个小镇必须知道这事儿。”他自言自语道。然后他急匆匆地剪了一条带子，缝好边，又在上面绣了几个大字：“一下打死七个。”
“What did I say, the town? no, the whole world shall hear of it,” he said; and his heart beat for joy as a lamb wags his tail. “我刚刚说什么了？小镇？不，我要让全世界都知道这事儿。”他说。他因欢喜而心跳加速，仿若一只羔羊不停地摆动着它的尾巴。
The tailor strapped the girdle round his waist and set out into the wide world, for he considered his workroom too small a field for his prowess. 小裁缝把带子系在腰上，准备投入到更宽广的世界中去。因为他觉得他现在的裁缝店太小了，他的才能得不到施展。
Before he set forth he looked round about him, to see if there was anything in the house he could take with him on his journey; but he found nothing except an old cheese, which he took possession of. 临走前，他四处望了望，看看屋子里有什么东西可以带着上路的。但是找来找去，他只找到了一块存放已久的奶酪，于是他把它带上了。
In front of the house he observed a bird that had been caught in some bushes, and this he put into his wallet beside the cheese. 走到房子前面，他又看见了之前在灌木丛中捉到的一只鸟，于是他把它放进奶酪旁边的袋子里。
Then he went on his way merrily, and being light and agile he never felt tired. 然后，他高高兴兴地上路了。因为一身轻松，身手敏捷，他从不感到累。
His way led up a hill, on the top of which sat a powerful giant, who was calmly surveying the landscape. 他爬上了一座小山。山顶上坐着一个强壮的巨人，此时他正镇定地俯瞰山中风景。
The little tailor went up to him, and greeting him cheerfully said: “Good-day, friend; there you sit at your ease viewing the whole wide world. 小裁缝走过去，欣喜地和他打招呼：“你好，朋友，你悠闲地坐在这里，是在观察这广阔的世界吧。
I’m just on my way there. 我也正要到世界上闯一闯。
What do you say to accompanying me?” 你愿意和我一起吗？”
The giant looked contemptuously at the tailor, and said: “What a poor wretched little creature you are!” 巨人轻蔑地看了看小裁缝，说：“你这个可怜的小家伙啊！”
“That’s a good joke,” answered the little tailor, and unbuttoning his coat he showed the giant the girdle. “那是个善意的玩笑。”小裁缝一边回答，一边解开他的外套，向巨人展示了那条布带，
“There now, you can read what sort of a fellow I am.” “现在，你可以看出我是什么样的人了吧。”
The giant read: “Seven at a blow”; and thinking they were human beings the tailor had slain, he conceived a certain respect for the little man. 巨人看了看：“一下打死七个”，以为小裁缝打死的是七个人，因此对他多了一些敬意。
But first he thought he’d test him, so taking up a stone in his hand, he squeezed it till some drops of water ran out. 不过，他还是想先考验考验他，于是拿起一块石头放在手里，使劲捏，直到捏出水来。
“Now you do the same,” said the giant, “if you really wish to be thought strong.” “现在你照着做吧，”巨人说道，“如果你想让人们觉得你很强壮的话。”
“Is that all?” said the little tailor; “that’s child’s play to me,” so he dived into his wallet, brought out the cheese, and pressed it till the whey ran out. “就那样？”小裁缝说，“对我来说不过是小儿科。”于是他从口袋里掏出奶酪，使劲压，直到乳浆直往外流。
“My squeeze was in sooth better than yours,” said he. “我捏得确实要比你好。”他说。
The giant didn’t know what to say, for he couldn’t have believed it of the little fellow. 巨人不知道说什么好，因为他无法相信这个小家伙。
To prove him again, the giant lifted a stone and threw it so high that the eye could hardly follow it. 为了再次考验他，巨人举起一块石头，扔得老高，用肉眼几乎无法看到。
“Now, my little pigmy, let me see you do that.” “轮到你了，小家伙，让我瞧瞧你怎么做。”
“Well thrown,” said the tailor; “but, after all, your stone fell to the ground; I’ll throw one that won’t come down at all.” “扔得好，”小裁缝说道，“但是，你的石头毕竟还是落地了，而我扔出去的根本不会掉下来。”
He dived into his wallet again, and grasping the bird in his hand, he threw it up into the air. 他再次把手伸进口袋，抓出一只鸟，往天上一抛。
The bird, enchanted to be free, soared up into the sky, and flew away never to return. 那只小鸟，获得了自由，冲向云霄，飞得远远的，再也没有回来。
“Well, what do you think of that little piece of business, friend?” asked the tailor. “好了，朋友，你看我之前说的小交易怎么样？”小裁缝问道。
“You can certainly throw,” said the giant; “but now let’s see if you can carry a proper weight.” “你扔得当然不错，”巨人说，“但是，现在让我们比比看，看你能不能扛起合适的重量。”
With these words he led the tailor to a huge oak tree which had been felled to the ground, and said: “If you are strong enough, help me to carry the tree out of the wood.” 话音一落，他就带着小裁缝来到一棵已倒在地上的大橡树前，说：“如果你够强壮，就帮我把这棵树抬出森林。”
“Most certainly,” said the little tailor: “just you take the trunk on your shoulder; I’ll bear the top and branches, which is certainly the heaviest part.” “当然可以，”小裁缝答道，“只要你把树干扛在肩上，我会负责树梢和树枝，当然，这是最重的部分。”
The giant laid the trunk on his shoulder, but the tailor sat at his ease among the branches; and the giant, who couldn’t see what was going on behind him, had to carry the whole tree, and the little tailor into the bargain. 巨人把树干放在肩膀上，而小裁缝轻松地坐在树枝上；巨人走在前面，看不到后面发生了什么，不得不扛起整棵树和小裁缝。
There he sat behind in the best of spirits, lustily whistling a tune, as if carrying the tree were mere sport. 小裁缝坐在后面，精神饱满，还一边哼着小调，好像扛一棵树不过是一种消遣。
The giant, after dragging the heavy weight for some time, could get on no further, and shouted out: “Hi! 巨人拖着沉重的树走了一会儿后，实在走不动了，叫道：“喂！
I must let the tree fall.” 我必须把树放下来。”
The tailor sprang nimbly down, seized the tree with both hands as if he had carried it the whole way and said to the giant: “Fancy a big lout like you not being able to carry a tree!” 小裁缝敏捷地跳下来，两手抓着树，好像他一路都扛着似的，然后对巨人说：“你这么大一个人居然扛不起一棵树！”
They continued to go on their way together, and as they passed by a cherry tree the giant grasped the top of it, where the ripest fruit hung, gave the branches into the tailor’s hand, and bade him eat. 他们继续往前走。路过一棵樱桃树的时候，巨人一把抓住树的顶端，那上面悬挂着熟透了的果子，然后把树枝放到小裁缝手里，命令他吃下去。
But the little tailor was far too weak to hold the tree down, and when the giant let go the tree swung back into the air, bearing the little tailor with it. 但是小裁缝太瘦弱了，根本没有力气往下拽着樱桃树。巨人一松手，樱桃树就带着小裁缝在空中摇摆。
When he had fallen to the ground again without hurting himself, the giant said: “What! do you mean to tell me you haven’t the strength to hold down a feeble twig?” 小裁缝掉到地上，并没有伤到自己。巨人说：“什么！你这意思是想告诉我你竟然没有力气抓住一根柔弱的树枝吗？”
“It wasn’t strength that was wanting,” replied the tailor; “do you think that would have been anything for a man who has killed seven at a blow? “力气不是重点，”小裁缝答道，“难道你认为对于一个一下打死七个的人来说，那也算事儿吗？
I jumped over the tree because the huntsmen are shooting among the branches near us. 我刚刚从那棵树上翻下来，是因为附近有猎人朝树枝上开枪。
Do you do the like if you dare.” 如果你敢，你也那样做做看。”
The giant made an attempt, but couldn’t get over the tree, and stuck fast in the branches, so that here too the little tailor had the better of him. 巨人试了一下，跳不过那棵树，还一下子卡在了树枝上，小裁缝因此更加佩服自己了。
“Well, you’re a fine fellow, after all,” said the giant; “come and spend the night with us in our cave.” “好吧，你是个好家伙，”巨人说道，“晚上到洞里和我们一起过夜吧。”
The little tailor willingly consented to do this, and following his friend they went on till they reached a cave where several other giants were sitting round a fire, each holding a roast sheep in his hand, of which he was eating. 小裁缝欣然答应，跟着他的朋友继续前行，来到一个洞里。那里坐着几个巨人，他们围着一堆火，每个人手里拿着一只烤羊在吃。
The little tailor looked about him, and thought: “Yes, there’s certainly more room to turn round in here than in my workshop.” 小裁缝环顾四周，心想：“嗯，这儿的空间的确比我那小裁缝店大多了。”
The giant showed him a bed and bade him lie down and have a good sleep. 巨人给小裁缝指了一张床，命令他躺下，好好睡一觉。
But the bed was too big for the little tailor, so he didn’t get into it, but crept away into the corner. 可是，这张床太大了，小裁缝根本不适应，于是，他慢慢地缩到了一个角落里。
At midnight, when the giant thought the little tailor was fast asleep, he rose up, and taking his big iron walking-stick, he broke the bed in two with a blow, and thought he had made an end of the little grasshopper. 午夜时分，巨人以为小裁缝熟睡了，于是爬起来，拿起他的大铁拐棍一挥，把床劈成了两半，心想这下了结了这只小蚱蜢。
At early dawn the giants went off to the wood, and quite forgot about the little tailor, till all of a sudden they met him trudging along in the most cheerful manner. 天刚亮，巨人们就出发去树林了，完全忘记了小裁缝，直到他们猛然看到他正欢快地走在路上。
The giants were terrified at the apparition, and, fearful lest he should slay them, they all took to their heels as fast as they could. 看到这个幽灵，巨人们惊恐万分，生怕小裁缝会杀死他们。于是，他们迅速提起脚后跟悄悄溜走了。
The little tailor continued to follow his nose, and after he had wandered about for a long time he came to the courtyard of a royal palace, and feeling tired he lay down on the grass and fell asleep. 小裁缝凭直觉继续往前走，游荡了好长一段时间后，来到了一座皇宫的庭院内。他感到累极了，于是躺在草地上睡着了。
While he lay there the people came, and looking him all over read on his girdle: “Seven at a blow.” 当他躺在那里的时候，有人来了，一边仔细地打量着他，一边读着他布条上的字：“一下打死七个。”
“Oh!” they said, “what can this great hero of a hundred fights want in our peaceful land? “天呐！”他们惊呼道，“这个身经百战的大英雄到我们这片和平之地上能干什么呢？
He must indeed be a mighty man of valor.” 他一定是一个伟大的勇士。”
They went and told the King about him, and said what a weighty and useful man he’d be in time of war, and that it would be well to secure him at any price. 他们跑去报告国王，说在战争的时候，他会是一个多么有分量且有用的人，还说不惜任何代价得到他也是值得的。
This counsel pleased the King, and he sent one of his courtiers down to the little tailor, to offer him, when he awoke, a commission in their army. 国王听了很开心，便派了一名大臣去小裁缝那里，等他醒后，就告诉他到部队服役的任命。
The messenger remained standing by the sleeper, and waited till he stretched his limbs and opened his eyes, when he tendered his proposal. 差使大臣一直站在熟睡的小裁缝旁边等着，直至他伸伸腿，张开眼睛，才向他提出这个建议。
“That’s the very thing I came here for,” he answered; “I am quite ready to enter the King’s service.” “我正是为这事儿来的，”小裁缝答道，“我早准备好为国王服务了。”
So he was received with all honor, and given a special house of his own to live in. 因此，他受到了最高规格的接待，还得到了一套专为他准备的房子。
But the other officers resented the success of the little tailor, and wished him a thousand miles away. 但是其他的官员对小裁缝的成功感到不满，都想拒他于千里之外。
“What’s to come of it all?” they asked each other; “if we quarrel with him, he’ll let out at us, and at every blow seven will fall. “我们该怎么办？”他们互相问道，“如果和他争吵，他会痛打我们，一次就倒下七个。
There’ll soon be an end of us.” 我们很快就全完了。”
So they resolved to go in a body to the King, and all to send in their papers. 于是他们决定集体去国王那里，每个人都递上自己的辞呈。
“We are not made,” they said, “to hold out against a man who kills seven at a blow.” “我们无法和一个一下能打死七个的人抗衡。”他们如是说。
The King was grieved at the thought of losing all his faithful servants for the sake of one man, and he wished heartily that he had never set eyes on him, or that he could get rid of him. 一想到为了一个人而失去所有的忠臣，国王就大为苦恼。他真心希望自己从来没有看见过小裁缝，这样就可以打发掉他了。
But he didn’t dare to send him away, for he feared he might kill him along with his people, and place himself on the throne. 但是他不敢把小裁缝打发走，因为他害怕小裁缝会杀死他和他的国民，然后取而代之，登上皇位。
He pondered long and deeply over the matter, and finally came to a conclusion. 经过一番深思熟虑之后，他终于想到了一个办法。
He sent to the tailor and told him that, seeing what a great and warlike hero he was, he was about to make him an offer. 他打发人去告诉小裁缝，说看他是一个伟大而好战的英雄，因而准备派给他一个任务。
In a certain wood of his kingdom there dwelled two giants who did much harm; by the way they robbed, murdered, burned, and plundered everything about them; “no one could approach them without endangering his life. 国内某个树林里住着两个巨人，为害颇广，他们烧杀抢夺，无恶不作。“接近他们的人没有不陷入生命危险的。
But if he could overcome and kill these two giants he should have his only daughter for a wife, and half his kingdom into the bargain; he might have a hundred horsemen, too, to back him up.” 但是如果他能战胜并杀死这两个巨人，他可以让小裁缝娶他唯一的女儿为妻，并且送他半个王国。他还可以让一百个骑士去支持他。”
“That’s the very thing for a man like me,” thought the little tailor; “one doesn’t get the offer of a beautiful princess and half a kingdom every day.” “正合我意，”小裁缝心想，“能得到一个漂亮的公主和半个王国的机会不是每天都有的。”
“Done with you,” he answered; “I’ll soon put an end to the giants. “一言为定，”小裁缝答道，“我很快就会搞定这两个巨人的。
But I haven’t the smallest need of your hundred horsemen; a fellow who can slay seven men at a blow need not be afraid of two.” 但是我根本就不需要你的一百个骑士，一个一下能打死七个的人不必害怕两个。”
The little tailor set out, and the hundred horsemen followed him. 小裁缝出发了，一百个骑士跟在他的后面。
When he came to the outskirts of the wood he said to his followers: “You wait here, I’ll manage the giants by myself”; and he went on into the wood, casting his sharp little eyes right and left about him. 快要进入树林的时候，小裁缝对他的同伴说：“你们就在这儿等我吧，我会自己搞定这两个巨人的。”接着他走进树林，犀利的小眼睛四处查看。
After a while he spied the two giants lying asleep under a tree, and snoring till the very boughs bent with the breeze. 不多久，他暗中发现两个巨人就躺在一棵树下，鼾声如雷，呼出的气能令树枝弯曲。
The little tailor lost no time in filling his wallet with stones, and then climbed up the tree under which they lay. 小裁缝赶紧往口袋里装满石头，然后爬到巨人上方的树上。
When he got to about the middle of it he slipped along a branch till he sat just above the sleepers, when he threw down one stone after the other on the nearest giant. 大约爬到树的中间时，他沿着一根树枝，滑到巨人的正上方坐着，然后接二连三地向离他最近的巨人扔石头。
The giant felt nothing for a long time, but at last he woke up, and pinching his companion said: “What did you strike me for?” 巨人很久都没有反应，不过最后还是醒了。他掐了一下他的同伴，说：“你为什么打我？”
“I didn’t strike you,” said the other, “you must be dreaming.” “我没有打你啊，”另一个巨人说道，“你一定是在做梦吧。”
They both lay down to sleep again, and the tailor threw down a stone on the second giant, who sprang up and cried: “What’s that for? 于是两人躺下继续睡。小裁缝这次往另一个巨人身上扔了一块石头，这个巨人跳起来叫道：“干什么呢？
Why did you throw something at me?” 你为什么要朝我扔东西？”
“I didn’t throw anything,” growled the first one. “我什么都没扔啊。”第一个巨人咆哮道。
They wrangled on for a time, till, as both were tired, they made up the matter and fell asleep again. 他们争吵了好一会儿，直到他俩都累了，胡乱编了个事，就又睡了。
The little tailor began his game once more, and flung the largest stone he could find in his wallet with all his force, and hit the first giant on the chest. 小裁缝又继续他的诡计,从口袋里掏出最重的一块石头，使出吃奶的劲儿，掷向第一个巨人，砸中了他的胸部。
“This is too much of a good thing!” he yelled, and springing up like a madman, he knocked his companion against the tree till he trembled. “实在是忍无可忍了！”巨人大叫，像疯子一样跳起来，把他的同伴按在树上一顿暴打，直到他发抖。
He gave, however, as good as he got, and they became so enraged that they tore up trees and beat each other with them, till they both fell dead at once on the ground. 他的同伴也以牙还牙。暴怒的他们把树都撕裂了，并以此为武器，互相打个不停，直到两人都倒在地上，死了。
Then the little tailor jumped down. 小裁缝这才跳下来。
“It’s a mercy,” he said, “that they didn’t root up the tree on which I was perched, or I should have had to jump like a squirrel on to another, which, nimble though I am, would have been no easy job.” “真是太仁慈了，”小裁缝说道，“他们居然没有将我呆的这棵树连根拔起，不然我得像只松鼠一样跳来跳去，虽然我身手敏捷，可那也不是件容易的事儿呀。”
He drew his sword and gave each of the giants a very fine thrust or two on the breast, and then went to the horsemen and said: “The deed is done, I’ve put an end to the two of them; but I assure you it has been no easy matter, for they even tore up trees in their struggle to defend themselves; but all that’s of no use against one who slays seven men at a blow.” 他抽出剑，对着巨人的胸部各刺了一两下，正中要害，然后走到骑士那里，说：“事成了，我干掉了他们两个。我保证，这绝不是个轻松的活儿。为了自保，他们把树都撕毁了。但是遇到一个一下能打死七个的对手，那是没有用的。”
“Weren’t you wounded?” asked the horsemen. “你没有受伤吗？”骑士们问道。
“No fear,” answered the tailor; “they haven’t touched a hair of my head.” “不要害怕，”小裁缝回答说,“他们伤不到我一根汗毛。”
But the horsemen wouldn’t believe him till they rode into the wood and found the giants weltering in their blood, and the trees lying around, torn up by the roots. 但是骑士们不相信，直到他们骑马冲进树林，发现两个巨人倒在血泊中，周围的树被连根拔起，倒了一地。
The little tailor now demanded the promised reward from the King, but he repented his promise, and pondered once more how he could rid himself of the hero. 小裁缝要求国王兑现事先承诺的奖赏。但是国王后悔了，于是再次思考怎么样让自己摆脱掉这个英雄。
“Before you obtain the hand of my daughter and half my kingdom,” he said to him, “you must do another deed of valor. 国王说：“在你得到我的女儿和半个王国之前，你必须勇敢地完成另一件事。
A unicorn is running about loose in the wood, and doing much mischief; you must first catch it.” 一只独角兽在树林里乱跑，作恶无数，你必须第一个抓住它。”
“I’m even less afraid of one unicorn than of two giants; seven at a blow, that’s my motto.” “我连两个巨人都不怕，还会怕一只独角兽吗？一下打死七个，这是我的座右铭。”
He took a piece of cord and an axe with him, went out to the wood, and again told the men who had been sent with him to remain outside. 他带上一根绳子和一把斧子，向森林走去。同样，他让随行的骑士在森林外等着。
He hadn’t to search long, for the unicorn soon passed by, and, on perceiving the tailor, dashed straight at him as though it were going to spike him on the spot. 他没有找很久，因为独角兽很快就从他旁边经过。它一看到小裁缝，就直直的向他冲过来，仿佛要他命丧当场。
“Gently, gently,” said he, “not so fast, my friend”; and standing still he waited till the beast was quite near, when he sprang lightly behind a tree; the unicorn ran with all its force against the tree, and rammed its horn so firmly into the trunk that it had no strength left to pull it out again, and was thus successfully captured. “温柔点，温柔点，”小裁缝说道，“不必这么快，我的朋友。”于是，他站着不动，直到独角兽离他很近了，他才轻轻一跳，躲到一棵树的后面。独角兽拼尽全力对着树跑去，兽角死死地撞进了树干里，再也没有力气拔出来，因此轻而易举地就被抓住了。
“Now I’ve caught my bird,” said the tailor, and he came out from behind the tree, placed the cord round its neck first, then struck the horn out of the tree with his axe, and when everything was in order led the beast before the King. “现在我抓到我的猎物了。”小裁缝说。他从树后面走出来，首先把绳子套在独角兽的脖子上，然后用斧头把树劈开，拔出兽角。一切准备妥当后，他把独角兽带到了国王跟前。
Still the King didn’t want to give him the promised reward and made a third demand. 国王还是不想给他事先承诺的奖赏，于是又提出了第三个要求。
The tailor was to catch a wild boar for him that did a great deal of harm in the wood; and he might have the huntsmen to help him. 小裁缝要去森林里抓一只作恶多端的野猪，他可以让猎人帮助他。
“Willingly,” said the tailor; “that’s mere child’s play.” “我愿意，”小裁缝说道，“那不过是小菜一碟。”
But he didn’t take the huntsmen into the wood with him, and they were well enough pleased to remain behind, for the wild boar had often received them in a manner which did not make them desire its further acquaintance. 但是他没有带着猎人一起进入树林，而猎人们也很高兴不需要跟着进去，因为他们实在没有进一步了解这头野猪的欲望。
As soon as the boar perceived the tailor it ran at him with foaming mouth and gleaming teeth, and tried to knock him down; but our alert little friend ran into a chapel that stood near, and got out of the window again with a jump. 这只野猪一看到小裁缝，就冲着他跑过来，唾沫横飞，牙齿发亮，企图撞倒小裁缝。但是警觉的小裁缝跑进了附近的一座小教堂里，又从教堂的窗户上一跃而出。
The boar pursued him into the church, but the tailor skipped round to the door, and closed it securely. 野猪追进了教堂，但是小裁缝一跃绕到门口，把门牢牢地关上了。
So the raging beast was caught, for it was far too heavy and unwieldy to spring out of the window. 愤怒的野猪就这样被捉住了，因为它过于笨重臃肿，根本不能从窗口跳出来。
The little tailor summoned the huntsmen together, that they might see the prisoner with their own eyes. 小裁缝把猎人们都召集起来，让他们亲眼看看这只被囚禁的野猪。
Then the hero betook himself to the King, who was obliged now, whether he liked it or not, to keep his promise, and hand him over his daughter and half his kingdom. 然后，小裁缝来到国王那里。不管愿不愿意，他都必须履行他的诺言，把他的女儿嫁给小裁缝，并交予半个王国。
Had he known that no hero-warrior, but only a little tailor stood before him, it would have gone even more to his heart. 要是他知道站在他眼前的根本不是勇士，而只是一个小裁缝，他会更心痛的。
So the wedding was celebrated with much splendor and little joy, and the tailor became a king. 因此，这场婚礼虽然非常豪华，却鲜有欢乐。小裁缝当上了国王。
After a time the Queen heard her husband saying one night in his sleep: “My lad, make that waistcoat and patch these trousers, or I’ll box your ears.” 过了一段时间后，一天夜里，皇后听到她睡梦中的丈夫说：“小伙子，去把那件背心做好，把裤子补好，不然我就打你耳光。”
Thus she learned in what rank the young gentleman had been born, and next day she poured forth her woes to her father, and begged him to help her to get rid of a husband who was nothing more nor less than a tailor. 因此，她知道了这位年轻绅士的出身，第二天便向父亲大吐苦水，并恳求父亲帮她摆脱自己那个其实只不过是一个裁缝的丈夫。
The King comforted her, and said: “Leave your bedroom door open to-night, my servants shall stand outside, and when your husband is fast asleep they shall enter, bind him fast, and carry him on to a ship, which shall sail away out into the wide ocean.” 国王安慰他女儿，说：“今晚把你卧室的门打开，我的仆人会站在门外，趁你丈夫熟睡的时候，他们会进去把他飞快地绑起来，带到一艘船上，而这艘船会驶进辽阔的海洋。”
The Queen was well satisfied with the idea, but the armor-bearer, who had overheard everything, being much attached to his young master, went straight to him and revealed the whole plot. 皇后对这个办法很满意。可是，一个穿着盔甲的侍从无意中听到了这些，出于对他年轻主人的无比忠诚，他直接走到主人那里，揭露了整个阴谋。
“I’ll soon put a stop to the business,” said the tailor. “我会很给让这件事画上句号。”小裁缝说道。
That night he and his wife went to bed at the usual time; and when she thought he had fallen asleep she got up, opened the door, and then lay down again. 那天晚上，小裁缝和他的妻子照常上床睡觉。皇后想着国王睡着了，就起身把门打开，然后又躺下。
The little tailor, who had only pretended to be asleep, began to call out in a clear voice: “My lad, make that waistcoat and patch those trousers, or I’ll box your ears. 小裁缝只是假装睡着了，他口齿清晰地说道：“小伙子，把那件背心做好，把裤子补好，不然我就打你耳光。
I have killed seven at a blow, slain two giants, led a unicorn captive, and caught a wild boar, then why should I be afraid of those men standing outside my door?” 我一下打死了七个，杀了两个巨人，捕了一只独角兽，抓了一只野猪，我为什么要害怕那些站在我门外的人呢？”
The men, when they heard the tailor saying these words, were so terrified that they fled as if pursued by a wild army, and didn’t dare go near him again. 站在门外的人听到小裁缝这么一说，都害怕得仓皇而逃，好像被野蛮的军队追赶着似的，并且再也不敢靠近他了。
So the little tailor was and remained a king all the days of his life. 于是，小裁缝保住了王位，一直到死。
