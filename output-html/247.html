母与女 MOTHER AND DAUGHTER
作者：D. H. 劳伦斯 by D. H. Lawrence
弗吉尼娅·博多恩有一份好工作：她是某个政府部门的部门负责人，并且，如同巴尔扎克那样确切地说，她每年能挣七百五十英镑。 Virginia Bodoin had a good job: she was head of a department in a certain government office, held a responsible position, and earned, to imitate Balzac and be precise about it, seven hundred and fifty pounds a year.
那已经够可观了。 That is already something.
她的母亲雷切尔·博多恩，年收入则在六百英镑左右，自从那个从来都不是很重要的丈夫消失之后，她母亲就一直依靠这笔收入在欧洲各大城市轮流居住。 Rachel Bodoin, her mother, had an income of about six hundred a year, on which she had lived in the capitals of Europe since the effacement of a never very important husband.
如今，经过几乎是完全分离和“自由”的几年之后，母女二人再次考虑安顿下来。 Now, after some years of virtual separation and “freedom”, mother and daughter once more thought of settling down.
这些年之后，她们两人已经变得更像一对夫妇而不像母女了。 They had become, in course of time, more like a married couple than mother and daughter.
她们的确非常了解彼此，而且各自对对方都有些“神经质”。 They knew one another very well indeed, and each was a little “nervous” of the other.
她们时聚时散，已有好几次了。 They had lived together and parted several times.
弗吉尼娅如今三十岁了，可是看不出她有结婚的打算。 Virginia was now thirty, and she didn’t look like marrying.
四年来，她和亨利·卢伯克，一个喜爱音乐却被娇纵坏了的年轻人，好得如同结了婚一般。 For four years she had been as good as married to Henry Lubbock, a rather spoilt young man who was musical.
后来，亨利让她失望了：有两个原因。 Then Henry let her down: for two reasons.
一是他不能容忍她的母亲。 He couldn’t stand her mother.
而她母亲也不能容忍他。 Her mother couldn’t stand him.
另外，凡是博多恩太太不能容忍的人，她就会想方设法欺压人家，让人变得悲惨不已。 And anybody whom Mrs Bodoin could not stand she managed to sit on, disastrously.
亨利遭到岳母的百般压制，感到十分苦恼。而弗吉尼娅呢，终究还是无可奈何地忠于自己的家族，站在了她母亲这一边。 So Henry had writhed horribly, feeling his mother-in-law sitting on him tight, and Virginia, after all, in a helpless sort of family loyalty, sitting alongside her mother.
弗吉尼娅并不是真的想欺压亨利。 Virginia didn’t really want to sit on Henry.
可是当她母亲教唆她时，她便没了主意。 But when her mother egged her on, she couldn’t help it.
因为，归根结底她的母亲支配着她，那是一种神秘的女性力量，与父母的权威没有丝毫关系。 For ultimately, her mother had power over her; a strange female power, nothing to do with parental authority.
弗吉尼娅早就把父母的权威抛到脑后了。 Virginia had long thrown parental authority to the winds.
然而，她的母亲拥有另一种更为微妙的支配形式，柔弱却令人毛骨悚然，因此当雷切尔说：让我们制服他吧！ But her mother had another, much subtler form of domination, female and thrilling, so that when Rachel said: Let’s squash him!
弗吉尼娅便不得不恶狠狠地、欢欣鼓舞地赶忙加入到这场游戏当中。 Virginia had to rush wickedly and gleefully to the sport.
而每逢亨利遭到排挤时，他都心知肚明。 And Henry knew quite well when he was being squashed.
所以，那就是他背弃文尼的原因之一。他称她文尼，博多恩太太对此极为反感，总是纠正他说：“我女儿叫弗吉尼娅——” So that was one of his reasons for going back on Vinny.—He called her Vinny, to the superlative disgust of Mrs Bodoin, who always corrected him: My daughter Virginia—
第二个原因，还是借用巴尔扎克的话说，弗吉尼娅那时是一文不名。 The second reason was, again to be Balzacian, that Virginia hadn’t a sou of her own.
亨利只有可怜的二百五十英镑。 Henry had a sorry two hundred and fifty.
弗吉尼娅呢，在二十四岁的时候，就已经能挣四百五十英镑了。 Virginia, at the age of twenty-four, was already earning four hundred and fifty.
不过，那可是她挣的。 But she was earning them.
而亨利靠着他那珍爱的音乐，每年才只能挣大约十二英镑。 Whereas Henry managed to earn about twelve pounds per annum, by his precious music.
他意识到自己很难再挣得更多。 He had realized that he would find it hard to earn more.
所以，除非娶一位能养他的妻子，否则，结婚便毫无可能。 So that marrying, except with a wife who could keep him, was rather out of the question.
文尼会继承她母亲的财产。 Vinny would inherit her mother’s money.
然而，博多恩太太拥有斯芬克斯那样的强健体魄和肌肉组织。 But then Mrs Bodoin had the health and muscular equipment of the Sphinx.
她会一直活下去，寻觅她可以吞噬的男人，然后吞噬他。 She would live forever, seeking whom she might devour, and devouring him.
亨利与文尼像夫妻一样，共同生活了两年：而且文尼感觉到他们确实结婚了，只差一个仪式罢了。 Henry lived with Vinny for two years, in the married sense of the words: and Vinny felt they were married, minus a mere ceremony.
不过，文尼总是有她母亲作后盾，尽管母亲经常远在巴黎或比亚里茨，但仍然有信件往来。 But Vinny had her mother always in the background; often as far back as Paris or Biarritz, but still, within letter reach.
母亲甚至在信中也会展开自己的裙子，镇定地打压亨利，而她从未意识到就在那时她淘气的脸上会露出会心的微笑。 And she never realized the funny little grin that came on her own elvish face when her mother, even in a letter, spread her skirts and calmly sat on Henry.
她从没有意识到，在精神上，她也已经迅速又顽皮地挤兑了他：她不能控制自己的行为，就如同潮汐涨落不能摆脱月亮的影响一样。 She never realized that in spirit she promptly and mischievously sat on him too: she could no more have helped it than the tide can help turning to the moon.
她连做梦都没想过他感受到了这一切，而且他那男性的虚荣心受到了极大的羞辱。 And she did not dream that he felt it, and was utterly mortified in his masculine vanity.
女人们通常是互相迷恋，着了迷之后，进而温柔地搂住她们认为自己全心全意爱着的男人的脖子。 Women, very often, hypnotize one another, and then, hypnotized, they proceed gently to wring the neck of the man they think they are loving with all their hearts.
若是那个男人不喜欢自己的脖子被搂住，她们便称之为彻底反常。 Then they call it utter perversity on his part, that he doesn’t like having his neck wrung.
她们认为他这是在拒绝一份真心真意的爱情。 They think he is repudiating a heart-felt love.
因为她们心醉魂迷了。 For they are hypnotized.
女人便这么不知不觉地相互迷恋着。 Women hypnotize one another, without knowing it.
最终，亨利退出了。 In the end, Henry backed out.
他发现自己被这两个女人贬得一无是处，一个是有着斯芬克斯那样肌肉的老女巫，另一个是着了魔的年轻女巫，她挥霍无度、古灵精怪而软弱无用，她极力地宠着他，却吞噬了他的精髓。 He saw himself being simply reduced to nothingness by two women, an old witch with muscles like the Sphinx, and a young, spell-bound witch, lavish, elvish and weak, who utterly spoilt him but who ate his marrow.
雷切尔会从巴黎写信来说：我亲爱的弗吉尼娅，由于我在投资方面发了一笔横财，所以我要和你一起分享。 Rachel would write from Paris: My dear Virginia, as I had a windfall in the way of an investment, I am sharing it with you.
随信附上一张二十英镑的支票。 You will find enclosed my cheque for twenty pounds.
你肯定会需要这笔钱给亨利买一套西服的，因为眼看春天就要到了，阳光很可能会把他的穷酸相都暴露出来的。 No doubt you will be needing it to buy Henry a suit of clothes, since the spring is apparently come, and the sunlight may be tempted to show him up for what he is worth.
我可不想我的女儿和一个像是街头乐手的人到处走动，不过得你自己付裁缝的手工钱，要不然以后还得你再付。——亨利得了一套西装，可那几乎就是一件“内萨斯之衣”，用稀薄的毒液吞噬着他。 I don’t want my daughter going around with what is presumably a street-corner musician, but please pay the tailor’s bill yourself, or you may have to do it over again later.—Henry got a suit of clothes, but it was as good as a shirt of Nessus, eating him away with subtle poison.
因此他退出了。 So he backed out.
他不是在双方剑拔弩张的时候猛然离开、突然溜走，或是夺路而逃。 He didn’t jump out, or bolt, or carve his way out at the sword’s point.
他应该算是逐渐淡出的，若即若离有一年多才彻底离开的。 He sort of faded out, distributing his departure over a year or more.
他喜欢文尼，简直离不开她，他也对她感到歉疚。 He was fond of Vinny, and he could hardly do without her, and he was sorry for her.
但最终他看出她不能离开她母亲。 But at length he couldn’t see her apart from her mother.
她是个年轻、软弱、爱挥霍浪费的女巫，是她那个手段强硬、巫婆般母亲的帮凶。 She was a young, weak, spendthrift witch, accomplice of her tough-clawed witch of a mother.
亨利与其他人确定了关系，在别处找到了立足之地，从而渐渐地解救了自己。 Henry made other alliances, got a good hold on elsewhere, and gradually extricated himself.
他拯救了自己的生命，可是他感到,自己浪费了许多青春和精力。 He saved his life, but he had lost, he felt, a good deal of his youth and marrow.
现在他开始发胖，变得有点儿臃肿，有点儿不起眼了。 He tended now to go fat, a little puffy, somewhat insignificant.
可他曾经帅气英俊、引人注目。 And he had been handsome and striking-looking.
当他离开这两个女巫师时，她们大声嚎叫了起来。 The two witches howled when he was lost to them.
可怜的弗吉尼娅真的半疯半癫了，她不知道如何是好。 Poor Virginia was really half crazy, she didn’t know what to do with herself.
她对母亲怀着一种强烈的抵触情绪。 She had a violent recoil from her mother.
而博多恩太太对女儿充满了强烈的鄙视：她竟然让这样一条上了钩的鱼从她手里溜掉了！她竟然让这样的一个人把她给抛弃了！——“我真搞不懂我的女儿是怎么被亨利·卢伯克这样的寄生虫引诱然后抛弃的！”她写道， Mrs Bodoin was filled with furious contempt for her daughter: that she should let such a hooked fish slip out of her hands! that she should allow such a person to turn her down!—“I don’t quite see my daughter seduced and thrown over by a sponging individual such as Henry Lubbock,” she wrote.
“可是既然事情已经发生了，我想这就是某个人的过错——” “But if it has happened, I suppose it is somebody’s fault—”
母女间的相互抵触持续了将近五年。 There was a mutual recoil, which lasted nearly five years.
可是那种魔力并未消散。 But the spell was not broken.
博多恩太太的心思从来没离开过女儿，而弗吉尼娅也总是意识到她的母亲就在宇宙中的某个地方。 Mrs Bodoin’s mind never left her daughter, and Virginia was ceaselessly aware of her mother, somewhere in the universe.
她们互通书信，时不时地也见见面，可是她们在逃避中保持着距离。 They wrote, and met at intervals, but they kept apart in recoil.
然而，这种魔力仍旧存在于她们之间，并渐渐地产生了作用。 The spell, however, was between them, and gradually it worked.
她们感到更亲近些了。 They felt more friendly.
博多恩太太来到了伦敦。 Mrs Bodoin came to London.
她和女儿住在同一家清静的旅馆里：过去三年来，弗吉尼娅在旅馆里一直租着两个房间。 She stayed in the same quiet hotel with her daughter: Virginia had had two rooms in a hotel for the past three years.
最终，她们想要一起租一套寓所。 And, at last, they thought of taking an apartment together.
弗吉尼娅如今三十多了。 Virginia was now over thirty.
她仍旧瘦弱、古怪而淘气，她的一只棕色的眼睛中略微投射出逗人喜爱的目光，脸上依旧露出古怪而扭曲的微笑，舒缓而低沉的声音如同细俏指尖般抚摸着男人。 She was still thin and odd and elvish, with a very slight and piquant cast in one of her brown eyes, and she still had her odd, twisted smile, and her slow, rather deep-toned voice, that caressed a man like the stroking of subtle finger-tips.
她依旧是一头自然卷发，略微有些凌乱。 Her hair was still a natural tangle of curls, a bit dishevelled.
她仍旧衣着自然典雅，只是显得有些别扭，看上去有点邋遢。 She still dressed with a natural elegance which tended to go wrong and a tiny bit sluttish.
她那昂贵全新的长筒袜上仍旧会有个破洞，而她一到客厅喝茶，依旧会脱掉鞋子，穿着长筒袜坐在那里。 She still might have a hole in her expensive and perfectly new stockings, and still she might have to take off her shoes in the drawing-room, if she came to tea, and sit there in her stockinged-feet.
不错，她有双漂亮的脚：她整个人都优雅标致。 True, she had elegant feet: she was altogether elegantly shaped.
可是这不能说明什么。 But it wasn’t that.
那既不是卖弄风骚也不是虚荣做作。 It was neither coquetry nor vanity.
原因很简单，她曾找了一位手艺精湛的鞋匠，花了五个几尼定做了一双非常简朴、自然、合脚的鞋，可是当她穿着这双鞋走上半英里，鞋就夹得她异常难受，她就只能脱下这鞋，甚至是坐在路边镶边石上也要脱掉它。 It was simply that, after having gone to a good shoemaker and paid five guineas for a pair of perfectly simple and natural shoes, made to her feet, the said shoes would hurt her excruciatingly, when she had walked half a mile in them, and she would simply have to take them off, even if she sat on the kerb to do it.
这就是命中注定的事。 It was a fatality.
她的双脚有点顽皮、有些懒惰，绝不愿规规矩矩地呆在漂亮顺脚的鞋中。 There was a touch of the gamin in her very feet, a certain sluttishness that wouldn’t let them stay properly in nice proper shoes.
事实上，她总是穿她母亲的旧鞋。——当然我自始至终都是穿母亲的旧鞋。 She practically always wore her mother’s old shoes.—Of course I go through life in mother’s old shoes.
如果她死了，离开了我，因而不再有旧鞋供我穿的话，我想我只能坐着轮椅行走了，她咧着嘴，怪模怪样地笑着说。 If she died and left me without a supply, I suppose I should have to go in a bathchair, she would say, with her odd twisted little grin.
她是如此优雅漂亮，却是个荡妇。 She was so elegant, and yet a slut.
这就是她的魅力所在，真的。 It was her charm, really.
这正好与她母亲相反。她们可以换着穿对方的鞋子和衣服，这似乎有点不可思议，因为博多恩太太看上去比女儿高大多了。 Just the opposite of her mother.They could wear each other’s shoes and each other’s clothes, which seemed remarkable, for Mrs Bodoin seemed so much the bigger of the two.
不过弗吉尼娅的肩很宽，她虽然偏瘦，但身躯高大，即使当她看上去弱不禁风、衣衫褴褛的时候也是如此。 But Virginia’s shoulders were broad, if she was thin, she had a strong frame, even when she looked a frail rag.
博多恩太太是那种六十岁左右的女人之一，身上充满了一种令人生畏的内在能量和某种强大的活力。 Mrs Bodoin was one of those women of sixty or so, with a terrible inward energy and a violent sort of vitality.
不过她成功地掩饰了这一点。 But she managed to hide it.
她坐着的时候双手叉握，神态极为镇定。 She sat with perfect repose, and folded hands.
人们会想：这真是一个沉着的妇人啊！ One thought: What a calm woman!
正如同在夜光下，人们看着一座沉默火山那白雪皑皑的顶峰，会想：多么平静啊！ Just as one may look at the snowy summit of a quiescent volcano, in the evening light, and think: What peace!
有一种奇特的强大能量控制着博多恩太太，让人感到好奇的是，许多超过五十岁的女人都受这种能量的控制，并且其表现形式通常都令人反感。 It was a strange muscular energy which possessed Mrs Bodoin, as it possesses, curiously enough, many women over fifty, and is usually distasteful in its manifestations.
也许这可以用来解释为什么年轻人无精打采。 Perhaps it accounts for the lassitude of the young.
不过博多恩太太意识到了她那些精力充沛的同龄人趣味低级，所以她培养出沉着的性情。 But Mrs Bodoin recognized the bad taste in her energetic coevals, so she cultivated repose.
沉——着，她说出这个两个音节的词时，能把第二个音节读得直冲云霄，这表露出她有太多太多被压抑的能量了。 Her very way of pronouncing the word, in two syllables: re-pose, making the second syllable run on into the twilight, showed how much suppressed energy she had.
面对着铁灰色头发和黑眉毛的问题，她极其聪明，决不去尝试靠染色让自己回复青春。 Faced with the problem of iron-grey hair and black eyebrows, she was too clever to try dyeing herself back into youth.
她仔细端详着自己那张脸，整个身材，然后作出乐观的判断。 She studied her face, her whole figure, and decided that it was positive.
这是无可挑剔的。 There was no denying it.
没有纤弱的体态，没有凹陷的双颊，没有弱不禁风凋花残枝之相。 There was no wispiness, no hollowness, no limp frail blossom-on-a-bending-stalk about her.
她的身材，丰腴强壮，曲线优雅，但不显胖。 Her figure, though not stout, was full, strong, and cambré.
她脸上有着一只贵族式的拱形鼻子，一双高贵又不可一世的灰色眼眸，脸颊颇长但很丰满。 Her face had an aristocratic arched nose, aristocratic, who-the-devil-are-you grey eyes, and cheeks rather long but also rather full.
这模样虽说不上令人神魂颠倒，也绝无年轻人的轻佻。 Nothing appealing or youthfully skittish here.
像任何一个独立的女人一样，她善用智慧，非常坚定地决心不让自己显得年少轻佻或是妩媚动人。 Like an independent woman, she used her wits, and decided most emphatically not to be either youthful or skittish or appealing.
她保持着自己的尊严，因为她喜爱这样。 She would keep her dignity, for she was fond of it.
她很积极。 She was positive.
她喜欢积极的状态。 She liked to be positive.
她已习惯了自己的积极。 She was used to her positivity.
所以她会这样积极下去。 So she would just be positive.
她偏向于十八世纪那个实证主义时代，求教于伏尔泰、尼农·德·朗克洛和蓬帕杜尔，求教于公爵夫人和侯爵先生。 She turned to the positive period; to the eighteenth century, to Voltaire, to Ninon de l’Enclos and the Pompadour, to Madame la Duchesse and Monsieur le Marquis.
她断定自己与蓬帕杜尔夫人或公爵夫人不太相像，不过几乎完全与伯爵先生是同一类人。 She decided that she was not much in the line of la Pompadour or la Duchesse, but almost exactly in the line of Monsieur le Marquis.
她是对的。 And she was right.
她银白色的头发干净利索地向后梳去，露出自信的眉毛和鬓角，头发剪得很短，略微向后伸展，加上她那张相当丰满的粉红面庞和拉成两道细细弦月形的黑色眉毛，配上她那只拱形的鼻子和那双饱满傲慢的眼睛，使她全然成了一位十八世纪上半叶的人了。 With hair silvering to white, brushed back clean from her positive brow and temples, cut short, but sticking out a little behind, with her rather full, pink face and thin black eyebrows plucked to two fine, superficial crescents, her arching nose and her rather full insolent eyes she was perfectly eighteenth-century, the early half.
她这种更像侯爵先生而非侯爵夫人的气派使她显得十分摩登。 That she was Monsieur le Marquis rather than Madame la Marquise made her really modern.
她的外表是完美的。 Her appearance was perfect.
她的衣服颜色是灰色和粉色优雅地搭配在一起，也许还稍带点深铁灰色，她佩戴的首饰是颜色柔和古典的人造宝石。 She wore delicate combinations of grey and pink, maybe with a darkening iron-grey touch, and her jewels were of soft old coloured paste.
她的举止恬静，同时透着警觉，非常镇定，但又极其自信。 Her bearing was a sort of alert repose, very calm, but very assured.
用一句俗话说，没有她跨不过的槛儿。 There was, to use a vulgarism, no getting past her.
她手头有几千英镑。 She had a couple of thousand pounds she could lay hands on.
而弗吉尼娅呢，自然地，总是欠债累累。 Virginia, of course, was always in debt.
可终归也不能对弗吉尼娅嗤之以鼻。 But, after all, Virginia was not to be sniffed at.

一年以后，此时弗吉尼娅三十二了，阿德里安已娶了一位富有的美国姑娘，同时还被调往驻华盛顿的公使馆工作，他一到伦敦便诚心诚意地来看弗吉尼娅了，忠诚地拜倒在她脚下，忠诚地认为她是最迷人的精神尤物，而且忠诚地认为弗吉尼娅本可以同他一起创造奇迹的，可是这种奇迹已经永远不会实现了，因为他此时已经结婚了。 A year later, when Virginia was thirty-two, Adrian, who had married a wealthy American girl and been transferred to a job in the legation at Washington in the meantime, faithfully came to see Virginia as soon as he was in London, faithfully kneeled at her feet, faithfully thought her the most wonderful spiritual being, and faithfully felt that she, Virginia, could have done wonders with him, which wonders would now never be done, for he had married in the meantime.
弗吉尼娅看上去面容枯槁、疲惫不堪。 Virginia was looking haggard and worn.
她与母亲的两人之家的计划没有成功。 The scheme of a ménage à deux with her mother had not succeeded.
而现在，工作使这个年轻女人疲惫不堪。 And now, work was telling on the younger woman.
不错，她灵巧敏捷，令人惊叹。 It is true, she was amazingly facile.
可她不能自始至终都依靠这样的灵巧敏捷。 But facility wouldn’t get her all the way.
她得去挣自己那份钱，而且挣得很辛苦。 She had to earn her money, and earn it hard.
她得勤恳工作，而且必须专心致志。 She had to slog, and she had to concentrate.
当她可以凭借灵敏的直觉而且不用担什么责任地工作时，工作使她感到兴奋。 While she could work by quick intuition and without much responsibility, work thrilled her.
但一旦她不得不踏踏实实地工作时，就像他们所说的那样，在一个真正要承担责任的岗位上，兢兢业业、勤勤恳恳、全神贯注，工作便弄得她疲惫不堪。 But as soon as she had to get down to it, as they say, grip and slog and concentrate, in a really responsible position, it wore her out terribly.
她不得不神经高度紧张地工作。 She had to do it all off her nerves.
她没有男人那样的战斗力。 She hadn’t the same sort of fighting power as a man.
一个男人可以唤起自己与生俱来的男人本性去拼命干好自己的工作，可一个女人不得不靠勇气，并且只能靠勇气。 Where a man can summon his old Adam in him to fight through his work, a woman has to draw on her nerves, and on her nerves alone.
因为女人的本性和这种工作毫无关系。 For the old Eve in her will have nothing to do with such work.
因此，精神上的责任感、心智上的专心致志和精神上的殚精竭虑可怕地折磨着一个女人，特别是当她作为一个部门的头儿，不是为别人工作的时候。 So that mental responsibility, mental concentration, mental slogging wear out a woman terribly, especially if she is head of a department, and not working for somebody.
就这样，可怜的弗吉尼娅被弄得精疲力竭。 So poor Virginia was worn out.
她变得骨瘦如柴。 She was thin as a rail.
她的精神彻底崩溃了。 Her nerves were frayed to bits.
可是她怎么也放不下那令人厌恶的工作。 And she could never forget her beastly work.
她常常在晚茶时分回到家里，一言不发，疲惫不堪。 She would come home at teatime speechless and done for.
一看见她这副样子，她母亲便痛苦不堪，真想说：是不是遇上什么麻烦了，弗吉尼娅？ Her mother, tortured by the sight of her, longed to say: Has anything gone wrong, Virginia?
你今天在办公室里遇到了什么特别烦心的事儿吗？——但她学会了保持沉默，一言不语。 Have you had anything particularly trying at the office today?—But she learned to hold her tongue, and say nothing.
这个问题对弗吉尼娅那可怜而劳累过度的神经会是无法忍受的最后一击，而尽管博多恩太太缄默忍让，也会有一点冒犯这位老妇人，戳到她内心痛处的小插曲。 The question would be the last straw to Virginia’s poor overwrought nerves, and there would be a little scene which, despite Mrs Bodoin’s calm and forbearance, offended the elder woman to the quick.
之前的痛苦经历使她学会了不去理会这孩子，就像人们不去理会一只易裂的硫酸桶一样。 She had learned, by bitter experience, to leave her child alone, as one would leave a frail tube of vitriol alone.
但是，当然，她不可能不挂念着弗吉尼娅。 But of course, she could not keep her mind off Virginia.
不挂念是不可能的。 That was impossible.
而不幸的弗吉尼娅，在工作压力以及她母亲那种令人生畏的不断关怀之下，处在了心力憔悴的最边缘。 And poor Virginia, under the strain of work and the strain of her mother’s awful ceaseless mind, was at the very end of her strength and resources.
博多恩太太一直不喜欢弗吉尼娅去工作。 Mrs Bodoin had always disliked the fact of Virginia’s doing a job.
而现在她则是憎恨这个事实了。 But now she hated it.
她对整个政府机构怀有强烈而恶毒的憎恨。它不但把弗吉尼娅困在那里，使她失去尊严，而且还把她，博多恩太太的女儿，变成了一个瘦削如柴、唠叨不休、令人生畏的老处女。 She hated the whole government office with violent and virulent hate.Not only was it undignified for Virginia to be tied up there, but it was turning her, Mrs Bodoin’s daughter, into a thin, nagging, fearsome old maid.
难道还有什么事更能使一个出生名门的爱尔兰女人完全变成一副英国人的德性，使她蒙受更大的耻辱吗？ Could anything be more utterly English and humiliating to a well-born Irishwoman?
整整一天，博多恩太太都在收拾这套公寓，熟练地织补其中一把锦缎椅子，擦亮几面威尼斯风格的镜子，一直擦到自己满意为止，挑选鲜花，买东西和料理家务，精心地照料家里的一切，下午精力不竭地接待了一些访客，喝完下午茶后离开会客厅上楼去写了几封信，洗个澡，精心打扮一番——她很乐于装扮自己——然后才像邹菊一样鲜艳亮丽地下来吃饭，不过比那静静的花儿更加精神饱满。 After a long day attending to the apartment, skilfully darning one of the brocade chairs, polishing the Venetian mirrors to her satisfaction, selecting flowers, doing certain shopping and housekeeping, attending perfectly to everything, then receiving callers in the afternoon, with never-ending energy, Mrs Bodoin would go up from the drawing-room after tea and write a few letters, take her bath, dress with great care—she enjoyed attending to her person—and come down to dinner as fresh as a daisy, but far more energetic than that quiet flower.
她现在准备好要度过一个完美的夜晚。 She was ready now for a full evening.
她痛苦焦急地感觉到弗吉尼娅就在家里，可是直到晚饭开始的时候才见到自己的女儿。 She was conscious, with gnawing anxiety, of Virginia’s presence in the house, but she did not see her daughter till dinner was announced.
弗吉尼娅悄然而入，然后直奔她自己的房间躲着不见人，也从不到客厅来用茶点。 Virginia slipped in, and away to her room unseen, never going into the drawing-room to tea.
如果博多恩太太听到女儿用钥匙开锁的声音，她便会快速地退回到某间屋子，直到弗吉尼娅安全通过。 If Mrs Bodoin heard her daughter’s key in the latch, she quickly retired into one of the rooms till Virginia was safely through.
对可怜的弗吉尼娅来说，当她从办公室回来时，仅仅是看到家里有人神经就会非常紧张。 It was too much for poor Virginia’s nerves even to catch sight of anybody in the house, when she came in from the office.
若听到客厅里那些来访者的喃喃细语声便更受不了了。 Bad enough to hear the murmur of visitors’ voices behind the drawing-room door.
于是博多恩太太便会纳闷：她怎么啦？ And Mrs Bodoin would wonder: How is she?
她今天晚上会怎么样？ How is she to-night?
我想知道她度过了怎样的一天？——这种挂念会徘徊在房子里，徘徊到弗吉尼娅在自己的房间里仰面躺着的地方。 I wonder what sort of a day she’s had?—And this thought would roam prowling through the house, to where Virginia was lying on her back in her room.
可是这位母亲不得不消磨着她的焦虑不安，直到晚饭时分。 But the mother would have to consume her anxiety till dinner-time.
那时弗吉尼娅就会出现，眼圈发黑、瘦骨嶙峋、神色紧张，一个刚下了班的年轻女人，浑身上下透着如此痕迹：衣着不得体，幽默中略带尖酸刻薄，受工作折磨，胃口不佳，对任何事都毫无兴趣。 And then Virginia would appear, with black lines under her eyes, thin, tense, a young woman out of an office, the stigma upon her: badly dressed, a little acid in humour, with an impaired digestion, not interested in anything, blighted by her work.
博多恩太太一看到她这副模样便觉得丢脸，但她完全能够克制住自己，只是轻描淡写地说几句无关痒痛的话，姿态优美地坐着，守着一桌纯粹为取悦弗吉尼娅而精心烹制的饭菜。 And Mrs Bodoin, humiliated at the very sight of her, would control herself perfectly, say nothing but the mere smooth nothings of casual speech, and sit in perfect form presiding at a carefully-cooked dinner thought out entirely to please Virginia.
那时，弗吉尼娅根本没在意自己吃了些什么。 Then Virginia hardly noticed what she ate.
博多恩太太渴望着一个充满活力的夜晚。 Mrs Bodoin was pining for an evening with life in it.
而弗吉尼娅会躺在长沙发上，然后打开扬声器。 But Virginia would lie on the couch and put on the loudspeaker.
或者她会在留声机上放一盘幽默唱片，被逗乐了之后，会再听一遍，又被逗乐了之后，再听一遍，一连听上六遍，一连被这样一盘略带幽默的唱片逗乐了六次，而博多恩太太都已经把这盘唱片的内容熟记于心了。 Or she would put a humorous record on the gramophone, and be amused, and hear it again, and be amused, and hear it again, six times, and six times be amused by a mildly funny record that Mrs Bodoin now knew off by heart.
“哎呀，弗吉尼娅，要是你乐意，我可以给你背诵那盘唱片，你就不必费事地去摇唱机了。”——而弗吉尼娅呢，停顿了一下，好像没有听清母亲说什么，然后回答道：“我确信你能，妈妈。” “Why, Virginia, I could repeat that record over to you, if you wished it, without your troubling to wind up that gramophone.”—And Virginia, after a pause in which she seemed not to have heard what her mother said, would reply, “I’m sure you could, mother”.
这简单的一句话表达出对雷切尔·博多恩本人、过去及其能力等所有一切的蔑视，蔑视她的精力、她的活力、她的智力、她的身体甚至是她本身的存在，这不禁令这位老妇人寒心。 And that simple speech would convey such volumes of contempt for all that Rachel Bodoin was or ever could be or ever had been, contempt for her energy, her vitality, her mind, her body, her very existence, that the elder woman would curl.
这就好似罗伯特·博多恩的灵魂极其恶毒地借女儿之口说话——然后，弗吉尼娅第七次放上了这张唱片。 It seemed as if the ghost of Robert Bodoin spoke out of the mouth of the daughter, in deadly venom.—Then Virginia would put on the record for the seventh time.
在尴尬难堪的第二年里，博多恩太太意识到这场游戏结束了。 During the second ghastly year, Mrs Bodoin realized that the game was up.
她成了一个精神垮掉的女人，一个再也没有目标和意义的女人了。 She was a beaten woman, a woman without object or meaning any more.
她那令人生畏的女性幽默之锤，曾经砸在了许多人的头上，实际上，砸过所有她曾经接触过的人，现在这重锤则向后抡去，砸在了她自己的头上。 The hammer of her awful female humour, which had knocked so many people on the head, all the people, in fact, that she had come into contact with, had at last flown backwards and hit herself on the head.
因为她女儿是她的另一个自我，她的另一面。 For her daughter was her other self, her alter ego.
博多恩太太一生的秘密、意义和力量都贮藏在这把铁锤上，这把对一切事物都要当头一击的活生生的幽默之锤。 The secret and the meaning and the power of Mrs Bodoin’s whole life lay in the hammer, that hammer of her living humour which knocked everything on the head.
幽默地给予每个人、每件事物当头一击一直都是她的欲望，她的激情。 That had been her lust and her passion, knocking everybody and everything humorously on the head.
她觉得从中受到了启发：这是一项使命。 She had felt inspired in it: it was a sort of mission.
并且她曾希望将这把锤子传给弗吉尼娅，她那聪颖、虽不可靠但仍旧真实的女儿，弗吉尼娅。 And she had hoped to hand on the hammer to Virginia, her clever, unsolid but still actual daughter, Virginia.
弗吉尼娅是雷切尔自我的延续。 Virginia was the continuation of Rachel’s own self.
弗吉尼娅是雷切尔的变体，是她的另外一个自我。 Virginia was Rachel’s alter ego, her other self.
可是，唉，这只说对了一半。 But, alas, it was a half-truth.
弗吉尼娅曾经有一个父亲。 Virginia had had a father.
这个一直被这位母亲完全忽略的事实，通过那锤子怪异的反弹，渐渐地被她所了解。 This fact, which had been utterly ignored by the mother, was gradually brought home to her by the curious recoil of the hammer.
弗吉尼娅还是她父亲的女儿。 Virginia was her father’s daughter.
在自然万物的结局当中，还有更不体面、更令人恐惧、更违反常情的事情吗？ Could anything be more unseemly, horrid, more perverse in the natural scheme of things?
因为罗伯特·博多恩已经被雷切尔的锤子彻底地、理所当然地当头击中。 For Robert Bodoin had been fully and deservedly knocked on the head by Rachel’s hammer.
可他竟然在博多恩太太自己的女儿，她的另一个自我，也就是弗吉尼娅的身上复活了，而且开始用一把有点儿恶毒的锤子回击了，那简直就是戴维的卵石与哥利亚的战斧在作战！还有什么比这更令人恶心的吗？ Could anything, then, be more disgusting than that he should resurrect again in the person of Mrs Bodoin’s own daughter, her own alter ego Virginia, and start hitting back with a little spiteful hammer that was David’s pebble against Goliath’s battle-axe!
可是那小小的卵石是致命的。 But the little pebble was mortal.
博多恩太太感觉它陷进了她的额头、她的太阳穴，她完了。 Mrs Bodoin felt it sink into her brow, her temple, and she was finished.
锤子从她手中无力地掉落下来。 The hammer fell nerveless from her hand.
现在这两个女人大部分时间里都是独处。 The two women were now mostly alone.
弗吉尼娅太劳累了，晚上就不想去招待客人了。 Virginia was too tired to have company in the evening.
因此就与留声机或扬声器为伴，或保持寂静沉默。 So there was the gramophone or loudspeaker, or else silence.
两个女人开始憎恶起这寓所来了。 Both women had come to loathe the apartment.
弗吉尼娅认为这是她母亲横行霸道的最后的绝招，她感觉自己被那独断的欧比松地毯，那令人厌恶的威尼斯式镜子，那过于精心摆弄的大束鲜花所欺压。 Virginia felt it was the last grand act of bullying on her mother’s part, she felt bullied by the assertive Aubusson carpet, by the beastly Venetian mirrors, by the big overcultured flowers.
她甚至觉得受到那精美饭菜的侮辱，于是再次怀念起索霍饭店和旅馆里她那两间简陋破旧的房间。 She even felt bullied by the excellent food, and longed again for a Soho restaurant and her two poky shabby rooms in the hotel.
她讨厌这个寓所，她憎恶每件事物。 She loathed the apartment: she loathed everything.
但她没有精力搬家了。 But she had not the energy to move.
她没精力做任何事情。 She had not the energy to do anything.
她慢悠悠地去干她的工作，其余时间里她就平躺在那里混时间。 She crawled to her work, and for the rest, she lay flat, gone.
正是弗吉尼娅精疲力竭的懒散模样真正击垮了博多恩太太。 It was Virginia’s worn-out inertia that really finished Mrs Bodoin.
那正是击碎她太阳穴骨的卵石。“不得不参加我女儿的葬礼，接受所有她办公室职员的慰问，不，那是最大的屈辱，我必须免遭其伤害。 That was the pebble that broke the bone of her temple: “To have to attend my daughter’s funeral, and accept the sympathy of all her fellow-clerks in her office, no, that is a final humiliation which I must spare myself.
不！ No!
如果弗吉尼娅一定要当个女职员的话，从今以后她必须自己负责任。 If Virginia must be a lady-clerk, she must be it henceforth on her own responsibility.
我要从她的生活中退出来。” I will retire from her existence.”
博多恩太太曾努力设法说服弗吉尼娅放弃工作来和她一起住。 Mrs Bodoin had tried hard to persuade Virginia to give up her work and come and live with her.
博多恩太太还曾提出把她收入的一半给弗吉尼娅。 She had offered her half her income.
都是白费力气。 In vain.
弗吉尼娅仍坚守在自己的岗位上。 Virginia stuck to her office.
好吧！ Very well!
就那样好了！——这间寓所真是一大败笔，博多恩太太真想，真想再把它撕得粉碎。 So be it!—The apartment was a fiasco, Mrs Bodoin was longing, longing to tear it to pieces again.
这把锤子最后的、最终的一击！——“弗吉尼娅，难道你不觉得我们最好处理掉这间寓所，像过去那样这里住住那里住住吗？ One last and final blow of the hammer!—“Virginia, don’t you think we’d better get rid of this apartment, and live around as we used to?
难道你不认为我们应该那样做吗？”——“但是你投进去的那些钱怎么办？ Don’t you think we’ll do that?”—“But all the money you’ve put into it?
而且租期是十年呢！”弗吉尼娅用一种懒洋洋的口吻叫喊道。——“不要紧！ And the lease for ten years!” cried Virginia, in a kind of inertia.—“Never mind!
我们愿意花这钱。 We had the pleasure of making it.
并且我们住在这里已经享受了该享受的乐趣。 And we’ve had as much pleasure out of living in it as we shall ever have.
现在我们最好处理掉它——尽快——难道你不觉得吗？” Now we’d better get rid of it—quickly—don’t you think?”
此时此刻，博多恩太太猛然伸展双臂把画从墙上扯了下来，卷起了那张欧比松式地毯，把瓷器从镶有象牙的橱柜中抓了出来。 Mrs Bodoin’s arms were twitching to snatch the pictures off the walls, roll up the Aubusson carpet, take the china out of the ivory-inlaid cabinet there and then, at that very moment.
“我们等到星期天再做决定吧。”弗吉尼娅说道。 “Let us wait till Sunday before we decide,” said Virginia.
“等到星期天！ “Till Sunday!
还有四天！ Four days!
要那么久吗？ As long as that?
难道我们各自心里还没做出决定吗？”博多恩太太说。 Haven’t we already decided in our own minds?” said Mrs Bodoin.
“无论如何，我们得等到星期天。”弗吉尼娅说道。 “We’ll wait till Sunday, anyhow,” said Virginia.
次日晚上，那个亚美尼亚人过来吃晚饭。 The next evening, the Armenian came to dinner.
弗吉尼娅叫他阿诺德，用法语发音就叫阿尔诺。 Virginia called him Arnold, with the French pronunciation, Arnault.
博多恩太太简直受不了他，而且怎么也记不住他的名字，那名字似乎相当拗口，她要么叫他亚美尼亚人，要么以蜜饯的名字称他为拉哈特·拉科姆，要么干脆称他叫土耳其软糖。 Mrs Bodoin, who barely tolerated him, and could never get his name, which seemed to have a lot of bouyoums in it, called him either the Armenian, or the Rahat Lakoum, after the name of the sweetmeat, or simply The Turkish Delight.
“阿尔诺今晚要来吃晚饭，妈妈。” “Arnault is coming to dinner to-night, Mother.”
“真的吗？ “Really!
土耳其软糖要来这里吃晚饭？ The Turkish Delight is coming here to dinner?
