范妮与安妮 Fanny and Annie
作者：D. H. 劳伦斯 By D. H. Lawrence
月台上到处是被火光照亮的黑脸庞，他在人群中转过身时，脸也被火光映得闪闪发光。在炉火的映照下，她瞥见了他晃动着的面孔，像一团漂浮的火苗。怀旧的情结和归乡的宿命像麻醉剂一样侵袭着她全身的血管。他那张一成不变的脸，此刻被火光照亮了！熊熊的炉火发出的摇曳而幽暗的红色火光直入云霄，照亮了这座小车站上那群乱哄哄的工人，照亮了他，然后熄灭了。 Flame-lurid his face as he turned among the throng of flame-lit and dark faces upon the platform.In the light of the furnace she caught sight of his drifting countenance, like a piece of floating fire.And the nostalgia, the doom of homecoming went through her veins like a drug.His eternal face, flame-lit now!The pulse and darkness of red fire from the furnace towers in the sky, lighting the desultory, industrial crowd on the wayside station, lit him and went out.
当然，他没有看到她。他被火光照着，所以没有看见！他总是那个样子：眉毛快连到一起，头上戴着那顶普通的帽子，脖子上围着条红黑相间的围巾，还打了个结。出来见她，甚至连硬领也不戴！火焰已经灭了，四下里一片漆黑。 Of course he did not see her.Flame-lit and unseeing!Always the same, with his meeting eyebrows, his common cap, and his red-and-black scarf knotted round his throat.Not even a collar to meet her!The flames had sunk, there was shadow.
她打开积满污垢的支线车厢门，开始往下拿包。当然，哪儿都找不到行李搬运工，但是有哈里——他隐约闪现在一小群人的外边，自然没看到她。 She opened the door of her grimy, branch-line carriage, and began to get down her bags.The porter was nowhere, of course, but there was Harry, obscure, on the outer edge of the little crowd, missing her, of course.
“在这儿！哈里！”她叫道，在昏暗的光线中挥动着她的伞。他连忙跑上前来。 ‘Here!Harry!’ she called, waving her umbrella in the twilight.He hurried forward.
“你回来啦？”他说，高兴地迎接她。她下了车，有点儿慌张，匆匆地亲了他一下。 ‘Tha’s come, has ter?’ he said, in a sort of cheerful welcome.She got down, rather flustered, and gave him a peck of a kiss.
“有两只提箱！”她说。 ‘Two suit-cases!’ she said.
当他攀上车厢去拿她的手提包时，她的内心在痛苦呻吟。车站后面那只大火炉一下子又喷出火光，直冲昏暗的天空。她感到那通红的火焰掠过了她的脸庞。她回来了，她永远地回来了。她的内心郁郁不乐。她怀疑自己能否禁受得了。 Her soul groaned within her, as he clambered into the carriage after her bags.Up shot the fire in the twilight sky, from the great furnace behind the station.She felt the red flame go across her face.She had come back, she had come back for good.And her spirit groaned dismally.She doubted if she could bear it.
她站在这个火光映照下的肮脏小站里，穿着精致的外套和裙子，戴着灰色的阔边丝绒帽，高挑而醒目。她戴着灰色的手套，手里拿着阳伞、珠子腰链和一只小皮箱，而哈里则提着她的包趔趔趄趄走下那列肮脏的小火车。 There, on the sordid little station under the furnaces, she stood, tall and distinguished, in her well-made coat and skirt and her broad grey velour hat.She held her umbrella, her bead chatelaine, and a little leather case in her grey-gloved hands, while Harry staggered out of the ugly little train with her bags.
“后面还有个大行李箱。”她欢快地说。但是，她并不觉得开心。铸铁厂那一对黑乎乎的烟囱喷出高高的火光，直入夜空。整个场景一片明亮。火车很乐意地等候着。它还会再等上十分钟。这一点她知道。一切都那么熟悉。 ‘There’s a trunk at the back,’ she said in her bright voice.But she was not feeling bright.The twin black cones of the iron foundry blasted their sky-high fires into the night.The whole scene was lurid.The train waited cheerfully.It would wait another ten minutes.She knew it.It was all so deadly familiar.
现在我们赶紧交代一下吧。她是一位贵夫人的女仆，三十岁了，回家乡来和她的初恋，一个翻砂工人结婚——在这之前的十二三年中，她对他一直若即若离。她为什么回来呢？她爱他吗？不。她并没有假装爱他。她曾经爱上了她那才华横溢、满腔抱负的表兄，但他抛弃了她，而且现在已不在人世了。她另外还有过几桩风流韵事，后来也都无疾而终。所以她回来了，突然回来与她的初恋情人结婚——这么多年来，他一直等着她，或者说一直单身。 Let us confess it at once.She was a lady’s maid, thirty years old, come back to marry her first-love, a foundry worker, after having kept him dangling, off and on, for a dozen years.Why had she come back?Did she love him?No. She didn’t pretend to.She had loved her brilliant and ambitious cousin, who had jilted her, and who had died.She had had other affairs which had come to nothing.So here she was, come back suddenly to marry her first-love, who had waited—or remained single—all these years.
当哈里以工人特有的大跨步走下月台，走向警卫的篷车的时候，她问道：“要不找个搬运工来搬这些行李？” ‘Won’t a porter carry those?’ she said, as Harry strode with his workman’s stride down the platform towards the guard’s van.
“我能行。”他说。 ‘I can manage,’ he said.
于是她拿着她的阳伞、腰链和小皮箱，跟在他后面。 And with her umbrella, her chatelaine, and her little leather case, she followed him.
大行李箱在那儿。 The trunk was there.
“我们请希瑟蔬菜水果店的运货马车帮忙捎一下吧。”他说。 ‘We’ll get Heather’s greengrocer’s cart to fetch it up,’ he said.
“没有出租马车吗？”范妮说，心里闷闷不乐，她很清楚这里并没有出租马车。 ‘Isn’t there a cab?’ said Fanny, knowing dismally enough that there wasn’t.
“我把它放在自动售货机旁，希瑟蔬菜水果店的车子会在八点半左右把它捎走。”他说。 ‘I’ll just put it aside o’ the penny-in-the-slot, and Heather’s greengrocers’ll fetch it about half past eight,’ he said.
他抓着箱子的两只把手，拎着它蹒跚着走过交叉路口，他一路踉跄着，两腿不时撞在箱子上。随后，他把箱子搁在那架红色的糖果机旁边。 He seized the box by its two handles and staggered with it across the level-crossing, bumping his legs against it as he waddled.Then he dropped it by the red sweet-meats machine.
“放在那儿安全吗？”她问道。 ‘Will it be safe there?’ she said.
“哎——跟家里一样安全。”他回答道。他回去取了另两只包。这样他们手里都提满了东西，开始沿着铸铁厂那又长又黑的大厂房吃力地爬上小山。她走在他的旁边——他是个名副其实的工人，提着行李吃力地向前走。愈来愈深的暮色下闪起红红的火光。铸铁厂里传来可怕而又缓慢的哐，哐，哐的打铁声，巨大的噪音，中间的间歇长得叫人难以忍受。 ‘Ay—safe as houses,’ he answered.He returned for the two bags.Thus laden, they started to plod up the hill, under the great long black building of the foundry.She walked beside him—workman of workmen he was, trudging with that luggage.The red lights flared over the deepening darkness.From the foundry came the horrible, slow clang, clang, clang of iron, a great noise, with an interval just long enough to make it unendurable.
与她到达格洛斯特的情况比较一下吧：女主人坐大马车，她则带着行李坐双轮轻便马车；过了河，车道旁有宜人的小树林；她坐在阿瑟的身旁，人人都对她彬彬有礼。 Compare this with the arrival at Gloucester: the carriage for her mistress, the dog-cart for herself with the luggage; the drive out past the river, the pleasant trees of the carriage-approach; and herself sitting beside Arthur, everybody so polite to her.
她回家乡来了——永远地回来了！挨着这个满手行李的人，吃力地爬着这座没完没了、令人讨厌的小山，她的心跳几乎都要停止了。多么落魄啊！多么落魄啊！此时此景，她没法像往常一样欢欣愉悦。她对这一切太熟悉了。要是不寻常的情况，倒还容易承受，可这却是再也熟悉不过的乏味的过去！ She had come home—for good!Her heart nearly stopped beating as she trudged up that hideous and interminable hill, beside the laden figure.What a come-down!What a come-down!She could not take it with her usual bright cheerfulness.She knew it all too well.It is easy to bear up against the unusual, but the deadly familiarity of an old stale past!
他把皮包放在一根路灯柱子下，想休息一会儿。两人就这样站在灯火下。路过的人都盯着她看，一边向哈里道晚安。他们几乎认不出她，她成了个外乡人。 He dumped the bags down under a lamp-post, for a rest.There they stood, the two of them, in the lamplight.Passers-by stared at her, and gave good-night to Harry.Her they hardly knew, she had become a stranger.
“包太重了吧，我来提一只吧。”她说。 ‘They’re too heavy for you, let me carry one,’ she said.
“提着走了一英里，还真觉得有点沉呢。”他回答。 ‘They begin to weigh a bit by the time you’ve gone a mile,’ he answered.
“我来拎那只小的吧。”她坚持说。 ‘Let me carry the little one,’ she insisted.
“你真想提，那就提一会儿吧。”他说着，将小旅行包递了过去。 ‘Tha can ha’e it for a minute, if ter’s a mind,’ he said, handing over the valise.
就这样，他们来到了山顶上这座丑陋小城镇的街市。所有人都盯着她看，天哪，他们都怎样盯着她看啊！电影院才刚开始入场，所以队伍一直排到了大街转角的地方。大伙儿都仔细地打量着她。“晚上好，哈里！”那些家伙都饶有兴趣地喊着。 And thus they arrived in the streets of shops of the little ugly town on top of the hill.How everybody stared at her; my word, how they stared!And the cinema was just going in, and the queues were tailing down the road to the corner.And everybody took full stock of her.‘Night, Harry!’ shouted the fellows, in an interested voice.
然而，他们到了她婶婶的家——小街上的一家小糖果铺。“叮铃”，他们摁了一下门铃，她的婶婶就从厨房里跑了过来。 However, they arrived at her aunt’s—a little sweet-shop in a side street.They ‘pinged’ the door-bell, and her aunt came running forward out of the kitchen.
“你回来啦，孩子！我想你们一定很想喝杯茶吧。你还好吗？” ‘There you are, child!Dying for a cup of tea, I’m sure.How are you?’
范妮的婶婶亲了亲她，范妮用尽全力才克制住自己，没有突然哭出来，她感到如此低落。也许，她是想喝茶。 Fanny’s aunt kissed her, and it was all Fanny could do to refrain from bursting into tears, she felt so low.Perhaps it was her tea she wanted.
“这行李把你累坏了吧。”范妮的婶婶对哈里说。 ‘You’ve had a drag with that luggage,’ said Fanny’s aunt to Harry.
“唉——我很乐意能放下来，”他边说边望着自己的手，手上已经被包的把手勒出了印痕。 ‘Ay—I’m not sorry to put it down,’ he said, looking at his hand which was crushed and cramped by the bag handle.
接着，他就出门去查看希瑟蔬菜水果铺的马车来没来。 Then he departed to see about Heather’s greengrocery cart.
范妮坐下喝茶时，她的婶婶，这个头发花白、面容秀丽、身材娇小的女人，怀着赞赏的心境看着她，替她感到惋惜。因为范妮很美：身材高挑、笔直，肤色洁白，鼻子精致高挺，一头浓密的褐发，一双大而明亮的灰色眼睛。一个热情的女人——一个叫人害怕的女人。她是如此高傲，内心却又如此炽热！她出身于一个生性热情的家族。 When Fanny sat at tea, her aunt, a grey-haired, fair-faced little woman, looked at her with an admiring heart, feeling bitterly sore for her.For Fanny was beautiful: tall, erect, finely coloured, with her delicately arched nose, her rich brown hair, her large lustrous grey eyes.A passionate woman—a woman to be afraid of.So proud, so inwardly violent!She came of a violent race.
需要有个女人来怜惜她。男人没有这样的勇气。可怜的范妮！她这么像一位大家闺秀，气质高雅，雍容华贵。可是，似乎一切都在跟她作对。这个俏丽而敏感的女人，总是带着一副紧张而又不自然的笑脸，似乎每次都注定要蒙受耻辱，伤心失望。 It needed a woman to sympathize with her.Men had not the courage.Poor Fanny!She was such a lady, and so straight and magnificent.And yet everything seemed to do her down.Every time she seemed to be doomed to humiliation and disappointment, this handsome, brilliantly sensitive woman, with her nervous, overwrought laugh.
“你这次当真回来了吗，孩子？”婶婶说。 ‘So you’ve really come back, child?’ said her aunt.
“真回来了，婶婶。”范妮说。 ‘I really have, Aunt,’ said Fanny.
“可怜的哈里！你知道，我真没法确定你是不是在拿他寻开心。” ‘Poor Harry!I’m not sure, you know, Fanny, that you’re not taking a bit of an advantage of him.’
“噢，婶婶，他已经等了这么久，也该等到他想要的一切了。”范妮苦笑了一下。 ‘Oh, Aunt, he’s waited so long, he may as well have what he’s waited for.’Fanny laughed grimly.
“是呀，孩子，他等了这么久，我都不知道这样对他是不是太残忍了。范妮，你知道的，我很喜欢他，虽然你也明白，我并不认为他配得上你。而且，我认为他自己也是这么想的，可怜的人。” ‘Yes, child, he’s waited so long, that I’m not sure it isn’t a bit hard on him.You know, I like him, Fanny—though as you know quite well, I don’t think he’s good enough for you.And I think he thinks so himself, poor fellow.’
“这个您可别这么肯定，婶婶。哈里虽然普普通通，人却不谦卑。只要他对女方有意思，哪怕那是女王，他也不会认为是高攀。” ‘Don’t you be so sure of that, Aunt.Harry is common, but he’s not humble.He wouldn’t think the Queen was any too good for him, if he’d a mind to her.’
“唔——要是他对自己有个恰当的看法，那倒还好。” ‘Well—It’s as well if he has a proper opinion of himself.’
“这得看您认为什么算是恰当，”范妮说，“不过他有他的优点——” ‘It depends what you call proper,’ said Fanny.‘But he’s got his good points—’
“噢，他人挺好，我很喜欢他，我确实很喜欢他。只不过，就像我跟你说的，他配不上你。” ‘Oh, he’s a nice fellow, and I like him, I do like him.Only, as I tell you, he’s not good enough for you.’
“我已经打定主意啦，婶婶。”范妮苦笑着说。 ‘I’ve made up my mind, Aunt,’ said Fanny, grimly.
“是呀，”婶婶若有所思地说，“都说，精诚所至，金石为开——” ‘Yes,’ mused the aunt.‘They say all things come to him who waits—’
“比他指望的还要多，是吧，婶婶？”范妮笑得很苦涩。 ‘More than he’s bargained for, eh, Aunt?’ laughed Fanny rather bitterly.
这位可怜的婶婶，这苦涩的笑让她为自己的侄女感到了心酸。 The poor aunt, this bitterness grieved her for her niece.
她们的谈话被店铺的门铃声打断了，接着听到哈里在喊：“是这里！”但是哈里没有立即进来，范妮大概一时又担心起他来，所以连忙站起身，走去铺子。她看见外边有一辆运货马车，于是走向门口。 They were interrupted by the ping of the shop-bell, and Harry’s call of ‘Right!’But as he did not come in at once, Fanny, feeling solicitous for him presumably at the moment, rose and went into the shop.She saw a cart outside, and went to the door.
刚站到门口，她就听见一个女人粗鄙的谩骂声从街对面的黑暗中传了过来： And the moment she stood in the doorway, she heard a woman’s common vituperative voice crying from the darkness of the opposite side of the road:
“你在这儿，是吧？我可要你好看，大老爷。我可要你好看，你等着瞧我敢不敢！” ‘Tha’rt theer, ar ter?I’ll shame thee, Mester.I’ll shame thee, see if I dunna.’
范妮吓了一跳，仔细朝对面的黑暗中望去，瞥见一个戴着黑色无边女帽的女人从一盏路灯下朝小街的那头走去。 Startled, Fanny stared across the darkness, and saw a woman in a black bonnet go under one of the lamps up the side street.
哈里和比尔·希瑟已经把那只大行李箱从小运货马车上拖下来。他们抬着箱子走上铺子外面的台阶，她连忙退到一旁。 Harry and Bill Heather had dragged the trunk off the little dray, and she retreated before them as they came up the shop step with it.
“把它搁在哪儿？”哈里问。 ‘Wheer shalt ha’e it?’ asked Harry.
“最好搬到楼上去。”范妮说。 ‘Best take it upstairs,’ said Fanny.
她先走上楼去点亮了煤气灯。 She went up first to light the gas.
希瑟走后，哈里坐下来喝茶，吃猪肉饼，这时范妮问道：“那个大喊大叫的女人是谁？” When Heather had gone, and Harry was sitting down having tea and pork pie, Fanny asked: ‘Who was that woman shouting?’
“不知道，这我可没法告诉你。总是冲着某人来的吧，我想。”哈里回答道。范妮看了看他，没再问下去了。 ‘Nay, I canna tell thee.To somebody, Is’d think,’ replied Harry.Fanny looked at him, but asked no more.
他三十二岁了，浅黄的头发，浅黄的胡子。操着浓重的乡音，像一个翻砂工人，事实也正是如此。不过女人们总是很喜欢他。他具有一种母亲的宝贝儿子的气质——热情、顽皮而十分敏感。 He was a fair-haired fellow of thirty-two, with a fair moustache.He was broad in his speech, and looked like a foundry-hand, which he was.But women always liked him.There was something of a mother’s lad about him—something warm and playful and really sensitive.
就算对范妮，他也是有吸引力的。令她非常懊恼的是他胸无大志。他是一个制模工，但是手艺平平。他已经三十二岁了，却连二十英镑也没攒起来。将来家里的花销得由她来提供。他不在乎。他就是不在乎。他根本没一点进取心。可也没有什么恶习——没有什么明显的恶习。但他就是无动于衷，边赚边花，一点也不在乎。然而，他看上去并不快乐。她记起他在火光照耀下的神情：心事重重，心不在焉。他坐在那儿吃着猪肉饼，鼓着两个腮帮子，她觉得他像是她的宿命。她为这个宿命感到愤怒。这倒不是因为他很粗鄙。他举止虽粗俗，但几乎是有意而为。事实上他本人并不粗俗。比如，饮食对他来说并不是特别重要，他并不贪吃。他也有自己的魅力，尤其是对于女人而言——他的金发，他敏感的个性，还有他能让女人感觉到她是高高在上的。但范妮了解她，了解他那古怪的、顽固而狭隘的性格，这几乎使她发狂。 He had his attractions even for Fanny.What she rebelled against so bitterly was that he had no sort of ambition.He was a moulder, but of very commonplace skill.He was thirty-two years old, and hadn’t saved twenty pounds.She would have to provide the money for the home.He didn’t care.He just didn’t care.He had no initiative at all.He had no vices—no obvious ones.But he was just indifferent, spending as he went, and not caring.Yet he did not look happy.She remembered his face in the fire-glow: something haunted, abstracted about it.As he sat there eating his pork pie, bulging his cheek out, she felt he was like a doom to her.And she raged against the doom of him.It wasn’t that he was gross.His way was common, almost on purpose.But he himself wasn’t really common.For instance, his food was not particularly important to him, he was not greedy.He had a charm, too, particularly for women, with his blondness and his sensitiveness and his way of making a woman feel that she was a higher being.But Fanny knew him, knew the peculiar obstinate limitedness of him, that would nearly send her mad.
他一直呆到九点半左右。她随他走到门口。 He stayed till about half past nine.She went to the door with him.
“你什么时候来？”他说着，把头朝着大致是他家的方向扬了扬。 ‘When are you coming up?’ he said, jerking his head in the direction, presumably, of his own home.
“我明天下午来。”她高兴地说。在范妮和他母亲古多尔太太之间，自然没有什么爱的流失。 ‘I’ll come tomorrow afternoon,’ she said brightly.Between Fanny and Mrs. Goodall, his mother, there was naturally no love lost.
她再次尴尬地轻轻吻了他一下，说了声晚安。 Again she gave him an awkward little kiss, and said good-night.
“你知道的，孩子，如果他看起来不是那么热情，你也不要惊讶，”她的婶婶说，“这是你的过错。” ‘You can’t wonder, you know, child, if he doesn’t seem so very keen,’ said her aunt.‘It’s your own fault.’
“噢，婶婶，要是他很热情，我反倒会受不了。他现在这样，我可以和他相处得更好。” ‘Oh, Aunt, I couldn’t stand him when he was keen.I can do with him a lot better as he is.’

第二天下午，范妮如约看望了哈里的家人。古多尔太太体态丰腴，梳着平整的中分头，是个庸俗顽固的女人，她惯坏了四个儿子和一个已出嫁的泼妇女儿。她属于那种强悍守旧的女人，容忍不了容貌、教育或者任何方式的炫耀。她极其讨厌标准的英语发音。她总用“你呀你呀”的乡音跟她未来的媳妇说话，还说：“你瞧，我可不像看起来那么笨。” Fanny paid the promised visit to his people the next afternoon.Mrs. Goodall was a large woman with smooth-parted hair, a common, obstinate woman, who had spoiled her four lads and her one vixen of a married daughter.She was one of those old-fashioned powerful natures that couldn’t do with looks or education or any form of showing off.She fairly hated the sound of correct English.She thee’d and tha’d her prospective daughter-in-law, and said: ‘i’m none as ormins’ as I look, seest ta.”

风琴随即演奏起来。唱这首赞美诗的同时，有人收集起了奉金。赞美诗唱完之后，就做祷告。 The organ tuned up promptly.During the hymn the offertory was taken.And after the hymn, the prayer.
恩德比先生来自诺森伯兰郡。和哈里一样，他也一直没能改变浓重的乡音。他为人有点单纯，可以说是一个为上帝效劳的傻瓜，一个古怪的单身汉，多愁善感，相貌丑陋，但却彬彬有礼。 Mr. Enderby came from Northumberland.Like Harry, he had never been able to conquer his accent, which was very broad.He was a little simple, one of God’s fools, perhaps, an odd bachelor soul, emotional, ugly, but very gentle.
“我们敬爱的主，亲爱的耶稣啊，如果有一个罪恶的阴影降临我们的丰收上，我们愿交给您去审判，因为您是我们的审判人。我们把我们的灵魂与不幸全呈上来给您——耶稣，我们毫无怨言。主啊，使我们远离鲁莽的言辞，约束我们，使我们不要有愚蠢的言论和思想，我们向您祈祷，我们的主耶稣，因为您知道一切，审判一切。” ‘And if, O our dear Lord, beloved Jesus, there should fall a shadow of sin upon our harvest, we leave it to Thee to judge, for Thou art judge.We lift our spirits and our sorrow, Jesus, to Thee, and our mouths are dumb.O, Lord, keep us from forward speech, restrain us from foolish words and thoughts, we pray Thee, Lord Jesus, who knowest all and judgest all.’
这样，牧师用他忧郁而洪亮的声音说着，又在主面前洗了双手。在祈祷时，范妮俯身向前，睁开了眼睛。她可以看到哈里圆圆的脑袋也向前低着。他面无表情、捉摸不透。这一打击使她不知所措。也许，她心中只有愤怒。 Thus the minister said in his sad, resonant voice, washed his hands before the Lord.Fanny bent forward open-eyed during the prayer.She could see the roundish head of Harry, also bent forward.His face was inscrutable and expressionless.The shock left her bewildered.Anger perhaps was her dominating emotion.
兴奋的听众开始窸窸窣窣起身，慢慢地走出小教堂，一边还饶有兴致地看着范妮，看着尼克松太太，还有哈里。尼克松太太身材矮小，傲慢地站在座位旁，面向通道，似乎在宣布，不用卷起袖子，她也可以对付任何人。范妮静静地坐着。幸好人们不必从她面前走过。而哈里呢，两耳绯红，正窘迫地从楼座走出来。响亮的风琴声盖过了人们下楼出教堂时的混乱。 The audience began to rustle to its feet, to ooze slowly and excitedly out of the chapel, looking with wildly-interested eyes at Fanny, at Mrs. Nixon, and at Harry.Mrs. Nixon, shortish, stood defiant in her pew, facing the aisle, as if announcing that, without rolling her sleeves up, she was ready for anybody.Fanny sat quite still.Luckily the people did not have to pass her.And Harry, with red ears, was making his way sheepishly out of the gallery.The loud noise of the organ covered all the downstairs commotion of exit.
当会众鱼贯而出时，牧师却令人费解地坐在布道坛上，默不作声，犹如一具骷髅。等最后一批徘徊不去的人伸长脖子注视着还坐在那儿的安妮，然后不情愿地离去以后，牧师才站起来，弓着身子顺着这座乡村小教堂走到门口，把门闩好。然后，他走回来，挨着这个默默无语的年轻女人坐下。 The minister sat silent and inscrutable in his pulpit, rather like a death’s-head, while the congregation filed out.When the last lingerers had unwillingly departed, craning their necks to stare at the still seated Fanny, he rose, stalked in his hooked fashion down the little country chapel and fastened the door.Then he returned and sat down by the silent young woman.
“这太不幸，太不幸了！”他悲叹道。“我很抱歉，真的，我非常抱歉，哎，真的很抱歉！”他叹息着结束了自己的话。 ‘This is most unfortunate, most unfortunate!’ he moaned.‘I am so sorry, I am so sorry, indeed, indeed, ah, indeed!’ he sighed himself to a close.
“这事太突然了，太意外了，这是一点。”范妮伶俐地说。 ‘It’s a sudden surprise, that’s one thing,’ said Fanny brightly.
“是啊，是的，的确是这样。真的，太出人意料了，没错。我不认识这个女人，不认识她。” ‘Yes—yes—indeed.Yes, a surprise, yes.I don’t know the woman, I don’t know her.’
“我认得她，”范妮说，“她是个坏女人。” ‘I know her,’ said Fanny.‘She’s a bad one.’
“哎！哎！”牧师叹道，“我不认识她。我不明白。我一点儿也不明白。但这太让人遗憾了，真的非常遗憾。我感到十分抱歉。” ‘Well!Well!’ said the minister.‘I don’t know her.I don’t understand.I don’t understand at all.But it is to be regretted, it is very much to be regretted.I am very sorry.’
范妮一直注视着教堂法衣室的门。楼座的楼梯不是连着教堂正厅，而是连着法衣室。她知道唱诗班的人都在那里窥视，想探出点消息。 Fanny was watching the vestry door.The gallery stairs communicated with the vestry, not with the body of the chapel.She knew the choir members had been peeping for information.
终于，哈里来了，手里拿着他的帽子，显得局促不安。 At last Harry came—rather sheepishly—with his hat in his hand.
“走吧。”范妮边说边站了起来。 ‘Well!’ said Fanny, rising to her feet.
“我们多了一个小插曲。”哈里说。 ‘We’ve had a bit of an extra,’ said Harry.
“我想是的。”范妮说。 ‘I should think so,’ said Fanny.
“一件非常不幸的事，太不幸了。你明白吗，哈里？我一点儿也不明白。” ‘A most unfortunate circumstance—a most unfortunate circumstance.Do you understand it, Harry?I don’t understand it at all.’
“噢，我知道。那个女人的女儿有孩子了，她却怪罪于我。” ‘Ah, I understand it.The daughter’s goin’ to have a childt, an’ ’er lays it on to me.’
“她不应该怪你吗？”范妮有点儿苛责地问。 ‘And has she no occasion to?’ asked Fanny, rather censorious.
“它要不是别的家伙的，那也不是我的。”哈里眼光瞥向一旁，说道。 ‘It’s no more mine than it is some other chap’s,’ said Harry, looking aside.
一阵沉默。 There was a moment of pause.
“哪个女孩？”范妮问。 ‘Which girl is it?’ asked Fanny.
“安妮——年纪小的那个——” ‘Annie—the young one—’
又是一阵沉默。 There followed another silence.
“我想我不认识她们吧，是吧？”牧师问。 ‘I don’t think I know them, do I?’ asked the minister.
“我想应该不认识。她们姓尼克松，母亲嫁给老鲍勃——她的第二任丈夫。她是个母老虎，把那姑娘逼成了现在这个样子。他们住在曼纳斯街。” ‘I shouldn’t think so.Their name’s Nixon—mother married old Bob for her second husband.She’s a tanger—’s driven the gel to what she is.They live in Manners Road.’
“怎么，那姑娘哪里不好了？”范妮尖锐地问。“我认识她时，她挺好的啊。” ‘Why, what’s amiss with the girl?’ asked Fanny sharply.‘She was all right when I knew her.’
“唉，她是挺好。只是经常跟一些男人们在酒吧里进进出出。”哈里说。 ‘Ay—she’s all right.But she’s always in an’ out o’ th’ pubs, wi’ th’ fellows,’ said Harry.
“这真糟糕！”范妮说。 ‘A nice thing!’ said Fanny.
哈里朝门外瞥了瞥。他只想快点离开这儿。 Harry glanced towards the door.He wanted to get out.
“让人太难受了，真的！”牧师缓缓地摇摇头。 ‘Most distressing, indeed!’The minister slowly shook his head.
“今天晚上怎么办，恩德比先生？”哈里低声问道，“您要我来吗？” ‘What about tonight, Mr. Enderby?’ asked Harry, in rather a small voice.‘Shall you want me?’
恩德比先生痛苦地抬起头，把一只手放到额头上。他茫然地打量了哈里好一会儿。这两个人之间有一种最细微的相似之处。 Mr. Enderby looked up painedly, and put his hand to his brow.He studied Harry for some time, vacantly.There was the faintest sort of a resemblance between the two men.
“来吧，”他回答，“是的，我想你得来。我想我们一定不要理会这事，尽量不要引起人们的议论。” ‘Yes,’ he said.‘Yes, I think.I think we must take no notice, and cause as little remark as possible.’
范妮犹豫了。随后她问哈里： Fanny hesitated.Then she said to Harry.
“但你会来吗？” ‘But will you come?’
他看着她。 He looked at her.
“嗯，我会来。”他说。 ‘Ay, I s’ll come,’ he said.
然后，他转向恩德比先生。 Then he turned to Mr. Enderby.
“好吧，再见，恩德比先生。”他说。 ‘Well, good-afternoon, Mr. Enderby, ’ he said.
“再见，哈里，再见。”牧师忧伤地回答。范妮跟着哈里走到门口，有很长一段时间，他们只是在黄昏中默默地走着。 ‘Good-afternoon, Harry, good-afternoon,’ replied the mournful minister.Fanny followed Harry to the door, and for some time they walked in silence through the late afternoon.
“那么，随便哪个有份，你就也有份吗？”范妮问。 ‘And it’s yours as much as anybody else’s?’ she said.
“嗯。”他简短地答道。 ‘Ay,’ he answered shortly.
接着，他们又一言不发地走了一段路，直到走到了哈里住的那条街的转角。范妮犹豫了。她是否应该继续往前走到她婶婶家？她该不该那样做？那样就意味着她将放弃这一切，永远地。哈里默默地站着。 And they went without another word, for the long mile or so, till they came to the corner of the street where Harry lived.Fanny hesitated.Should she go on to her aunt’s?Should she?It would mean leaving all this, for ever.Harry stood silent.
一种固执的脾气使她跟着他一起转弯走上通向他家的那条路。等他们走进起居室，全家人都在那儿：哈里的父母、姐姐吉尼、吉尼的丈夫和孩子们，以及哈里的两个兄弟。 Some obstinacy made her turn with him along the road to his own home.When they entered the house-place, the whole family was there, mother and father and Jinny, with Jinny’s husband and children and Harry’s two brothers.
“他们告诉我，你被人骂了。”古多尔太太严厉地说。 ‘You’ve been having yours ears warmed, they tell me,’ said Mrs. Goodall grimly.
“谁告诉你的？”哈里简短地问了一句。 ‘Who telled thee?’ asked Harry shortly.
“玛吉和卢克当时都在场。” ‘Maggie and Luke’s both been in.’
“你看上去还不错，是吧！”吉尼插嘴道。 ‘You look well, don’t you!’ said interfering Jinny.
哈里没有回答，走去把帽子挂了起来。 Harry went and hung his hat up, without replying.
“上楼来吧，把帽子摘了。”古多尔太太几乎是温和地对范妮说。要是范妮这时抛弃了她儿子，那才叫她懊恼呢。 ‘Come upstairs and take your hat off,’ said Mrs. Goodall to Fanny, almost kindly.It would have annoyed her very much if Fanny had dropped her son at this moment.
“那么，她怎么说呢？”父亲悄悄地问哈里，头朝范妮刚走过的楼梯方向点了点。 ‘What’s ’er say, then?’ asked the father secretly of Harry, jerking his head in the direction of the stairs whence Fanny had disappeared.
“还没说什么。”哈里说。 ‘Nowt yet,’ said Harry.
“就算她现在甩了你，你也活该。”吉尼说。“我敢打赌，你和安妮·尼克松这件事准没错。” ‘Serve you right if she chucks you now,’ said Jinny.‘I’ll bet it’s right about Annie Nixon an’ you.’
“你打赌的事太多了。”哈里说。 ‘Tha bets so much,’ said Harry.
“没错——可你也不能否认。”吉尼说。 ‘Yi—but you can’t deny it,’ said Jinny.
“我要是想否认，我就能否认。” ‘I can if I’ve a mind.’
他父亲诧异地看着他。 His father looked at him inquiringly.
“那要不是比尔·鲍尔，或特德·斯莱尼，或他们六七个人的，就也不是我的。”哈里对他父亲说。 ‘It’s no more mine than it is Bill Bower’s, or Ted Slaney’s, or six or seven on ’em,’ said Harry to his father.
父亲默默地点了点头。 And the father nodded silently.
“在法庭上，你是脱不了干系的。”吉尼说。 ‘That’ll not get you out of it, in court,’ said Jinny.
楼上，范妮回避了他母亲所做的种种试探，没有表明她的意思。在古多尔太太愤愤不平的注视下，为了冷静下来，她梳了梳头，洗了洗手，然后在脸上抹了一层极淡的粉。这似乎在宣布独立。然而，老太太什么也没说。 Upstairs Fanny evaded all the thrusts made by his mother, and did not declare her hand.She tidied her hair, washed her hands, and put the tiniest bit of powder on her face, for coolness, there in front of Mrs. Goodall’s indignant gaze.It was like a declaration of independence.But the old woman said nothing.
她们下来参加星期日茶点会，茶点除了果子馅饼和蛋糕外，还有沙丁鱼、罐头鲑鱼和罐头桃子。大家闲聊起来。话题是尼克松一家和这起丑闻。 They came down to Sunday tea, with sardines and tinned salmon and tinned peaches, besides tarts and cakes.The chatter was general.It concerned the Nixon family and the scandal.
“她是个满嘴脏话的女人。”吉尼这样形容尼克松太太。“她也照样能大谈上帝的神圣教堂，她就这么干过。这可是自打她不信仰宗教以来，第一次踏进教堂。她是个恶魔，向来就是这么个角色。妈妈，您还记得我们住在大楼那儿的时候，她是怎样对待鲍勃的孩子们的吗？我记得我还是个小姑娘时，她常在寒冷的院子里给她们洗澡，免得她们溅湿屋子。要是她们在地板上弄个印迹，她就会把她们打个半死，还有她那些骂她们的话！我还记得，有个星期六，鲍勃的亲生女儿加里逃跑了，当时她继母正要给她洗澡，她就光着身子跑了，你还记得吗，妈妈？她躲到了斯梅德利的巷子里——那时正是刈草的季节——谁也找不到她。她在那里藏了一个晚上，是不是，妈妈？谁也找不到她。哎呀，这事闹得沸沸扬扬的。他们星期天上午才找到她——” ‘Oh, she’s a foul-mouthed woman,’ said Jinny of Mrs. Nixon.‘She may well talk about God’s holy house, she had.It’s first time she’s set foot in it, ever since she dropped off from being converted.She’s a devil and she always was one.Can’t you remember how she treated Bob’s children, mother, when we lived down in the Buildings?I can remember when I was a little girl she used to bathe them in the yard, in the cold, so that they shouldn’t splash the house.She’d half kill them if they made a mark on the floor, and the language she’d use!And one Saturday I can remember Garry, that was Bob’s own girl, she ran off when her stepmother was going to bathe her—ran off without a rag of clothes on—can you remember, mother?And she hid in Smedley’s closes—it was the time of mowing-grass—and nobody could find her.She hid out there all night, didn’t she, mother?Nobody could find her.My word, there was a talk.They found her on Sunday morning—’
“弗雷德·库茨警告过她，她要是再敢碰这些孩子们，就把她身上的骨头一根根打断。”父亲插了一句。 ‘Fred Coutts threatened to break every bone in the woman’s body, if she touched the children again,’ put in the father.
“不管怎样，他们还是吓到她了。”吉尼说。“但是，她对自己的两个孩子几乎也同样恶毒。而且谁都看得出，她一直逼迫着老鲍勃，最终把他变得软绵绵的。” ‘Anyhow, they frightened her,’ said Jinny.‘But she was nearly as bad with her own two.And anybody can see that she’s driven old Bob till he’s gone soft.’
“嗯，跟烂糊一样软弱。”杰克·古多尔说。“要不是弟兄们补贴他，不要说一星期，他甚至连一天的工资也挣不到。” ‘Ah, soft as mush,’ said Jack Goodall.‘’E’d never addle a week’s wage, nor yet a day’s if th’ chaps didn’t make it up to him.’
“哎呀，他要是不把一星期的工资交给她，她可会拧下他的脑袋。”吉尼说。 ‘My word, if he didn’t bring her a week’s wage, she’d pull his head off,’ said Jinny.
“不过，她除了那张臭嘴外，倒也是个清白女人，而且还很正派。”古多尔太太说。“她像条牛头狗那样，不爱跟人家交往。从不让任何人靠近她家房子，从不和任何人来往。” ‘But a clean woman, and respectable, except for her foul mouth,’ said Mrs. Goodall.‘Keeps to herself like a bull-dog.Never lets anybody come near the house, and neighbours with nobody.’
“很难弄明白她。”古多尔先生说，他是个沉默寡言、喜欢遮遮掩掩的人。 ‘Wanted it thrashed out of her,’ said Mr. Goodall, a silent, evasive sort of man.
“鲍勃喝酒的钱是从哪儿来的，这倒是个迷。”吉尼说。 ‘Where Bob gets the money for his drink from is a mystery,’ said Jinny.
“弟兄们请他喝的。”哈里说。 ‘Chaps treats him,’ said Harry.
“唔，他生来就有一双你从没见过的、容易受惊的兔子眼。”吉尼说。 ‘Well, he’s got the pair of frightenedest rabbit-eyes you’d wish to see,’ said Jinny.
“唉，我想，还带着一种酒鬼的杀人眼神。”古多尔太太说。 ‘Ay, with a drunken man’s murder in them, I think,’ said Mrs. Goodall.
喝完茶，大家继续聊着，直到又要出发去小教堂了。 So the talk went on after tea, till it was practically time to start off to chapel again.
“你得去准备一下了，范妮。”古多尔太太说。 ‘You’ll have to be getting ready, Fanny,’ said Mrs. Goodall.
“我今晚不去了。”范妮突然说。家里人一下子全愣住了。“妈妈，我今晚留下来陪你。”她加了一句。 ‘I’m not going tonight,’ said Fanny abruptly.And there was a sudden halt in the family.‘I’ll stop with you tonight, Mother,’ she added.
“太好了，我的孩子。”古多尔太太说道，既满意又安心。 ‘Best you had, my gel,’ said Mrs. Goodall, flattered and assured.
