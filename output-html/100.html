女孩与习惯 The Girl and the Habit
作者：欧·亨利 by O. Henry
习惯——由于惯例或频繁重复而形成的一种自然倾向。 Habit—a tendency or aptitude acquired by custom or frequent repetition.
除了一个灵感的源泉之外，批评家们把灵感的其他所有源泉都批评个遍。我们不得不从那个源泉去找说教的题材。当我们从古代作家那里汲取灵感时，他们洋洋自得地找出我们作品中与别人类似的地方。当我们努力反映现实生活时，他们又批评我们试图模仿亨利·乔治、乔治·华盛顿、华盛顿·欧文和欧文·巴切勒。当我们写东部和西部时，他们又指责我们模仿杰西·詹姆斯和亨利·詹姆斯。我们写自己的心里话——他们却说我们大概得了肝病。我们引用《马太福音》或者——呃——对了，《申命记》里的话，但是我们的想法还没有形成，想法就被牧师连敲带打地给吓跑了。因此，我们被逼得走投无路，要找题材，只好求助于那部古老可靠的、道貌岸然的、不容置疑的参考书——详解字典。 The critics have assailed every source of inspiration save one.To that one we are driven for our moral theme.When we levied upon the masters of old they gleefully dug up the parallels to our columns.When we strove to set forth real life they reproached us for trying to imitate Henry George, George Washington, Washington Irving, and Irving Bacheller.We wrote of the West and the East, and they accused us of both Jesse and Henry James.We wrote from our heart—and they said something about a disordered liver.We took a text from Matthew or—er—yes, Deuteronomy, but the preachers were hammering away at the inspiration idea before we could get into type.So, driven to the wall, we go for our subject-matter to the reliable, old, moral, unassailable vade mecum—the unabridged dictionary.
梅里亚姆小姐是欣克尔的出纳员。欣克尔是市中心的一个大饭店。它就位于报纸上所说的“金融区”。每天从十二点到两点，欣克尔里都挤满了饥肠辘辘的顾客——信差、速记员、经纪人、矿山股票持有人、推销员、尚未获得专利权的发明人，以及有钱人。 Miss Merriam was cashier at Hinkle’s.Hinkle’s is one of the big downtown restaurants.It is in what the papers call the “financial district.”Each day from 12 o’clock to 2 Hinkle’s was full of hungry customers—messenger boys, stenographers, brokers, owners of mining stock, promoters, inventors with patents pending—and also people with money.
欣克尔的出纳并不是份闲职。欣克尔为很多顾客提供鸡蛋、吐司和煎饼以及咖啡；并且为更多的顾客供应午饭（这个名称同“大餐”差不多）。可以这样说，在欣克尔那里吃早餐的人好比小分队，而吃午饭的人就是大部队。 The cashiership at Hinkle’s was no sinecure.Hinkle egged and toasted and griddle-caked and coffeed a good many customers; and he lunched (as good a word as “dined”) many more.It might be said that Hinkle’s breakfast crowd was a contingent, but his luncheon patronage amounted to a horde.
梅里亚姆小姐坐在桌旁的凳子上，桌子的三面被一道高高的、结实的铜丝网围着。铜丝网底部有一个拱形洞，当你把钱和侍者给的账单从这个洞里递过去时，你的心也会跟着怦怦直跳。 Miss Merriam sat on a stool at a desk inclosed on three sides by a strong, high fencing of woven brass wire.Through an arched opening at the bottom you thrust your waiter’s check and the money, while your heart went pit-a-pat.
因为梅里亚姆小姐又漂亮又能干。她能从一张两美元的钞票中收你四十五美分，把零钱找给你，同时拒绝你的求婚——下一位！——你的机会没了——请不要挤。她能够冷静地收你的钱，找给你正确的零钱，赢得你的心，指点放牙签的位置，对你的身价作出正确的评估，如果说布拉德斯特里特的评估上下相差一千元的话，她的评估不会相差二厘五毫，她做这一切所用的时间，比你用欣克尔的五味瓶往鸡蛋上撒胡椒的时间还少。 For Miss Merriam was lovely and capable.She could take 45 cents out of a $2 bill and refuse an offer of marriage before you could—Next!—lost your chance—please don’t shove.She could keep cool and collected while she collected your check, give you the correct change, win your heart, indicate the toothpick stand, and rate you to a quarter of a cent better than Bradstreet could to a thousand in less time than it takes to pepper an egg with one of Hinkle’s casters.
有一句古老而庄严的俚语提到“觊觎宝座的炯炯目光”。投射到这个年轻女出纳员座位上的也是炯炯目光。这个比喻是别人说的。 There is an old and dignified allusion to the “fierce light that beats upon a throne.”The light that beats upon the young lady cashier’s cage is also something fierce.The other fellow is responsible for the slang.
欣克尔的每位男性顾客，从电报局的小厮到场外的经纪人，都爱慕梅里亚姆小姐。当他们付账的时候，他们都会使尽丘比特的一切计谋去追求她。从铜丝网里投去的有微笑、眼色、赞美、深情的誓言、共进晚餐的邀请、叹息、憔悴的容貌、善意的逗弄，这一切都遭到聪明的梅里亚姆小姐与之相对的愉快的调侃。 Every male patron of Hinkle’s, from the A. D. T. boys up to the curbstone brokers, adored Miss Merriam.When they paid their checks they wooed her with every wile known to Cupid’s art.Between the meshes of the brass railing went smiles, winks, compliments, tender vows, invitations to dinner, sighs, languishing looks and merry banter that was wafted pointedly back by the gifted Miss Merriam.
没有比年轻女出纳员更有优势的职位了。她坐在那里，很容易成为商业之宫的女王；她是金钱和问候的女公爵，是银元和赞美的女伯爵，是爱情和午餐的头牌演员。你从她那里得到一个微笑，即使零钱里有一枚一角的加拿大硬币，你也会毫无怨言地走开。你回味着她扔给你的那一两句愉快的话，就像吝啬鬼在数他们的金银财宝；你用五美元付账，零钱数也不数就往口袋里一揣。也许那道难以逾越的网增加了她的魅力——无论如何，她是一个穿着衬衫的天使，完美、整洁、有魅力、眼睛明亮、成熟、机警——她集普绪喀、喀耳刻、阿忒于一体，既让你心神不宁，又叫你同钞票分家。 There is no coign of vantage more effective than the position of young lady cashier.She sits there, easily queen of the court of commerce; she is duchess of dollars and devoirs, countess of compliments and coin, leading lady of love and luncheon.You take from her a smile and a Canadian dime, and you go your way uncomplaining.You count the cheery word or two that she tosses you as misers count their treasures; and you pocket the change for a five uncomputed.Perhaps the brass-bound inaccessibility multiplies her charms—anyhow, she is a shirt-waisted angel, immaculate, trim, manicured, seductive, bright-eyed, ready, alert—Psyche, Circe, and Ate in one, separating you from your circulating medium after your sirloin medium.
在欣克尔就餐的年轻男士付钱的时候都会跟出纳员开玩笑或直接表达爱慕之情。很多人会更直接，他们丢下戏票和巧克力来暗示深情的誓言。中年男子坦率地谈论着香橙花，通常用凋谢的花瓣来暗示哈勒姆旅馆。一个已经被警察压榨干的经纪人，经常向梅里亚姆小姐求婚，这比他吃饭还有规律。 The young men who broke bread at Hinkle’s never settled with the cashier without an exchange of badinage and open compliment.Many of them went to greater lengths and dropped promissory hints of theatre tickets and chocolates.The older men spoke plainly of orange blossoms, generally withering the tentative petals by after-allusions to Harlem flats.One broker, who had been squeezed by copper proposed to Miss Merriam more regularly than he ate.
在忙碌的午饭时刻，梅里亚姆小姐边收钱，边说话，大致说的是： During a brisk luncheon hour Miss Merriam’s conversation, while she took money for checks, would run something like this:
“你好，哈斯金斯先生——先生？——天生的，谢谢您——别这么冒失……你好，约翰尼——十美分，十五美分，二十美分——赶紧走吧，不然他们要炒你鱿鱼了……不好意思——请再数一遍——哦，没关系……歌舞剧吗？——谢谢；我不看那个——星期三晚上我和西蒙斯先生去看《赫达·加布勒》里的卡特……不好意思，我以为那是二十五美分的硬币呢……二十五美分加七十五美分不是一美元吗——你还是喜欢火腿加白菜。我知道了，比利……你在跟谁说话？——哦——您要的东西马上就到…… “Good morning, Mr. Haskins—sir?—it’s natural, thank you—don’t be quite so fresh...Hello, Johnny—ten, fifteen, twenty—chase along now or they’ll take the letters off your cap...Beg pardon—count it again, please—Oh, don’t mention it...Vaudeville?—thanks; not on your moving picture—I was to see Carter in Hedda Gabler on Wednesday night with Mr. Simmons...’Scuse me, I thought that was a quarter...Twenty-five and seventy-five’s a dollar—got that ham-and-cabbage habit yet.I see, Billy...Who are you addressing?—say—you’ll get all that’s coming to you in a minute...
哦，胡说！巴希特先生——你老是骗人——不是吗？好吧，也许有一天我会嫁给你——三美元，四美元六十五美分，你给的是五美元……请你把这些话留着自己听吧……十美分？——不好意思，账单上写的是七十美分——好吧，也许这不是七而是一……哦，你喜欢这个发型吗，桑德斯先生？——有的人喜欢往上拢，不过他们说长得秀气的人梳这种发型好看……十枚是五十美分……走吧，伙计；别把这儿当作康奈游乐场的售票处……呃？——在梅西百货公司——合身吗？哦，不，并不太凉快——这一季流行薄料子……欢迎下次光临——你已经是第三次啦——什么？——忘了吧——我认识那个……六十五美分？——威尔逊先生，你一定涨工资了……德弗雷斯特先生，星期二下午我在第六大道看见你的——什么？——哦，天哪！——她是谁？……怎么了？——为什么，这钱根本不能用——什么？——哥伦比亚半岛？——好吧，这又不是南美洲……是的，我最喜欢什锦的——星期五？——太抱歉了，我星期五有柔道课——那么，星期四……谢谢——这已经是我今天早上第十六遍听到了——我猜我一定很漂亮……请别说那种话——你认为我是谁？……为什么，韦斯特布鲁克先生——你真这么想？——这个主意！——一美元——八十美分加二十美分总共一美元——还是非常感谢，但是我从来没有坐过男人的汽车——你的姑妈？——好吧，那是不同的——也许……请你别胡来——我想你的账单是十五美分——请靠边走，让别人……你好，本——星期四晚上再来？——有位先生要送一盒巧克力来……四十美分加六十美分不是一美元吗，再加一美元就是两美元……” Oh, fudge!Mr. Bassett—you’re always fooling—no—?Well, maybe I’ll marry you some day—three, four and sixty-five is five...Kindly keep them remarks to yourself, if you please...Ten cents?—’scuse me; the check calls for seventy—well, maybe it is a one instead of a seven...Oh, do you like it that way, Mr. Saunders?—some prefer a pomp; but they say this Cleo de Merody does suit refined features...and ten is fifty...Hike along there, buddy; don’t take this for a Coney Island ticket booth...Huh?—why, Macy’s—don’t it fit nice?Oh, no, it isn’t too cool—these light-weight fabrics is all the go this season...Come again, please—that’s the third time you’ve tried to—what?—forget it—that lead quarter is an old friend of mine...Sixty-five?—must have had your salary raised, Mr. Wilson...I seen you on Sixth Avenue Tuesday afternoon, Mr. De Forest—swell?—oh, my!—who is she?...What’s the matter with it?—why, it ain’t money—what?—Columbian half?—well, this ain’t South America...Yes, I like the mixed best—Friday?—awfully sorry, but I take my jiu-jitsu lesson on Friday—Thursday, then...Thanks—that’s sixteen times I’ve been told that this morning—I guess I must be beautiful...Cut that out, please—who do you think I am?...Why, Mr. Westbrook—do you really think so?—the idea!—one—eighty and twenty’s a dollar—thank you ever so much, but I don’t ever go automobile riding with gentlemen—your aunt?—well, that’s different—perhaps...Please don’t get fresh—your check was fifteen cents, I believe—kindly step aside and let...Hello, Ben—coming around Thursday evening?—there’s a gentleman going to send around a box of chocolates, and...forty and sixty is a dollar, and one is two...”
一天下午，眩晕女神——她的另一个名字是幸运女神——突然将一位年老、富有而又古怪的银行家打倒在地，当时他走过欣克尔饭馆的门口，正要去搭电车。搭电车的富有而又古怪的银行家——请让开：还有别人呢。 About the middle of one afternoon the dizzy goddess Vertigo—whose other name is Fortune—suddenly smote an old, wealthy and eccentric banker while he was walking past Hinkle’s, on his way to a street car.A wealthy and eccentric banker who rides in street cars is—move up, please; there are others.
当时一个撒玛利亚人，一个法利赛人，一个男人还有一个警察，第一时间把银行家麦克拉姆齐抬进了欣克尔饭馆。当这个年老但打不跨的银行家睁开眼的时候，他看到了一个美丽的女孩俯在他面前，面带怜惜而温柔的微笑，正用牛肉茶给他敷额头，用盛在暖锅里的冰冷的东西为他擦手。麦克拉姆齐先生叹了口气，震掉了马甲上的一颗纽扣，带着感恩的心凝视着他的救命女恩人，然后渐渐恢复了意识。 A Samaritan, a Pharisee, a man and a policeman who were first on the spot lifted Banker McRamsey and carried him into Hinkle’s restaurant.When the aged but indestructible banker opened his eyes he saw a beautiful vision bending over him with a pitiful, tender smile, bathing his forehead with beef tea and chafing his hands with something loria out of a chafing-dish.Mr. McRamsey sighed, lost a vest button, gazed with deep gratitude upon his fair preserveress, and then recovered consciousness.
期待看到浪漫故事的人都到海滨图书馆去吧！银行家麦克拉姆齐先生有位上了年纪、受人尊敬的夫人，他对梅里亚姆小姐的感情是父亲般的。他很感兴趣地和她谈了半个小时——跟他作生意时的谈话是不一样的。第二天，他带了麦克拉姆齐夫人一起来看她。这对老夫妇孩子很少——只有一个已经结婚的女儿，住在布鲁克林。 To the Seaside Library all who are anticipating a romance!Banker McRamsey had an aged and respected wife, and his sentiments toward Miss Merriam were fatherly.He talked to her for half an hour with interest—not the kind that went with his talks during business hours.The next day he brought Mrs. McRamsey down to see her.The old couple were childless—they had only a married daughter living in Brooklyn.
我们不如把这个短篇小说写得更短点儿，这位美丽的出纳员赢得了这对善良老夫妇的心。他们一次又一次地来欣克尔；他们邀请她去他们东区第七十几街的老式但是依旧华丽的家里作客。梅里亚姆小姐招人喜爱，她的直率、热情洋溢，如风暴一般向他们袭来。他们无数次地说梅里亚姆小姐多么像不在他们身边的女儿。已经出嫁的、住在布鲁克林的女儿，身段像菩萨似的，面容则是艺术摄影师所期望的。梅里亚姆小姐则是曲线、微笑、玫瑰叶、珍珠、绸缎和生发油广告的混合体。多么糊涂的父母啊。 To make a short story shorter, the beautiful cashier won the hearts of the good old couple.They came to Hinkle’s again and again; they invited her to their old-fashioned but splendid home in one of the East Seventies.Miss Merriam’s winning loveliness, her sweet frankness and impulsive heart took them by storm.They said a hundred times that Miss Merriam reminded them so much of their lost daughter.The Brooklyn matron, née Ramsey, had the figure of Buddha and a face like the ideal of an art photographer.Miss Merriam was a combination of curves, smiles, rose leaves, pearls, satin and hair-tonic posters.Enough of the fatuity of parents.
在这对富有的夫妇和梅里亚姆小姐认识一个月后的一天下午，她来到欣克尔面前，辞去了出纳员的工作。 A month after the worthy couple became acquainted with Miss Merriam, she stood before Hinkle one afternoon and resigned her cashiership.
“他们打算收养我，”她跟剥削她的饭店老板说，“这对老夫妻很有趣，但非常可爱。他们家里才讲究呢！喂，欣克尔，多说也没有用——我要么穿着棕色的衣服，戴着护目镜，坐在飞快的汽车里，要么至少嫁给一个公爵。不过，我仍然有点儿舍不得离开这里。我已经做了这么久的出纳，做别的事总有点儿不习惯。我会十分怀念顾客们排队付账时同我打趣的情景。但是我不会错失这个机会的。欣克尔，他们真的很好；我知道我要有好日子过了。你还欠我九元二十六分和半天的工资。如果你不乐意，就把半天的工资扣掉吧，欣克尔。” “They’re going to adopt me,” she told the bereft restaurateur.“They’re funny old people, but regular dears.And the swell home they have got!Say, Hinkle, there isn’t any use of talking—I’m on the à la carte to wear brown duds and goggles in a whiz wagon, or marry a duke at least.Still, I somehow hate to break out of the old cage.I’ve been cashiering so long I feel funny doing anything else.I’ll miss joshing the fellows awfully when they line up to pay for the buckwheats and.But I can’t let this chance slide.And they’re awfully good, Hinkle; I know I’ll have a swell time.You owe me nine-sixty-two and a half for the week.Cut out the half if it hurts you, Hinkle.”
他们收养她了。梅里亚姆小姐成了罗莎·麦克拉姆齐小姐。而且她很适应这个转变。美丽只是皮相，不过神经离皮肤非常近。神经——但是到了这里，你是不是急切地想回过头去仔细看看故事开头的引文呢？ And they did.Miss Merriam became Miss Rosa McRamsey.And she graced the transition.Beauty is only skin-deep, but the nerves lie very near to the skin.Nerve—but just here will you oblige by perusing again the quotation with which this story begins?
麦克拉姆齐夫妇就像斟国产香槟酒一样毫不吝啬地花钱培养他们收养的女儿。服装商、舞蹈教师、家庭教师赚了这些钱。梅——呃——麦克拉姆齐小姐很领情、很孝顺，尽量忘却欣克尔饭馆。美国姑娘的适应性是值得赞许的，在极大部分时间里，欣克尔饭馆也确实从她的记忆和言语中消失了。 The McRamseys poured out money like domestic champagne to polish their adopted one.Milliners, dancing masters and private tutors got it.Miss—er—McRamsey was grateful, loving, and tried to forget Hinkle’s.To give ample credit to the adaptability of the American girl, Hinkle’s did fade from her memory and speech most of the time.
不是每个人都记得海特斯伯里伯爵到美国东区第七十几街来到新闻。他只是个不大不小的伯爵，没有欠款，也没有什么惊人的举动。但是你一定会记得慈善妇女会在沃尔多夫·阿斯托利亚饭店举行义卖的那个晚上。因为你在那里，还用饭店的信笺写了个便条给范妮，为的是让她看看——你没有？好吧；那个晚上孩子生病了，一定是。 Not every one will remember when the Earl of Hitesbury came to East Seventy—Street, America.He was only a fair-to-medium earl, without debts, and he created little excitement.But you will surely remember the evening when the Daughters of Benevolence held their bazaar in the W—f-A—a Hotel.For you were there, and you wrote a note to Fannie on the hotel paper, and mailed it, just to show her that—you did not?Very well; that was the evening the baby was sick, of course.
在义卖会上，麦克拉姆齐一家很引人注目。梅——呃——麦克拉姆齐小姐优雅美丽。海特斯伯里伯爵自从来美国观光的时候起就深深地被她吸引了。在慈善义卖会上，他们的事情可以变得明朗化了。伯爵和公爵差不多。或者更好。他的地位也许低一点儿，但是他外在的欠债也更少。 At the bazaar the McRamseys were prominent.Miss Mer—er—McRamsey was exquisitely beautiful.The Earl of Hitesbury had been very attentive to her since he dropped in to have a look at America.At the charity bazaar the affair was supposed to be going to be pulled off to a finish.An earl is as good as a duke.Better.His standing may be lower, but his outstanding accounts are also lower.
我们曾经的年轻女出纳员被指派去了一个货摊。她的任务是以惊人的高价把毫不值钱的小玩意儿卖给上流人士和自以为是的人。义卖的善款将用来给贫民窟的孩子们吃一顿丰盛的圣诞晚餐——喂！那么你有没有想过他们另外的三百六十四天该怎么过？ Our ex-young-lady-cashier was assigned to a booth.She was expected to sell worthless articles to nobs and snobs at exorbitant prices.The proceeds of the bazaar were to be used for giving the poor children of the slums a Christmas din—Say! Did you ever wonder where they get the other 364?
麦克拉姆齐小姐——美丽动人、兴奋紧张、容光焕发——在她的小货摊里忙碌着。一个仿制的有着拱形洞的铜丝网像栅栏一样围在她面前。 Miss McRamsey—beautiful, palpitating, excited, charming, radiant—fluttered about in her booth.An imitation brass network, with a little arched opening, fenced her in.
一会儿伯爵来了，自信、优雅、大方，带着爱慕——非常爱慕的神情来到窗口。 Along came the Earl, assured, delicate, accurate, admiring—admiring greatly, and faced the open wicket.
“你是如此迷人，你知道——你确实可爱——亲爱的。”他陶醉地说。 “You look chawming, you know—’pon my word you do—my deah,” he said, beguilingly.
麦克拉姆齐小姐突然转过身来。 Miss McRamsey whirled around.
“别戏弄我，”她冷静尖刻地说，“你知道你在跟谁说话吗？你的账单。哦，上帝啊！——” “Cut that joshing out,” she said, coolly and briskly.“Who do you think you are talking to?Your check, please.Oh, Lordy!—”
义卖市场上的主顾们一阵混乱，纷纷向一个摊位挤去。海特斯伯里伯爵站在旁边，困惑地捋着他那浅黄色的小胡子。 Patrons of the bazaar became aware of a commotion and pressed around a certain booth.The Earl of Hitesbury stood near by pulling a pale blond and puzzled whisker.
“麦克拉姆齐小姐晕倒了。”有人解释说。 “Miss McRamsey has fainted,” some one explained.
