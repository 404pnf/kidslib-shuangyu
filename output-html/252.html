第一章 CHAPTER I
一辆小型的四号蒸汽机车，铿锵作响地从塞尔斯顿摇摇晃晃地驶来——七节货车的车厢塞得满满的。它咆哮着出现在拐角处，似乎有着惊人的速度，但荆豆丛中一匹受到它惊吓的小马驹一阵慢跑就将它远远地抛在身后，荆豆在寒冷的午后仍微微地摇曳着。一个女人沿着通往安德伍德方向的铁路线走着，她退回到树篱丛中，将篮子挽在一边，看着火车踏板向前行进。她被困在震颤的黑色车厢与树篱间，显得很不起眼。敞篷货车重重地撞击着铁轨，一节一节地缓缓驶过。接着，货车便转弯朝着矮树林驶去。那儿栎树的枯叶无声洒落，鸟儿收回搁在轨道旁猩红色的尾巴，匆匆逃进已悄然笼罩灌木林的暮色中。野外，发动机的烟尘不断飘落，粘附在杂草上。田野阴沉而荒凉，在那使人产生奇思怪想的沼泽地带、芦苇丛生的坑塘里，家禽已经不在桤木林中奔忙，而是栖息在涂了焦油的窝棚中。矿坑口隐隐出现在坑塘远处，在午后慵懒的光线中，阳光如红疮般舔舐着覆满灰尘的井沿。就在远处，坐落着布林斯利煤矿那尖塔状的烟囱和笨重黝黑的轮轴头架。两只轮子向着天空飞速旋转，卷扬机发出阵阵响声。矿工们被卷了出来。 The small locomotive engine, Number 4, came clanking, stumbling down from Selston—with seven full waggons.It appeared round the corner with loud threats of speed, but the colt that it startled from among the gorse, which still flickered indistinctly in the raw afternoon, outdistanced it at a canter.A woman, walking up the railway line to Underwood, drew back into the hedge, held her basket aside, and watched the footplate of the engine advancing.The trucks thumped heavily past, one by one, with slow inevitable movement, as she stood insignificantly trapped between the jolting black waggons and the hedge; then they curved away towards the coppice where the withered oak leaves dropped noiselessly, while the birds, pulling at the scarlet hips beside the track, made off into the dusk that had already crept into the spinney.In the open, the smoke from the engine sank and cleaved to the rough grass.The fields were dreary and forsaken, and in the marshy strip that led to the whimsey, a reedy pit-pond, the fowls had already abandoned their run among the alders, to roost in the tarred fowl-house.The pit-bank loomed up beyond the pond, flames like red sores licking its ashy sides, in the afternoon’s stagnant light.Just beyond rose the tapering chimneys and the clumsy black head-stocks of Brinsley Colliery.The two wheels were spinning fast up against the sky, and the winding-engine rapped out its little spasms.The miners were being turned up.
火车一边鸣笛，一边开进了煤矿旁铁路线上宽敞的站台，那里停靠着一排一排的货车。 The engine whistled as it came into the wide bay of railway lines beside the colliery, where rows of trucks stood in harbour.
工人们或独行，或随后跟着，或三五成群，如幽灵般四散回家去了。在平行排列的铁轨边坐落着一幢小屋，位于煤渣小路向下的三个台阶处。一大根枯瘦的葡萄藤紧紧抓附在房屋上，似乎要撕掉这用瓦铺成的屋顶。院子是用砖头围起来的，四周种着一些冷冷的报春花。更远处，长长的花园沿坡而下，一直延伸到一段覆着灌木丛的小河道。那儿有一些小枝繁茂的苹果树、洋李子树和东倒西歪的卷心菜。小路边缀满散乱的粉红色菊花，如粉红色的衣服挂在灌木丛上。在花园的半道上，一个女人弯腰从盖着毛毡的鸡窝里走了出来。她关上门，扣上锁，掸掉她白色围裙上的一些小碎物，接着直起了身子。 Miners, single, trailing and in groups, passed like shadows diverging home.At the edge of the ribbed level of sidings squat a low cottage, three steps down from the cinder track.A large bony vine clutched at the house, as if to claw down the tiled roof.Round the bricked yard grew a few wintry primroses.Beyond, the long garden sloped down to a bush-covered brook course.There were some twiggy apple trees, winter-crack trees, and ragged cabbages.Beside the path hung dishevelled pink chrysanthemums, like pink cloths hung on bushes.A woman came stooping out of the felt-covered fowl-house, half-way down the garden.She closed and padlocked the door, then drew herself erect, having brushed some bits from her white apron.
她是一个神态高傲的高挑女人，相貌俊秀，眉毛十分浓黑。她顺滑的黑发被从中间恰到好处地分开。有好一阵子，她一动不动地站着，看着矿工们沿着铁路走来，然后她转身朝小河道走去。她的脸平静而坚毅，她紧闭的嘴透出幡然醒悟的气息。过了一阵子，她喊道：“约翰！” She was a tall woman of imperious mien, handsome, with definite black eyebrows.Her smooth black hair was parted exactly.For a few moments she stood steadily watching the miners as they passed along the railway: then she turned towards the brook course.Her face was calm and set, her mouth was closed with disillusionment.After a moment she called: “John!”
没有人答应。她等了一会儿，然后清楚地喊道：“你在哪里？” There was no answer.She waited, and then said distinctly: “Where are you?”
“在这里！”灌木丛中，一个小孩愠怒地回答道。 “Here!” replied a child’s sulky voice from among the bushes.
女人透过暮色望过去。 The woman looked piercingly through the dusk.
“你在小溪边吗？”她严厉地问道。 “Are you at that brook?” she asked sternly.
听到后，小孩从像鞭子一样向上生长的覆盆子灌木丛里钻了出来。这是一个五岁的结实的小男孩。他挑衅般地站在那儿，一动不动。 For answer the child showed himself before the raspberry-canes that rose like whips.He was a small, sturdy boy of five.He stood quite still, defiantly.
“噢！”母亲略感宽慰地说道： “Oh!” said the mother, conciliated.
“我以为你在下面那条湿漉漉的小溪边呢——你还记得我跟你说过什么吧——” “I thought you were down at that wet brook—and you remember what I told you—”
男孩没动，也没有回答。 The boy did not move or answer.
“来，回家。”女人的声音更加温柔，“天快黑了。你外公的火车正沿着铁路驶下来呢！” “Come, come on in,” she said more gently, “it’s getting dark.There’s your grandfather’s engine coming down the line!”
小男孩慢悠悠地向前走着，心怀不满，一言不发。他穿着裤子和马甲，对于这个年纪的孩子来说，布料过于厚重和粗糙。它们显然是从成年男子的衣服上裁下来的。 The lad advanced slowly, with resentful, taciturn movement.He was dressed in trousers and waistcoat of cloth that was too thick and hard for the size of the garments.They were evidently cut down from a man’s clothes.
当他们慢慢走向房子的时候，他扯下一束束散碎的菊花，然后把花瓣一把把地丢在路上。 As they went slowly towards the house he tore at the ragged wisps of chrysanthemums and dropped the petals in handfuls along the path.
“不要那样做——看着真讨厌。”母亲说道。孩子不再摘花了，但女人却突然怜惜地折下一枝细枝，上面有三四朵了无生气的花朵，然后把它们贴在自己的脸上。当母亲和儿子来到院子里时，她的手停在半空中犹豫了一下，她没有把花扔在一旁，而是别在了围裙的系带上。母子俩站在三级台阶下，望着对面铁轨站台那些匆匆回家的矿工们。小火车的车轮迫近了。突然间，火车头从房子经过，赫然停在了院门对面。 “Don’t do that—it does look nasty,” said his mother.He refrained, and she, suddenly pitiful, broke off a twig with three or four wan flowers and held them against her face.When mother and son reached the yard her hand hesitated, and instead of laying the flower aside, she pushed it in her apron-band.The mother and son stood at the foot of the three steps looking across the bay of lines at the passing home of the miners.The trundle of the small train was imminent.Suddenly the engine loomed past the house and came to a stop opposite the gate.
火车司机——一个矮个子男人，满嘴留着灰白的络腮胡——从驾驶室探出身子，那驾驶室比女人高出许多。 The engine-driver, a short man with round grey beard, leaned out of the cab high above the woman.
“有茶吗？”他欢快而又亲切地说道。 “Have you got a cup of tea?” he said in a cheery, hearty fashion.
那是她父亲。她走了进去，说她可以泡一些来。她很快就回来了。 It was her father.She went in, saying she would mash.Directly, she returned.
“我星期天没来看你。”灰白络腮胡子的小个子男人开始说道。 “I didn’t come to see you on Sunday,” began the little grey-bearded man.
“我没指望你会来。”女儿说道。 “I didn’t expect you,” said his daughter.
火车司机皱了皱眉头，接着又恢复了他那快乐、轻松的神色，他说道： The engine-driver winced; then, reassuming his cheery, airy manner, he said:
“噢，你听到了吗？那么，你认为怎么样呢——？” “Oh, have you heard then?Well, and what do you think—?”
“我觉得这够快的了。”她答道。 “I think it is soon enough,” she replied.
听到她小小的责备，小个子男人做了个不耐烦的手势，然后哄骗加威胁地冰冷地说道： At her brief censure the little man made an impatient gesture, and said coaxingly, yet with dangerous coldness:
“好啦，一个男人该做些什么？对于我这样年纪的男人，像个陌生人一样坐在自己的壁炉边，这不能算像样的生活。如果我再婚，这看起来似乎又有点晚——这关其他人什么事？” “Well, what’s a man to do?It’s no sort of life for a man of my years, to sit at my own hearth like a stranger.And if I’m going to marry again it may as well be soon as late—what does it matter to anybody?”
女人没有回答，转身走进屋子。驾驶室的男人傲慢地站立着，直到她拿着一杯茶和一个上面放着黄油面包的盘子回来。她走上台阶，站在嘶嘶作响的火车的踏板旁。 The woman did not reply, but turned and went into the house.The man in the engine-cab stood assertive, till she returned with a cup of tea and a piece of bread and butter on a plate.She went up the steps and stood near the footplate of the hissing engine.
“你不必给我拿黄油面包。”她父亲说道。“但一杯茶，”他赞赏地啜了一口，“倒非常不错。”男人又抿了几口，然后说道：“我听说沃尔特又犯病了。” “You needn’t ’a’ brought me bread an’ butter,” said her father.“But a cup of tea”—he sipped appreciatively—“it’s very nice.”He sipped for a moment or two, then: “I hear as Walter’s got another bout on,” he said.
“他什么时候不犯病？”女人挖苦地说。 “When hasn’t he?” said the woman bitterly.
“我听说在去之前，他在“纳尔逊贵族酒馆”吹牛说他要花半个金币。” “I heared tell of him in the ‘Lord Nelson’ braggin’ as he was going to spend that before he went: half a sovereign that was.”
“什么时候？”女人问道。 “When?” asked the woman.
“一个周六晚上——我知道那是真的。” “A’ Sat’day night—I know that’s true.”
“很有可能。”她苦笑着。“他给了我23先令。” “Very likely,” she laughed bitterly.“He gives me twenty-three shillings.”
“唉，当一个男人花钱办不成事，却行同禽兽的时候，倒是件好事！”灰白胡须的男人说道。 “Aye, it’s a nice thing, when a man can do nothing with his money but make a beast of himself!” said the grey-whiskered man.
女人将头扭向一边。她父亲咽下最后一点茶，然后把杯子递给她。 The woman turned her head away.Her father swallowed the last of his tea and handed her the cup.
“唉。”他擦了擦嘴巴叹气道。“这也算是个法子，这是——” “Aye,” he sighed, wiping his mouth.“It’s a settler, it is—”
他把手放在操作杆上。发动机拉紧沉吟着，火车又轰隆隆地朝交叉口开去。女人再次朝对面的铁轨望去。夜色正笼罩着铁轨和货车上面的天空，一群阴沉而忧郁的矿工仍在往家走。卷扬机匆匆运转着，间或有短暂的停顿。伊丽莎白·贝茨看着那令人烦闷的人流，然后走进了屋子。她丈夫没有回来。 He put his hand on the lever.The little engine strained and groaned, and the train rumbled towards the crossing.The woman again looked across the metals.Darkness was settling over the spaces of the railway and trucks: the miners, in grey sombre groups, were still passing home.The winding-engine pulsed hurriedly, with brief pauses.Elizabeth Bates looked at the dreary flow of men, then she went indoors.Her husband did not come.
厨房很小，充满着炉火的光；煤块堆积在烟囱口，烧得红彤彤。屋内所有的活力好像就在那白色温暖的壁炉里，在映射着红色炉火的铁制壁炉挡板上。桌布已为晚茶铺就，杯子在暗影中闪着微光。屋子后面，最低的一级楼梯延伸到屋子里，男孩坐在那里，用刀使劲儿地鼓捣一块白木。他几乎隐没在了暗影里。已经四点半了。可是他们得等父亲回来才能开始喝茶。当母亲看着她的儿子闷闷不乐地摆弄木头时，她在他的沉默与执拗中看到了自己的影子，在他那只顾自己、漠不关心中看到了他父亲的影子。她的思绪似乎被她丈夫占据了。他很可能早就路过自己家，偷偷溜过家门口去其他地方喝酒，让家人空等他回来吃饭。她瞟了一眼钟，然后拿起马铃薯去院子里滤干。小溪那边的花园和田野笼罩在无尽的黑暗中。当她端着深平底锅起来，把热气腾腾的水倒入身后的黑夜里时，她看见铁路线和田野的空旷处那边，沿山而上的公路旁的黄灯已被点亮。 The kitchen was small and full of firelight; red coals piled glowing up the chimney mouth.All the life of the room seemed in the white, warm hearth and the steel fender reflecting the red fire.The cloth was laid for tea; cups glinted in the shadows.At the back, where the lowest stairs protruded into the room, the boy sat struggling with a knife and a piece of whitewood.He was almost hidden in the shadow.It was half-past four.They had but to await the father’s coming to begin tea.As the mother watched her son’s sullen little struggle with the wood, she saw herself in his silence and pertinacity; she saw the father in her child’s indifference to all but himself.She seemed to be occupied by her husband.He had probably gone past his home, slunk past his own door, to drink before he came in, while his dinner spoiled and wasted in waiting.She glanced at the clock, then took the potatoes to strain them in the yard.The garden and fields beyond the brook were closed in uncertain darkness.When she rose with the saucepan, leaving the drain steaming into the night behind her, she saw the yellow lamps were lit along the high road that went up the hill away beyond the space of the railway lines and the field.
然后，她又看了看那些成群结队回家的男人，现在人越来越少了。 Then again she watched the men trooping home, fewer now and fewer.
屋内炉火要熄灭了，房间映在一片暗红中。女人将深平底锅放在炉盘上，接着，把糊状的布丁放在炉嘴上。然后她坐下来，一动也不动。谢天谢地，门外传来匆忙而轻快的脚步声。有人在门闩上停顿了一下，然后一个小女孩儿走进来，开始脱去她那身出门的行头，用帽子拨开遮住她眼睛的一团金褐相间的卷发。 Indoors the fire was sinking and the room was dark red.The woman put her saucepan on the hob, and set a batter pudding near the mouth of the oven.Then she stood unmoving.Directly, gratefully, came quick young steps to the door.Someone hung on the latch a moment, then a little girl entered and began pulling off her outdoor things, dragging a mass of curls, just ripening from gold to brown, over her eyes with her hat.
她母亲斥责她从学校回来得太晚，说在这样阴暗的冬日里，她本该把她关在家里。 Her mother chid her for coming late from school, and said she would have to keep her at home the dark winter days.
“为什么呀，妈妈，天还没怎么黑呢。灯都还没有点，而且爸爸也还没回家啊。” “Why, mother, it’s hardly a bit dark yet.The lamp’s not lighted, and my father’s not home.”
“没，他还没回家。但现在都差一刻到5点了！你看见他的影子没？” “No, he isn’t.But it’s a quarter to five!Did you see anything of him?”
孩子变得严肃起来。她用一双惆怅的蓝色大眼睛看着她的母亲。 The child became serious.She looked at her mother with large, wistful blue eyes.
“没有，妈妈，我压根没看见过他。哎！他是不是上来后就直接走过家门去老布林斯利酒馆了？他不会的，妈妈，因为我没看过他在那里。” “No, mother, I’ve never seen him.Why?Has he come up an’ gone past, to Old Brinsley?He hasn’t, mother, ’cos I never saw him.”
“他会留神的。”母亲挖苦地说道，“他会小心不让你们看见他。但你可以肯定的是，他已经坐在‘威尔士王子酒馆’了。不然他不会这么晚的。” “He’d watch that,” said the mother bitterly, “he’d take care as you didn’t see him.But you may depend upon it, he’s seated in the ‘Prince o’ Wales’.He wouldn’t be this late.”
女孩儿可怜巴巴地看着她母亲。 The girl looked at her mother piteously.
“让我们喝茶吧，妈妈，可以吗？”女孩说道。 “Let’s have our teas, mother, should we?” said she.
母亲把约翰叫到桌前。她再次打开门，朝对面笼罩着铁路线的黑暗中望去。四处荒无人迹，她已听不到卷扬机的声音。 The mother called John to table.She opened the door once more and looked out across the darkness of the lines.All was deserted: she could not hear the winding-engines.
“或许，”她对自己说：“他得停下来处理些事情吧。” “Perhaps,” she said to herself, “he’s stopped to get some ripping done.”
他们坐下来用茶。约翰坐在桌子一头，靠在门边，身子几乎隐没在黑暗中。他们相互避开彼此的脸。女孩面向壁炉挡板蹲着，在火前慢慢地翻动着一块厚厚的面包。小家伙的脸在暗影上投下一个昏暗的轮廓，他坐在那里，看着姐姐在红彤彤的火光中变得美丽的身影。 They sat down to tea.John, at the end of the table near the door, was almost lost in the darkness.Their faces were hidden from each other.The girl crouched against the fender slowly moving a thick piece of bread before the fire.The lad, his face a dusky mark on the shadow, sat watching her who was transfigured in the red glow.
“我真的觉得火光很美。”女孩说道。 “I do think it’s beautiful to look in the fire,” said the child.
“你这么认为？”母亲说道，“为什么？” “Do you?” said the mother. “Why?”
“它那么红，到处有小窟窿——而且它让人感觉非常舒服，你能直接闻到它。” “It’s so red, and full of little caves—and it feels so nice, and you can fair smell it.”
“马上就需要加燃料了。”母亲答道：“如果你父亲回来了，他就会继续说当一个男人流着汗从矿坑回家时不需要炉火。——一个酒吧就总是足够温暖了。” “It’ll want mending directly,” replied her mother, “and then if your father comes he’ll carry on and say there never is a fire when a man comes home sweating from the pit.—A public-house is always warm enough.”
一阵沉默后，男孩抱怨道：“快点，安妮。” There was silence till the boy said complainingly: “Make haste, our Annie.”
“好啦，我正在弄！我不能让火烤得快些，对不？” “Well, I am doing!I can’t make the fire do it no faster, can I?”
“她不停地说废话，这样她就能慢慢弄了。”男孩抱怨着。 “She keeps wafflin’ it about so’s to make ’er slow,” grumbled the boy.
“不要想得那么坏，孩子。”母亲回应道。 “Don’t have such an evil imagination, child,” replied the mother.
不久，黑暗的屋子里忙活开了，传来嘎吱嘎吱的松脆响声。母亲吃得很少。她快速地喝完自己的茶，坐着想事情。当她站起身的时候，她那僵硬挺直的头明显露出了愤怒。她看着挡板里的布丁，爆发了： Soon the room was busy in the darkness with the crisp sound of crunching.The mother ate very little.She drank her tea determinedly, and sat thinking.When she rose her anger was evident in the stern unbending of her head.She looked at the pudding in the fender, and broke out:
“一个男人连晚饭都不回家吃，这简直不像话！如此他愿意活得这么龌龊，我不明白我为什么还要担心他。经过自己的家门不进，却去了酒馆，而我在这儿守着晚饭等他——” “It is a scandalous thing as a man can’t even come home to his dinner!If it’s crozzled up to a cinder I don’t see why I should care.Past his very door he goes to get to a public-house, and here I sit with his dinner waiting for him—”
女人走了出去。她把煤一块一块地放到红红的炉火上，暗影映在墙上，直到屋子几乎完全笼罩在黑暗中。 She went out.As she dropped piece after piece of coal on the red fire, the shadows fell on the walls, till the room was almost in total darkness.

房子里安静极了。伊丽莎白·贝茨摘下帽子和披肩，然后把小地毯卷起来。弄完后，她坐了下来。这时已经九点过几分了。矿井那边卷扬机飞速旋转的噗噗声吓了她一跳，它下降时绳子上的制动器刺耳地呼呼作响。她又一次感到血液涌动的痛，她把手放到一边，大声说道：“天哪！——这才九点多一点！”她责备着自己。 The house was quiet.Elizabeth Bates took off her hat and shawl, and rolled back the rug.When she had finished, she sat down.It was a few minutes past nine.She was startled by the rapid chuff of the winding-engine at the pit, and the sharp whirr of the brakes on the rope as it descended.Again she felt the painful sweep of her blood, and she put her hand to her side, saying aloud, “Good gracious!—it’s only the nine o’clock deputy going down,” rebuking herself.
她一动不动地坐着，听外面的动静。这样过了半个小时以后，她已经疲惫不堪了。 She sat still, listening.Half an hour of this, and she was wearied out.
“我这样做究竟是为什么？”她可怜巴巴地自语道，“这样只会伤害我自己。” “What am I working myself up like this for?”she said pitiably to herself, “I s’ll only be doing myself some damage.”
她又拿出针线活。 She took out her sewing again.
差一刻十点时，外面响起了脚步声。只有一个人！她望着门，等待门被打开。这是一个上了年岁的女人，戴着黑色的无边帽，披着黑色的羊毛披肩——是他的妈妈。她约摸60岁上下，面色苍白，有着一双蓝色眼睛，脸上满是皱纹，令人悲悯。她关上门，然后急躁地转向儿媳妇。 At a quarter to ten there were footsteps.One person!She watched for the door to open.It was an elderly woman, in a black bonnet and a black woollen shawl—his mother.She was about sixty years old, pale, with blue eyes, and her face all wrinkled and lamentable.She shut the door and turned to her daughter-inlaw peevishly.
“唉，莉齐，我们到底该怎么办，我们到底该怎么办？”她哭喊着。 “Eh, Lizzie, whatever shall we do, whatever shall we do!” she cried.
伊丽莎白猛然往后缩了缩。 Elizabeth drew back a little, sharply.
“怎么了，妈？”她问道。 “What is it, mother?” she said.
老妇人顾自坐到沙发上。 The elder woman seated herself on the sofa.
“我不知道，孩子，我不能告诉你！”她慢慢地摇着头。伊丽莎白坐那里，焦急而烦恼地望着她。 “I don’t know, child, I can’t tell you!”—she shook her head slowly.Elizabeth sat watching her, anxious and vexed.
“我不知道。”孩子的祖母答道，深深地叹了口气。“我的麻烦总是没个尽头，没个尽头啊。我确信我所遭受的事情已经够多了——！”她哭泣着，任由眼泪流淌，没有去擦。 “I don’t know,” replied the grandmother, sighing very deeply.“There’s no end to my troubles, there isn’t.The things I’ve gone through, I’m sure it’s enough—!”She wept without wiping her eyes, the tears running.
“但，妈妈。”伊丽莎白打断道，“你到底什么意思？怎么了？” “But, mother,” interrupted Elizabeth, “what do you mean?What is it?”
祖母慢慢地揩了揩眼睛。伊丽莎白的直截了当止住了她涌泉般的泪水。她慢慢地擦了擦眼睛。 The grandmother slowly wiped her eyes.The fountains of her tears were stopped by Elizabeth’s directness.She wiped her eyes slowly.
“可怜的孩子啊！唉，你这个可怜的人啊！”她哀叹道。 “Poor child!Eh, you poor thing!” she moaned.
“我不知道我们该做些什么——和你一样，我也不知道——出事情了，确实出事情了！” “I don’t know what we’re going to do, I don’t—and you as you are—it’s a thing, it is indeed!”
伊丽莎白等待着。 Elizabeth waited.
“他死了吗？”她问道，说这话时，她的心激烈地翻腾着。然而，这个问题实在是太口无遮拦了，她微微地感到一阵羞愧。 “Is he dead?” she asked, and at the words her heart swung violently, though she felt a slight flush of shame at the ultimate extravagance of the question.
她的话着实吓到了老妇人，几乎让她信以为真了。 Her words sufficiently frightened the old lady, almost brought her to herself.
“别这么说，伊丽莎白！我们该希望不至于那么糟。不，愿主宽恕我们吧，伊丽莎白。我睡觉前正要坐下来喝杯水的时候，杰克·里格利进来了，他说：‘贝茨太太，你最好去下铁路那边吧。沃尔特出事了。你最好去陪她坐会，直到我们把他弄回家。’我还没来得及问他一句话，他就走了。然后，我戴上帽子就径直下来了，莉齐。我心想：‘唉，那可怜的孩子，如果有人突然来告诉她，不晓得她会怎么样。’你不必为这事烦心，莉齐——你知道你怀孕了。怀了多长时间了，六个月——还是五个月，莉齐？唉！”——老妇人摇了摇她的头——“时光飞逝，时光飞逝啊！唉！” “Don’t say so, Elizabeth!We’ll hope it’s not as bad as that; no, may the Lord spare us that, Elizabeth.Jack Rigley came just as I was sittin’ down to a glass afore going to bed, an’ ’e said, ‘’Appen you’ll go down th’ line, Mrs. Bates.Walt’s had an accident.’Appen you’ll go an’ sit wi’ ’er till we can get him home.’I hadn’t time to ask him a word afore he was gone.An’ I put my bonnet on an’ come straight down, Lizzie.I thought to myself, ‘Eh, that poor blessed child, if anybody should come an’ tell her of a sudden, there’s no knowin’ what’ll ’appen to ’er.’You mustn’t let it upset you, Lizzie—or you know what to expect.How long is it, six months—or is it five, Lizzie?Ay!”—the old woman shook her head—“time slips on, it slips on!Ay!”
伊丽莎白的心绪并不在此。如果他死了——她能不能靠这微薄的抚恤金过活，而她又能挣什么钱？——她快速地盘算着。如果他受伤了——他们不会带他去医院——照料他是多么地烦人！——但或许她能让他远离酗酒和他那令人讨厌的生活方式。她会的——在他生病的时候。一想到那景象，她的眼睛里不禁涌满了泪水。她又开始了多少无谓的伤感？——她转而考虑孩子们。无论如何，她对于孩子们都是绝对不可或缺的。把他们抚养成人是她的职责。 Elizabeth’s thoughts were busy elsewhere.If he was killed—would she be able to manage on the little pension and what she could earn?—she counted up rapidly.If he was hurt—they wouldn’t take him to the hospital—how tiresome he would be to nurse!—but perhaps she’d be able to get him away from the drink and his hateful ways.She would—while he was ill.The tears offered to come to her eyes at the picture.But what sentimental luxury was this she was beginning?—She turned to consider the children.At any rate she was absolutely necessary for them.They were her business.
“唉！”老妇人又叹道，“想起他给我第一份工资的时候，似乎才是一两个礼拜前的事情。唉——他是个好小伙子啊，伊丽莎白，他确实是，以他自己的方式。我不知道为什么他会成为这样一个烦人的家伙，我不知道。在家时，他是个快活的小伙子，精神饱满的。但毫无疑问，他现在有一堆毛病，他确实有！我希望主能饶恕他，让他改过。我希望如此，我希望如此。你和他之前有不和的情况，伊丽莎白，你们确实有。但他和我在一起的时候是个十分快乐的小伙子，他是的，我能向你保证。我不知道这是怎么了——” “Ay!”repeated the old woman, “it seems but a week or two since he brought me his first wages.Ay—he was a good lad, Elizabeth, he was, in his way.I don’t know why he got to be such a trouble, I don’t.He was a happy lad at home, only full of spirits.But there’s no mistake he’s been a handful of trouble, he has!I hope the Lord’ll spare him to mend his ways.I hope so, I hope so.You’ve had a sight o’ trouble with him, Elizabeth, you have indeed.But he was a jolly enough lad wi’ me, he was, I can assure you.I don’t know how it is...”
老妇人以一种单调、恼人的声音继续沉思自语着。这时候，伊丽莎白正聚精会神地思考着。当她听到卷扬机快速地噗噗作响、制动器尖利的嗖嗖声时，着实吓了一跳。然后她听到引擎慢下来，制动器也再没发出声音。老妇人没有留意。伊丽莎白心神不宁地等待着。婆婆说着说着，渐渐陷入沉默。 The old woman continued to muse aloud, a monotonous irritating sound, while Elizabeth thought concentratedly, startled once, when she heard the winding-engine chuff quickly, and the brakes skirr with a shriek.Then she heard the engine more slowly, and the brakes made no sound.The old woman did not notice.Elizabeth waited in suspense.The mother-inlaw talked, with lapses into silence.
“但他不是你的儿子，莉齐，这就是不同之处。不管他怎么样，我记得他小时候的样子，然后我学着了解他，体谅他。你一定得体谅他——” “But he wasn’t your son, Lizzie, an’ it makes a difference.Whatever he was, I remember him when he was little, an’ I learned to understand him and to make allowances.You’ve got to make allowances for them—”
十点半了，老妇人说道：“可是从头到尾都是麻烦，麻烦不嫌老，不会嫌你老的——”此时门“砰”一声开了，台阶上传来沉重的脚步声。 It was half-past ten, and the old woman was saying: “But it’s trouble from beginning to end; you’re never too old for trouble, never too old for that—” when the gate banged back, and there were heavy feet on the steps.
“我要走了，莉齐，让我走吧。”老妇人哭着说道，站起身。但伊丽莎白已经站在门边。那里有个穿着矿井工作服的男人。 “I’ll go, Lizzie, let me go,” cried the old woman, rising.But Elizabeth was at the door.It was a man in pit-clothes.
“他们正弄他过来，太太。”他说。伊丽莎白的心有一刻停止了跳动。然后，她再次心潮澎湃，这几乎令她窒息。 “They’re bringin’ ’im, Missis,” he said.Elizabeth’s heart halted a moment.Then it surged on again, almost suffocating her.
“他？——严重吗？”她问道。 “Is he—is it bad?” she asked.
男人扭过头去，望着黑暗： The man turned away, looking at the darkness:
“医生说他已经死了几个小时了。他看见他在灯厢里。” “The doctor says ’e ’d been dead hours.’E saw ’im i’ th’ lamp-cabin.”
老妇人站在伊丽莎白身后，跌坐在椅子上，双手交叉，哭喊着：“啊，我的孩子，我的孩子！” The old woman, who stood just behind Elizabeth, dropped into a chair, and folded her hands, crying: “Oh, my boy, my boy!”
“嘘——”伊丽莎白说着，紧皱的眉头抽动着。“安静点，妈，别吵醒孩子们。无论如何，我都不想让他们下来！” “Hush!” said Elizabeth, with a sharp twitch of a frown.“Be still, mother, don’t waken th’ children: I wouldn’t have them down for anything!”
老妇人轻轻地哽咽着，身体颤抖着。那男人走开了。伊丽莎白上前一步。 The old woman moaned softly, rocking herself.The man was drawing away.Elizabeth took a step forward.
“怎么弄的？”她问道。 “How was it?” she asked.
“嗯，我也说不准。”男人答道，非常局促不安。“他还在干他的活时，大家都走了，一大堆东西就从他头上砸下来了。” “Well, I couldn’t say for sure,” the man replied, very ill at ease.“’E wor finishin’ a stint an’ th’ butties ’ad gone, an’ a lot o’ stuff come down atop ’n ’im.”
“就把他砸碎了？”寡妇颤栗着哭喊道。 “And crushed him?” cried the widow, with a shudder.
“没。”男人说，“压在了他背上。他脸朝下，石头没碰到他。但他被困住了。看起来他是窒息而死的。” “No,” said the man, “it fell at th’ back of ’im.’E wor under th’ face, an’ it niver touched ’im.It shut ’im in.It seems ’e wor smothered.”
伊丽莎白往后缩了缩。她听到她身后的老妇人在哭泣。 Elizabeth shrank back.She heard the old woman behind her cry:
“什么？——他说是什么？” “What?—what did ’e say it was?”
男人高声应道：“他是窒息而死的！” The man replied, more loudly: “’E wor smothered!”
老妇人随即失声痛哭，这倒使伊丽莎白感到宽慰。 Then the old woman wailed aloud, and this relieved Elizabeth.
“噢，妈妈。”她把手放到老妇人身上，说，“别吵醒孩子们，别吵醒孩子们。” “Oh, mother,” she said, putting her hand on the old woman, “don’t waken th’ children, don’t waken th’ children.”
她不自觉地轻轻抽泣着，而老妇人则颤抖哭泣着。伊丽莎白记起来他们正在把他往家送，她必须得准备好。“他们会把他放在客厅里。”她对自己说，面色苍白地站着，不知所措。 She wept a little, unknowing, while the old mother rocked herself and moaned.Elizabeth remembered that they were bringing him home, and she must be ready.“They’ll lay him in the parlour,” she said to herself, standing a moment pale and perplexed.
然后她点燃一根蜡烛，走进小房间去了。那里面空气湿冷，但她不能生火，那里没有壁炉。她放下蜡烛，环顾四周。蜡烛的火光映在虹彩玻璃上，闪烁着，照在两个装着粉色菊花的花瓶上和暗色的桃花心木的家具上。屋子里有菊花发出的冰冷的、死亡般的味道。伊丽莎白站在那里，看着那些花。她转过身，计算着长沙发和小衣橱之间的空间是否能放下他。她把椅子推到一旁。这就有了空间放下他，还可以在他周围走动。然后她取来一块红色的旧桌布和另一块破布，把它们铺在地上以节省点地毯。她颤栗着离开客厅，从梳妆台抽屉中拿出一件干净的衬衫，把它放到火边烘着。她婆婆则一直抽搐着，坐在椅子上呜咽着。 Then she lighted a candle and went into the tiny room.The air was cold and damp, but she could not make a fire, there was no fireplace.She set down the candle and looked round.The candle-light glittered on the lustre-glasses, on the two vases that held some of the pink chrysanthemums, and on the dark mahogany.There was a cold, deathly smell of chrysanthemums in the room.Elizabeth stood looking at the flowers.She turned away, and calculated whether there would be room to lay him on the floor, between the couch and the chiffonier.She pushed the chairs aside.There would be room to lay him down and to step round him.Then she fetched the old red tablecloth, and another old cloth, spreading them down to save her bit of carpet.She shivered on leaving the parlour; so, from the dresser-drawer she took a clean shirt and put it at the fire to air.All the time her mother-inlaw was rocking herself in the chair and moaning.
“你得从那儿挪开一下，妈。”伊丽莎白说，“他们要把他抬进来了。进来坐这摇椅。” “You’ll have to move from there, mother,” said Elizabeth.“They’ll be bringing him in.Come in the rocker.”
老母亲机械地站起来，然后坐在炉火边，继续痛哭着。伊丽莎白走进储藏室，又拿了一支蜡烛。在那里，在盖着光秃秃瓦片下的小阁楼里，她听到他们来了。她静静地站在储藏室的门口听着。她听到他们经过房子的尽头，笨拙地走下那三级台阶，杂乱的脚步声和低声细语混杂着。老妇人默不作声。男人们进到院子里。 The old mother rose mechanically, and seated herself by the fire, continuing to lament.Elizabeth went into the pantry for another candle, and there, in the little penthouse under the naked tiles, she heard them coming.She stood still in the pantry doorway, listening.She heard them pass the end of the house, and come awkwardly down the three steps, a jumble of shuffling footsteps and muttering voices.The old woman was silent.The men were in the yard.
然后伊丽莎白听到马修斯——矿井经理——说：“你先进来，吉姆。留神！” Then Elizabeth heard Matthews, the manager of the pit, say: “You go in first, Jim.Mind!”
门开了，两个女人看着一个矿工抬着担架的一头退着进了屋，她们能看见担架上死者那钉着钉子的矿井靴。两个抬担架的停了停，前面的男人弯腰躲过门楣。 The door came open, and the two women saw a collier backing into the room, holding one end of a stretcher, on which they could see the nailed pit-boots of the dead man.The two carriers halted, the man at the head stooping to the lintel of the door.
“你们想把他放哪？”长着白胡子的矮个子经理问道。 “Wheer will you have him?” asked the manager, a short, white-bearded man.
伊丽莎白回过神来，从储藏室拿着没有点燃的蜡烛走了过来。 Elizabeth roused herself and came from the pantry carrying the unlighted candle.
“客厅里。”她说。 “In the parlour,” she said.
“这里，吉姆！”经理指着，两个抬担架的转身进了小房间。 “In there, Jim!” pointed the manager, and the carriers backed round into the tiny room.
当他们费劲地掉转担架通过两道门口时，他们盖在尸体身上的大衣掉落了下来，女人们看见了她们的男人腰部以上光着身子，因为要脱光衣服躺着干活。老妇人开始呜咽，声音低沉、令人恐惧。 The coat with which they had covered the body fell off as they awkwardly turned through the two doorways, and the women saw their man, naked to the waist, lying stripped for work.The old woman began to moan in a low voice of horror.
“把担架放这边。”经理急急忙忙地说，“把衣服给他盖上。现在当心，当心！看你——！” “Lay th’ stretcher at th’ side,” snapped the manager, “an’ put ’im on th’ cloths.Mind now, mind!Look you now—!”
其中的一个男人碰掉了一个插着菊花的花瓶。他不好意思地看了看，然后将担架放下。伊丽莎白没看她的丈夫。她一进房间，就去拾起那摔碎的花瓶和花。 One of the men had knocked off a vase of chrysanthemums.He stared awkwardly, then they set down the stretcher.Elizabeth did not look at her husband.As soon as she could get in the room, she went and picked up the broken vase and the flowers.
“等一等！”她说。 “Wait a minute!” she said.
她用抹布把水擦干的时候，三个男人默不作声地等待着。 The three men waited in silence while she mopped up the water with a duster.
“唉，怪事啊，真是怪事，确实是！”经理说着，忧虑而困惑地揉着他的眉毛。“我这辈子没见过这样的事，从没有！他没有理由离开啊。我一辈子从来没见过这样的事情！石头“呼”的一声砸在他身上，然后把他困在里面。不足四尺的空间，那里没有——而且那几乎没有碰到他。” “Eh, what a job, what a job, to be sure!”the manager was saying, rubbing his brow with trouble and perplexity.“Never knew such a thing in my life, never!He’d no business to ha’ been left.I never knew such a thing in my life!Fell over him clean as a whistle, an’ shut him in.Not four foot of space, there wasn’t—yet it scarce bruised him.”
他俯身看着死者——倾卧着，半裸着，全身满是煤灰污垢。 He looked down at the dead man, lying prone, half naked, all grimed with coal-dust.
“‘真是奇怪。’”医生说。“这是我所知道的最可怕的事。看起来好像是事先策划好的。石头正好砸到他，把他困在里面，就像一个捕鼠器。”他猛然用手做了个向下的手势。 “‘’Sphyxiated,’ the doctor said.“It IS the most terrible job I’ve ever known.Seems as if it was done o’ purpose.Clean over him, an’ shut ’im in, like a mouse-trap”—he made a sharp, descending gesture with his hand.
矿工们站在一旁，绝望地往一侧猛摇脑袋。 The colliers standing by jerked aside their heads in hopeless comment.
这件恐怖的事情使他们所有人汗毛直立。 The horror of the thing bristled upon them all.
然后他们听到女孩在楼上尖声喊着：“妈妈，妈妈——是谁呀？妈妈，是谁呀？” Then they heard the girl’s voice upstairs calling shrilly: “Mother, mother—who is it?Mother, who is it?”
伊丽莎白连忙走到楼梯口，然后打开门。 Elizabeth hurried to the foot of the stairs and opened the door:
“去睡觉！”她厉声命令道。“你在喊什么？马上去睡觉——什么事也没有——” “Go to sleep!” she commanded sharply.“What are you shouting about?Go to sleep at once—there’s nothing—”
然后，她开始爬楼梯。他们能够听到她在楼板上和小卧室里的灰泥地板上走路的声音。他们能清楚地听到她说： Then she began to mount the stairs.They could hear her on the boards, and on the plaster floor of the little bedroom.They could hear her distinctly:
“现在又怎么回事？——你什么毛病，傻瓜？”——她的声音透着焦虑和一种不真实的温和。 “What’s the matter now?—what’s the matter with you, silly thing?”—her voice was much agitated, with an unreal gentleness.
“我以为是有人来了。”小女孩儿带着哭腔说，“他回来没？” “I thought it was some men come,” said the plaintive voice of the child.“Has he come?”
“是的，他们把他弄回来了。没有什么可大惊小怪的。现在去睡觉，乖孩子。” “Yes, they’ve brought him.There’s nothing to make a fuss about.Go to sleep now, like a good child.”
他们听见她在卧室说话的声音，他们等待着她给孩子们盖好被褥。 They could hear her voice in the bedroom, they waited whilst she covered the children under the bedclothes.
“他醉了吗？”女孩胆怯地低声问道。 “Is he drunk?” asked the girl, timidly, faintly.
“没有！没有——他没有！他——睡着了。” “No!No—he’s not!He—he’s asleep.”
“他在楼下睡着了？” “Is he asleep downstairs?”
“是的——别出声了。” “Yes—and don’t make a noise.”
一阵沉寂，然后，男人们又听到孩子惊恐地问：“那是什么声音？” There was silence for a moment, then the men heard the frightened child again: “what’s that noise?”
“什么也没有，我告诉你了，有什么好操心的？” “It’s nothing, I tell you, what are you bothering for?”

老妇人睁开她的双眼看着他。她被这一打断惊住了，似乎很是疑惑。 The old woman opened her eyes and looked at him.She was shocked by this interruption, and seemed to wonder.
“几点钟了？”——孩子可怜巴巴地轻声问了最后一个问题，闷闷不乐地缩回去睡觉了。 “What time is it?”—the plaintive thin voice of the child, sinking back unhappily into sleep, asked this last question.
“十点。”母亲愈发轻柔地答道。她那时一定在俯身吻孩子们。 “Ten o’clock,” answered the mother more softly.Then she must have bent down and kissed the children.
马修斯示意男人们离开。他们戴上帽子，拿起了担架。跨过尸体，他们踮着脚尖走出了屋子。他们直到离醒着的孩子们很远的时候，才开始说话。 Matthews beckoned to the men to come away.They put on their caps and took up the stretcher.Stepping over the body, they tiptoed out of the house.None of them spoke till they were far from the wakeful children.
伊丽莎白下来时，发现她婆婆独自一人在客厅的地板上，趴在死者身上，眼泪滴落在他身上。 When Elizabeth came down she found her mother alone on the parlour floor, leaning over the dead man, the tears dropping on him.
“我们得把他安葬了。”妻子说道。她放上水壶，然后转身跪在他脚边，开始解开捆扎在一起的皮质鞋带。屋子阴冷潮湿，只点了一支蜡烛，所以光线很昏暗，她不得不弓着身子，几乎将脸贴到地板上。她终于脱下了他重重的靴子，将它们放在一旁。 “We must lay him out,” the wife said.She put on the kettle, then returning knelt at the feet, and began to unfasten the knotted leather laces.The room was clammy and dim with only one candle, so that she had to bend her face almost to the floor.At last she got off the heavy boots and put them away.
“这会儿您得帮帮我。”她轻声对老妇人道。她们一起剥掉了这男人的衣服。 “You must help me now,” she whispered to the old woman.Together they stripped the man.
当她们站起身，看着他带着死亡朴素的肃穆尊严躺在那里时，女人们带着恐惧和敬意呆立着。有好一会儿，她们俯身看着死者，静静地站立，老母亲啜泣着。伊丽莎白想收回说过的话。她看着他，那么神圣而自顾自地躺着。她和他好像没有任何瓜葛。她不能接受这一点。她俯过身，将她的手放在他身上，似乎要索取自己那份权利。他还是暖和的，因为他死去的那个矿井很热。他母亲将他的脸捧在手间，断断续续地咕哝着。老人的泪如同潮湿叶子上的水滴接连洒落，母亲不是在哭泣，只是她的眼泪就这样流淌着。伊丽莎白抱住丈夫的尸体，脸颊和嘴唇紧贴着他。她似乎在聆听，在询问，设法找出一些线索。但她不能。她似乎被丈夫驱赶开了。他仿佛坚不可摧。 When they arose, saw him lying in the naïve dignity of death, the women stood arrested in fear and respect.For a few moments they remained still, looking down, the old mother whimpering.Elizabeth felt countermanded.She saw him, how utterly inviolable he lay in himself.She had nothing to do with him.She could not accept it.Stooping, she laid her hand on him, in claim.He was still warm, for the mine was hot where he had died.His mother had his face between her hands, and was murmuring incoherently.The old tears fell in succession as drops from wet leaves; the mother was not weeping, merely her tears flowed.Elizabeth embraced the body of her husband, with cheek and lips.She seemed to be listening, inquiring, trying to get some connection.But she could not.She was driven away.He was impregnable.
她起身，走进厨房，把热水倒进盆里，拿了肥皂、法兰绒布和一条柔软的毛巾。 She rose, went into the kitchen, where she poured warm water into a bowl, brought soap and flannel and a soft towel.
“我一定要给他洗洗。”她说。 “I must wash him,” she said.
然后，老母亲僵直起身，看着她仔细地给他洗脸，小心地用法兰绒布轻轻擦洗着他嘴边那浅黄色的大胡子。她心底充满了深深的恐惧，所以她要服侍他。老妇人嫉妒地说： Then the old mother rose stiffly, and watched Elizabeth as she carefully washed his face, carefully brushing the big blond moustache from his mouth with the flannel.She was afraid with a bottomless fear, so she ministered to him.The old woman, jealous, said:
“让我来给他擦！”随即她跪在另一边，伊丽莎白洗的时候她慢慢地给他擦干。她那硕大的黑帽子不时擦到儿媳妇头上的黑发。她们这样默默地干了很久。她们从没有忘记这就是死亡，而接触到这男人的尸体则给她们每一个人一些各不相同的奇特的情愫。巨大的恐惧占据了她们的心，母亲觉得躺在地上的人被送回了她的子宫里，作为母亲她被否定了；妻子感到她的灵魂与他完全隔绝，她肚子里的孩子成为了她身体之外的一个重担。 “Let me wipe him!”—and she kneeled on the other side drying slowly as Elizabeth washed, her big black bonnet sometimes brushing the dark head of her daughter.They worked thus in silence for a long time.They never forgot it was death, and the touch of the man’s dead body gave them strange emotions, different in each of the women; a great dread possessed them both, the mother felt the lie was given to her womb, she was denied; the wife felt the utter isolation of the human soul, the child within her was a weight apart from her.
这事终于完成了。他是一个身形俊美的男人，他的脸上没有显露出一点醉酒的痕迹。他皮肤白皙，一身壮肉，四肢匀称。但是，他已经死了。 At last it was finished.He was a man of handsome body, and his face showed no traces of drink.He was blonde, full-fleshed, with fine limbs.But he was dead.
“保佑他吧。”他母亲低语道，一直注视着他的脸，充满恐惧地说，“亲爱的孩子——保佑他吧！”她饱含母爱，带着一丝微弱恐惧的声音说道。 “Bless him,” whispered his mother, looking always at his face, and speaking out of sheer terror.“Dear lad—bless him!”She spoke in a faint, sibilant ecstasy of fear and mother love.
伊丽莎白再次跌坐在地板上，将自己的脸靠着他的脖子，颤抖着，战栗着。可她不得不再次离开。他死了，而她鲜活的血肉之躯无法与之抗衡。巨大的恐惧和疲惫攥住了她：她是如此地徒劳。她的生命就像这样消逝着。 Elizabeth sank down again to the floor, and put her face against his neck, and trembled and shuddered.But she had to draw away again.He was dead, and her living flesh had no place against his.A great dread and weariness held her: she was so unavailing.Her life was gone like this.
“他如牛奶般洁白，如十二月大的婴儿般清白，保佑他吧，我亲爱的孩子！”老母亲低声自语着，“他没有一块疤痕，清澈、纯净、洁白、美丽如一个初生的小孩。”她自豪地嘀咕着。伊丽莎白还是掩着自己的脸。 “White as milk he is, clear as a twelve-month baby, bless him, the darling!”the old mother murmured to herself.“Not a mark on him, clear and clean and white, beautiful as ever a child was made,” she murmured with pride.Elizabeth kept her face hidden.
“他安静地走了，莉齐——安静得像睡着了。他很漂亮吧，这宝贝？唉——他一定安息了，莉齐。或许他早弄好了，莉齐，困在里面的时候。他有时间哩。如果他没有安息，他看起来不会像这样。宝贝，亲爱的宝贝。啊，但他也曾尽情地笑过。我最爱听那笑了。他非常尽情地大笑了，莉齐，就像个小伙子——” “He went peaceful, Lizzie—peaceful as sleep.Isn’t he beautiful, the lamb?Ay—he must ha’ made his peace, Lizzie.’Appen he made it all right, Lizzie, shut in there.He’d have time.He wouldn’t look like this if he hadn’t made his peace.The lamb, the dear lamb.Eh, but he had a hearty laugh.I loved to hear it.He had the heartiest laugh, Lizzie, as a lad—”
伊丽莎白抬头看了看。这男人的嘴缩回去了，在胡须的覆盖下微微张开。这双眼睛，半闭着，晦暗中显不出光彩。他的生命如云烟，燃烧后消逝，给她留下一个隔绝和完全陌生的他。她知道对于她来说，他是怎样的一个陌生人。在她的子宫里，恐惧结成了冰，因为曾和她生活过的这具血肉之躯成了相隔的路人。这就是生活的全部意义么——在生命热度的掩饰下，那彻底、完全的隔绝？她害怕地转过脸。这事实太过致命。他们之间什么也没有了，尽管他们曾走到一起，反复将他们的裸体交给对方。曾经，每一次他占有她的肉体之后，他们就是两个隔绝的人，如同现在一样遥远。他同她一样不负责任。她子宫里的孩子就像块冰。因为当她看着死去的男人时，她冰冷、超然的理智就会清晰地说着：“我是谁？我一直在做什么？我一直在同一个并不存在的丈夫抗争着。他却一直存在着。我做了什么错事？和我一直生活在一起的到底是什么？现实就躺在眼前——这个男人。”而她的灵魂因恐惧在她心中死去：她知道她从未理解过他，他也从未了解过她，他们在冥冥中相遇，在冥冥中相斗，却不知道他们遇见了谁，抑或在同谁斗。但是，现在她明白了。她看着男人，沉默了。因为她过去错了。她曾说他是个矛盾存在，她曾觉得和他很熟悉。但是他始终还是离开了，过着她所从未有过的生活，感受她所未有过的感受。 Elizabeth looked up.The man’s mouth was fallen back, slightly open under the cover of the moustache.The eyes, half shut, did not show glazed in the obscurity.Life with its smoky burning gone from him, had left him apart and utterly alien to her.And she knew what a stranger he was to her.In her womb was ice of fear, because of this separate stranger with whom she had been living as one flesh.Was this what it all meant—utter, intact separateness, obscured by heat of living?In dread she turned her face away.The fact was too deadly.There had been nothing between them, and yet they had come together, exchanging their nakedness repeatedly.Each time he had taken her, they had been two isolated beings, far apart as now.He was no more responsible than she.The child was like ice in her womb.For as she looked at the dead man, her mind, cold and detached, said clearly: “Who am I?What have I been doing?I have been fighting a husband who did not exist.HE existed all the time.What wrong have I done?What was that I have been living with?There lies the reality, this man.”—And her soul died in her for fear: she knew she had never seen him, he had never seen her, they had met in the dark and had fought in the dark, not knowing whom they met nor whom they fought.And now she saw, and turned silent in seeing.For she had been wrong.She had said he was something he was not; she had felt familiar with him.Whereas he was apart all the while, living as she never lived, feeling as she never felt.
她带着恐惧和羞愧看着那具她曾经误解的赤裸裸的身体。他是她孩子们的父亲。她的灵魂似乎被抽离出了身体。她兀自站立着。她看着他裸露的尸身，感到很羞愧，仿佛她曾经错看了他。毕竟，那就是他自己。对她来说，这很令她恐惧。她看着他的脸，然后把自己的脸扭向墙边。因为，他的神色与她的不同，他的做法亦有别于她的做法。她曾否认他作为自我的存在——现在她明白了。她曾拒绝他的自我——而这就是她曾经的生活，还有他的生活——她感谢死亡，死亡重塑了真相。而她知道她并未死去。 In fear and shame she looked at his naked body, that she had known falsely.And he was the father of her children.Her soul was torn from her body and stood apart.She looked at his naked body and was ashamed, as if she had denied it.After all, it was itself.It seemed awful to her.She looked at his face, and she turned her own face to the wall.For his look was other than hers, his way was not her way.She had denied him what he was—she saw it now.She had refused him as himself.—And this had been her life, and his life.—She was grateful to death, which restored the truth.And she knew she was not dead.
她心中始终充满着对他的悲伤和怜悯。他蒙受了多少痛苦？这个无助的男人经受了多少的恐惧！她因莫大的痛苦而身体僵直。她没能帮到他。他被残酷地伤害了，这个裸露着的男人，这另一个存在，而她却无法做出补偿。还有孩子们——但孩子们是属于活着的人的。这个死去的男人同他们没有任何关系。他和她只是生命通道，经过这通道，生命流动而产生了孩子们。她是一位母亲——但她现在知道作为一名妻子是多么可怕。而已经死去的他，一定感觉到了作为一个丈夫是多么可怕。她觉得在另外那个世界，他之于她会是一个陌生人。如果他们在那里相遇——在来世，他们只会为之前的一切而羞愧。因某些神秘的原因，孩子出生了——从他俩那里。但孩子们并没有让他们成为一体。现在他死了，她知道她与他永生永世地分离了，他和她永生永世再没了关系。她明白她生命的这段插曲结束了。他们曾在生活中相互否定。现在他退场了。一阵剧痛掠过她心头。那么就这样了结吧：在他死去之前很久，他们之间曾变得那么无望。但他曾是她的丈夫。但那多么微不足道啊！—— And all the while her heart was bursting with grief and pity for him.What had he suffered?What stretch of horror for this helpless man!She was rigid with agony.She had not been able to help him.He had been cruelly injured, this naked man, this other being, and she could make no reparation.There were the children—but the children belonged to life.This dead man had nothing to do with them.He and she were only channels through which life had flowed to issue in the children.She was a mother—but how awful she knew it now to have been a wife.And he, dead now, how awful he must have felt it to be a husband.She felt that in the next world he would be a stranger to her.If they met there, in the beyond, they would only be ashamed of what had been before.The children had come, for some mysterious reason, out of both of them.But the children did not unite them.Now he was dead, she knew how eternally he was apart from her, how eternally he had nothing more to do with her.She saw this episode of her life closed.They had denied each other in life.Now he had withdrawn.An anguish came over her.It was finished then: it had become hopeless between them long before he died.Yet he had been her husband.But how little!—
“你拿来了他的衬衣没，伊丽莎白？” “Have you got his shirt, ’Lizabeth?”
伊丽莎白转过身，没有回答，虽然她尽情哭泣且按照她婆婆所期望的那样行事。但她不能这么做，她沉默了。她走进厨房，拿回衣服。 Elizabeth turned without answering, though she strove to weep and behave as her mother-inlaw expected.But she could not, she was silenced.She went into the kitchen and returned with the garment.
“烘干了。”她说道，四处地抓着棉衬衫在试着什么。她几乎羞于触摸他。她、或任何其他人有什么权利把手放在他身上呢，当她触到他身体的时候，是那么地谦卑不已。给他穿上衣服是一件艰难的差事。他是如此沉重而钝拙。一种可怕的恐惧一直攥着她：那就是他如此沉重、了无生气、毫无反应地离开了。于她来说，他们之间那遥远距离带来的恐惧是难以承受的——这是一个她必须跨越的、无边无际的鸿沟。 “It is aired,” she said, grasping the cotton shirt here and there to try.She was almost ashamed to handle him; what right had she or anyone to lay hands on him; but her touch was humble on his body.It was hard work to clothe him.He was so heavy and inert.A terrible dread gripped her all the while: that he could be so heavy and utterly inert, unresponsive, apart.The horror of the distance between them was almost too much for her—it was so infinite a gap she must look across.
终于干完了。她们用一块床单盖住他——盖着脸，让他躺着。接着，她闩好小客厅的门，以免孩子们看见那里躺着什么。然后，她平静下来，开始着手收拾厨房。她知道她屈从了生活，生活就是她现在的主宰。但从死亡——她的终极主宰那里，她却带着恐惧和羞愧畏缩了。 At last it was finished.They covered him with a sheet and left him lying, with his face bound.And she fastened the door of the little parlour, lest the children should see what was lying there.Then, with peace sunk heavy on her heart, she went about making tidy the kitchen.She knew she submitted to life, which was her immediate master.But from death, her ultimate master, she winced with fear and shame.
