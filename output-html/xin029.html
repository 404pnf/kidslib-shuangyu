Enter a Dragoon 晚到的骑兵
by Thomas Hardy 作者：托马斯·哈代
I lately had a melancholy experience (said the gentleman who is answerable for the truth of this story). 不久前，我经历了一件不太愉快的事情（这是一位先生的讲述，他可以为这个故事的真实性负责）。
It was that of going over a doomed house with whose outside aspect I had long been familiar—a house, that is, which by reason of age and dilapidation was to be pulled down during the following week. 那天，我去察看一座即将不复存在的房屋。长久以来，我很熟悉那座房子的外观，由于它年久失修，下个星期就要被拆除了。
Some of the thatch, brown and rotten as the gills of old mushrooms, had, indeed, been removed before I walked over the building. 破破烂烂的茅草屋顶有如干蘑菇的褐色菌褶。事实上，在我走到这座房子这里之前，那屋顶已被拆掉了一部分。
Seeing that it was only a very small house—which is usually called a ‘cottage-residence’—situated in a remote hamlet, and that it was not more than a hundred years old, if so much, I was led to think in my progress through the hollow rooms, with their cracked walls and sloping floors, what an exceptional number of abrupt family incidents had taken place therein—to reckon only those which had come to my own knowledge. 这房子非常小，人们往往称这样的房子为“农家小屋”。它坐落在一个僻静的小村庄里，最多不过有一百年的历史。因此，当我走过一间间空荡荡的屋子，看到龟裂的墙壁和倾斜的地板时，不禁开始猜测，这里发生过多少家庭变故——只算我知道的那些。
And no doubt there were many more of which I had never heard. 毫无疑问，还有许多变故是我闻所未闻的。
It stood at the top of a garden stretching down to the lane or street that ran through a hermit-group of dwellings in Mellstock parish. 房子坐落在果园的尽头，果园一直延伸到街边。小街两侧是一片隐蔽的民居，属于麦尔斯托克教区。
From a green gate at the lower entrance, over which the thorn hedge had been shaped to an arch by constant clippings, a gravel path ascended between the box edges of once trim raspberry, strawberry, and vegetable plots, towards the front door. 果园的较矮入口的大门是绿色的，门上方的荆棘树篱由于常常修剪，形成了一个拱顶。一条碎石小径向上延伸到房子前门，两侧是曾修剪得整整齐齐的覆盆子、草莓以及蔬菜地。
This was in colour an ancient and bleached green that could be rubbed off with the finger, and it bore a small long-featured brass knocker covered with verdigris in its crevices. 矮门的绿漆旧得泛白，用手指就能蹭掉。门上有一只年代久远的黄铜小门环，门环的缝隙间覆满了铜锈。
For some years before this eve of demolition the homestead had degenerated, and been divided into two tenements to serve as cottages for farm labourers; but in its prime it had indisputable claim to be considered neat, pretty, and genteel. 近些年来，这座宅子日益破败，被分成两间，供田里干活的人居住，直至如今面临被拆除的命运。但在它的全盛时期，这座房子毫无疑问是整洁、漂亮而优雅的。
The variety of incidents above alluded to was mainly owing to the nature of the tenure, whereby the place had been occupied by families not quite of the kind customary in such spots—people whose circumstances, position, or antecedents were more or less of a critical happy-go-lucky cast. 前面提到的种种变故，主要是由于先后租住在这里的家庭与当地的普通家庭有所不同。而当地人大都乐天知命，任由命运安排自己的境遇、地位和出身。
And of these residents the family whose term comprised the story I wish to relate was that of Mr. Jacob Paddock the market-gardener, who dwelt there for some years with his wife and grown-up daughter. 我想讲的这个故事就是关于其中一家的。这家的主人雅各布·帕多克先生是个果农，他和妻子以及成年的女儿在这里住过一些年头。
I 一
An evident commotion was agitating the premises, which jerked busy sounds across the front plot, resembling those of a disturbed hive. 帕多克家显然正处于骚动之中，房子前边的菜地传出忙乱的声音，如同受到惊扰的蜂巢。
If a member of the household appeared at the door it was with a countenance of abstraction and concern. 每个出现在门口的家庭成员，都是一副心不在焉、忧心忡忡的神情。
Evening began to bend over the scene; and the other inhabitants of the hamlet came out to draw water, their common well being in the public road opposite the garden and house of the Paddocks. 夜幕降临了。小村庄里的其他居民纷纷出来提水，村里公用的水井就在帕多克家房子以及果园对面的那条公路上。
Having wound up their bucketsfull respectively they lingered, and spoke significantly together. 村民们把各自的桶装满水摇上来后，依然流连在井边，意味深长地交谈着。
From their words any casual listener might have gathered information of what had occurred. 从他们的谈话中，任何无心的听者都能推断出发生了什么事。
The woodman who lived nearest the site of the story told most of the tale. 故事的大半内容是由住得离当事人最近的伐木工讲述的。
Selina, the daughter of the Paddocks opposite, had been surprised that afternoon by receiving a letter from her once intended husband, then a corporal, but now a sergeant-major of dragoons, whom she had hitherto supposed to be one of the slain in the Battle of the Alma two or three years before. 对面帕多克夫妇的女儿塞利娜，这天下午意外地收到了昔日未婚夫的来信。那小伙子从前是个下士，如今是一名骑兵军士长，而塞利娜一直以为他已在两三年前的阿尔马河战役中阵亡了。
‘She picked up wi’en against her father’s wish, as we know, and before he got his stripes,’ their informant continued. “大伙儿都知道，她不顾她爹的意思选了他，那时候他连军衔都没有哩，”“情报员”伐木工继续说道，
‘Not but that the man was as hearty a feller as you’d meet this side o’ London. “不过，他倒跟咱们伦敦南边的小伙子一样，是个实诚人。
But Jacob, you see, wished her to do better, and one can understand it. 可是你们也知道，雅各布想让她找个更好的，这个可以理解。
However, she was determined to stick to him at that time; and for what happened she was not much to blame, so near as they were to matrimony when the war broke out and spoiled all.’ 可她那时候下定决心非要跟他在一起。后来发生的事情也不能全怪她，俩人眼看就要结婚了，结果打起仗来了，一切都完了。”
‘Even the very pig had been killed for the wedding,’ said a woman, ‘and the barrel o’ beer ordered in. “就连婚礼用的猪都杀好了，”一个女人说道，“啤酒也订了一桶。
O, the man meant honourable enough. 唉，那小伙子倒是真心实意的。
But to be off in two days to fight in a foreign country—’twas natural of her father to say they should wait till he got back.’ 可他两天之后就要去国外打仗了，她爹让他们等他回来再结婚，也是很自然的事儿。”
‘And he never came,’ murmured one in the shade. “可他再也没回来。”树阴下有个人咕哝道。
‘The war ended but her man never turned up again. “仗打完了，可她男人再也没露面。
She was not sure he was killed, but was too proud, or too timid, to go and hunt for him.’ 她不确定他是不是死了，又不肯去找他，不是因为好面子，就是因为不敢。”
‘One reason why her father forgave her when he found out how matters stood was, as he said plain at the time, that he liked the man, and could see that he meant to act straight. “她爹弄明白是怎么回事儿之后，倒是原谅了她。他当时明明白白说了，原谅她的一个原因就是他挺喜欢那小伙子，看得出他是真心实意的。
So the old folks made the best of what they couldn’t mend, and kept her there with ’em, when some wouldn’t. 所以尽管于事无补，老夫妇俩还是尽力而为，把闺女留在身边。别的爹妈可不一定做得到这一点。
Time has proved seemingly that he did mean to act straight, now that he has writ to her that he’s coming. 时间证明他好像确实是认真的，因为他写信给她说他要回来了。
She’d have stuck to him all through the time, ’tis my belief; if t’other hadn’t come along.’ 我觉得，要不是冒出来另一个人，她肯定一直对他忠心耿耿的呢。”
‘At the time of the courtship,’ resumed the woodman, ‘the regiment was quartered in Casterbridge Barracks, and he and she got acquainted by his calling to buy a penn’orth of rathe-ripes off that tree yonder in her father’s orchard—though ’twas said he seed her over hedge as well as the apples. “他追求她的时候，”伐木工继续说道，“他的兵团正驻扎在卡斯特桥兵营。他要花一便士买她爹果园里那棵苹果树上早熟的苹果，他俩就这样认识了——不过据说他看见苹果的时候，就发现篱笆后面的她了。
He declared ’twas a kind of apple he much fancied; and he called for a penn’orth every day till the tree was cleared. 他说他很喜欢这种苹果，于是就每天来买一便士的，直到树上的果子都被摘光。
It ended in his calling for her.’ 然后，他就开始追求她了。”
‘’Twas a thousand pities they didn’t jine up at once and ha’ done wi’ it. “真遗憾他们没马上结婚，结果什么都完了。”
‘Well; better late than never, if so be he’ll have her now. “唉，都说晚来总比不来好，要真是这样，他现在还是能娶她的。
But, Lord, she’d that faith in ’en that she’d no more belief that he was alive, when a’ didn’t come, than that the undermost man in our churchyard was alive. 可是，天哪，当年他没回来，她就确信他死了，像咱们教堂墓地里最底下埋着的人一样，确确实实死了。
She’d never have thought of another but for that—O no!’ 要不是这样，她心里也绝对不会有别人——唉，绝不会！”
‘’Tis awkward, altogether, for her now.’ “总之，现在情况对她来说有点儿尴尬。”
‘Still she hadn’t married wi’ the new man. “她还没有跟另一个人成婚。
Though to be sure she would have committed it next week, even the licence being got, they say, for she’d have no banns this time, the first being so unfortunate.’ 不过，他们下周本来就要结婚了，据说连结婚证都领到了。她这回不发结婚公告了，因为上次太倒霉了。”
‘Perhaps the sergeant-major will think he’s released, and go as he came.’ “没准儿军士长会觉得这是个解脱，然后就从哪儿来回哪儿去了呢。”
‘O, not as I reckon. “唉，我估计不会。
Soldiers bain’t particular, and she’s a tidy piece o’ furniture still. 士兵尤其不会这样想，再说她还是那么漂亮的一个小可人儿。
What will happen is that she’ll have her soldier, and break off with the master-wheelwright, licence or no—daze me if she won’t.’ 她肯定会选择士兵，跟那个造车轮的师傅分手，什么结婚证不结婚证的——她要不这样，我倒奇怪了。”
In the progress of these desultory conjectures the form of another neighbour arose in the gloom. 正当大家胡乱猜测之时，又一个乡邻的身影从暗处出现了。
She nodded to the people at the well, who replied ‘G’d night, Mrs. Stone,’ as she passed through Mr. Paddock’s gate towards his door. 她一边穿过帕多克先生家的大门，朝他家房门走去，一边冲井边的人们点点头。人们纷纷回应：“晚上好，斯通太太。”
She was an intimate friend of the latter’s household, and the group followed her with their eyes up the path and past the windows, which were now lighted up by candles inside. 斯通太太是帕多克一家的好朋友。人们目送她走上小径，经过窗子。那些窗子已被屋内的蜡烛照得透亮。
II 二
Mrs. Stone paused at the door, knocked, and was admitted by Selina’s mother, who took her visitor at once into the parlour on the left hand, where a table was partly spread for supper. 斯通夫人在门前停下，敲敲门，塞利娜的母亲给她开了门，马上把她迎进左边的客厅，那里摆放着一张餐桌，不过晚餐还没有完全备好。
On the ‘beaufet’ against the wall stood probably the only object which would have attracted the eye of a local stranger in an otherwise ordinarily furnished room, a great plum-cake guarded as if it were a curiosity by a glass shade of the kind seen in museums—square, with a wooden back like those enclosing stuffed specimens of rare feather or fur. 客厅的布置十分平常，唯一能吸引当地陌生来客眼球的大概就是墙边“食柜”上摆着的一样东西了。那是一个葡萄干大蛋糕，像奇珍异宝似的被玻璃罩保护着。玻璃罩是博物馆里常见的那种，呈方形，背面是木质的，一般用来装有珍稀羽毛或毛皮的动物标本。
This was the mummy of the cake intended in earlier days for the wedding-feast of Selina and the soldier, which had been religiously and lovingly preserved by the former as a testimony to her intentional respectability in spite of an untoward subsequent circumstance, which will be mentioned. 这个已经风化的蛋糕原来是为塞利娜和那位士兵的婚宴准备的。尽管后来发生了不幸，塞利娜还是虔诚而静心地把这个蛋糕保存了起来，以证明自己的心意。我们后面会讲到她的不幸。
This relic was now as dry as a brick, and seemed to belong to a pre-existent civilization. 这件纪念品如今干得像是一块砖，看上去像是史前文明留下来的。
Till quite recently, Selina had been in the habit of pausing before it daily, and recalling the accident whose consequences had thrown a shadow over her life ever since—that of which the water-drawers had spoken—the sudden news one morning that the Route had come for the—th Dragoons, two days only being the interval before departure; the hurried consultation as to what should be done, the second time of asking being past but not the third; and the decision that it would be unwise to solemnize matrimony in such haphazard circumstances, even if it were possible, which was doubtful. 塞利娜习惯了每天在这个蛋糕跟前停一会儿，回想那突如其来的不幸，也就是井边提水的村民们所说的那件事。这个习惯一直持续到前不久。那件事发生后，她的人生便被蒙上了阴影。那天早上，突然传来消息说骑兵团即将奉令开拔，离开的日子就在两天后。当时，他们第二次结婚公告已经通过，却等不到第三次了，于是匆忙征求意见该怎么办。最后，大家认为在如此忙乱的情形下举行结婚仪式是不明智的，况且仪式来不来得及办还很难说。
Before the fire the young woman in question was now seated on a low stool, in the stillness of reverie, and a toddling boy played about the floor around her. 此时，这位年轻女子正坐在炉火前的一张矮凳上，静静地出神。一个蹒跚学步的小男孩在她身畔的地板上玩耍着。
‘Ah, Mrs. Stone!’ said Selina, rising slowly. “啊，斯通太太！”塞利娜边说边慢慢站起身来，
‘How kind of you to come in. “你来了真是太好了。
You’ll bide to supper? 等吃了晚饭再走吧？
Mother has told you the strange news, of course?’ 妈妈肯定已经把那个奇怪的消息告诉你了吧？”
‘No. “还没有。
But I heard it outside, that is, that you’d had a letter from Mr. Clark—Sergeant-Major Clark, as they say he is now—and that he’s coming to make it up with ’ee.’ 但我听外边的人说了，说你收到克拉克先生的一封信——他们说他现在是军士长了——他要来跟你重续前缘。”
‘Yes; coming to-night—all the way from the north of England where he’s quartered. “是的，他今晚就来——大老远从他驻扎的英格兰北部赶来。
I don’t know whether I’m happy or—frightened at it. 他要来了，我不知道我是高兴，还是——害怕。
Of course I always believed that if he was alive he’d come and keep his solemn vow to me. 当然，我一直认为如果他还活着，肯定会信守他对我的神圣誓言，回来找我。
But when it is printed that a man is killed—what can you think?’ 但是，当时他阵亡的消息都白纸黑字印在纸上了——我还能指望什么呢？”
‘It was printed?’ “印在纸上了？”
‘Why, yes. “唉，是呀。
After the Battle of the Alma the book of the names of the killed and wounded was nailed up against Casterbridge Town Hall door. 阿尔马河战役之后，伤亡人员名册就钉在卡斯特桥市政厅门口。
’Twas on a Saturday, and I walked there o’ purpose to read and see for myself; for I’d heard that his name was down. 那是个星期六，我走到那儿，想亲眼看看名册，因为我听说那上面有他的名字。
There was a crowd of people round the book, looking for the names of relations; and I can mind that when they saw me they made way for me—knowing that we’d been just going to be married—and that, as you may say, I belonged to him. 名册旁边围了一群人，都在找自己亲属的名字。看得出来，他们一看见我就给我让路——因为大家都知道我们本来马上就要结婚了，也知道我——可以说是已经属于他了。
Well, I reached up my arm, and turned over the farrels of the book, and under the “killed” I read his surname, but instead of “John” they’d printed “James,” and I thought ’twas a mistake, and that it must be he. 唉，于是我就抬起胳膊，一页页翻阅名册，在‘阵亡名单’一栏下，我看到了他的姓，但名字不是‘约翰’，而是‘詹姆斯’。我想他们是把他的名字印错了，那肯定是他。
Who could have guessed there were two nearly of one name in one regiment.’ 谁能想到一个兵团里有两个名字那么相近的人呢。”
‘Well—he’s coming to finish the wedding of ’ee as may be said; so never mind, my dear. “唉——他可能就是来跟你履行婚约的吧。所以不要担心了，亲爱的。
All’s well that ends well.’ 结果好就是好。”
‘That’s what he seems to say. “他信里似乎是这个意思。
But then he has not heard yet about Mr. Miller; and that’s what rather terrifies me. 但他写信时还不知道米勒先生的事呢，我怕的就是这一点。
Luckily my marriage with him next week was to have been by licence, and not banns, as in John’s case; and it was not so well known on that account. 幸好我和米勒先生下周的婚事只办了结婚证书，而不像和约翰那回是发结婚公告，所以知道的人没那么多。
Still, I don’t know what to think.’ 但我心里还是没个主意。”
‘Everything seems to come just ’twixt cup and lip with ’ee, don’t it now, Miss Paddock. “你的事好像总是好事多磨，对不对，帕多克小姐？
Two weddings broke off—’tis odd! 两桩婚事都吹了——这可不多见！
How came you to accept Mr. Miller, my dear?’ 你怎么会接受米勒先生呢，亲爱的？”
‘He’s been so good and faithful! “他一直对我那么好，那么忠诚！
Not minding about the child at all; for he knew the rights of the story. 他知道实情，却一点儿也不介意我有孩子。
He’s dearly fond o’ Johnny, you know—just as if ’twere his own—isn’t he, my duck? 要知道，他非常喜欢约翰尼——把他当成自己的儿子——是不是，宝贝儿？
Do Mr. Miller love you or don’t he?’ 米勒先生爱不爱你？”
‘Iss! “爱！
An’ I love Mr. Miller,’ said the toddler. 我也爱米勒先生！”小娃娃答道。
‘Well, you see, Mrs. Stone, he said he’d make me a comfortable home; and thinking ’twould be a good thing for Johnny, Mr. Miller being so much better off than me, I agreed at last, just as a widow might—which is what I have always felt myself; ever since I saw what I thought was John’s name printed there. “唔，你瞧，斯通太太，米勒先生说会给我一个舒适的家，而且他比我富裕多了，我觉得这对约翰尼是件好事。我最后终于接受了他，一个寡妇还能指望什么呢——自从我认定约翰的名字在阵亡名单上，我就一直把自己当个寡妇。
I hope John will forgive me!’ 但愿约翰能原谅我！”
‘So he will forgive ’ee, since ’twas no manner of wrong to him. “他会原谅你的，因为对他来说你一点儿也没做错。
He ought to have sent ’ee a line, saying ’twas another man.’ 他本该写封信给你的，告诉你阵亡名单上是另一个人。”
Selina’s mother entered. 塞利娜的母亲走了进来。
‘We’ve not known of this an hour, Mrs. Stone,’ she said. “我们知道这消息还不到一个钟头呢，斯通太太，”她说，
‘The letter was brought up from Lower Mellstock Post-office by one of the school children, only this afternoon. “信今天下午才到，是一个上学的孩子从下麦尔斯托克邮局捎来的。
Mr. Miller was coming here this very night to settle about the wedding doings. 米勒先生今晚正要过来商定婚礼的安排。
Hark! 听！
Is that your father? 是你爸爸回来了吗？
Or is it Mr. Miller already come?’ 还是米勒先生已经来了？”
The footsteps entered the porch; there was a brushing on the mat, and the door of the room sprung back to disclose a rubicund man about thirty years of age, of thriving master-mechanic appearance and obviously comfortable temper. 门廊上响起了脚步声，接着是在门口地垫上蹭鞋子的声音。房门开了，一个三十岁左右、面色红润的男子出现在门口，他有一副技术工人的强壮身躯，而且明显脾气不错。
On seeing the child, and before taking any notice whatever of the elders, the comer made a noise like the crowing of a cock and flapped his arms as if they were wings, a method of entry which had the unqualified admiration of Johnny. 来人一看到孩子，还没来得及注意到屋里的大人们，就一边模仿公鸡打鸣，一边像拍打翅膀似的拍打着两臂。约翰尼对这种出场方式喜欢得不得了。
‘Yes—it is he,’ said Selina constrainedly advancing. “是的——是他来了。”塞利娜说着，不自然地迎上前去。
‘What—were you all talking about me, my dear?’ said the genial young man when he had finished his crowing and resumed human manners. “怎么——你们刚才在谈论我吗，亲爱的？”年轻人和颜悦色地问道。他模仿完公鸡打鸣，恢复了常人的举止。
‘Why what’s the matter,’ he went on. “哎呀，怎么了？”他接着问道，
‘You look struck all of a heap.’ “你们看起来惊慌失措的。”
Mr. Miller spread an aspect of concern over his own face, and drew a chair up to the fire. 米勒先生把一把椅子拖到炉火前，脸上也露出了担忧的神情。
‘O mother, would you tell Mr. Miller, if he don’t know?’ “唉，妈妈，要是米勒先生还不知道，你能告诉他吗？”
‘Mister Miller! and going to be married in six days!’ he interposed. “是六天后就要结婚的米勒先生！”他插嘴道。
‘Ah—he don’t know it yet!’ murmured Mrs. Paddock. “啊——看来他还不知道呢！”帕多克太太咕哝道。
‘Know what?’ “知道什么？”
‘Well—John Clark—now Sergeant-Major Clark—wasn’t shot at Alma after all. “呃——约翰·克拉克——现在是军士长克拉克了——原来他没有在阿尔马河战役中阵亡。
’Twas another of almost the same name.’ 死的是跟他名字很像的一个人。”
‘Now that’s interesting! “这下有意思了！
There were several cases like that.’ 以前也出过几次这样的事。”
‘And he’s home again; and he’s coming here to-night to see her.’ “而且他又回来了，今晚就要来见她。”
‘Whatever shall I say, that he may not be offended with what I’ve done?’ interposed Selina. “我该说什么，他才能不为我的所作所为生气呢？”塞利娜插话道。
‘But why should it matter if he be?’ “可他生气又有什么关系呢？”
‘O! “唉！
I must agree to be his wife if he forgives me—of course I must.’ 他要是原谅了我，我就必须答应跟他结婚——我当然必须答应。”
‘Must! “必须！
But why not say nay, Selina, even if he do forgive ’ee?’ 可你为什么不拒绝呢，塞利娜，即使他真的原谅了你？”
‘O no! “哦，不！
How can I without being wicked? 我要是拒绝他，就太狠心了！
You were very very kind, Mr. Miller, to ask me to have you; no other man would have done it after what had happened; and I agreed, even though I did not feel half so warm as I ought. 米勒先生，你向我求婚，真是太善良了。经过了那些事，没有其他男人会向我求婚的，我也答应了，尽管我没有感觉到应有的那种温暖，连一半都没有。
Yet it was entirely owing to my believing him in the grave, as I knew that if he were not he would carry out his promise; and this shows that I was right in trusting him.’ 然而，我答应你完全是因为我确信他已经死了，因为我知道如果他没死，他一定会履行承诺。今天的事证明了我信任他没有错。”
‘Yes...He must be a goodish sort of fellow,’ said Mr. Miller, for a moment so impressed with the excellently faithful conduct of the sergeant-major of dragoons that he disregarded its effect upon his own position. “是啊……他肯定是个不错的家伙。”米勒先生说道。一时间，他也被骑兵军士长高尚、忠诚的行为深深打动了，忘记了对方的行为会给自己的处境带来怎样的不利影响。
He sighed slowly and added, ‘Well, Selina, ’tis for you to say. 他缓缓地叹了口气，又说道：“好吧，塞利娜。这件事由你来决定。
I love you, and I love the boy; and there’s my chimney-corner and sticks o’ furniture ready for ’ee both.’ 我爱你，也爱你的儿子。我已经为你们俩把壁炉和几件家具都准备好了。”
‘Yes, I know! “是的，我知道！
But I mustn’t hear it any more now,’ murmured Selina quickly. 可我现在一点儿也听不下去了，”塞利娜急匆匆地咕哝道，
‘John will be here soon. “约翰很快就要到了。
I hope he’ll see how it all was when I tell him. 但愿我告诉他之后，他能理解我当时的处境。
If so be I could have written it to him it would have been better.’ 我要是早点儿写封信告诉他就好了，那样可能会好些。”
‘You think he doesn’t know a single word about our having been on the brink o’t. “你觉得他完全不知道我们马上就要结婚了。
But perhaps it’s the other way—he’s heard of it and that may have brought him. 但没准儿相反呢——没准儿他就是听说了这个消息才赶来的。”
‘Ah—perhaps he has!’ she said brightening. “啊——没准儿他就是听说了！”她说道，面露喜色，
‘And already forgives me.’ “而且已经原谅我了。”
‘If not, speak out straight and fair, and tell him exactly how it fell out. “他要是还没听说，你就直截了当、一五一十地把事情的来龙去脉告诉他。
If he’s a man he’ll see it.’ 如果他是个男子汉，就会理解的。”
‘O he’s a man true enough. “唉，他的确是个男子汉。
But I really do think I shan’t have to tell him at all, since you’ve put it to me that way!’ 但是你跟我那么一解释，我真觉得我连告诉他的必要都没有了！”
As it was now Johnny’s bedtime he was carried upstairs, and when Selina came down again her mother observed with some anxiety, ‘I fancy Mr. Clark must be here soon if he’s coming; and that being so, perhaps Mr. Miller wouldn’t mind—wishing us good-night! since you are so determined to stick to your sergeant-major.’ 约翰尼的睡觉时间到了，塞利娜抱他上楼去。她再下楼时，她母亲语带担忧地说：“我想克拉克先生要来的话，肯定马上就要到了，那么，也许米勒先生不介意——先跟我们道晚安吧。既然你下定决心追随你的军士长。”
A little bitterness bubbled amid the closing words. 最后这句话带着一丝苦涩。
‘It would be less awkward, Mr. Miller not being here—if he will allow me to say it.’ “米勒先生不在场，可能会少些尴尬——如果他允许我这样说。”
‘To be sure; to be sure,’ the master-wheelwright exclaimed with instant conviction, rising alertly from his chair. “那当然，那当然。”车轮师傅立刻就被说服了，一边大声说着，一边利索地从椅子上站起身。
‘Lord bless my soul,’ he said, taking up his hat and stick, ‘and we to have been married in six days! “上帝保佑！”他边说边拿起帽子和手杖，“我们本来六天后就要结婚了！
But Selina—you’re right. 但是塞利娜——你是对的。
You do belong to the child’s father since he’s alive. 既然孩子的爸爸还活着，你的确属于他。
I’ll try to make the best of it.’ 我会尽力接受这一切的。”
Before the generous Miller had got further there came a knock to the door accompanied by the noise of wheels. 宽宏大量的米勒还没来得及说下去，敲门声就响了起来，随之而来的还有车轮声。
‘I thought I heard something driving up!’ said Mrs Paddock. “我好像听到有车子过来！”帕多克太太说道。
They heard Mr. Paddock, who had been smoking in the room opposite, rise and go to the door, and in a moment a voice familiar enough to Selina was audibly saying, ‘At last I am here again—not without many interruptions! 他们听到之前在对面房间抽烟的帕多克先生站起身来去开门，很快，传来了一个对塞利娜而言再熟悉不过的声音：“我总算又来了——真是一波三折啊！
How is it with ’ee, Mr. Paddock? 你怎么样呀，帕多克先生？
And how is she? 她呢，她怎么样？
Thought never to see me again, I suppose?’ 你们以为再也见不到我了，对吧？”
A step with a clink of spurs in it struck upon the entry floor. 一只脚踏在了门口的地板上，伴随着马刺的咯噔声。
‘Danged if I bain’t catched!’ murmured Mr. Miller, forgetting company-speech. “我要是被发现就完蛋了！”米勒先生咕哝道，连礼貌的语言都忘了，
‘Never mind—I may as well meet him here as elsewhere; and I should like to see the chap, and make friends with en, as he seems one o’ the right sort.’ “没关系——我就是不在这儿碰到他，在别处也有可能碰到。我倒想见见这小伙子，跟他交个朋友，因为他似乎人不错。”
He returned to the fireplace just as the sergeant-major was ushered in. 他刚回到壁炉前，军士长就被领了进来。
III 三
He was a good specimen of the long-service soldier of those days; a not unhandsome man, with a certain undemonstrative dignity, which some might have said to be partly owing to the stiffness of his uniform about his neck, the high stock being still worn. 他可以算是那时候长期服役老兵的一个典型：长相颇为英俊，又带着几分含蓄和严肃。有人可能会说，这种印象一部分是由于他还围着高高的宽大硬领巾，使得制服的领子十分挺。
He was much stouter than when Selina had parted from him. 比起和塞利娜分别的时候，现在的他结实多了。
Although she had not meant to be demonstrative she ran across to him directly she saw him, and he held her in his arms and kissed her. 尽管塞利娜没想刻意表示亲切，但一看到他，还是径直跑了过去，而他则把她揽到怀里，亲吻着。
Then in much agitation she whispered something to him, at which he seemed to be much surprised. 接着，她颇为激动地朝他耳语了几句，他似乎大吃一惊。
‘He’s just put to bed,’ she continued. “我刚刚安顿他睡觉了，”她继续说道，
‘You can go up and see him. “你可以上楼看看他。
I knew you’d come if you were alive! 我早就知道，你要是还活着，一定会回来！
But I had quite gi’d you up for dead. 不过，我以为你死了，已经不怎么指望你来了。
You’ve been home in England ever since the war ended?’ 战争一结束你就回到英格兰了吗？”
‘Yes, dear.’ “是的，亲爱的。”
‘Why didn’t you come sooner?’ “那你怎么不早点儿来呢？”
‘That’s just what I ask myself! “我也这么问我自己呢！
Why was I such a sappy as not to hurry here the first day I set foot on shore! 我怎么那么傻，没在上岸的第一天就赶到这儿来！
Well, who’d have thought it—you are as pretty as ever!’ 唉，谁想得到呢——你还像原来一样漂亮！”
He relinquished her to peep upstairs a little way, where, by looking through the ballusters, he could see Johnny’s cot just within an open door. 他放开她，往楼上走了几步，悄悄看了看孩子。透过栏杆他可以看到约翰尼的婴儿床，就在一扇打开的门内。
On his stepping down again Mr. Miller was preparing to depart. 他下楼时，米勒先生正准备离开。
‘Now, what’s this? “嗳，这是什么意思？
I am sorry to see anybody going the moment I’ve come,’ expostulated the sergeant-major. 我可不愿意看到我刚来就有人要走，”军士长抗议道，
‘I thought we might make an evening of it. “我以为我们能痛快地玩一个晚上呢。
There’s a nine gallon cask o’ “Phoenix” beer outside in the trap, and a ham, and half a rawmil’ cheese; for I thought you might be short o’ forage in a lonely place like this; and it struck me we might like to ask in a neighbour or two. 外面马车上有一桶九加仑的‘凤凰’啤酒、一大块火腿，还有半桶干酪，因为我想着你们在这么偏远的地方可能会缺少吃的。对了，我突然想到，我们不妨请一两个邻居过来。
But perhaps it would be taking a liberty?’ 不过，我这样自作主张，会不会有些失礼？”
‘O no, not at all,’ said Mr. Paddock, who was now in the room, in a judicial measured manner. “哦，不，一点儿也不失礼，”帕多克先生进了屋子，以一种法官般的审慎态度说道，
‘Very thoughtful of ’ee, only ’twas not necessary, for we had just laid in an extry stock of eatables and drinkables in preparation for the coming event.’ “你考虑得很周到，只可惜没有必要了，因为我们为了准备即将到来的大日子，刚刚额外贮存了一批吃的喝的。”
‘’Twas very kind, upon my heart,’ said the soldier, ‘to think me worth such a jocund preparation, since you could only have got my letter this morning.’ “想想你们这么高兴地迎接我，我从心底感激你们的好意，”士兵说道，“因为你们最快也就是今天早上才接到我的信。”
Selina gazed at her father to stop him, and exchanged embarrassed glances with Miller. 塞利娜盯着她父亲，不让他再说下去，并和米勒先生尴尬地对视了一眼。
Contrary to her hopes Sergeant-Major Clark plainly did not know that the preparations referred to were for something quite other than his own visit. 和她所期望的相反，克拉克军士长全然不知塞利娜父亲提到的准备其实和他的来访毫无关系。
The movement of the horse outside, and the impatient tapping of a whip-handle upon the vehicle reminded them that Clark’s driver was still in waiting. 门外传来马儿的响动，还有鞭子的手柄敲动车身的焦急声音。人们这才想起克拉克的车夫还在外面等着。
The provisions were brought into the house, and the cart dismissed. 人们把食物搬进房子，把马车打发走了。
Miller, with very little pressure indeed, accepted an invitation to supper, and a few neighbours were induced to come in to make up a cheerful party. 米勒几乎是欣然接受了晚餐邀请，几个邻居也被请了过来，好增添些快活的气氛。
During the laying of the meal, and throughout its continuance, Selina, who sat beside her first intended husband, tried frequently to break the news to him of her engagement to the other—now terminated so suddenly, and so happily for her heart, and her sense of womanly virtue. 从晚餐端上桌，一直到吃完饭，塞利娜一直坐在她的第一任未婚夫身旁，屡次想把她和另一个人订婚的消息告诉他——不过，这婚约现在突然解除了，她感到由衷高兴，感觉自己恪守了女人的美德。
But the talk ran entirely upon the late war; and though fortified by half a horn of the strong ale brought by the sergeant-major she decided that she might have a better opportunity when supper was over of revealing the situation to him in private. 可谈话完全是围绕不久前的战争展开的。尽管塞利娜用角制酒杯喝了半杯军士长带来的烈性啤酒，胆子壮了些，但她仍然决定还是等到晚餐结束后，再找个更好的时机私下把实情告诉他。
Having supped, Clark leaned back at ease in his chair and looked around. 晚饭后，克拉克自在地靠在椅背上，环顾四周。
‘We used sometimes to have a dance in that other room after supper, Selina dear, I recollect. “亲爱的塞利娜，我想起来了，从前我们有时会在晚饭后去那间屋子跳舞。
We used to clear out all the furniture into this room before beginning. 开始前，我们会把那个屋里的家具全都移到这个屋里来。
Have you kept up such goings on?’ 你们后来还一直这样吗？”
‘No, not at all!’ said his sweetheart, sadly. “不，一次也没有过！”他的心上人忧伤地答道。
‘We were not unlikely to revive it in a few days,’ said Mr. Paddock. “几天后，我们倒不是没有可能再跳一次舞，”帕多克先生说，
‘But, howsomever, there’s seemingly many a slip, as the saying is.’ “不过，常言道，杯到嘴边还会失手啊。”
‘Yes, I’ll tell John all about that by and by!’ interposed Selina; at which, perceiving that the secret which he did not like keeping was to be kept even yet, her father held his tongue with some show of testiness. “是的，我一会儿就都告诉约翰！”塞利娜插嘴道。一听这话，塞利娜的父亲意识到，即使他不愿藏着这个秘密，他还是得继续藏下去。他住了嘴，脸上表现出明显的不悦。
The subject of a dance having been broached, to put the thought in practice was the feeling of all. 既然提到了跳舞，大家就觉得应该把这个想法付诸实践。
Soon after the tables and chairs were borne from the opposite room to this by zealous hands, and two of the villagers sent home for a fiddle and tambourine, when the majority began to tread a measure well known in that secluded vale. 很快，热心的人们就把对面房间的桌椅搬到了这间屋子，两位村民还差人回家去取小提琴和小手鼓。这时，大多数人便踩着这偏僻村庄里流行的舞步，跳起舞来。
Selina naturally danced with the sergeant-major, not altogether to her father’s satisfaction, and to the real uneasiness of her mother, both of whom would have preferred a postponement of festivities till the rashly anticipated relationship between their daughter and Clark in the past had been made fact by the church’s ordinances. 塞利娜自然是和军士长跳舞。她父亲对此不甚满意，她母亲更是十分不安。两位家长的本意是，等女儿和克拉克之前的轻率婚约得到教堂的法令认可之后再举行庆祝活动。
They did not, however, express a positive objection, Mr. Paddock remembering, with self-reproach, that it was owing to his original strongly expressed disapproval of Selina’s being a soldier’s wife that the wedding had been delayed, and finally hindered—with worse consequences than were expected; and ever since the misadventure brought about by his government he had allowed events to steer their own courses. 然而，两人都没有明确表示反对，因为帕多克先生记得，正是因为他起初强烈反对塞利娜和军人结婚，婚事才被耽搁了，最终没有结成——而后果又是出乎意料地严重，他对此十分自责。自这场因他干涉而起的不幸之后，他便对一切都撒手不管，听天由命了。
‘My tails will surely catch in your spurs, John!’ murmured the daughter of the house, as she whirled around upon his arm with the rapt soul and look of a somnambulist. “我的裙摆肯定会挂到你的马刺上，约翰！”房主的女儿喃喃道。她在他的臂弯里旋转，内心欣喜若狂，脸上带着梦游般的表情，
‘I didn’t know we should dance, or I would have put on my other frock.’ “我先前不知道我们要跳舞，不然我就穿另一条裙子了。”
‘I’ll take care, my love. “我会当心的，亲爱的。
We’ve danced here before. 我们以前也在这儿跳过舞。
Do you think your father objects to me now? 你觉得现在你爸爸还不喜欢我吗？
I’ve risen in rank. 我的军衔升高了。
I fancy he’s still a little against me.’ 可我觉得他对我还是有点儿反感。”
‘He has repented, times enough.’ “他后悔过太多次了。”
‘And so have I! “我也是！
If I had married you then ’twould have saved many a misfortune. 要是我当时就娶了你，该省掉多少不幸啊。
I have sometimes thought it might have been possible to rush the ceremony through somehow before I left; though we were only in the second asking, were we? 我有时会想，我走之前想办法赶紧把仪式办了，没准儿都是有可能的。不过当时我们才贴了第二次结婚公告，对吗？
And even if I had come back straight here when we returned from the Crimea, and married you then, how much happier I should have been!’ 即使是部队从克里米亚半岛返回后，我直接就回来娶你，我也会比现在高兴得多啊！”
‘Dear John, to say that! “亲爱的约翰，你还说呢！
Why didn’t you?’ 当时你为什么没回来呀？”
‘O—dilatoriness and want of thought, and a fear of facing your father after so long. “唉——有事耽搁了，我自己也没考虑好，再说隔了那么久，我不敢面对你爸爸。
I was in hospital a great while, you know. 要知道，我住了好长时间的院。
But how familiar the place seems again! 不过，这地方对我来说又熟悉起来了！
What’s that I saw on the beaufet in the other room? 那间屋里的食柜上摆着什么东西？
It never used to be there. 以前它可不在那儿。
A sort of withered corpse of a cake—not an old bride-cake surely?’ 它像是个干巴巴的旧蛋糕——肯定不是个结婚蛋糕吧？”
‘Yes, John, ours. “是的，约翰，是我们的结婚蛋糕。
’Tis the very one that was made for our wedding three years ago.’ 正是三年前为我们的婚礼准备的那一个。”
‘Sakes alive! “我的老天！
Why, time shuts up together, and all between then and now seems not to have been! 唉，时间一晃就过去了，从那时到现在，中间的一切就跟没发生过似的！
What became of that wedding-gown that they were making in this room, I remember—a bluish, whitish, frothy thing?’ 他们在这间屋子里缝制的那件结婚礼服呢？我记得那裙子很轻巧，带点儿蓝又泛着白。”
‘I have that too.’ “我也还留着呢。”
‘Really!... “真的……
Why, Selina—’ 哎呀，塞利娜——”
‘Yes!’ “是的，留着呢！”
‘Why not put it on now?’ “为什么不现在把它穿上呢？”
‘Wouldn’t it seem—. “那样会不会显得有点儿——
And yet, O how I should like to! 唉，可是我多想穿啊！
It would remind them all, if we told them what it was, how we really meant to be married on that bygone day!’ 要是告诉大家那是我当初的结婚礼服，所有人就会记起，我们当初是多么真心地想要结婚啊！”
Her eyes were again laden with wet. 她的眼眶又蓄满了泪水。
‘Yes...The pity that we didn’t—the pity!’ “是啊……真遗憾我们没结成婚，太遗憾了——太遗憾了！”

Having become accustomed by her residence at home to the business carried on by her father, she surprised them one day by going off with the child to Chalk-Newton, in the direction of the town of Ivell, and opening a miniature fruit and vegetable shop, attending Ivell market with her produce. 她之前住在家里，已经熟悉了父亲的生意。有一天，她突然带着孩子去了乔克－牛顿，伊夫尔镇那边的一个小村子，开了一家小小的蔬果店，并把她种植的农产品拿到伊夫尔的市场上卖。这让她的父母大吃一惊。
Her business grew somewhat larger, and it was soon sufficient to enable her to support herself and the boy in comfort. 她的生意做大了些，很快就足以让自己和儿子过上舒适的日子了。
She called herself ‘Mrs. John Clark’ from the day of leaving home, and painted the name on her signboard—no man forbidding her. 从离家那天起，她就自称“约翰·克拉克太太”，并把这个名字写在商店招牌上——没人阻止她这样做。
By degrees the pain of her state was forgotten in her new circumstances, and getting to be generally accepted as the widow of a sergeant-major of dragoons—an assumption which her modest and mournful demeanour seemed to substantiate—her life became a placid one, her mind being nourished by the melancholy luxury of dreaming what might have been her future in New Zealand with John, if he had only lived to take her there. 在新的环境里，她渐渐忘记了自己的痛苦处境，人们也慢慢接受了她，认为她就是一位龙骑兵军士长的遗孀——她低调、忧伤的举止似乎足以证明她的身份。在宁静的生活中，她幻想着假如约翰还活着，带她去了新西兰，她的未来将会是什么样子。她的心在这忧伤而又愉悦的白日梦中得到了滋养。
Her only travels now were a journey to Ivell on market-days, and once a fortnight to the churchyard in which Clark lay, there to tend, with Johnny’s assistance, as widows are wont to do, the flowers she had planted upon his grave. 她所有的外出活动仅限于在集市的日子去趟伊夫尔，再就是每隔两周去一次安葬克拉克的教堂墓地，在约翰尼的帮助下照料她在他墓前种下的花儿——寡妇们通常都这么做。
On a day about eighteen months after his unexpected decease, Selina was surprised in her lodging over her little shop by a visit from Bartholomew Miller. 军士长意外死亡后约一年半的一天，巴塞洛缪·米勒拜访了塞利娜位于小店楼上的住处，她吃了一惊。
He had called on her once or twice before, on which occasions he had used without a word of comment the name by which she was known. 他之前来拜访过她一两次，也用大家熟知的“克拉克太太”这个名字来称呼她，并不作评论。
‘I’ve come this time,’ he said, ‘less because I was in this direction than to ask you, Mrs. Clark, what you mid well guess. “我这次来，”他说，“不是正好顺路，而是来请求你的。你大概猜得到我请求什么，克拉克太太。
I’ve come o’ purpose, in short.’ 简单说吧，我是来求婚的。”
She smiled. 她笑了。
‘’Tis to ask me again to marry you?’ “是再次请求我嫁给你吗？”
‘Yes, of course. “对啊，当然了。
You see, his coming back for ’ee proved what I always believed of ’ee, though others didn’t. 你瞧，他回来找你，恰恰证明了你的尊严，我一直相信你，尽管其他人不信。
There’s nobody but would be glad to welcome you to our parish again, now you’ve showed your independence and acted up to your trust in his promise. 你已经表明了你的独立，你信任他的承诺，并付诸行动。现在，没有人不欢迎你回到我们教区了。
Well, my dear, will you come?’ 好啦，亲爱的，你会回来吗？”
‘I’d rather bide as Mrs. Clark, I think,’ she answered. “我想，我还是宁愿继续当克拉克太太，”她回答道，
‘I am not ashamed of my position at all; for I am John’s widow in the eyes of Heaven.’ “我一点儿也不为我的身份感到羞耻，因为老天承认我是约翰的遗孀。”
‘I quite agree—that’s why I’ve come. “我很赞同——这也是我为什么会来。
Still, you won’t like to be always straining at this shop-keeping and market-standing; and ’twould be better for Johnny if you had nothing to do but tend him.’ 但是，你也不愿意总这么辛苦地开店、赶集吧，再说，如果你能什么都不做一心照料约翰尼的话，对他也更好。”
He here touched the only weak spot in Selina’s resistance to his proposal—the good of the boy. 他这番话击中了塞利娜拒绝同他结婚的唯一软肋——为孩子好。
To promote that there were other men she might have married offhand without loving them if they had asked her to; but though she had known the worthy speaker from her youth, she could not for the moment fancy herself happy as Mrs. Miller. 为了孩子，要是有别的她不爱的男人向她求婚，她说不定也会随便嫁了。然而，尽管她从小就认识眼前这个可敬的男人，此时想到要成为米勒太太，却还是开心不起来。
He paused awhile. 他沉默了片刻。
‘I ought to tell ’ee, Mrs. Clark,’ he said by and by, ‘that marrying is getting to be a pressing question with me. “我得告诉你，克拉克太太，”一会儿，他又说道，“对我来说，结婚已经是个很紧迫的问题了。
Not on my own account at all. 这丝毫不是为了我自己。
The truth is, that mother is growing old, and I am away from home a good deal, so that it is almost necessary there should be another person in the house with her besides me. 实话实说，我妈妈上了年纪，我又经常不在家，所以，除了我之外，家里几乎必须得再有一个人陪着她。
That’s the practical consideration which forces me to think of taking a wife, apart from my wish to take you; and you know there’s nobody in the world I care for so much.’ 这个实际问题迫使我不得不考虑娶个妻子，而且我也是真心想娶你，你知道的，这世上我最在乎的，除了你再没有别人了。”
She said something about there being far better women than she, and other natural commonplaces; but assured him she was most grateful to him for feeling what he felt, as indeed she sincerely was. 她说世上还有比她强得多的女人，还说了些其他这种场合下自然要说的客套话。但她要他相信，她非常感激他对她的感情，事实上她也是真的很感激。
However, Selina would not consent to be the useful third person in his comfortable home—at any rate just then. 然而，塞利娜还是不愿意到他舒适的家里去当那个有用的第三人——至少此时此刻不愿意。
He went away, after taking tea with her, without discerning much hope for him in her good-bye. 他同她喝过茶就走了。她的道别没有让他感到太多希望。

After that evening she saw and heard nothing of him for a great while. 那晚之后，她很久没再见过他，也没听到过他的任何消息。
Her fortnightly journeys to the sergeant-major’s grave were continued, whenever weather did not hinder them; and Mr. Miller must have known, she thought, of this custom of hers. 只要天气允许，她还是每两周带孩子去军士长的墓地。她想，米勒先生肯定知道她的这个习惯。
But though the churchyard was not nearly so far from his homestead as was her shop at Chalk-Newton, he never appeared in the accidental way that lovers use. 然而，尽管从他家到墓地比从他家到她在乔克－牛顿的店近得多，他却从来没有像通常的恋人那样，假装同她不期而遇。
An explanation was forthcoming in the shape of a letter from her mother, who casually mentioned that Mr. Bartholomew Miller had gone away to the other side of Shottsford-Forum to be married to a thriving dairyman’s daughter that he knew there. 不久，她从她母亲的一封来信中得到了解释。信中不经意间提到，巴塞洛缪·米勒先生去了肖茨福德农场的另一边，娶了他认识的一个富裕奶牛场主的女儿。
His chief motive, it was reported, had been less one of love than a wish to provide a companion for his aged mother. 据说他结婚的主要动机不是爱情，而是想给他年迈的母亲找个伴儿。
Selina was practical enough to know that she had lost a good and possibly the only opportunity of settling in life after what had happened, and for a moment she regretted her independence. 塞利娜足够现实，知道这样一来，她便失去了一个安顿下来的好机会——而且很有可能是唯一的机会。有那么一会儿，她对自己的独立感到后悔了。
But she became calm on reflection, and to fortify herself in her course started that afternoon to tend the sergeant-major’s grave, in which she took the same sober pleasure as at first. 但细想之后，她便平静下来。为了增强自己走下去的决心，那天下午她便启程去给军士长扫墓，感受那种一如既往的、庄重的快乐。
On reaching the churchyard and turning the corner towards the spot as usual, she was surprised to perceive another woman, also apparently a respectable widow, and with a tiny boy by her side, bending over Clark’s turf, and spudding up with the point of her umbrella some ivy-roots that Selina had reverently planted there to form an evergreen mantle over the mound. 到了墓地，她像往常一样转过一个拐角，往军士长的墓走去。这时，她惊奇地发现另一个女人正站在克拉克墓前的草地上，俯身用伞尖铲掉一些常春藤的根。常春藤是塞利娜满心虔诚种在那里的，好让坟上有个常青的保护罩。那女人显然也是个体面的寡妇，身边跟着一个小男孩。
‘What are you digging up my ivy for!’ cried Selina, rushing forward so excitedly that Johnny tumbled over a grave with the force of the tug she gave his hand in her sudden start. “你把我的常春藤挖出来干什么！”塞利娜叫道。她激动地冲上前去，拉着约翰尼的手猛地一拽，孩子被一座墓绊倒了。
‘Your ivy?’ said the respectable woman. “你的常春藤？”那个体面女人问道。
‘Why yes! “嗨，当然了！
I planted it there—on my husband’s grave.’ 是我种在那儿的——种在我丈夫坟上的。”
‘Your husband’s!’ “你丈夫！”
‘Yes. “对。
The late Sergeant-Major Clark. 就是已故的克拉克军士长。
Anyhow, as good as my husband, for he was just going to be.’ 反正他实际上等于是我丈夫，因为他死前我们马上就要结婚了。”
‘Indeed. “真的？
But who may be my husband, if not he? 可谁又是我丈夫呢，除了他？
I am the only Mrs. John Clark, widow of the late Sergeant-Major of Dragoons, and this is his only son and heir.’ 我是唯一的约翰·克拉克太太，这位已故骑兵军士长的遗孀，这孩子是他唯一的子嗣。”
‘How can that be?’ faltered Selina, her throat seeming to stick together as she just began to perceive its possibility. “怎么可能呢？”塞利娜结结巴巴地说。她开始意识到这女人说的话有可能是真的，喉头一阵发紧，
‘He had been—going to marry me twice—and we were going to New Zealand.’ “他正要——再跟我办一次婚事——而且我们要一起去新西兰。”
‘Ah!—I remember about you,’ returned the legitimate widow calmly and not unkindly. “啊——我想起你来了，”那合法的寡妇平静而不失礼貌地答道，
‘You must be Selina; he spoke of you now and then, and said that his relations with you would always be a weight on his conscience. “你一定是塞利娜吧。他时不时会说起你，说他和你的关系将一直是他良心上的负担。
Well; the history of my life with him is soon told. 好吧，下面我来给你讲讲我和他的故事。
When he came back from the Crimea he became acquainted with me at my home in the north, and we were married within a month of first knowing each other. 他从克里米亚回来后，在我北部的家乡认识了我，我们相识不到一个月就结婚了。
Unfortunately, after living together a few months, we could not agree; and after a particularly sharp quarrel, in which, perhaps, I was most in the wrong—as I don’t mind owning here by his graveside—he went away from me, declaring he would buy his discharge and emigrate to New Zealand, and never come back to me any more. 不幸的是，共同生活几个月后，我们发现彼此合不来。有一次我们吵得特别厉害，那次可能大部分是我的错——我不介意在他坟墓旁边承认这一点。之后，他便离开了我，说他要拿到退伍许可，移民到新西兰去，再也不回我身边了。
The next thing I heard was that he had died suddenly at Mellstock at some low carouse; and as he had left me in such anger to live no more with me, I wouldn’t come down to his funeral, or do anything in relation to him. 随后我就听说他突然死在了麦尔斯托克某次低俗的狂欢宴会上。鉴于他离开我时那么生气，再也不肯和我共同生活，我也不肯来参加他的葬礼，或者做任何跟他有关的事情。
’Twas temper, I know, but that was the fact. 是我任性，我知道，但实情就是如此。
Even if we had parted friends it would have been a serious expense to travel three hundred miles to get there, for one who wasn’t left so very well off...I am sorry I pulled up your ivy-roots; but that common sort of ivy is considered a weed in my part of the country.’ 即使我们是友好分手，跑300英里赶到这儿来也是一大笔开销，因为他可没给我剩下多少钱……我连根拔了你的常春藤，真是抱歉，不过在我们北方人看来，这常见的常春藤就是一种杂草。”
