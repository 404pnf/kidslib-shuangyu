The Atheist’s Mass 无神论者做弥撒
by Honoré de Balzac 作者：巴尔扎克
Bianchon, a physician to whom science owes a fine system of theoretical physiology, and who, while still young, made himself a celebrity in the medical school of Paris, that central luminary to which European doctors do homage, practised surgery for a long time before he took up medicine. 毕安训是一名内科医生，在理论生理学方面颇有建树，年纪虽轻，却已身处欧洲医界所推崇的学术核心——巴黎医学院的名流之列。从事内科医学之前，他曾长时间从事外科。
His earliest studies were guided by one of the greatest of French surgeons, the illustrious Desplein, who flashed across science like a meteor. 他早年受法国伟大的外科医生、鼎鼎有名的德普兰的指导。德普兰曾像一颗流星一样在学界一闪而过。
By the consensus even of his enemies, he took with him to the tomb an incommunicable method. 甚至连他的对手都一致认同，他把一种无法传承的方法带入了坟墓。
Like all men of genius, he had no heirs; he carried everything in him, and carried it away with him. 像一切天才人物一样，他没有继承人。他与生俱来的一切，终将随他入土。
The glory of a surgeon is like that of an actor: they live only so long as they are alive, and their talent leaves no trace when they are gone. 荣耀之于外科医生如同荣耀之于演员，只在他们活着的时候存在，等他们离世了，他们的才华也会随着一并消弭。
Actors and surgeons, like great singers too, like the executants who by their performance increase the power of music tenfold, are all the heroes of a moment. 演员、外科医生、著名歌手以及通过演奏将音乐感染力扩大十倍的演奏家，他们也不过是一时的英杰。
Desplein is a case in proof of this resemblance in the destinies of such transient genius. 流星般匆匆而过的天才所拥有的宿命往往相似，德普兰便是一例。
His name, yesterday so famous, to-day almost forgotten, will survive in his special department without crossing its limits. 他的名字，昨日那般显赫，今日却几被淡忘，只会在其所处的专门领域存在，而无法越界。
For must there not be some extraordinary circumstances to exalt the name of a professor from the history of Science to the general history of the human race? 因为只有在很特殊的情况下，某位教授的大名才能突破科学史而彪炳人类通史，不是吗？
Had Desplein that universal command of knowledge which makes a man the living word, the great figure of his age? 凭借他学贯天人的渊博学识，德普兰是否能成为其所处时代的杰出人物呢？
Desplein had a godlike eye; he saw into the sufferer and his malady by an intuition, natural or acquired, which enabled him to grasp the diagnostics peculiar to the individual, to determine the very time, the hour, the minute when an operation should be performed, making due allowance for atmospheric conditions and peculiarities of individual temperament. 德普兰具有如神的眼光，他能凭借一种与生俱来的抑或是后天养成的直觉，看透患者内心及所患疾病。这种直觉使得他能针对不同个体作出准确的诊断，进而确定手术的时间，具体到小时和分钟，同时充分考虑到气候状况以及个体特质。
To proceed thus, hand in hand with nature, had he then studied the constant assimilation by living beings, of the elements contained in the atmosphere, or yielded by the earth to man who absorbs them, deriving from them a particular expression of life? 这一过程与大自然相得益彰。那么，他是否研究过大气中所包含的，或地球提供给人吸收的元素通过生物体不断结合，从而产生特殊的生命形式呢？
Did he work it all out by the power of deduction and analogy, to which we owe the genius of Cuvier? 他是通过演绎与类比获知这一过程的吗？正如天才居维叶一般？
Be this as it may, this man was in all the secrets of the human frame; he knew it in the past and in the future, emphasizing the present. 无论如何，他洞悉人体结构的奥妙，知晓其过往与未来，看重其现状。
But did he epitomize all science in his own person as Hippocrates did and Galen and Aristotle? 然而，他是否像希波克拉底、盖伦和亚里士多德那样，身兼诸学精要？
Did he guide a whole school towards new worlds? 他是否引导一个学派走向新殿堂？
No. 没有。
Though it is impossible to deny that this persistent observer of human chemistry possessed that antique science of the Mages, that is to say, knowledge of the elements in fusion, the causes of life, life antecedent to life, and what it must be in its incubation or ever it is, it must be confessed that, unfortunately, everything in him was purely personal. 不可否认，这位对生命化学孜孜不倦的研究者掌握着古老的魔术，即生命所需要素的融合、生命的起因与延续，还有生命产生之前和之后的知识。但必须要承认的是，可惜他所掌握的一切只属于个人。
Isolated during his life by his egoism, that egoism is now suicidal of his glory. 他的一生因自我主义而封闭，如今他的荣耀也因自私而葬送。
On his tomb there is no proclaiming statue to repeat to posterity the mysteries which genius seeks out at its own cost. 他的墓前没有雕像向后世讲述这位天才倾尽毕生心血发现的奥秘。
But perhaps Desplein’s genius was answerable for his beliefs, and for that reason mortal. 但或许德普兰的天赋来源于信念，也正因为如此，才终有一死。
To him the terrestrial atmosphere was a generative envelope; he saw the earth as an egg within its shell; and not being able to determine whether the egg or the hen first was, he would not recognize either the cock or the egg. 在他看来，地球大气层是一个能孕育万物的外壳。他将地球视作一个带壳的鸡蛋，既然无法证明是先有的鸡还是先有的蛋，他便对二者都不认可。
He believed neither in the antecedent animal nor the surviving spirit of man. 他既不相信人类由动物进化而来，也不相信死后灵魂不灭。
Desplein had no doubts; he was positive. 他凡事不抱怀疑，而是判断果决。
His bold and unqualified atheism was like that of many scientific men, the best men in the world, but invincible atheists—atheists such as religious people declare to be impossible. 科学家是世上最优秀的人，他就跟他们一样，是个彻底的无神论者，他坦率地坚持无神论，就像宗教人士坚持神的存在一样执著。
This opinion could scarcely exist otherwise in a man who was accustomed from his youth to dissect the creature above all others—before, during, and after life; to hunt through all his organs without ever finding the individual soul, which is indispensable to religious theory. 若非从年轻时起就经常出色地解剖未出生的、活着的，或是死去的生命体，他是不会有这一观点的。他研究了每一个器官，却没有察觉到宗教理论所坚持的必不可少的灵魂。
When he detected a cerebral centre, a nervous centre, and a centre for aerating the blood—the first two so perfectly complementary that in the latter years of his life he came to a conviction that the sense of hearing is not absolutely necessary for hearing, nor the sense of sight for seeing, and that the solar plexus could supply their place without any possibility of doubt—Desplein, thus finding two souls in man, confirmed his atheism by this fact, though it is no evidence against God. 他发现了人体有大脑中枢、神经中枢和血液供氧中枢，前两者完美地互相补充。于是，晚年时他确信视觉未必是“看”所必需的，听觉亦未必是“听”所必需的，因为腹腔神经丛可以毫无疑问地代替它们的功能。因此，德普兰在人体里发现两个“灵魂”，这一事实更坚定了他的无神论，尽管这并不表示上帝不存在。
This man died, it is said, in final impenitence, as do, unfortunately, many noble geniuses, whom God may forgive. 据说，这个人最终也未做忏悔，就像许多杰出的天才一样，就这样不幸地死去。上帝或许会原谅他们吧。
The life of this man, great as he was, was marred by many meannesses, to use the expression employed by his enemies, who were anxious to diminish his glory, but which it would be more proper to call apparent contradictions. 杰出如他，也不免被那些急于抹杀他荣耀的对手所诋毁，借这些人的话来说，他一生有许多瑕疵。与其称之为“瑕疵”，倒不如称之为显而易见的矛盾更为合适。
Envious people and fools, having no knowledge of the determinations by which superior spirits are moved, seize at once on superficial inconsistencies, to formulate an accusation and so to pass sentence on them. 嫉妒者与愚蠢者不懂杰出者的取舍，而是立刻抓住一些外在的矛盾，便去诟病非议继而以罪责相加。
If, subsequently, the proceedings thus attacked are crowned with success, showing the correlations of the preliminaries and the results, a few of the vanguard of calumnies always survive. 结果，即使所非议的事情获得成功，表明初步计划与结论之间的关联，一些先前的诽谤仍然会有留有影响。
In our day, for instance, Napoleon was condemned by our contemporaries when he spread his eagle’s wings to alight in England: only 1822 could explain 1804 and the flatboats at Boulogne. 例如，在我们这个时代，当拿破仑想将他的鹰翼伸到英国时就曾受到指责，只有到了1822年才能够解释1804年所发生的一切，还有停泊在布洛涅的平底船到底是怎么回事。
As, in Desplein, his glory and science were invulnerable, his enemies attacked his odd moods and his temper, whereas, in fact, he was simply characterized by what the English call eccentricity. 德普兰的荣耀与学术是无懈可击的，他的对手便转而攻击他古怪的脾气与性情。而事实上，他最鲜明的特征就是英国人所称的“怪癖”。
Sometimes very handsomely dressed, like Crebillon the tragical, he would suddenly affect extreme indifference as to what he wore; he was sometimes seen in a carriage, and sometimes on foot. 有时，他就像悲剧作家克雷比永一样，衣着潇洒，有时却突然对自己的着装满不在乎；有时人们看见他乘坐马车，有时见他步行。
By turns rough and kind, harsh and covetous on the surface, but capable of offering his whole fortune to his exiled masters—who did him the honor of accepting it for a few days—no man ever gave rise to such contradictory judgements. 他时而疾言厉色，时而温良和蔼，表面上贪财吝啬，却又能够将自己所有的财富赠予流放在外的老师，而这些老师也乐于赏脸，一度也曾接受馈赠。没人像他一样招致诸多大相径庭的评价。
Although to obtain a black ribbon, which physicians ought not to intrigue for, he was capable of dropping a prayer-book out of his pocket at Court, in his heart he mocked at everything; he had a deep contempt for men, after studying them from above and below, after detecting their genuine expression when performing the most solemn and the meanest acts of their lives. 尽管为了得到那条本不该是内科医生希冀的黑色丝带，他可能会在王宫里刻意从口袋中掉落祈祷书。但在他心里，他对一切都是不屑一顾的。他深深地鄙视着人们，因为他把人从头到脚都研究过了，在人一生最庄严和最卑微的行为中，他都能看到最真实的一面。
The qualities of a great man are often federative. 伟人的品质往往是综合的。
If among these colossal spirits one has more talent than wit, his wit is still superior to that of a man of whom it is simply stated that “he is witty.” 如果在一群伟人中，某个人才华甚于机智，那么他的机智相对于一般的“机智之人”而言，则更胜一筹。
Genius always presupposes moral insight. 天赋往往是以道德洞察力为前提依据的。
This insight may be applied to a special subject; but he who can see a flower must be able to see the sun. 这种洞察力或许该运用到某一学科，但是能够看见花朵的人必定能够看得见太阳。
The man who on hearing a diplomate he has saved ask, “How is the Emperor?” could say, “The courtier is alive; the man will follow!”—that man is not merely a surgeon or a physician, he is prodigiously witty also. 当那个人听到被他救活的外交官问他：“皇帝怎么样了？”，他会答道：“大臣还活着，皇帝自会无恙。”那么这个人就不仅是一名外科或内科医生，而且还是一个异常机智诙谐的人。
Hence a patient and diligent student of human nature will admit Desplein’s exorbitant pretensions, and believe—as he himself believed—that he might have been no less great as a minister than he was as a surgeon. 因此一个孜孜不倦耐心研究人性的人，必会认可德普兰夸张的自负，并相信——德普兰本人也认为如此——他若当部长并不会逊色于做医生。
Among the riddles which Desplein’s life presents to many of his contemporaries, we have chosen one of the most interesting, because the answer is to be found at the end of the narrative, and will avenge him for some foolish charges. 在许多同时代的人看来，德普兰的生活充满了不解之谜，我们选择了其中最有意思的来讲述，因为在文章结尾处，将会给出答案，这也会帮他洗清冤屈。
Of all the students in Desplein’s hospital, Horace Bianchon was one of those to whom he most warmly attached himself. 德普兰所在医院的所有学生中，他最关心的一个是霍勒斯·毕安训。
Before being a house surgeon at the Hotel-Dieu, Horace Bianchon had been a medical student lodging in a squalid boarding house in the Quartier Latin, known as the Maison Vauquer. 在成为市立医院实习医生之前，霍勒斯·毕安训是一名医科学生，寄宿在位于拉丁区一所名为伏盖的破公寓内。
This poor young man had felt there the gnawing of that burning poverty which is a sort of crucible from which great talents are to emerge as pure and incorruptible as diamonds, which may be subjected to any shock without being crushed. 这个可怜的年轻人在那里饱受贫困的煎熬，这是一种磨炼，伟大的天才人物最终会被锻造成纯洁无瑕、坚硬无比的钻石，经受得住任何打击。
In the fierce fire of their unbridled passions they acquire the most impeccable honesty, and get into the habit of fighting the battles which await genius with the constant work by which they coerce their cheated appetites. 在熊熊燃烧的激情之火中，他们被锻炼成至诚至信之人，养成艰苦奋斗的习惯，以不懈的努力去战胜虚无的欲望。
Horace was an upright young fellow, incapable of tergiversation on a matter of honor, going to the point without waste of words, and as ready to pledge his cloak for a friend as to give him his time and his night hours. 霍勒斯是一个正直的年轻人，在事关荣誉的问题不会拐弯抹角，说话直奔主题，随时愿意为朋友抵押自己的大衣，也愿意为他们牺牲自己的时间甚至熬夜。
Horace, in short, was one of those friends who are never anxious as to what they may get in return for what they give, feeling sure that they will in their turn get more than they give. 简而言之，霍勒斯属于这样一种朋友，他从不担心自己的付出会得到什么样的回报，他确信自己得到的回报会大于付出。
Most of his friends felt for him that deeply-seated respect which is inspired by unostentatious virtue, and many of them dreaded his censure. 质朴的美德让他的多数朋友深深地尊重他，许多朋友甚至害怕他的批评。
But Horace made no pedantic display of his qualities. 霍勒斯展现这些品质时，却绝不会显得迂腐。
He was neither a puritan nor a preacher; he could swear with a grace as he gave his advice, and was always ready for a jollification when occasion offered. 他既非清教徒，也非布道者，在给别人提建议时会有风度地赌咒骂人，遇到机会也会去凑凑热闹。
A jolly companion, not more prudish than a trooper, as frank and outspoken—not as a sailor, for nowadays sailors are wily diplomates—but as an honest man who has nothing in his life to hide, he walked with his head erect, and a mind content. 他是个不错的伙伴，不像骑兵那样假道学，他的坦诚直率也不像水手那样，因为如今的水手都像外交官一样狡黠。他就像个真诚的年轻人，生活中无所隐瞒。他走起路来昂首阔步，志得意满。
In short, to put the facts into a word, Horace was the Pylades of more than one Orestes—creditors being regarded as the nearest modern equivalent to the Furies of the ancients. 总之，将这些事实浓缩成一句话，霍勒斯是许多俄瑞斯忒斯眼中的皮拉德斯，而债主们则是复仇女神在今天最真实的形象。
He carried his poverty with the cheerfulness which is perhaps one of the chief elements of courage, and, like all people who have nothing, he made very few debts. 他欣然地接受贫困，那或许是他勇气的重要来源之一。像所有一无所有的人一样，他很少借债。
As sober as a camel and active as a stag, he was steadfast in his ideas and his conduct. 如骆驼般冷静，似雄鹿般有活力，他的思想与行为坚定无比。
The happy phase of Bianchon’s life began on the day when the famous surgeon had proof of the qualities and the defects which, these no less than those, make Doctor Horace Bianchon doubly dear to his friends. 霍勒斯·毕安训的优点和缺点一样让他的朋友们感到亲切。当那位著名的外科医生了解到他这一点时，他的幸运时刻来临了。
When a leading clinical practitioner takes a young man to his bosom, that young man has, as they say, his foot in the stirrup. 当一位临床医学的领军人物将这个年轻人庇护在自己的翅膀下精心栽培时，人们都说这位年轻人前程无虞。
Desplein did not fail to take Bianchon as his assistant to wealthy houses, where some complimentary fee almost always found its way into the student’s pocket, and where the mysteries of Paris life were insensibly revealed to the young provincial; he kept him at his side when a consultation was to be held, and gave him occupation; sometimes he would send him to a watering-place with a rich patient; in fact, he was making a practice for him. 德普兰每次去富人家诊疗，都让毕安训作为助理跟着，一些答谢费总能落到他这个学生的腰包，一些巴黎生活的神秘无意间显现在这位外省来的年轻人面前。有会诊时，他让毕安训呆在自己这边工作。有时也会让他随有钱的病人去矿泉疗养院。事实上，他有意让他提前实践。
The consequence was that in the course of time the Tyrant of surgery had a devoted ally. 结果，一段时间后，这位外科医学上的暴君培养出了一名忠心的追随者。
These two men—one at the summit of honor and of his science, enjoying an immense fortune and an immense reputation; the other a humble Omega, having neither fortune nor fame—became intimate friends. 这两个人，一个正处于事业与荣誉的巅峰状态，享有巨大的财富与极高的赞誉，而另一个只是无名之辈，既无财富也无声誉，而他们却成了亲密无间的朋友。
The great Desplein told his house surgeon everything; the disciple knew whether such or such a woman had sat on a chair near the master, or on the famous couch in Desplein’s surgery, on which he slept. 伟大的德普兰对这位实习医生无所不谈，这位徒弟清楚某个女人是否坐过老师旁边的椅子上，或者坐过手术室德普兰睡觉的那张有名的长沙发。
Bianchon knew the mysteries of that temperament, a compound of the lion and the bull, which at last expanded and enlarged beyond measure the great man’s torso, and caused his death by degeneration of the heart. 毕安训了解这位兼有狮子和公牛性情特点的老师不为人知的秘密，这一性情不断扩展与膨胀，最终他的身躯不堪承受，心脏功能恶化导致其死亡。
He studied the eccentricities of that busy life, the schemes of that sordid avarice, the hopes of the politician who lurked behind the man of science; he was able to foresee the mortifications that awaited the only sentiment that lay hid in a heart that was steeled, but not of steel. 他研究德普兰匆忙一生中所有的怪癖，那些贪得无厌的阴谋以及隐藏在医生头衔背后对政治的希冀。德普兰有一颗冷漠的心，但绝非天生就无情，其中埋藏的唯一一点儿感情等来的却是失望的结果，对此毕安训能够预见。
One day Bianchon spoke to Desplein of a poor water-carrier of the Saint-Jacques district, who had a horrible disease caused by fatigue and want; this wretched Auvergnat had had nothing but potatoes to eat during the dreadful winter of 1821. 有一天，毕安训对德普兰讲述了一个圣雅克地区贫苦的运水工的事，这位运水工由于劳累和生活困苦而得了可怕的疾病。1821年那个可怕的冬天，这个可怜的奥弗涅人除了土豆以外没有任何东西可以吃。
Desplein left all his visits, and at the risk of killing his horse, he rushed off, followed by Bianchon, to the poor man’s dwelling, and saw, himself, to his being removed to a sick house, founded by the famous Dubois in the Faubourg Saint-Denis. 德普兰弃他的患者于不顾，在毕安训的追随下，骑着马飞奔到了那个可怜人的住处，差点儿把马累垮。他亲自带这个可怜人搬到了著名的杜布瓦在圣德尼市郊创建的疗养院。
Then he went to attend the man, and when he had cured him he gave him the necessary sum to buy a horse and a water-barrel. 然后，他去给那个人看病，并且在治愈后，给了他一笔钱让他买了一匹马和一个水桶。
This Auvergnat distinguished himself by an amusing action. 这个奥弗涅人由于这段佳话出名了。
One of his friends fell ill, and he took him at once to Desplein, saying to his benefactor, “I could not have borne to let him go to any one else!” 他的一个朋友生病，他便立刻带他去见德普兰，并对这位恩人说：“我不忍心让他去找其他人。”
Rough customer as he was, Desplein grasped the water-carrier’s hand, and said, “Bring them all to me.” 虽然这个病人并不富贵体面，但德普兰还是握住运水工的手说：“把他们都带到我这里来吧。”
He got the native of Cantal into the Hotel-Dieu, where he took the greatest care of him. 他把那个康塔尔人送进市立医院，在那里精心照顾他。
Bianchon had already observed in his chief a predilection for Auvergnats, and especially for water carriers; but as Desplein took a sort of pride in his cures at the Hotel-Dieu, the pupil saw nothing very strange in that. 毕安训已经注意到他的老师对奥弗涅人有一种偏爱，尤其是对运水工。但因为德普兰对自己在市立医院的工作一向颇感自豪，这个学生却也未曾注意到有什么奇怪之处。
One day, as he crossed the Place Saint-Sulpice, Bianchon caught sight of his master going into the church at about nine in the morning. 一天早晨，毕安训在穿过圣叙尔皮斯广场时，看到他的老师大约在九点钟时走进了一所教堂。
Desplein, who at that time never went a step without his cab, was on foot, and slipped in by the door in the Rue du Petit-Lion, as if he were stealing into some house of ill fame. 那时，德普兰从来都是以马车代步的，那天他却步行到教堂，然后从小狮街那边的那扇门溜了进去，仿佛溜进了什么名声不好的地方。
The house surgeon, naturally possessed by curiosity, knowing his master’s opinions, and being himself a rabid follower of Cabanis (Cabaniste en dyable, with the y, which in Rabelais seems to convey an intensity of devilry)—Bianchon stole into the church, and was not a little astonished to see the great Desplein, the atheist, who had no mercy on the angels—who give no work to the lancet, and cannot suffer from fistula or gastritis—in short, this audacious scoffer kneeling humbly, and where? 这位实习医生自然会受到好奇心的驱使，因为他知道老师的立场，而且他自己也是个激进的卡巴尼斯主义者，卡巴尼斯主义者都坚持唯物主张。他悄悄走进教堂，当他看到伟大的德普兰，这位毫不怜惜天使（天使倒用不着他动手术刀，因为他们不会生瘘管或胃炎），放肆嘲笑上帝的无神论者，正谦卑地跪在那里时，着实大吃一惊，这在什么地方呢？
In the Lady Chapel, where he remained through the mass, giving alms for the expenses of the service, alms for the poor, and looking as serious as though he were superintending an operation. 这是圣母堂，在这里他听了整场弥撒，为礼拜仪式的花费捐款，为穷人施舍财物，他表情严肃，仿佛在监督一场手术。
“He has certainly not come here to clear up the question of the Virgin’s delivery,” said Bianchon to himself, astonished beyond measure. “他来这里肯定不是为了弄清楚圣母产子的问题，”毕安训暗想，他感到非常震惊，
“If I had caught him holding one of the ropes of the canopy on Corpus Christi day, it would be a thing to laugh at; but at this hour, alone, with no one to see—it is surely a thing to marvel at!” “如果在圣体节我撞到他手牵华盖上的绳子游行，只会一笑置之。然而，此刻他是独自一人，没人看见，这确实令人惊讶！”
Bianchon did not wish to seem as though he were spying the head surgeon of the Hotel-Dieu; he went away. 毕安训不想弄得好像自己在偷偷监视市立医院的首席外科医生一样，于是便离开了。
As it happened, Desplein asked him to dine with him that day, not at his own house, but at a restaurant. 恰巧那天德普兰请他吃饭，不是在家里，而是在外面的餐厅。
At dessert Bianchon skilfully contrived to talk of the mass, speaking of it as mummery and a farce. 吃甜点的时候，毕安训巧妙迂回，谈到了教堂的弥撒，将其说成是做作的表演和闹剧。
“A farce,” said Desplein, “which has cost Christendom more blood than all Napoleon’s battles and all Broussais’ leeches. “这场闹剧，”德普兰说，“使基督徒抛洒的热血比拿破仑所有的战役和布鲁塞所有的蚂蝗让人流的血都多。
The mass is a papal invention, not older than the sixth century, and based on the Hoc est corpus. 弥撒是教皇发明的，至今不超过六个世纪，其依据是‘这是我的身体’这句话。
What floods of blood were shed to establish the Fete-Dieu, the Festival of Corpus Christi—the institution by which Rome established her triumph in the question of the Real Presence, a schism which rent the Church during three centuries! 为确立圣体瞻仰节，无数人抛洒鲜血。罗马教廷通过确立这一节日，证明自己在圣体存在问题上所取得的胜利，而这一问题所引发的教派分裂使得教会动荡了近三个世纪！
The wars of the Count of Toulouse against the Albigenses were the tail end of that dispute. 图卢兹伯爵和阿尔比教派的战争为这场争端画上了句号。
The Vaudois and the Albigenses refused to recognize this innovation.” 伏多瓦派和阿尔比派都拒绝承认这一发明。”
In short, Desplein was delighted to disport himself in his most atheistical vein; a flow of Voltairean satire, or, to be accurate, a vile imitation of the Citateur. 随后，德普兰又颇有兴致地阐述他的无神论观点，作了一大串伏尔泰式的嘲讽，或者更确切地说，是对《语录》的拙劣模仿。
“Hallo! where is my worshiper of this morning?” said Bianchon to himself. “嘿！今天早上那个做弥撒的人到哪里去了？”毕安训想。
He said nothing; he began to doubt whether he had really seen his chief at Saint-Sulpice. 他什么都没有说，他开始怀疑早晨是否真的在圣叙尔皮斯教堂见到过自己的老师。
Desplein would not have troubled himself to tell Bianchon a lie, they knew each other too well; they had already exchanged thoughts on quite equally serious subjects, and discussed systems de natura rerum, probing or dissecting them with the knife and scalpel of incredulity. 德普兰不必大费周折去撒谎，因为他们彼此十分了解。在同样重要的问题上他们早已彼此交换过观点，并且讨论过《物性论》的体系，用质疑的手术刀对这些问题进行过解剖分析。
Three months went by. 三个月过去了。
Bianchon did not attempt to follow the matter up, though it remained stamped on his memory. 毕安训没有想去探究这件事，尽管它已经深深地烙在了他的脑海里。
One day that year, one of the physicians of the Hotel-Dieu took Desplein by the arm, as if to question him, in Bianchon’s presence. 这一年的某天，市立医院的一名内科医生拽着德普兰的胳膊，当着毕安训的面似乎在质问他。
“What were you doing at Saint-Sulpice, my dear master?” said he. “亲爱的老师，您在圣叙尔皮斯教堂做什么呢？”他问。
“I went to see a priest who has a diseased knee-bone, and to whom the Duchesse d’Angouleme did me the honor to recommend me,” said Desplein. “我去见一位膝盖骨有毛病的神父，我有幸得到了昂古莱姆公爵夫人的推荐。”德普兰回答。
The questioner took this defeat for an answer; not so Bianchon. 质疑者就此作罢，而毕安训却没有。
“Oh, he goes to see damaged knees in church!—He went to mass,” said the young man to himself. “哦，他去教堂是为了膝盖有疾的牧师吗？他去是为了做弥撒。”这位年轻人心想。
Bianchon resolved to watch Desplein. 毕安训决定观察德普兰。
He remembered the day and hour when he had detected him going into Saint-Sulpice, and resolved to be there again next year on the same day and at the same hour, to see if he should find him there again. 他记得见到德普兰去圣叙尔皮斯教堂的日子和钟点，于是他决定明年的同一天、同一钟点再去那里，看是否能再看见他。
In that case the periodicity of his devotion would justify a scientific investigation; for in such a man there ought to be no direct antagonism of thought and action. 那样的话，德普兰有规律的虔诚表现就更值得去研究。因为对于这样一个人而言，思想与行为之间是不应该存在明显的对立的。
Next year, on the said day and hour, Bianchon, who had already ceased to be Desplein’s house surgeon, saw the great man’s cab standing at the corner of the Rue de Tournon and the Rue du Petit-Lion, whence his friend jesuitically crept along by the wall of Saint-Sulpice, and once more attended mass in front of the Virgin’s altar. 第二年，毕安训已不是德普兰的实习生了。在同一天的同一时间，他看到这个伟大医生的马车停在图尔农街和小狮街相汇处的角落里，他这个朋友沿着圣叙尔皮斯教堂的墙脚悄悄溜了进去，再一次在圣母祭坛前做起弥撒。
It was Desplein, sure enough! 这就是德普兰，错不了！
The master-surgeon, the atheist at heart, the worshiper by chance. 这就是那位杰出的外科医生，内心深处是无神论者，偶尔才来做弥撒。
The mystery was greater than ever; the regularity of the phenomenon complicated it. 这个秘密更加扑朔迷离，这一规律性的现象使之更加复杂。
When Desplein had left, Bianchon went to the sacristan, who took charge of the chapel, and asked him whether the gentleman were a constant worshiper. 德普兰离开后，毕安训走到教堂司事身旁，询问他那位先生是否常来。
“For twenty years that I have been here,” replied the man, “M. Desplein has come four times a year to attend this mass. “我在这里待了二十年了，”那人答道，“德普兰先生每年有四次会来参加弥撒。
He founded it.” 是他创办的弥撒。”
“A mass founded by him!” said Bianchon, as he went away. “他创办的弥撒！”毕安训离开时思忖着，
“This is as great a mystery as the Immaculate Conception—an article which alone is enough to make a physician an unbeliever.” “这就像圣灵感孕一样神秘，单凭圣灵感孕这一条就足以让一名医生不信教。”
Some time elapsed before Doctor Bianchon, though so much his friend, found an opportunity of speaking to Desplein of this incident of his life. 尽管两人是好朋友，但毕安训医生过了一段时间后，才找到机会与德普兰谈起这件事。
Though they met in consultation, or in society, it was difficult to find an hour of confidential solitude when, sitting with their feet on the fire-dogs and their head resting on the back of an armchair, two men tell each other their secrets. 虽然在会诊或社交场合他们会见面，但很难找到一小时独处的时间来把脚放在壁炉架上，头靠在扶手椅背上，倾诉彼此的秘密。
At last, seven years later, after the Revolution of 1830, when the mob invaded the Archbishop’s residence, when Republican agitators spurred them on to destroy the gilt crosses which flashed like streaks of lightning in the immensity of the ocean of houses; when Incredulity flaunted itself in the streets, side by side with Rebellion, Bianchon once more detected Desplein going into Saint-Sulpice. 最后，过了七年，1830年革命之后，当暴民冲进大主教的府邸时，当在共和党的鼓动下，他们将浩瀚的房屋之海中那闪电一般的镀金十字架摧毁时，当街道上处处充斥着怀疑与反叛时，毕安训再一次看到德普兰走进圣叙尔皮斯教堂。
The doctor followed him, and knelt down by him without the slightest notice or demonstration of surprise from his friend. 毕安训跟着德普兰进入教堂，并跪在他的旁边。德普兰丝毫没有注意到毕安训，也没有表现出一点儿惊讶。
They both attended this mass of his founding. 两人都参加了他创办的弥撒。
“Will you tell me, my dear fellow,” said Bianchon, as they left the church, “the reason for your fit of monkishness? “亲爱的朋友，愿意告诉我吗？”离开教堂时，毕安训问道，“您为何每隔一阵就表现得这样虔诚？
I have caught you three times going to mass—You! 我已经三次撞见您做弥撒了，就是您！
You must account to me for this mystery, explain such a flagrant disagreement between your opinions and your conduct. 您必须向我揭开这个秘密，解释清楚您的思想与行为之间这种巨大的差异。
You do not believe in God, and yet you attend mass? 您不相信上帝，可是您为什么又会做弥撒呢？
My dear master, you are bound to give me an answer.” 我亲爱的老师，您一定得回答我。”
“I am like a great many devout people, men who on the surface are deeply religious, but quite as much atheists as you or I can be.” “我像许多虔诚的人一样，他们表面上笃信宗教，但事实上却像你我一样是无神论者。”
And he poured out a torrent of epigrams on certain political personages, of whom the best known gives us, in this century, a new edition of Moliere’s Tartufe. 接着他滔滔不绝地挖苦了一大堆著名的政客，其中最广为人知的那位，向我们展示了莫里哀笔下的答尔丢夫在本世纪的新版形象。
“All that has nothing to do with my question,” retorted Bianchon. “您所说的与我的问题没任何关系。”毕安训抱怨道，
“I want to know the reason for what you have just been doing, and why you founded this mass.” “我想知道您方才为什么那么做，另外，您为什么要创办这场弥撒？”
“Faith! my dear boy,” said Desplein, “I am on the verge of the tomb; I may safely tell you about the beginning of my life.” “相信我！亲爱的孩子，”德普兰说，“我是一个快进坟墓的人了。把我早年的事情告诉你也无妨。”
At this moment Bianchon and the great man were in the Rue des Quatre-Vents, one of the worst streets in Paris. 此时毕安训和伟大的医生正站在四面风街上，这是巴黎最糟糕的街道之一。
Desplein pointed to the sixth floor of one of the houses looking like obelisks, of which the narrow door opens into a passage with a winding staircase at the end, with windows appropriately termed “borrowed lights”—or, in French, jours de souffrance. 德普兰指着一所房子的七楼，这所房子的形状像是方尖碑，窄门面向一条通道，通道尽头是弯弯曲曲的楼梯，有几扇取光的窗户，用法语说就是“采光窗”。
It was a greenish structure; the ground floor occupied by a furniture-dealer, while each floor seemed to shelter a different and independent form of misery. 这是一座浅绿色的建筑，一层住着一位家具商，其余各层似乎都住着遭遇各不相同的可怜人。
Throwing up his arm with a vehement gesture, Desplein exclaimed: 德普兰激动地一挥手臂，叹道：
“I lived up there for two years.” “我在上面住过两年。”
“I know; Arthez lived there; I went up there almost every day during my first youth; we used to call it then the pickle-jar of great men! “我知道，阿泰兹就曾住过那里。我年轻时几乎每天都去那儿，我们曾经将它称为伟人的‘腌菜坛’！
What then?” 后来呢？”
“The mass I have just attended is connected with some events which took place at the time when I lived in the garret where you say Arthez lived; the one with the window where the clothes line is hanging with linen over a pot of flowers. “我方才参加的弥撒与我早年住的那间阁楼里发生的事情有关，就是你说阿泰兹住过的那间——窗外晾衣绳上挂着衣服，衣服下面还有一盆花的那间。
My early life was so hard, my dear Bianchon, that I may dispute the palm of Paris suffering with any man living. 亲爱的毕安训，我早年的生活十分窘迫，比任何一个在巴黎的人吃的苦都多。
I have endured everything: hunger and thirst, want of money, want of clothes, of shoes, of linen, every cruelty that penury can inflict. 我什么样的苦都吃过：没得吃也没得喝，没钱，没衣服，没鞋子，没日用的织物，贫困带来的一切困苦我都承受过。
I have blown on my frozen fingers in that pickle-jar of great men, which I should like to see again, now, with you. 在那个伟人的腌菜坛里，我对着冻僵的手指哈着热气，现在我想跟你一起再看看那地方。
I worked through a whole winter, seeing my head steam, and perceiving the atmosphere of my own moisture as we see that of horses on a frosty day. 整个冬天我不停地工作，都能看得见自己头上冒水汽，水汽是我自己身体里的水分，就像冰霜天气里马身上冒的汽一样。
I do not know where a man finds the fulcrum that enables him to hold out against such a life. 我不知道一个人哪来的力量支撑去对抗这样的一种生活。
“I was alone, with no one to help me, no money to buy books or to pay the expenses of my medical training; I had not a friend; my irascible, touchy, restless temper was against me. “我孤身一人，没有人帮助，买不起书，也交不起医学院的学费。我没有朋友，暴躁、敏感、不安的性格使我交不到朋友。”
No one understood that this irritability was the distress and toil of a man who, at the bottom of the social scale, is struggling to reach the surface. 没有人理解我暴躁脾气的背后体现的是一个社会最底层的人一心想往上爬的痛苦和艰辛。
Still, I had, as I may say to you, before whom I need wear no draperies, I had that ground-bed of good feeling and keen sensitiveness which must always be the birthright of any man who is strong enough to climb to any height whatever, after having long trampled in the bogs of poverty. 但是，我可以跟你说，在你面前我可以毫无保留，我本性心肠很软，易被感动，这是任何一个在贫困的沼泽中艰难跋涉后，攀登上高峰的人所具有的本性。
I could obtain nothing from my family, nor from my home, beyond my inadequate allowance. 除了我寥寥无几的生活费外，我从家庭和故乡得不到任何资助。
In short, at that time, I breakfasted off a roll which the baker in the Rue du Petit-Lion sold me cheap because it was left from yesterday or the day before, and I crumbled it into milk; thus my morning meal cost me but two sous. 总之，那时候，我早餐只吃一个小圆面包，那是小狮街面包店老板便宜卖给我的，因为面包是一两天前剩下的。我将它弄碎泡在牛奶里吃，因此早餐只需要花两个苏。
I dined only every other day in a boarding-house where the meal cost me sixteen sous. 我隔一天才吃一次公寓供的晚饭，那需要十六个苏。
You know as well as I what care I must have taken of my clothes and shoes. 你跟我一样清楚，衣服和鞋子我得多小心地穿。
I hardly know whether in later life we feel grief so deep when a colleague plays us false as we have known, you and I, on detecting the mocking smile of a gaping seam in a shoe, or hearing the armhole of a coat split, I drank nothing but water; I regarded a cafe with distant respect. 我不知道后来当我们被同行算计时，那心情是否比得上你我当初发现一只鞋子裂了口，或者是听见衣服袖口撕开一道缝那样难过。我只能喝水，对咖啡却只能敬而远之。
Zoppi’s seemed to me a promised land where none but the Lucullus of the pays Latin had a right of entry. 佐皮咖啡馆对我而言仿佛一片乐土，只有拉丁语国家卢库勒斯那样的人才有权进去。
‘Shall I ever take a cup of coffee there with milk in it?’ said I to myself, ‘or play a game of dominoes?’ ‘我什么时候能在那里喝上一杯牛奶咖啡？’我心里想，‘或者玩一局多米诺骨牌？’
“I threw into my work the fury I felt at my misery. “我把困境中感受到的愤怒转化成了工作的动力。
I tried to master positive knowledge so as to acquire the greatest personal value, and merit the position I should hold as soon as I could escape from nothingness. 我努力掌握有用的知识，以此获得最大的个人价值，一旦脱离了一贫如洗的生活后，能够配得上我到时候所处的位置。
I consumed more oil than bread; the light I burned during these endless nights cost me more than food. 我用掉的灯油比吃掉的面包还多，这些漫漫长夜的照明费超过了我的食品花费。
It was a long duel, obstinate, with no sort of consolation. 这种努力是漫长的、艰苦的，而且还得不到慰藉。
I found no sympathy anywhere. 没有任何人同情我。
To have friends, must we not form connections with young men, have a few sous so as to be able to go tippling with them, and meet them where students congregate? 要想交朋友，不是必须得和年轻人来往吗？口袋里得有几个苏，好和他们去喝几杯，或者去学生聚会的场所？
And I had nothing! 可是，我一贫如洗！
And no one in Paris can understand that nothing means nothing. 在巴黎，没有人能懂，一贫如洗就意味着一无所有。
When I even thought of revealing my beggary, I had that nervous contraction of the throat which makes a sick man believe that a ball rises up from the oesophagus into the larynx. 有时，我甚至想让人知道我穷困潦倒，而我的喉咙却发紧，就像一个病人感觉一个小球从食管升到了咽喉。
“In later life I have met people born to wealth who, never having wanted for anything, had never even heard this problem in the rule of three: A young man is to crime as a five-franc piece is to X. “后来，我遇到一些生来就很富有的人，他们从来就没缺过什么，甚至从未听说过这个比例运算问题：年轻人之于犯罪，就等于五法郎之于未知数。
—These gilded idiots say to me, ‘Why did you get into debt? 这些有钱的白痴问我：‘你怎么会欠债呢？
Why did you involve yourself in such onerous obligations?’ 为什么你把自己卷入到沉重的债务中？’
They remind me of the princess who, on hearing that the people lacked bread, said, ‘Why do not they buy cakes?’ 这让我想起了那个故事，公主听说人们吃不上面包时说：‘他们为什么不买蛋糕呢？’
I should like to see one of these rich men, who complain that I charge too much for an operation,—yes, I should like to see him alone in Paris without a sou, without a friend, without credit, and forced to work with his five fingers to live at all! 我希望看到那些抱怨我手术费收得太高的富人中的一员——是的，我希望看到他孤身一人在巴黎，身无分文，没有朋友，借不到债，被迫靠自己的双手去生活！
What would he do? 他会做什么呢？

But he believed. 但是他有信仰。
He had a religious conviction; had I any right to dispute it? 他有坚定的宗教信仰，我有什么权利去反驳这一点吗？
He had spoken to me timidly of masses said for the repose of the dead; he would not impress it on me as a duty, thinking that it would be a form of repayment for his services. 他曾胆怯地对我说弥撒可以让死者安息，他并未强求我为他做弥撒，他怕这会成为让我回报他的一种方式。
As soon as I had money enough I paid to Saint-Sulpice the requisite sum for four masses every year. 当我有钱了之后，就给圣叙尔皮斯教堂弥撒所需的钱，让他们每年做四次弥撒。
As the only thing I can do for Bourgeat is thus to satisfy his pious wishes, on the days when that mass is said, at the beginning of each season of the year, I go for his sake and say the required prayers; and I say with the good faith of a sceptic—‘Great God, if there is a sphere which Thou hast appointed after death for those who have been perfect, remember good Bourgeat; and if he should have anything to suffer, let me suffer it for him, that he may enter all the sooner into what is called Paradise.’ 我唯一能为布尔雅做的，就是满足他虔诚的愿望，在弥撒的当天，也就是每个季节的第一天，我都去参加，并按要求祈祷。我以一个无神论者的善意说：‘伟大的上帝，若真的有一个地方是你用来安置生前完美的人，请不要忘记善良的布尔雅。如果有什么不幸要他忍受的话，请让我代替他吧，请保佑他尽快进入那个叫做天堂的地方。’
“That, my dear fellow, is as much as a man who holds my opinions can allow himself. “我的朋友，对于我这样信仰的人来说，那就是我能做的一切。
But God must be a good fellow; He cannot owe me any grudge. 而上帝肯定是个好人，是不会怨恨我的。
I swear to you, I would give my whole fortune if faith such as Bourgeat’s could enter my brain.” 我向你起誓，如果布尔雅的信仰能进入我的大脑，我愿意献出所有财产。”
Bianchon, who was with Desplein all through his last illness, dares not affirm to this day that the great surgeon died an atheist. 在德普兰最后的病痛中，毕安训一直陪伴着他，直到今天毕安训都不敢确定，这位伟大的医生过世时还是不是个无神论者。
Will not those who believe like to fancy that the humble Auvergnat came to open the gate of Heaven to his friend, as he did that of the earthly temple on whose pediment we read the words—“A grateful country to its great men.” 那些信教的人愿意猜想，是那个卑微的奥弗涅人为他的朋友德普兰开启了天堂之门，正如他曾为他开启人生殿堂之门一样，那殿堂的三角楣饰上写着“感激所有伟人的国度”。
PARIS, January 1836. 1836年1月于巴黎
