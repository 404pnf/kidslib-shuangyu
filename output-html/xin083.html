Chapter I 第一章
The traveller in school-books, who vouched in dryest tones for the fidelity to fact of the following narrative, used to add a ring of truth to it by opening with a nicety of criticism on the heroine’s personality. 课本中讲故事的流浪者会扯着干哑的嗓子吹嘘下面故事的真实性。他在开场白中常常对女主角的性格稍加评论，让故事显得很真实。
People were wrong, he declared, when they surmised that Baptista Trewthen was a young woman with scarcely emotions or character. 他声称，人们把巴普蒂斯塔·特鲁森看作是一个几乎没有感情、性格模糊的年轻女子，这种看法是错误的。
There was nothing in her to love, and nothing to hate—so ran the general opinion. 她身上既没有可爱之处，也没有可憎之处——一般人都这么认为。
That she showed few positive qualities was true. 诚然，她身上很少能展现出优良的品质。
The colours and tones which changing events paint on the faces of active womankind were looked for in vain upon hers. 她不同于一些活泼女子，任何外部事情的变化都不能让她的面容有一丝变化。
But still waters run deep; and no crisis had come in the years of her early maidenhood to demonstrate what lay hidden within her, like metal in a mine. 但静水流深，早年的她没经历过危难，因此她那如矿藏般深藏的内心也就无从显示出来。
She was the daughter of a small farmer in St. Maria’s, one of the Isles of Lyonesse beyond Off-Wessex, who had spent a large sum, as there understood, on her education, by sending her to the mainland for two years. 她是一个小农场主的女儿，来自外威塞克斯里昂尼斯群岛上的圣玛丽亚岛，她的父亲在她的教育上不惜重金（在当地看来是这样的），将她送往内陆学习了两年。
At nineteen she was entered at the Training College for Teachers, and at twenty-one nominated to a school in the country, near Tor-upon-Sea, whither she proceeded after the Christmas examination and holidays. 她十九岁进入师范学院，二十一岁到“多赫海”附近的一所乡村学校任教，圣诞节考试和假期结束后，她前往那里任教。
The months passed by from winter to spring and summer, and Baptista applied herself to her new duties as best she could, till an uneventful year had elapsed. 冬去春来，春末夏至，几个月过去了，巴普蒂斯塔尽最大努力集中精神履行新的职责，平淡的一年就这么过去了。
Then an air of abstraction pervaded her bearing as she walked to and fro, twice a day, and she showed the traits of a person who had something on her mind. 后来，在每天两次往返的路上，她的神情举止有些心不在焉，看起来一副心事重重的样子。
A widow, by name Mrs. Wace, in whose house Baptista Trewthen had been provided with a sitting-room and bedroom till the school-house should be built, noticed this change in her youthful tenant’s manner, and at last ventured to press her with a few questions. 在校舍完工前，巴普蒂斯塔·特鲁森暂时住在寡妇韦斯夫人的家里，韦斯夫人向她提供了一间起居室和卧室。这时，韦斯夫人注意到了她的这位年轻房客身上所发生的变化，终于鼓起勇气，问了几个问题。
‘It has nothing to do with the place, nor with you,’ said Miss Trewthen. “不是住处或者您的关系。”特鲁森小姐说道。
‘Then it is the salary?’ “那就是薪水了？”
‘No, nor the salary.’ “不，也不是薪水。”
‘Then it is something you have heard from home, my dear.’ “那就是收到家里的什么信了吧，亲爱的。”
Baptista was silent for a few moments. 巴普蒂斯塔沉默了一会儿。
‘It is Mr. Heddegan,’ she murmured. “是赫德甘先生，”她喃喃地说道，
‘Him they used to call David Heddegan before he got his money.’ “在他发迹前，人们叫他大卫·赫德甘。”
‘And who is the Mr. Heddegan they used to call David?’ “人们曾经称之为大卫·赫德甘的那位先生是谁？”
‘An old bachelor at Giant’s Town, St. Maria’s, with no relations whatever, who lives about a stone’s throw from father’s. “是圣玛丽亚巨人镇的一个单身汉，无亲无故，住处离我父亲家约一箭之遥。
When I was a child he used to take me on his knee and say he’d marry me some day. 我小的时候，他常常抱我坐到他的膝盖上，说有一天要娶我。
Now I am a woman the jest has turned earnest, and he is anxious to do it. 现在我长大了，玩笑话成了真，现在他急着娶我。
And father and mother says I can’t do better than have him.’ 我父母跟我说，嫁给他是最好的选择了。”
‘He’s well off?’ “他很有钱？”
‘Yes—he’s the richest man we know—as a friend and neighbour.’ “是的——他是我们认识的邻里朋友中最有钱的。”
‘How much older did you say he was than yourself?’ “你刚才说他比你大多少？”
‘I didn’t say. “我刚才没说。
Twenty years at least.’ 至少二十岁。”
‘And an unpleasant man in the bargain perhaps?’ “而且这个人还很讨厌，对不对？”
‘No—he’s not unpleasant.’ “不，也不是令人讨厌。”
‘Well, child, all I can say is that I’d resist any such engagement if it’s not palatable to ’ee. “哦，孩子，我能说的就是，如果你不喜欢这门婚事的话，我会反对的。
You are comfortable here, in my little house, I hope. 我希望你在我的小房子里住得舒适。
All the parish like ’ee: and I’ve never been so cheerful, since my poor husband left me to wear his wings, as I’ve been with ’ee as my lodger.’ 整个教区都喜欢你，自从我可怜的丈夫去世后，我从来没有像你住过来之后这么快乐。”
The schoolmistress assured her landlady that she could return the sentiment. 女教师向女房东保证，会报答她的这份情谊。
‘But here comes my perplexity,’ she said. “但是我要讲我的困惑了。”她说，
‘I don’t like keeping school. “我不喜欢在学校工作。
Ah, you are surprised—you didn’t suspect it. 哦，您觉得意外——您没这么想过。
That’s because I’ve concealed my feeling. 那是因为我一直以来掩藏了自己的感受。
Well, I simply hate school. 唉，我就是讨厌学校。
I don’t care for children—they are unpleasant, troublesome little things, whom nothing would delight so much as to hear that you had fallen down dead. 我不喜欢孩子——这些小东西不但讨人厌，还会惹麻烦，听到你倒地死了，他们会高兴得要命。
Yet I would even put up with them if it was not for the inspector. 即使这样，如果不是学监的话，我还可以忍受他们。
For three months before his visit I didn’t sleep soundly. 距离他来视察还有三个月，我就睡不着觉了。
And the Committee of Council are always changing the Code, so that you don’t know what to teach, and what to leave untaught. 校董会总是改变教学规则，你都不知道哪些该教，哪些不该教。
I think father and mother are right. 我觉得父母说得对。
They say I shall never excel as a schoolmistress if I dislike the work so, and that therefore I ought to get settled by marrying Mr. Heddegan. 他们说，如果我这么不喜欢教书，老师肯定当不好，因此，我应该嫁给赫德甘先生，过安定的生活。
Between us two, I like him better than school; but I don’t like him quite so much as to wish to marry him.’ 与学校相比，我更喜欢赫德甘，但我对他的喜欢也没到想嫁给他的程度。”
These conversations, once begun, were continued from day to day; till at length the young girl’s elderly friend and landlady threw in her opinion on the side of Miss Trewthen’s parents. 这些谈话一旦开始，就一天一天地继续下去，直到最后，这位上了年纪的朋友兼房东站到了特鲁森小姐父母这一边。
All things considered, she declared, the uncertainty of the school, the labour, Baptista’s natural dislike for teaching, it would be as well to take what fate offered, and make the best of matters by wedding her father’s old neighbour and prosperous friend. 她表示，考虑各方面因素，如学校工作既不安定又辛苦、巴普蒂斯塔天生讨厌教书等等，最好还是听从命运的安排，嫁给这位富有的老邻居兼父亲的老朋友，则可能是最好的选择。
The Easter holidays came round, and Baptista went to spend them as usual in her native isle, going by train into Off-Wessex and crossing by packet from Pen-zephyr. 复活节到来，巴普蒂斯塔与以往一样回到小岛上的家乡度过假期。她先坐火车到外威塞克斯，再从彭热法尔坐邮船过海。
When she returned in the middle of April her face wore a more settled aspect. 四月中旬她从家中回到学校后，脸上的神情更加安定了。
‘Well?’ said the expectant Mrs. Wace. “怎么样？”韦斯夫人满怀期待地说。
‘I have agreed to have him as my husband,’ said Baptista, in an off-hand way. “我已经答应嫁给他了，”她脱口而出，
‘Heaven knows if it will be for the best or not. “天知道这是不是最好的选择。
But I have agreed to do it, and so the matter is settled.’ 但我已经决定嫁给他，事情就这么定了。”
Mrs. Wace commended her; but Baptista did not care to dwell on the subject; so that allusion to it was very infrequent between them. 韦斯夫人称赞了她一番，但巴普蒂斯塔不愿意在这个问题上过多细谈，因此之后两个人也很少谈及这方面的事情。
Nevertheless, among other things, she repeated to the widow from time to time in monosyllabic remarks that the wedding was really impending; that it was arranged for the summer, and that she had given notice of leaving the school at the August holidays. 但在谈论其他事情的时候，她也不时在寡居的房东面前简略地提及，婚礼真的临近了，就安排在夏天，她已经通知学校，将会在八月假期离开学校。
Later on she announced more specifically that her marriage was to take place immediately after her return home at the beginning of the month aforesaid. 后来，她更明确地告诉房东，八月初回家后，马上就举行婚礼。
She now corresponded regularly with Mr. Heddegan. 现在她与赫德甘先生定期通信。
Her letters from him were seen, at least on the outside, and in part within, by Mrs. Wace. 韦斯夫人至少看到了赫德甘来信的信封和部分内容。
Had she read more of their interiors than the occasional sentences shown her by Baptista she would have perceived that the scratchy, rusty handwriting of Miss Trewthen’s betrothed conveyed little more matter than details of their future housekeeping, and his preparations for the same, with innumerable ‘my dears’ sprinkled in disconnectedly, to show the depth of his affection without the inconveniences of syntax. 如果韦斯夫人能够读到更多内容而非仅仅巴普蒂斯塔给她看的只言片语，她就会发现，特鲁森小姐未婚夫潦草生疏的字迹写的几乎全是未来家务的细节以及他对处理家务的打算，中间不连贯地夹杂着无数个“亲爱的”表达深情，但没影响到句式表述。
Chapter II 第二章
It was the end of July—dry, too dry, even for the season, the delicate green herbs and vegetables that grew in this favoured end of the kingdom tasting rather of the watering-pot than of the pure fresh moisture from the skies. 七月底，天气干燥，即使就这个季节来说，也太干燥了。这里虽处英国境内备受上天眷顾的一角，但嫩绿的药草和蔬菜也无法一尝天空清新纯净的湿润，只能品尝喷水壶的滋味。
Baptista’s boxes were packed, and one Saturday morning she departed by a waggonette to the station, and thence by train to Pen-zephyr, from which port she was, as usual, to cross the water immediately to her home, and become Mr. Heddegan’s wife on the Wednesday of the week following. 巴普蒂斯塔的箱子已经收拾好了。一个周六的早上，她先乘着四轮轻便马车到火车站，然后乘火车到彭热法尔，与往常一样，从那里直接坐船回家，在下周三成为赫德甘先生的妻子。
She might have returned a week sooner. 她本可以提前一周回去的。
But though the wedding day had loomed so near, and the banns were out, she delayed her departure till this last moment, saying it was not necessary for her to be at home long beforehand. 婚期已然临近，结婚预告已经发出去了，但她将回家推迟到最后一刻，认为没必要这么早就回去。
As Mr. Heddegan was older than herself, she said, she was to be married in her ordinary summer bonnet and grey silk frock, and there were no preparations to make that had not been amply made by her parents and intended husband. 她说，赫德甘先生年纪比自己大，结婚时她只需戴着平常的夏帽，穿上常穿的灰丝裙，她的父母以及未来的丈夫已经充分作好了一切准备。
In due time, after a hot and tedious journey, she reached Pen-zephyr. 一段炎热乏味的旅途之后，她按时到达了彭热法尔。
She here obtained some refreshment, and then went towards the pier, where she learnt to her surprise that the little steamboat plying between the town and the islands had left at eleven o’clock; the usual hour of departure in the afternoon having been forestalled in consequence of the fogs which had for a few days prevailed towards evening, making twilight navigation dangerous. 她用了些茶点，然后去了码头，在码头她意外得知往返于该镇与各岛间的小汽船已经于11点离港。几天来，傍晚时分连续下雾，航行危险，平时的发船时间由下午提前了。
This being Saturday, there was now no other boat till Tuesday, and it became obvious that here she would have to remain for the three days, unless her friends should think fit to rig out one of the island’ sailing-boats and come to fetch her—a not very likely contingency, the sea distance being nearly forty miles. 今天是周六，下周二之前不会再发船。毫无疑问，她将会在这里呆上三天，除非她的朋友们觉得应该置备好岛上的一艘小船，过来接她，而这种情况基本不可能，因为两地的海上距离接近四十英里。
Baptista, however, had been detained in Pen-zephyr on more than one occasion before, either on account of bad weather or some such reason as the present, and she was therefore not in any personal alarm. 巴普蒂斯塔以前也在彭热法尔滞留过不止一次，或者是由于天气恶劣，或者是由于现在这样的原因，因而她并没有感到惊慌。
But, as she was to be married on the following Wednesday, the delay was certainly inconvenient to a more than ordinary degree, since it would leave less than a day’s interval between her arrival and the wedding ceremony. 但是，她下周三就要结婚，这次耽搁造成的不便肯定要多于平常时候，到家时离婚礼只有不到一天的时间了。
Apart from this awkwardness she did not much mind the accident. 除了这方面的麻烦外，她对此事并不太在意。
It was indeed curious to see how little she minded. 看到她多么不在意才让人感到奇怪。
Perhaps it would not be too much to say that, although she was going to do the critical deed of her life quite willingly, she experienced an indefinable relief at the postponement of her meeting with Heddegan. 或许，这么说也不怎么过分，尽管去完成这样的终身大事是她心甘情愿的，但是得知可以推迟与赫德甘先生见面，她又感到了一种无以名状的轻松。
But her manner after making discovery of the hindrance was quiet and subdued, even to passivity itself; as was instanced by her having, at the moment of receiving information that the steamer had sailed, replied ‘Oh,’ so coolly to the porter with her luggage, that he was almost disappointed at her lack of disappointment. 得知无法坐船，她的反应平静默然，顺从得有些消极；例如当她得知汽船已经启程，她的回答是一个冷冷的“哦”，帮她提行李的脚夫对她没有一丝失望的反应都感到悻悻。
The question now was, should she return again to Mrs. Wace, in the village of Lower Wessex, or wait in the town at which she had arrived. 现在的问题是，她应当回下威塞克斯村韦斯夫人那里，还是在到达的这个小镇上等待。
She would have preferred to go back, but the distance was too great; moreover, having left the place for good, and somewhat dramatically, to become a bride, a return, even for so short a space, would have been a trifle humiliating. 她本来愿意回去，但距离太远了；况且，她已经永远离开那个地方了，而且结婚的决定也颇引人关注，因此回去哪怕是呆很短的时间，也会有点儿让人感到丢脸。
Leaving, then, her boxes at the station, her next anxiety was to secure a respectable, or rather genteel, lodging in the popular seaside resort confronting her. 她把行李箱留在了火车站，之后她就急着在她面前这座人口密集的海滨旅游胜地找到一个体面或者高雅的住处。
To this end she looked about the town, in which, though she had passed through it half-a-dozen times, she was practically a stranger. 为此，她在这座镇子上四处寻找，虽然她曾经路过这里六次，但实际上根本不熟悉这里。
Baptista found a room to suit her over a fruiterer’s shop; where she made herself at home, and set herself in order after her journey. 巴普蒂斯塔找到了一间合适的房间，楼下是水果店，在这里她感到回家般惬意，在旅途之后休整下来。
An early cup of tea having revived her spirits she walked out to reconnoitre. 她先喝了一碗茶，提了提神，便走到外面四处看看。
Being a schoolmistress she avoided looking at the schools, and having a sort of trade connection with books, she avoided looking at the booksellers; but wearying of the other shops she inspected the churches; not that for her own part she cared much about ecclesiastical edifices; but tourists looked at them, and so would she—a proceeding for which no one would have credited her with any great originality, such, for instance, as that she subsequently showed herself to possess. 作为教师，她避免看到学校，由于工作与书有关，她不去看那些书店，同时，她也厌倦了其他店铺，于是只看教堂；不是因为她关心教会那高高的建筑，而是因为旅行者都望着那里，因此她也朝那里张望；没有人认为她的所作所为是什么创举——这是后来发生的事情中，她所展现出的东西。
The churches soon oppressed her.
She tried the Museum, but came out because it seemed lonely and tedious. 她试着去参观博物馆，但很快也出来了，因为那里感觉寂寞乏味。
Yet the town and the walks in this land of strawberries, these headquarters of early English flowers and fruit, were then, as always, attractive. 这里盛产草莓，是英格兰早期的花果中心，小镇与来往的道路一如既往地引人入胜。
From the more picturesque streets she went to the town gardens, and the Pier, and the Harbour, and looked at the men at work there, loading and unloading as in the time of the Phoenicians. 从如画般的大街上，她漫步到镇上的花园、码头、港口，看着在那里工作的人们装卸着货物，像在腓尼基时期一样。
‘Not Baptista? “这不是巴普蒂斯塔吗？
Yes, Baptista it is!’ 是的，就是巴普蒂斯塔！”
The words were uttered behind her. 身后有人说话。
Turning round she gave a start, and became confused, even agitated, for a moment. 她转过身来，吃了一惊，一时困惑甚至焦虑起来。
Then she said in her usual undemonstrative manner, ‘O—is it really you, Charles?’ 接着，她用平常那不动声色的语气说：“哦，真的是你吗，查尔斯？”
Without speaking again at once, and with a half-smile, the new-comer glanced her over. 来人不急于马上回答，半笑着打量起她来。
There was much criticism, and some resentment—even temper—in his eye. 他的眼中充满批评、些许憎恶，甚至愤怒。
‘I am going home,’ continued she. “我要回家，”她继续说，
‘But I have missed the boat.’ “但没赶上船。”
He scarcely seemed to take in the meaning of this explanation, in the intensity of his critical survey. 他极度不满地打量着她，似乎根本没听明白她的解释。
‘Teaching still? “还教书哪？
What a fine schoolmistress you make, Baptista, I warrant!’ he said with a slight flavour of sarcasm, which was not lost upon her. 巴普蒂斯塔，你是位多么好的老师啊，我保证！”他略带嘲讽地说道，她也听出来了。
‘I know I am nothing to brag of,’ she replied. “我知道自己没什么可炫耀的，”她回答说，
‘That’s why I have given up.’ “所以，我已经放弃了。”
‘O—given up? “哦，放弃了？
You astonish me.’ 你真让我吃惊。”
‘I hate the profession.’ “我讨厌这个职业。”
‘Perhaps that’s because I am in it.’ “或许因为我干这个职业。”
‘O no, it isn’t. “哦不，不是。
But I am going to enter on another life altogether. 但我要开始一种全新的生活了。
I am going to be married next week to Mr. David Heddegan.’ 下周我将嫁给大卫·赫德甘先生。”
The young man—fortified as he was by a natural cynical pride and passionateness—winced at this unexpected reply, notwithstanding. 之前这个年轻人还保持着与生俱来、愤世嫉俗的傲气与活力，但听到这个出乎意料的回答后，他皱起眉来。
‘Who is Mr. David Heddegan?’ he asked, as indifferently as lay in his power. “大卫·赫德甘先生是谁？”他问道，尽力显出漠不关心的样子。
She informed him the bearer of the name was a general merchant of Giant’s Town, St. Maria’s island—her father’s nearest neighbour and oldest friend. 她告诉他，这个人是圣玛丽亚岛巨人镇的一名普通商人，同时也是他父亲的老朋友和距她家最近的邻居。
‘Then we shan’t see anything more of you on the mainland?’ inquired the schoolmaster. “这么说，我们在大陆上不会常常看到你了？”男教师问道。

She saw that he suspected nothing, and it made her task the harder. 她看到他什么都没怀疑，反而让她的任务更加艰巨。

‘Indeed!’ “是吗！”
‘Not a very long time before—a short time. “不是结婚前很长时间——离结婚时间很近。
And it is about a lover,’ she faltered. 事关一个情人。”她支支吾吾地说。

‘In hopes!’ “希望！”
‘Well, yes.’ “是的。”

‘I met my old sweetheart. “当时我碰到了我过去的情人。

She spoke desperately. 她绝望地说着。

How well it falls out!’ he exclaimed, snapping his, fingers over his head. 结局太好了！”他欢呼着，把举到头顶的指节掰得嘎嘣作响，

She looked at him without uttering a sound, till, as he still continued smiling joyfully, she said, ‘O—what do you mean! 她望着他，一声不响，看到他仍然快活地笑着，她说：“噢，你这是什么意思！

‘No—no! “不，不。
O, mee deer, your story helps me out of the most heart-aching quandary a poor man ever found himself in! 噢，亲爱的，你的故事帮助我这个可怜人解决了最痛心的窘境！
You see, it is this—I’ve got a tragedy, too; and unless you had had one to tell, I could never have seen my way to tell mine!’ 你看，是这样的——我也有个悲剧；如果不是你也有个悲剧要告诉我，我永远不知道该怎么告诉你！”
‘What is yours—what is it?’ she asked, with altogether a new view of things. “你的是什么呢？”她问道，完全从一个全新的角度看待。
‘Well—it is a bouncer; mine is a bouncer!’ said he, looking on the ground and wiping his eyes. “是个大悲剧，我的是个弥天大谎！”他注视着地面说道，擦拭着眼睛。
‘Not worse than mine?’ “不会比我的糟糕吧？”
‘Well—that depends upon how you look at it. “这取决于你怎么看了。
Yours had to do with the past alone; and I don’t mind it. 你的只是过去的事情，因此我就不计较了。
You see, we’ve been married a month, and it don’t jar upon me as it would if we’d only been married a day or two. 你看我们都结婚一个月了，就算我们刚结婚一两天，我也不会这么难受。
Now mine refers to past, present, and future; so that—’ 我的悲剧指的是过去，现在和未来，所以——”
‘Past, present, and future!’ she murmured. “过去、现在、未来！”她喃喃地说，
‘It never occurred to me that you had a tragedy, too.’ “我从没想到你也有个悲剧。”

‘In fact, four.’ “事实上，四个悲剧。”

‘I will—I will. “我会的，我会的。
But be considerate, I beg ’ee, mee deer. 亲爱的，我恳求你，体谅些。
Well—I wasn’t a bachelor when I married ’ee, any more than you were a spinster. 结婚的时候，我不是个单身汉，正如你不是个单身女人。
Just as you was a widow-woman, I was a widow-man.’ 你是寡妇，而我是鳏夫。”
‘Ah!’ said she, with some surprise. “啊！”她有些惊讶地说，
‘But is that all?—then we are nicely balanced,’ she added, relieved. “就这些吗？那我们就扯平了。”她长舒一口气，说道。
‘No—it is not all. “不，还没完。
There’s the point. 关键在这里。
I am not only a widower.’ 我不仅仅是个鳏夫。”
‘O, David!’ “噢，大卫！”
‘I am a widower with four tragedies—that is to say, four strapping girls—the eldest taller than you. “我是个有四个悲剧的鳏夫——就是说四个壮实的女儿——最大的比你还高。
Don’t ’ee look so struck—dumb-like! 不要这么惊讶，都说不出话来了！
It fell out in this way. 事情是这样的。
I knew the poor woman, their mother, in Pen-zephyr for some years; and—to cut a long story short—I privately married her at last, just before she died. 我在彭热法尔认识了那个可怜的女人，她们的母亲，长话短说吧，在她临终前不久，我终于私下与她结婚了。

I have not had courage to break it to ’ee, but I’ve seen lately that it would soon come to your ears, and that hev worried me.’ 我没有勇气同你说，但最近我发现，这件事情很快就要传到你的耳朵里了，这让我担心。”
‘Are they educated?’ said the ex-schoolmistress. “她们念过书吗？”之前做过教师的巴普蒂斯塔问道。
‘No. “没有。
I am sorry to say they have been much neglected; in truth, they can hardly read. 很遗憾，她们没得到足够的照顾；她们基本不识字。
And so I thought that by marrying a young schoolmistress I should get some one in the house who could teach ’em, and bring ’em into genteel condition, all for nothing. 因此我想如果娶一个年轻的女教师，这样家里就有人无偿教她们了，让她们有教养些。
You see, they are growed up too tall to be sent to school.’ 你明白，她们长得太高了，不能送去学校了。”
‘O, mercy!’ she almost moaned. “噢，可怜可怜我吧！”她几乎是呻吟了，
‘Four great girls to teach the rudiments to, and have always in the house with me spelling over their books; and I hate teaching, it kills me. “教四个大女孩基本知识，整天在家要研究她们的课本；我讨厌教书，简直是要我死。
I am bitterly punished—I am, I am!’ 我受到了严厉的处罚——是的，是的！”
‘You’ll get used to ’em, mee deer, and the balance of secrets—mine against yours—will comfort your heart with a sense of justice. “亲爱的，你会习惯她们的，两个秘密相抵——我的跟你的——会让你有一种公平感，心里好受些。
I could send for ’em this week very well—and I will! 我本周就把她们接来——就这么办吧！
In faith, I could send this very day. 说实话，我今天就该去接她们来。
Baptista, you have relieved me of all my difficulty!’ 巴普蒂斯塔，你帮我解脱了所有的困难。”
Thus the interview ended, so far as this matter was concerned. 就此问题的谈话就这么结束了。
Baptista was too stupefied to say more, and when she went away to her room she wept from very mortification at Mr. Heddegan’s duplicity. 巴普蒂斯塔目瞪口呆地说不出话来，回屋后，她哭了，为赫德甘先生的表里不一感到羞辱。
Education, the one thing she abhorred; the shame of it to delude a young wife so! 教书是她唯一痛恨的东西，这样欺骗年轻的妻子，太可耻了！
The next meal came round. 又该吃饭了。
As they sat, Baptista would not suffer her eyes to turn towards him. 他们坐下时，巴普蒂斯塔都不愿意看他一眼。
He did not attempt to intrude upon her reserve, but every now and then looked under the table and chuckled with satisfaction at the aspect of affairs. 他也没打算打搅她，只是不时地看着桌子下面发笑，对事情表示满意。
‘How very well matched we be!’ he said, comfortably. “我们多般配啊！”他惬意地说。
Next day, when the steamer came in, Baptista saw her husband rush down to meet it; and soon after there appeared at her door four tall, hipless, shoulderless girls, dwindling in height and size from the eldest to the youngest, like a row of Pan pipes; at the head of them standing Heddegan. 第二天，汽船开来的时候，巴普蒂斯塔眼见她的丈夫冲上前去迎接；不久，她的门外就站了四位高个女孩，个个屁股削平，肩膀耷拉着，从大到小，一字排开，高度逐渐递减，就好像一列排箫，赫德甘在最头上站着。
He smiled pleasantly through the grey fringe of his whiskers and beard, and turning to the girls said, ‘Now come forrard, and shake hands properly with your stepmother.’ 透过灰白的络腮胡子他露出高兴的笑容，转身对女孩们说：“快走上前，礼貌地和你们的继母握手。”
Thus she made their acquaintance, and he went out, leaving them together. 她们就这样认识了，他走出去，留下她们在一起。
On examination the poor girls turned out to be not only plain-looking, which she could have forgiven, but to have such a lamentably meagre intellectual equipment as to be hopelessly inadequate as companions. 了解了一下，她发现这些可怜的女孩不仅相貌平平——这一点她还可以原谅，而且才智方面也差得可怜，简直无可救药，和她们作伴实在是没指望。
Even the eldest, almost her own age, could only read with difficulty words of two syllables; and taste in dress was beyond their comprehension. 就连最大的那个——几乎与她差不多大，读双音节词还有困难；而且她们根本不懂得什么穿衣品位。
In the long vista of future years she saw nothing but dreary drudgery at her detested old trade without prospect of reward. 展望未来，她看到的只是重操她痛恨的旧业，做这样乏味却又得不到回报的苦差事。
She went about quite despairing during the next few days—an unpromising, unfortunate mood for a woman who had not been married six weeks. 之后几天她绝望地开始了工作，一个结婚不到六个星期的女人出现了这种前途暗淡、霉运缠身的情绪。
From her parents she concealed everything. 她对父母隐瞒了一切。
They had been amongst the few acquaintances of Heddegan who knew nothing of his secret, and were indignant enough when they saw such a ready-made household foisted upon their only child. 在赫德甘的相识中，巴普蒂斯塔的父母是少数不知道他秘密的人。他们看到他把这样一堆现成的家人强加于自己的独女身上，感到很气愤。
But she would not support them in their remonstrances. 但她并不支持他们的抗议。
‘No, you don’t yet know all,’ she said. “不，你们没了解所有的事情。”她说。
Thus Baptista had sense enough to see the retributive fairness of this issue. 巴普蒂斯塔很理智地看待这件事中惩罚的公平性。
For some time, whenever conversation arose between her and Heddegan, which was not often, she always said, ‘I am miserable, and you know it. 有一阵子，无论何时与赫德甘谈话，虽然谈话并不经常发生，她总是说：“我很悲惨，你知道。
Yet I don’t wish things to be otherwise.’ 但我宁愿这样。”
But one day when he asked, ‘How do you like ’em now?’ her answer was unexpected. 但有一天他问起：“你现在觉得她们怎么样了？”她的回答却出乎意料。
‘Much better than I did,’ she said, quietly. “比之前好多了。”她轻声说，
‘I may like them very much some day.’ “可能有一天，我会很喜欢她们的。”
This was the beginning of a serener season for the chastened spirit of Baptista Heddegan. 巴普蒂斯塔·赫德甘饱受煎熬的精神迎来了平静的季节。
She had, in truth, discovered, underneath the crust of uncouthness and meagre articulation which was due to their Troglodytean existence, that her unwelcomed daughters had natures that were unselfish almost to sublimity. 那些不受欢迎的女儿由于原始人般的生活，显得粗鲁、不善言辞，但在这些外表下，事实上她却发现了她们天性中崇高的无私。
The harsh discipline accorded to their young lives before their mother’s wrong had been righted, had operated less to crush them than to lift them above all personal ambition. 在母亲的错误得到纠正之前，她们年轻的生命经受的严苛没有压垮她们，而使她们超越了一切个人野心。
They considered the world and its contents in a purely objective way, and their own lot seemed only to affect them as that of certain human beings among the rest, whose troubles they knew rather than suffered. 她们用完全客观的视角看待世界和世界的事物，而在她们看来，自己的命运与他人一样，她们是认识命运中的痛苦，而不是亲身体验痛苦。
This was such an entirely new way of regarding life to a woman of Baptista’s nature, that her attention, from being first arrested by it, became deeply interested. 对于巴普蒂斯塔这样的人来说，这是一种全新的看待生活的视角，她先是被这种视角吸引，后来对它产生了浓厚的兴趣。
By imperceptible pulses her heart expanded in sympathy with theirs. 在不知不觉中，她的内心渐渐生出了与她们的共鸣。
The sentences of her tragi-comedy, her life, confused till now, became clearer daily. 对她的悲喜剧、对她的生活的判决，曾经是模糊的，直到现在才一天天清晰起来。
That in humanity, as exemplified by these girls, there was nothing to dislike, but infinitely much to pity, she learnt with the lapse of each week in their company. 在她们的陪伴下，一个星期又一个星期过去了，她从这些女孩身上认识到，人身上没有什么可恨之处，只有太多让人同情。
She grew to like the girls of unpromising exterior, and from liking she got to love them; till they formed an unexpected point of junction between her own and her husband’s interests, generating a sterling friendship at least, between a pair in whose existence there had threatened to be neither friendship nor love. 她渐渐喜欢上这些外表看起来平凡的女孩子，从喜欢又上升到爱；最后她们出人意料地成为连接她与丈夫兴趣的纽带，至少，她们在一对过去认为既不可能存在友情也不可能存在爱情的夫妇中间，建立起一种纯粹的友谊。
